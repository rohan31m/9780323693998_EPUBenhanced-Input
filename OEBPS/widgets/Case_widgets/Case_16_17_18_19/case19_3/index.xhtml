Objectives
  • • Describe the major metabolic effects of obesity.
  • • Discuss the humoral and neural mechanisms of appetite and satiety.
  • • Discuss the pathophysiological consequences and natural history of obesity.

A 47-year-old obese man presented with a 1-month history of fatigue and shortness of breath on exertion that was more severe than usual. He worked as a librarian and had a sedentary lifestyle. He weighed 406 pounds and was 5 feet 9 inches tall. Examination revealed a blood pressure of 148/96 mm Hg.


1
Which objective measure is used to judge obesity?
Show answer

Weight is judged using the body mass index (BMI), defined as the weight in kilograms divided by square of the height in meters (kg/m2). Obesity is defined as BMI of >30. In this individual, the BMI was 60.

2
What is the role of fat distribution in obesity-associated diseases?
Show answer

The distribution of stored fat is important: central, or visceral, obesity, in which fat accumulates in the trunk and in the abdominal cavity, is associated with a much higher risk for several diseases (e.g., metabolic syndrome, cardiovascular disease) than is diffuse accumulation of fat in subcutaneous tissue.

3
What are the humoral and neural mechanisms that control appetite and satiety?
Show answer

When sufficient energy is stored in adipose tissue and the individual is well fed, afferent adiposity signals (insulin, leptin, ghrelin, peptide YY) are delivered to the central neuronal processing units, in the hypothalamus. Here the adiposity signals inhibit anabolic circuits and activate catabolic circuits. The effector arms of these central circuits then influence energy balance by inhibiting food intake and promoting energy expenditure. This in turn reduces the energy stores, and proadiposity signals are blunted. Conversely, when energy stores are low, the available anabolic circuits take over, at the expense of catabolic circuits, to generate energy stores in the form of adipose tissue. Adiponectin, which is produced in adipose tissue, has multiple effects that influence these circuits: it directs fatty acids to muscle for oxidation, decreases the influx of fatty acids to the liver, and decreases glucose production in the liver, which causes an increase in insulin sensitivity and protects against the metabolic syndrome. In addition to its metabolic effects, adiponectin has antidiabetic, antiinflammatory, anti-atherogenic, antiproliferative, and cardioprotective effects. Its serum levels are lower in obese than in lean individuals.


4
What are the major metabolic abnormalities that are associated with obesity?
Show answer

Hyperlipidemia, hypercholesterolemia, low HDL cholesterol, type II diabetes, and metabolic syndrome are associated with obesity.

5
What is metabolic syndrome?
Show answer

Metabolic syndrome refers to a constellation of metabolic abnormalities common in obese patients. It is characterized by visceral or intra-abdominal adiposity, insulin resistance, hyperinsulinemia, glucose intolerance, hypertension, hypertriglyceridemia, and low HDL cholesterol.

Past medical history was significant for type 2 diabetes for the past 10 years and hypertension for the past 8 years. Laboratory testing revealed a hemoglobin A1C level of 8.7% (target ≤6.0%) and a fasting glucose level of 158 mg/dL (70–110 mg/dL).

6
How does increased adiposity lead to these metabolic changes?
Show answer

Obesity causes peripheral resistance to insulin and may indirectly decrease the sensitivity of islet cells to glucose. Obese individuals also often have evidence of systemic inflammation, which itself can contribute to insulin resistance. The precise relationship between obesity and systemic inflammation is uncertain; adipokines (cytokine-like factors released from adipocytes) are suspected to have a role. Activation of the inflammasome by free fatty acids may contribute by stimulating production of cytokines such as IL-1, which induce insulin resistance in tissues.

7
For which cardiovascular conditions is this patient at risk? What are the likely mechanisms?
Show answer

• Coronary artery disease and its complications (myocardial infarction [MI], ischemic heart disease, congestive heart failure) due to hypercholesterolemia, hypertension, and type II diabetes, all of which promote atherosclerosis of coronary arteries.

• Cor pulmonale (right-sided heart hypertrophy and failure) due to sleep apnea, hypoxemia, and chronic pulmonary vessel vasoconstriction.

• Stroke due to hypertension and atherosclerosis involving the carotid arteries and cerebral vessels.

  • Heart, gross
  • Right ventricular hypertrophy
  • Trabecular hypertrophy
8
Which types of malignancies are increased in obese patients? What are the mechanisms?
Show answer

Obesity is associated with multiple malignancies. The increased risk is greatest for cancers of the esophagus, pancreas, colon and rectum, breast, endometrium, kidney, thyroid, and gallbladder. Several mechanisms have been proposed:

• Insulin resistance in obese individuals leads to hyperinsulinemia and elevated levels of insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1), which stimulates insulin and insulin-like growth factor receptors. These receptors have tyrosine kinase activity and drive signals that stimulate growth and antagonize cell death, both of which may contribute to oncogenesis.

• Androgens are converted to estrogen by adipocytes, a reaction catalyzed by the enzyme aromatase. Estrogen production by adipocytes is enhanced in obese individuals due to the increased mass of adipose tissue and also because of other metabolic changes that result in increased synthesis of androgens by the ovaries and the adrenal glands. The resulting estrogen excess is associated with increased risk of breast and endometrial cancer.

• The proinflammatory state that is associated with obesity may itself be carcinogenic.


9
For which forms of hepatobiliary disease is this patient at risk?
Show answer

Obesity is associated with (1) fatty liver (steatosis); (2) nonalcoholic steatohepatitis, possibly due to insulin resistance and injury caused by toxic effects of free fatty acids; and (3) cholesterol gallstone-related disease, due to hypersaturation of bile with cholesterol.

  • Liver, histology
  • Fat vacuoles

The patient was treated with antihypertensive medications and his diabetes medications were adjusted. The possibility of bariatric surgery was discussed; however, he was lost to follow-up. Eighteen months later, he presented with a 2-week history of abdominal pain that he describes as “heartburn.”

10
What are some possible explanations for his symptoms?
Show answer

Gastroesophageal reflux is common in obese individuals, perhaps due to increased pressure on the stomach by abdominal fat or development of a hiatal hernia. Another possibility is angina, which can masquerade as heartburn. Due to the hypertriglyceridemia and low HDL seen in obesity, these patients have increased risk of coronary artery disease. Gallstone disease is also more common in obese patients and can cause colicky abdominal pain.

Objectives
  • • Describe the functions and mechanisms of action of steroid hormones.
  • • Compare and contrast the pathogenesis of the various causes of Cushing syndrome.
  • • Describe the tests used to differentiate the different causes of Cushing syndrome.

A 40-year-old woman presented to her physician with a 3-month history of weakness, primarily of her shoulders and thighs. She also reported increasing thirst, headache, and easy bruising. Review of systems revealed that her menstrual flow was decreased and had become irregular and that she felt depressed. She stated that she takes a daily multivitamin, but no medications. On physical examination, her face appeared puffy and she demonstrated truncal obesity. There were purple striae on the lateral aspects of her abdomen. Her blood pressure was 180/110 mm Hg. Strength in the proximal muscles of her shoulders and hips was reduced.

1
What is the probable diagnosis based on the clinical findings?
Show answer

“Moon” facies, truncal obesity, striae, hypertension, proximal muscle weakness, and menstrual abnormalities suggest a diagnosis of Cushing syndrome.

2
A diagnosis of Cushing syndrome was suspected. Which outpatient test should be performed to confirm or exclude the diagnosis?
Show answer

The simplest outpatient screening test to diagnose Cushing syndrome is the overnight plasma dexamethasone suppression test, in which 1 mg of dexamethasone is administered at 11:00 P.M. and a plasma cortisol is drawn at 8:00 A.M. the next day. Normally, the administered steroid inhibits the production of ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone) from the pituitary and hence reduces production of endogenous steroid hormone (feedback inhibition). If the plasma cortisol is less than 5 μg/dL, a diagnosis of Cushing syndrome is ruled out. When feedback inhibition fails, plasma cortisol of 10 μg/dL or more is consistent with the diagnosis of Cushing syndrome. At this point, further investigation is necessary to establish the cause.

Additional laboratory tests were ordered. Fasting serum glucose was 300 mg/dL (70–110 mg/dL). An overnight dexamethasone suppression test demonstrated a morning serum cortisol 15 μg/dL (<5 μg/dL).

3
What are the exogenous and endogenous causes of hypercortisolism?
Show answer

The vast majority of cases of hypercortisolism are due to therapeutic administration of exogenous glucocorticoids. Endogenous hypercortisolism is categorized as ACTH-dependent or ACTH-independent. ACTH-dependent hypercortisolism due to ACTH-secreting pituitary adenomas, which is responsible for approximately 70% of cases, is referred to as Cushing disease; Cushing syndrome refers to all other causes of hypercortisolism. Ectopic ACTH secretion by nonpituitary tumors, especially small cell carcinoma of the lung, is another cause of ACTH-dependent hypercortisolism. In most cases, ACTH-independent hypercortisolism is due to adrenal adenomas and carcinomas and less commonly due to macronodular hyperplasia of the adrenal gland.

Which tests are used to establish the diagnosis and to distinguish the endogenous causes of hypercortisolism?
Show answer

The laboratory diagnosis of Cushing syndrome is based on the following: (1) the 24-hour urine free-cortisol concentration, which is increased; and (2) loss of normal diurnal pattern of cortisol secretion. Determining the cause of Cushing syndrome depends on the serum ACTH and measurement of urinary steroid excretion after administration of dexamethasone (dexamethasone suppression test). The results of these tests fall into three general patterns:

• In pituitary Cushing syndrome, the most common form, ACTH levels are elevated and cannot be suppressed by the administration of a low dose of dexamethasone. Hence, there is no reduction in urinary excretion of corticosteroids. After higher doses of injected dexamethasone, however, the pituitary responds by reducing ACTH secretion, which is reflected by suppression of urinary steroid secretion.

• If Cushing syndrome is due to ectopic ACTH secretion, ACTH level is elevated, but its secretion is completely insensitive to low or high doses of exogenous dexamethasone.

• When Cushing syndrome is caused by an adrenal tumor, the ACTH level is quite low because of feedback inhibition of the pituitary. As with ectopic ACTH secretion, both low-dose and high-dose dexamethasone fail to suppress cortisol excretion.

The patient was admitted to the hospital for additional testing. Her plasma cortisol at 9:00 A.M. was 28 μg/dL (10–25 μg/dL) and at 6:00 P.M. was 25 μg/dL ( <10 μg/dL). The patient's 24-hour urine free cortisol was 200 μg/day ( <50 μg/day). Serum ACTH was 125 pg/mL (20–100 pg/mL). A low-dose dexamethasone suppression test with urine collected for 17-hydroxycorticosteroids (17-OHCS) yielded 12 mg of 17-OHCS/day ( <4 mg/day). The patient underwent a high-dose dexamethasone suppression test, which revealed suppression of urinary 17-OHCS by 70% to 3.6 mg/day after high-dose dexamethasone.

5
What is the definitive diagnosis based on the laboratory test results?
Show answer

This patient has Cushing syndrome based on (1) increased urine free cortisol; (2) lack of diurnal rhythm of serum cortisol (normally cortisol is significantly higher in the morning than in the evening); and (3) failure to suppress cortisol in the plasma and urine overnight in the low-dose dexamethasone suppression test. The fact that urinary 17-OHCS were suppressed by high-dose dexamethasone supports a diagnosis of pituitary adenoma (Cushing disease); if there were no suppression, the possibility of ectopic ACTH secretion (e.g., from a small cell lung carcinoma) is more likely.

6
What is the biochemical basis for this patient’s clinical symptoms?
Show answer

The clinical features of this patient are primarily due to excess cortisol, which is the major glucocorticoid, but mineralocorticoids also contribute. High levels of cortisol cause sodium and water retention, resulting in hypertension and potassium wasting with consequent hypokalemic alkalosis. The anti-insulin effects of cortisol, which stimulates hepatic gluconeogenesis and decreases peripheral glucose uptake, can cause impaired glucose tolerance or diabetes. The proteolytic effect of cortisol can result in osteoporosis, muscle wasting with weakness (myopathy), and thinning of the skin with increased bruising and purple striae. Adrenal androgens, such as DHEA and androstenedione, and their peripheral conversion to testosterone, account for the common findings of androgen excess, including acne, hirsutism, and menstrual disturbance. Psychiatric disturbance, particularly depression, may be a prominent feature of hypercortisolism.

Thereafter, the patient underwent imaging studies of the brain and adrenal glands. A 0.4 cm pituitary mass was identified and the adrenal glands were found to be diffusely enlarged.

7
How would the adrenal glands in this patient differ morphologically from the glands of patients on long-term corticosteroid therapy?
Show answer

In patients with Cushing syndrome (caused by a pituitary adenoma or primary adrenal cortical hyperplasia), the glands will be enlarged and nodular due to the increased ACTH stimulation, as in this example from a patient with a pituitary adenoma. Histologically, the cells will be enlarged and full of lipid droplets. The proliferating cells resemble normal zona fasciculata showing fairly uniform nuclei centrally and cytoplasm distended by lipid droplets. The high lipid content is because the cells store and utilize cholesterol as the substrate for producing steroid hormones in the smooth ER. By contrast, exogenous corticosteroid therapy suppresses ACTH release, leading to atrophy of adrenal cortical cells and depletion of lipids. In this image, from a patient treated with exogenous steroids, the reddish-brown strip of adrenal cortex is thinned to 1–2 mm, and the combined gland weight was less than 5 gm.

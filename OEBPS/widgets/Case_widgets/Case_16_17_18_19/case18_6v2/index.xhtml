Plaques and Scales

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast the clinical presentation, morphologic appearance, and etiology of the papulosquamous dermatoses.
  2. • Describe the various bullous diseases of the skin in terms of the pathogenesis, morphologic appearance, and clinical presentation.

An 18-year-old man presented to the dermatologist after developing itchy, scaly, erythematous lesions on the skin of his elbows, scalp, and knees over several months. Physical exam revealed well-demarcated plaques covered by silver-white scales (so-called “papulosquamous lesions”). The scaling was surrounded by erythema, and when the scale was lifted from one of the plaques, multiple minute bleeding points became visible.

  • Clinical presentation
  • Plaques
1
Which are the common dermatoses that can show papulosquamous lesions and how do their clinical appearances differ?
Show answer

Papulosquamous disorders present with both scales (“squames”) and papules. Three common papulosquamous dermatoses are psoriasis, eczematous dermatitis, and dermatophytosis (fungal infection). The silver-white color and the sharp demarcation seen in this patient are characteristic of psoriasis. The lesions of psoriasis often present on the knees, elbows, and scalp. The lower back and periumbilical region are also common sites of involvement. Eczematous dermatitis is often less well circumscribed and can include vesicles in addition to erythema and desquamation with scaly crusts, especially in early stages. In dermatophytosis, the lesions often are round with a pronounced red, scaly border. The lesions of psoriasis often present on the knees, elbows, and scalp. The lower back and periumbilical region are also common sites of involvement.

  • Eczematous dermatitis
  • Red areas
  • Desquamation
  • Dermatophytosis
  • Red scaly border

A biopsy was performed at the edge of one of the lesions.

  • Histology, low power
  • Elongated rete ridges
  • Parakeratotic scale
  • Histology, high power
  • Microabscess
  • Parakeratosis
  • Biopsy, PAS stain
  • Fungal organisms
2
What is the histologic differential diagnosis?
Show answer

The differential diagnosis should include psoriasis, chronic spongiotic dermatitis, and dermatophytosis. Features that would suggest psoriasis at low power include uniform hyperplasia of the rete ridges, with thinning of the epidermis above the dermal papillae and parakeratotic scale. At high power, parakeratosis (retention of nuclei in the stratum corneum), the absence of a granular layer, and neutrophils in the stratum corneum (forming a microabscess in this example) are typical. In chronic spongiotic dermatitis, the hyperplasia of the rete ridges is less regular, and the granular layer is generally expanded. The inflammatory infiltrate is generally scant. Dermatophytosis can be excluded with a periodic acid–Schiff stain, which highlights fungal organisms, which when present are typically in the stratum corneum.

The histologic findings, combined with the clinical findings, were consistent with psoriasis. Therapy was initiated and resulted in partial remission of the patient's symptoms.

3
Which other diseases are associated with psoriasis?
Show answer

Patients with psoriasis have a higher incidence of obesity, type 2 diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, cardiovascular disease, Crohn disease, and multiple sclerosis. Incidence of depression is also higher in psoriatic patients.


4
What is the likely pathogenesis of psoriasis?
Show answer

This chronic inflammatory dermatosis is caused by a T-cell response to some as yet unknown, presumed self antigen in the skin. The T cells differentiate into predominantly Th17 cells, but there may also be Th1 cells in the lesions. The identification of these T cells has led to the development of highly effective anticytokine therapies for the disease. The success of these therapies provides convincing proof for the pathogenic role of Th17 (and Th1) responses in psoriasis.

5
In addition to psoriasis, which other disease presents with plaques and papules?
Show answer

Lichen planus typically presents with pruritic, purple, polygonal papules that may become confluent. Most frequently, the lesions are seen on the wrists and the lower arms. Patients with lichen planus often have mucosal lesions as well that affect the oral mucosa and the genital mucosa, especially the glans penis. White, reticulated (netlike) lesions can be seen on the oral and genital mucosa. Unlike psoriasis, surface scale is not seen.


  • Lichen planus
  • Flat-topped, red papules
  • Confluent papules
6
What are the diagnostic histologic features of lichen planus and how do these differ from the features of psoriasis?
Show answer

Histologically, lichen planus is characterized by a thickened epidermis with marked orthokeratosis (hyperkeratosis without parakeratosis), hypergranulosis (increased thickness of the stratum granulosum), and a brisk, bandlike lymphocytic infiltrate at the dermal-epidermal junction associated with destruction of the basal keratinocytes. The necrotic keratinocytes (Civatte bodies) are deeply eosinophilic and shrunken and are typical of lichen planus. By contrast, psoriasis is characterized by parakeratosis and loss of the granular cell layer.

  • Lichen planus
  • Civatte bodies
  • Lymphocytic infiltrate
  • Hypergranulosis
  • Orthokeratosis
7
What are some examples of inflammatory bullous disorders?
Show answer

Although vesicles and bullae (blisters) occur as secondary phenomena in several unrelated conditions (e.g., herpesvirus infection, spongiotic dermatitis), there is a group of autoimmune disorders in which blisters are the primary and most distinctive feature. Blistering in these diseases tends to occur at specific levels within the skin, a morphologic distinction that is critical for diagnosis. The most important bullous diseases of the skin are pemphigus (vulgaris and foliaceous) and bullous pemphigoid.


Schematic representation of different types of blisters. (A) In subcorneal blisters, the stratum corneum forms the roof of the bulla (as in pemphigus foliaceus). (B) In a suprabasilar blister, a portion of the epidermis, including the stratum corneum, forms the roof (as in pemphigus vulgaris). (C) In a subepidermal blister, the entire epidermis separates from the dermis (as in bullous pemphigoid).

8
How does pemphigus vulgaris differ clinically from pemphigus foliaceous?
Show answer

Pemphigus vulgaris involves both mucosa and skin, especially on the scalp, face, axillae, and points of pressure. The lesions are superficial flaccid vesicles and bullae that rupture easily, leaving deep and often extensive erosions covered with a serum crust. Pemphigus foliaceus, a rare, milder form of pemphigus, results in bullae that are mainly confined to the skin. The blisters in this disorder are superficial, with more limited zones of erythema and crusting.

  • Clinical presentation
  • Pemphigus vulgaris
  • Pemphigus foliaceous
9
What is the pathogenesis of pemphigus vulgaris and foliaceous?
Show answer

Pemphigus vulgaris and pemphigus foliaceus are autoimmune diseases caused by antibody-mediated (type II) hypersensitivity reactions. The pathogenic antibodies are IgG autoantibodies that bind to intercellular desmosomal proteins (desmoglein types 1 and 3 in pemphigus vulgaris, desmoglein 1 alone in pemphigus foliaceous). By direct immunofluorescence study, lesional sites show a characteristic fishnet-like pattern of intercellular IgG deposits.


  • Immunofluorescence
  • Dermis
  • Epidermis
  • Fishnet-like pattern
10
What would be the expected histologic findings in these two diseases?
Show answer

Pemphigus vulgaris is characterized by suprabasal blisters (on left), whereas pemphigus foliaceous demonstrates more superficial subcorneal blisters (on right). Both entities demonstrate acantholysis, dissolution of the intercellular bridges that connect squamous epithelial cells. Acantholytic cells dissociate from one another, lose their polyhedral shape and become rounded. The single layer of intact basal cells that forms the blister base in pemphigus vulgaris has been likened to a row of tombstones. Variable superficial dermal infiltration by lymphocytes, macrophages, and eosinophils accompanies each type of pemphigus.

  • Histology
  • Blister base
  • Ancantholytic cells
  • Subcorneal blister
  • Suprabasal blister
11
How does bullous pemphigoid differ from pemphigus vulgaris?
Show answer

The lesions of bullous pemphigoid do not rupture as readily as in pemphigus and usually heal without scarring. Blistering in bullous pemphigoid is triggered by the linear deposition of autoreactive IgG antibodies and complement in the epidermal basement membrane. Reactivity also occurs in the basement membrane attachment plaques (hemidesmosomes), where most bullous pemphigoid antigen (most commonly type XVII collagen) is located. Bullous pemphigoid and pemphigus vulgaris are caused by similar pathogenic mechanisms, but differ in their clinical presentation and course due to variation in the nature and location of the target antigen (hemidesmosomes in bullous pemphigoid, desmosomes in pemphigus). Histologically, bullous pemphigoid is characterized by subepidermal, nonacantholytic blisters with an eosinophil-rich infiltrate.

  • Clinical presentation
  • Tense bullae
  • Immunofluorescence
  • Epidermis
  • Dermis
  • Dermoepidermal junction
  • Bullous pemphigoid
  • Eosinophlic infiltrate
  • Subepidermal vesicle

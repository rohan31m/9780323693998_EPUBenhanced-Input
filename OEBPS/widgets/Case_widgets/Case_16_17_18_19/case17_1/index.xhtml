Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast epidural, subdural, and subarachnoid hemorrhage with respect to etiology, clinical presentation, and survival.
  2. • State the causes and consequences of cerebral edema.
  3. • List the etiologies and describe the pathogenesis, clinical manifestations, and pathologic complications of increased intracranial pressure.
  4. • Locate the sites of herniation of brain tissue and the respective neural structures involved.

A 29-year-old motorcyclist skidded on an icy patch in the road and was thrown onto the pavement. Witnesses reported that, after impact and a brief period of consciousness, the patient became disoriented and rapidly lost consciousness. Emergency medical personnel stabilized the patient and transported him to a local hospital.

In the Emergency Department, the patient was found to have a fracture of the right lateral skull. Ophthalmologic examination revealed a dilated right pupil.

1
Based on the clinical history, what would a head CT show?
Show answer

The clinical history is classic for an epidural hemorrhage: head trauma with skull fracture followed by a short period of lucidity and subsequent rapid progression of neurologic decline.

A head CT was performed and revealed a right lateral skull fracture and associated hemorrhage. The hemorrhage had a biconvex shape and did not cross the coronal suture. There is a mass effect on the right side of the brain, compressing the underlying structures and shifting them towards the midline.

  • CT scan
  • Skull fracture
  • Hemorrhage
2
What is this patient’s diagnosis?
Show answer

This patient has an acute epidural hemorrhage, which can occur after rupture of one of the meningeal arteries (usually the middle meningeal artery), almost always as the result of skull fracture.

3
What is the most likely mechanism of the patient’s dilated right pupil?
Show answer

A unilateral dilated pupil can be seen when there is damage to cranial nerve III, the oculomotor nerve. This nerve provides motor and parasympathetic innervation to multiple ocular structures, including the sphincter pupillae, which constricts the pupil. When this nerve is damaged, the pupil dilates due to unopposed action of the dilator pupillae muscle. Causes of CN III palsy include increased intracranial pressure (which causes pressure on the nerve), diabetes, multiple sclerosis and myasthenia gravis. In this case, swelling of the right side of the brain is causing increased pressure on CN III, leading to the “blown” pupil.

While the patient was being prepared for emergency surgery, his breathing became labored and gasping and progressed to respiratory arrest. Resuscitative efforts were unsuccessful. Postmortem examination confirmed the presence of a skull fracture involving the squamous portion of the right temporal bone. Approximately 100 mL of fresh blood was present between the dura mater and the skull.

  • Epidural hemorrhage
  • Dura
  • Hemorrhage
  • Skull
4
Which are the three sites that bleeding can occur between the surface of the brain and the skull?
Show answer

Bleeding between the surface of the brain and the skull can be seen in the epidural space, subdural space, and subarachnoid space. Epidural hematomas are caused by arterial tears, usually of the middle meningeal artery. By contrast, subdural hematomas develop between the arachnoid mater and the inner surface of the dura. They occur whenever there are tears in the veins (bridging veins) connecting the surface of the brain to one of the dural sinuses (usually the superior sagittal sinus). Subarachnoid hemorrhage occurs when there is bleeding into the space between the pia and arachnoid mater.

Grossly, the brain showed widespread flattening of gyral crests and narrowing of sulci caused by compression of the expanding brain against the inner surface of the skull. Since the hemorrhage was epidural, no blood is present on the brain surface (in contrast to a subdural bleed). Additional changes seen in the brain included right uncal gyral herniation, hemorrhage in the right medial occipital cortex, cerebellar tonsillar herniation, Duret hemorrhage (secondary brainstem hemorrhage caused by tearing of penetrating arteries and veins of the upper brainstem) and flattening of the ventral brainstem.

  • Brain, gross
  • Flat gyri
  • Narrow sulci
  • Brainstem and cerebellum
  • Duret hemorrhage
  • Pons
  • Cerebellum
5
What is herniation and what are the different types of herniation that can occur?
Show answer

Herniation is the displacement of brain tissue past rigid dural folds (the falx and tentorium) or through openings in the skull because of increased intracranial pressure. Herniation is mostly associated with mass effect, either diffuse (generalized brain edema) or focal (tumors, abscesses, or hemorrhages). If the expansion is sufficiently severe, herniation may occur in multiple anatomic locations.

  • • Subfalcine (cingulate) herniation occurs when unilateral or asymmetric expansion of a cerebral hemisphere displaces the cingulate gyrus under the falx. This may lead to compression of the anterior cerebral artery and its branches.
  • • Transtentorial (uncinate) herniation occurs when the medial aspect of the temporal lobe is compressed against the free margin of the tentorium. The third cranial nerve can be compromised, as in this patient, resulting in pupillary dilation and impairment of ocular movements on the side of the lesion. Caudal displacement of the cerebellum and brainstem may also be associated with hemorrhagic necrosis of the cerebellar tonsils and with secondary hemorrhage within the brainstem (called Duret hemorrhage).
  • • Tonsillar herniation refers to displacement of the cerebellar tonsils through the foramen magnum.
6
What are the similarities and differences between epidural and subdural hematomas?
Show answer

Both epidural and subdural hemorrhages are associated with trauma (though trauma can cause hemorrhages in any intracranial site). Epidural hemorrhages are typically seen following trauma that causes rapid deceleration of the head (such as the motorcycle accident in this case) whereas subdural hemorrhages can occur after mild trauma, usually in elderly patients. In this population, senile atrophy of the brain causes stretching of the veins that extend from the cerebral hemispheres through the subarachnoid and subdural space to the dural sinuses. Epidural hemorrhages are due to an arterial tear and tend to enlarge rapidly. By contrast, subdural hemorrhages are due to venous damage and progress more slowly. While epidural hemorrhages are characterized by a period of post-traumatic lucidity that quickly progresses, subdural hemorrhages evolve more slowly.


7
How do subdural hemorrhages resolve over time?
Show answer

Subdural hematomas organize by lysis of the clot (about 1 week), growth of granulation tissue from the dural surface into the hematoma (2 weeks), and fibrosis (1–3 months). Subdural hematomas commonly rebleed, presumably from the thin-walled vessels of the granulation tissue, leading to microscopic findings consistent with hemorrhages of varying ages. The risk of repeat bleeding is greatest in the first few months after the initial hemorrhage

8
In which clinical settings do subarachnoid hemorrhages occur?
Show answer

Like epidural and subdural hematomas, subarachnoid hemorrhage is also usually caused by head trauma. Traumatic subarachnoid hemorrhages are usually caused by bruises of the surface of the brain, termed contusions. Injury to such areas of brain causes leakage of blood into the superficial brain parenchyma and, from there, into the adjacent subarachnoid space. Subarachnoid hemorrhage may also occur in the absence of trauma: ruptured saccular (berry) aneurysms are the most common cause of spontaneous (nontraumatic) subarachnoid hemorrhage. Patients usually present with sudden onset of severe headache, often with rapid neurologic deterioration.

9
What was this patient’s cause of death?
Show answer

As with any expanding intracranial mass, epidural hematomas cause an increase in intracranial pressure that can cause herniation of the brain. Cerebellar tonsillar herniation is caused by compression and distortion of the adjacent brainstem, which contains vital respiratory centers. Compromise of these areas may cause respiratory arrest and is a common terminal event in patients with expanding intracranial lesions (as in this case).

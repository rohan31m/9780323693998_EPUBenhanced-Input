Objectives
  1. • Describe the pathophysiology and clinical features of rheumatoid arthritis.
  2. • Compare and contrast the pathogenesis, morphologic findings, and clinical findings of osteoarthritis and rheumatoid arthritis.

A 40-year-old woman presented to her physician with a 6-month history of fatigue, anorexia, and generalized malaise. She stated that it was difficult to ambulate on waking, but after about 30 minutes she felt better and was able to move about the house. She also said that stiffness and pain in her wrists and fingers made it difficult to do routine chores in the kitchen. She was especially concerned about the recent swelling in the joints of her fingers.

On physical examination, her knees were swollen and tender, and motion in her knees and hips was limited. Her knees and both wrist joints were warm, but with no erythema. The proximal interphalangeal joints of both hands were warm and enlarged by nonbony fusiform swelling.

The patient had the following laboratory findings:

HLA-DR4 Positive
Rheumatoid factor Positive at a titer of 1:500
Erythrocyte sedimentation rate Increased
Serum anti citrullinated peptide antibodies (ACPA) Positive

1
What is the diagnosis in this case?
Show answer

This is a classic case of rheumatoid arthritis (RA), a chronic, relapsing, inflammatory disease. Although the principal target is the synovial lining of joints, the disease also affects many organ systems, including skin, heart, lungs, blood vessels, and muscle.

2
What is the etiology of RA?
Show answer

RA is an autoimmune disease. The inflammatory lesions are likely mediated by CD4+ T cells and cytokines. Approximately 80% of individuals with RA have autoantibodies (usually IgM) to the Fc portion of IgG, called rheumatoid factor; its role in pathogenesis is unclear. Anti-citrullinated peptide antibodies (ACPA) have been identified as a relatively specific marker for RA. They are markers of an immune response against citrullinated peptides. It is unknown if ACPA contribute to joint lesions.

3
What is the significance of the patient’s HLA-DR4 positivity?
Show answer

About 50% of the risk of developing RA is related to inherited genetic susceptibility. Specific HLA-DRB1 alleles are linked to rheumatoid arthritis, and these alleles share a common sequence of amino acids (“shared epitopes”) in a polymorphic region of the β chain. The shared epitope is located in the antigen-binding cleft of the DR molecule. This location is presumably the specific binding site of the putative arthritis-inducing peptides (“arthritogens”) that initiate the inflammatory synovitis. The nature of these peptides remains unknown.

4
Which cytokines are implicated in the pathogenesis of RA, which cells do they affect, and which actions do they mediate?
Show answer

IFN-γ from Th1 cells activates macrophages and resident synovial cells; IL-17 from Th17 cells recruits neutrophils and monocytes; TNF and IL-1 from macrophages stimulate resident synovial cells to secrete proteases that destroy hyaline cartilage; and RANKL expressed on activated T cells stimulates bone resorption. TNF and IL-1 can also cause the systemic signs and symptoms noted in this case.

Over the next 25 years, the patient was treated with a variety of medications including methotrexate, tumor necrosis factor inhibitors, and other immune system modulators; however, her disease continued to progress. Physical exam revealed symmetric involvement of both hands, with ulnar deviation of the metacarpophalangeal joints and swelling of the fingers. Rheumatoid nodules were also present. Plain radiograph showed symmetric destruction of the metacarpophalangeal joints and the small bones of the wrist. The joint spaces are narrowed due to destruction of the articular cartilage and there is ulnar subluxation.

Which histologic changes would be seen in this patient’s synovium?
Show answer

The synovium would be markedly proliferative, creating a papillary or villous appearance, and would be infiltrated by numerous lymphocytes, some organized into B-cell follicles and dense lymphoid aggregates, plasma cells, and macrophages, as well as activated fibroblasts. The hyperplastic and inflamed synovium becomes highly vascularized and may erode into adjacent bone and cartilage; this inflammatory lesion is referred to as a pannus.

What is the proposed mechanism of cartilage damage in RA?
Show answer

During active inflammation, the activated T cells and macrophages in the synovium generate a plethora of cytokines and growth factors. IL-1 and TNF are likely key mediators in the cytokine cascade. In addition, inflammatory mediators released from the pannus stimulate osteoclast activity, which results in erosion and destruction of the subchondral bone. RANKL, released from the surface of lymphocytes by metalloproteinases, also activates osteoclasts, which erode the subchondral bone. The invading and overriding pannus may isolate the articular cartilage from the synovial fluid, leading to further degeneration of articular cartilage.

7
Which histologic features would be seen in a biopsy of one of the soft tissues of the patient’s left arm?
Show answer

The subcutaneous nodules are called rheumatoid nodules. These occur in approximately 25% of patients with RA, usually those with severe disease. They arise in areas of the skin that are subject to pressure, including the ulnar aspect of the forearm, elbows, occiput, and lumbosacral area. Microscopically, they resemble necrotizing granulomas with a central zone of fibrinoid necrosis surrounded by a prominent rim of palisaded histiocytes (activated macrophages) and numerous lymphocytes and plasma cells.

8
Which clinical and radiologic features distinguish rheumatoid arthritis from osteoarthritis?
Show answer

Osteoarthritis typically affects the large weight-bearing joints in older patients, especially those with a history of obesity or strenuous, repetitive activity (e.g., marathon running). Manifestations of systemic inflammation are not seen. Rheumatoid arthritis, by comparison, typically presents with morning pain and stiffness that improve with activity; activity can worsen the symptoms of osteoarthritis. In osteoarthritis, radiologic examination shows narrowing of the joint space, subchondral bone sclerosis, osteophytes (arrows) and subchondral cysts (which can also be seen on gross examination).

9
What is the etiology of osteoarthritis?
Show answer
The lesions of osteoarthritis (OA) stem from degeneration of the articular cartilage and its disordered repair. There are three phases: (1) chondrocyte injury, related to genetic and biochemical factors; (2) early OA, in which chondrocytes proliferate and secrete inflammatory mediators, collagens, proteoglycans, and proteases, which act together to remodel the cartilaginous matrix and initiate secondary inflammatory changes in the synovium and subchondral bone; and (3) late OA, in which repetitive injury and chronic inflammation lead to chondrocyte drop out, marked loss of cartilage, and extensive subchondral bone changes.
10
What are the histologic features of osteoarthritis?
Show answer

In early osteoarthritis, the proliferating chondrocytes form clusters. Matrix water content increases and proteoglycan concentration decreases such that fissures and clefts form, causing fibrillation of the articular surface. As chondrocytes die, full-thickness portions of the cartilage are sloughed, forming loose bodies (joint mice). The exposed subchondral bone plate burnishes the opposing surface, giving it the appearance of polished ivory (bone eburnation)

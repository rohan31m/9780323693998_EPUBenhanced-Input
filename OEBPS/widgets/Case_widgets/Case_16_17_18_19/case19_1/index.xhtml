Get The Lead Out

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  • • Describe the clinical, laboratory and morphologic findings of lead poisoning.
  • • Compare and contrast the risk factors, clinical presentation, and pathophysiology of mercury, arsenic, and cadmium poisoning.

A 4 year-old girl was brought to the pediatrician after her mother witnessed her having a grand mal seizure. She reported that the child had been vomiting for the previous 3 days, had been less active over the past several weeks, and was easily tired. On questioning, the mother related that their meals often consisted of “fast food” or “junk food,” and rarely included meat or dairy products. She said that she sometimes found her daughter eating dirt in the backyard. They lived in a duplex house built in the inner city in the early 1900s that had not had its interior repainted in memory.

Initial examination showed a lethargic child who was pale. Her exam was otherwise unremarkable.

Laboratory values were as follows:

Hemoglobin 8.2 gm/dL (11–13 gm/dL)
Hct 24.9% (35%–44%)
RDW 21.5% (11.6%–14.6%)
MCV 68 µm3 (80–100 µm3)
WBC 8,000/mm3(4,500–11,000 mm3)
Platelets 350,000/mm3 (150,000–400,000/mm3)
1
What is the differential diagnosis for this patient’s anemia? Which tests should be ordered?
Show answer

The patient has microcytic anemia, which is most commonly caused by iron deficiency or thalassemia. Given the patient’s poor diet, iron deficiency is likely.

Appropriate tests would include serum iron, total iron binding capacity, transferrin saturation, and ferritin.


Serum iron concentration 17 mg/dL (28–122 mg/dL)
Transferrin saturation 3 % (6.5%–39%)
Serum ferritin 15 ng/mL (22–158 ng/mL)
Total iron binding capacity 425 g/dL (260–385 g/dL)
2
Which additional blood test is indicated?
Show answer

The second set of laboratory values confirms iron deficiency anemia. Although seizures are not associated with iron deficiency anemia, they can be seen in the setting of severe lead poisoning. Iron deficiency may exacerbate lead poisoning by increasing lead absorption from the gut, since iron deficiency alters the expression of proteins that influence lead uptake. The most direct way to assess lead exposure is to obtain a blood lead level. A blood level of >5 micrograms/dL is considered elevated.

This child’s blood lead level was 102 micrograms/dL, a level that is indicative of severe lead poisoning.

3
What is the most likely etiology of this patient’s lead poisoning?
Show answer

One of the most important causes of lead poisoning in children is leaded paint. Use of leaded paint was banned in 1978, but it is still found in many older homes in the U.S. Exposure in this child may have been exacerbated by pica (eating of odd materials, such as paint chips), a behavioral change that is seen in some individuals with iron deficiency. Other sources of lead that can affect children include soil contaminated with leaded gasoline, maternal-fetal transmission from a mother who has lead poisoning, contaminated water (e.g., as in Flint, Michigan), “herbal” remedies, and contaminated toys. In adults, most lead exposure is occupational and is seen in plumbers, lead miners, auto repairers, and construction workers (e.g., renovators of houses with leaded paint).

4
What are the major tissues and cell types that are affected by lead poisoning?
Show answer

Hematopoietic tissues: Lead inhibits several enzymes that are required for heme synthesis, thereby impairing production of hemoglobin and red cells. High levels of lead may also damage red cell membranes, leading to hemolysis.

Neurons: Lead is toxic to central and peripheral neurons. In children, lead poisoning leads to irreversible cognitive defects, its most feared consequence. Effects are seen at even low levels of exposure. Seizure and encephalopathy are indicative of severe lead poisoning (levels >100 microgram/dL). Peripheral neuropathy leading to findings such as wrist drop (due to radial nerve damage) is classic in adults with chronic lead poisoning but is rare in children.

Kidney: Lead disrupts normal renal tubular function, leading to loss of glucose and amino acids in the urine. Chronic poisoning may cause interstitial nephritis.

GI tract: Lead poisoning is associated with abdominal pain (colic) and vomiting.

Bones: Lead interferes with certain aspects of calcium metabolism, leading to bone abnormalities in children.


5
Where in the body does lead accumulate?
Show answer

Lead may accumulate in the physes (arrows) in children, causing increased bone density detected as radiodense “lead lines” in growth plates.

6
Which treatment is indicated for this patient?
Show answer

This patient requires anti-seizure medication and chelation therapy to remove lead. In addition, the appropriate state agency should be contacted to remove lead from the home.

7
How does lead act as a poison?
Show answer

Lead has a high affinity for sulfhydryl groups in proteins and is believed to interfere with the activity of enzymes that are required for normal cell function in a number of tissues. Two of the affected enzymes are involved in heme synthesis: aminolevulinic acid dehydratase and delta ferrochelatase. Lead also inhibits sodium- and potassium-dependent ATPases in cell membranes, an effect that may increase the fragility of red cells, causing hemolysis.

8
Which other common toxin also binds sulfhydryl groups?
Show answer

Mercury also binds to sulfhydryl groups in certain proteins with high affinity.

9
What are the main sources of mercury exposure?
Show answer

The main sources of exposure to mercury are contaminated fish (methyl mercury) and mercury vapors released from metallic mercury in dental amalgams, a possible occupational hazard for dental workers.

10
Which organs are most commonly affected by mercury poisoning?
Show answer

The developing brain is extremely sensitive to methyl mercury, one of the three forms of mercury: metallic mercury, inorganic mercury compounds (mostly mercuric chloride), and organic mercury (mostly methyl mercury). To protect against potential fetal brain damage, the Centers for Disease Control (CDC) has recommended that pregnant women avoid consumption of fish known to contain high levels of mercury (e.g., swordfish, shark, bluefish). Kidneys and the adult brain are also susceptible to mercury toxicity. The lipid solubility of methyl mercury and metallic mercury facilitate their accumulation in the brain, disturbing neuromotor, cognitive, and behavioral functions.

11
Which antioxidant protects against mercury toxicity?
Show answer

Intracellular glutathione, by acting as sulfhydryl donor, is the main protective mechanism against mercury-induced CNS and kidney damage.

12
Which organ systems are affected by arsenic?
Show answer

If ingested in large quantities, arsenic causes acute gastrointestinal, cardiovascular, and CNS toxicities that are often fatal. Neurologic effects usually occur 2 to 8 weeks after exposure and consist of a sensorimotor neuropathy that causes paresthesias, numbness, and pain. Chronic exposure to arsenic causes skin changes consisting of hyperpigmentation and hyperkeratosis.

13
What is the mechanism of arsenic toxicity?
Show answer

Arsenic interferes with mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation by replacing the phosphates in adenosine triphosphate with trivalent arsenic. Arsenic also has pleiotropic effects on the activity of a number of other enzymes and ion channels, and these too may contribute to certain toxicities.

14
Which malignancies are associated with arsenic exposure?
Show answer

Squamous cell carcinomas of the lung and skin and urothelial carcinoma of the bladder are associated with arsenic exposure.

15
How do arsenic-associated skin carcinomas differ from sun exposure-associated skin carcinomas?
Show answer

Arsenic-induced skin tumors are often multiple and usually appear on the palms and soles. By contrast, these sites are uncommonly affected in sun exposure-associated carcinomas of the skin.

16
What is the proposed mechanism for carcinogenesis due to arsenic?
Show answer

Although the mechanism is not known, it is thought to involve defects in nucleotide excision repair mechanisms that protect against DNA damage.

17
Which organs are affected by cadmium exposure?
Show answer

The principal toxic effects of excess cadmium take the form of obstructive lung disease caused by necrosis of alveolar epithelial cells, and renal tubular damage that may progress to end-stage renal disease.

18
What are the primary sources of cadmium exposure?
Show answer

It is an occupational and environmental pollutant generated by mining, electroplating, and production of nickel-cadmium batteries, which are usually disposed of as household waste. Cadmium can contaminate the soil and plants directly or through fertilizers and irrigation water. Food is the most important source of cadmium exposure for the general population.

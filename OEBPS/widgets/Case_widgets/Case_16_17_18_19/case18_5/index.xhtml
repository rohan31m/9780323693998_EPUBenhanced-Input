The Flesh Is Weak

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast the major clinical and pathologic features of primary myopathy and denervation atrophy.
  2. • Describe the classifications of primary myopathic disorders.
  3. • Compare and contrast Duchenne and Becker muscular dystrophy.

The parents of a 2-year-old boy brought their son to the pediatrician because he still could not walk, although his older sister began walking at 14 months. The boy's mother was particularly worried because her maternal uncle had died of a muscle disorder when he was 18.

1
What is the likely nature of the disease affecting this child?
Show answer

Difficulty walking, along with death of a maternal uncle due to a muscular disorder, suggests an inherited muscular disorder affecting male members of the family, possibly an X-linked disease.

2
What are the different types of inherited disorders of skeletal muscle?
Show answer

There are two primary categories of inherited muscular disorders: (1) muscular dystrophies, which are associated with progressive muscular injury in patients who have normal muscle function at birth (e.g., Duchenne muscular dystrophy, Becker muscular dystrophy) and (2) congenital myopathies that present in infancy and are static or may improve over time (e.g., Ullrich congenital muscular dystrophy, merosin deficiency).

Physical examination revealed moderate weakness of the lower extremities without noticeable atrophy. In fact, the child's calves were disproportionately large, considering their lack of strength. The boy had difficulty standing from a seated position and demonstrated mild upper extremity weakness. Laboratory tests showed serum total creatine kinase to be 155,700 U/L (25–90 U/L).

3
What do increased levels of creatine kinase suggest?
Show answer

Creatine kinase is found primarily in cardiac muscle, skeletal muscle, and brain tissue and has multiple roles in cellular energy metabolism. Creatine kinase consists of two subunits: B (brain type) and M (muscle type) and, therefore, three different isoenzymes exist (CK-MM, CK-MB, and CK-BB). Their tissue distribution is variable: approximately 98% of the CK in skeletal muscle is the MM isozyme (the remainder being CK-MB) while in cardiac muscle, 15%–40% is CK-MB (the remainder being CK-MM). The increased levels of CK in this patient suggest that the patient’s skeletal muscle is being damaged.

The physician then performed nerve conduction studies (NCS), an electromyography, and a muscle biopsy. The NCS showed normal sensory and motor nerve conduction while the electromyographic study revealed myopathic changes.

4
What is meant by the term myopathy?
Show answer

The term myopathy indicates a primary, or intrinsic, muscle abnormality, in contrast to the muscle changes that occur when normal innervation is interrupted (denervation atrophy). There are many different forms of myopathy, including inflammatory myopathies (myositis), metabolic myopathies (e.g., glycogen storage diseases), congenital myopathies, and muscular dystrophies.

5
What is the significance of the absence of sensory and motor deficits on the NCS?
Show answer

Primary muscle diseases (myopathies) must be distinguished from secondary neuropathic changes caused by disorders that disrupt muscle innervation. Both neuropathic and myopathic processes cause weakness and muscle fiber atrophy. Neuropathic diseases include motor neuron diseases (e.g., spinal muscular atrophy and amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, unlikely candidates for this patient) and motor neuropathies (e.g., Guillain-Barré, Krabbe disease). The absence of sensory and motor deficits suggests this is most likely a primary muscle disorder.

A muscle biopsy was performed and demonstrated characteristic features of a chronic myopathic process, including fiber size variability, reflecting the presence of atrophic, hypertrophic, degenerating, and regenerating fibers. Regenerating myofibers can be recognized by their basophilic staining. A mild increase in connective tissue around individual muscle fibers (endomysial fibrosis) was also seen.

An immunohistochemical stain for dystrophin was performed (normal control, inset). The skeletal muscle showed complete absence of dystrophin. Genetic testing revealed a large deletion in the dystrophin gene.

  • Atrophic myofibers
  • Hypertrophic myofiber
  • Regenerating myofiber
  • Degenerating myofiber
  • Endomysial fibrosis
  • Dystrophin IHC
6
Why did the calves appear to be enlarged?
Show answer

Chronic muscle damage outpaces the capacity for repair, leading to muscle weakness and fatty replacement of muscle tissue by collagen and fat cells making the muscles appear enlarged.

7
What are the two major types of dystrophinopathies and what are their pathogeneses?
Show answer

Both Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD) and Becker muscular dystrophy (BMD) are due to mutations in the dystrophin gene, which is located on the short arm of the X chromosome. Dystrophin, an intracellular protein, forms an interface between the cytoskeletal proteins and a group of transmembrane proteins, the dystroglycans and the sarcoglycans. These transmembrane proteins have interactions with the extracellular matrix. Dystrophin is a very large gene, spanning 2.3 million base pairs and, therefore, is susceptible to sporadic mutations. DMD is typically due to deletions or frameshift mutations that result in the complete absence of dystrophin (as seen in this patient’s muscle biopsy). By contrast, BMD is more commonly due to point mutations that yield residual but defective dystrophin. The severity of disease in the dystrophinopathies correlates with the extent of dystrophin deficiency.


8
How does the absence of dystrophin result in this patient’s symptoms?
Show answer

Dystrophin is found in skeletal and cardiac muscle, brain, and peripheral nerves as part of the dystrophin-glycoprotein complex. This complex stabilizes the muscle cell during contraction and may be involved in cell signaling through interactions with other proteins. Dystrophin-glycoprotein complex defects are thought to make muscle cells vulnerable to transient membrane tears during contraction, and they may also disrupt intracellular signaling. The result is myofiber degeneration that with time outpaces the capacity for repair. The dystrophin-glycoprotein complex also is important for cardiac muscle function; this explains why cardiomyopathy eventually develops in many patients.

9
What are the clinical differences between DMD and BMD?
Show answer

DMD usually manifests by age 5 and progresses relentlessly; patients typically become wheelchair-bound by their teens. Because dystrophin is expressed in cardiac and skeletal muscle, cardiac abnormalities (arrhythmias, congestive heart failure) are seen in some patients in addition to skeletal muscle weakness and atrophy. Most patients die of their disease by age 20, usually as a result of profound weakness, respiratory compromise, pulmonary infections, or cardiac decompensation. The clinical manifestations of BMD vary, but typically are less severe than the manifestations of DMD, with a later onset and slower progression of symptoms. As in the case of Duchenne muscular dystrophy, cardiac abnormalities may occur in patients with Becker muscular dystrophy and sometimes dominate the clinical picture.

10
How is this patient’s maternal uncle’s death related to his disease?
Show answer

DMD shows X-linked inheritance, and it is likely that the patient’s maternal uncle also had DMD. Due to the large size of the gene, sporadic mutations are not uncommon and account for approximately 30% of new cases of DMD.

The patient was treated with supportive care; however, his disease progressed. Six years later, a second muscle biopsy was performed, which confirmed disease progression: there was extensive variation in myofiber size, fatty replacement, and endomysial fibrosis. At the age of 23, the patient died secondary to complications from respiratory failure.

  • Fatty replacement
  • Variation in myofiber size
  • Fibrosis
11
In addition to myopathies and denervation atrophy, what other type of disorder can be associated with muscle fiber weakness?
Show answer

Another important cause of muscle weakness is disease affecting the neuromuscular junction. Infant botulinism should be considered in children before their first birthday: before the age of 12 months, Clostridium botulinum spores can germinate in the gut due to the relative lack of gastric acid. Raw honey is a source of C. botulinum spores and is not recommended in children less than one year old. Botulism toxin binds to presynaptic cholinergic receptors and irreversibly blocks acetylcholine release. Myasthenia gravis is another neuromuscular junction disorder that is seen in young adult men and women (third decade) and older men (sixth decade). It is characterized by autoantibody-mediated inhibition and injury of acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction. Myasthenia gravis may manifest at any age, although most cases occur in adults. Muscle biopsy specimens from patients with myasthenia gravis typically reveal varying degrees of disuse atrophy and occasional lymphoid aggregates, but no other significant changes.

Objectives
  • • Discuss the major metabolic effects of insulin and the classification and diagnosis of diabetes.
  • • Differentiate between type 1 and type 2 diabetes with respect to pathogenesis and clinical presentation.
  • • Describe the pathogenesis of the long-term complications of diabetes, especially the vascular complications, and the major organs involved by vascular disease.
  • • Describe the pathogenesis of the acute metabolic complications of diabetes.

A 14-year-old girl was brought to the Emergency Department with a 2-week history of fatigue and weight loss. Her parents report that she has been drinking lots of water (up to 1 gallon/day) and urinating frequently. Over the last 12 hours, she experienced worsening nausea and abdominal pain with vomiting. She was drowsy and did not respond to questions. Physical exam revealed a thin girl with a blood pressure of 90/55 mm Hg, a pulse rate of 110 bpm, and deep, sighing respirations (Kussmaul respiration).

What is the most likely diagnosis for this patient?
Show answer

Based on current criteria, this patient’s markedly increased blood glucose, in a random sample, is diagnostic for diabetes. Her young age and thin body habitus are consistent with type 1 diabetes, an autoimmune disease resulting in destruction of beta cells in the pancreatic islets. The combination of urinary ketones and low blood pH are a feature of ketoacidosis, which is a life-threatening complication of type 1 diabetes.

2
What is the normal function of insulin in the body?
Show answer

Insulin and glucagon have opposing regulatory effects on glucose homeostasis. During fasting states, low insulin and high glucagon levels facilitate hepatic gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis (glycogen breakdown) while decreasing glycogen synthesis, thereby raising blood sugar. Thus, fasting plasma glucose levels are determined primarily by hepatic glucose output. Following a meal, insulin levels rise and glucagon levels fall in response to the large glucose load. Insulin promotes glucose uptake and utilization in tissues (discussed later). Skeletal muscle is the major insulin-responsive tissue for postprandial glucose utilization and is critical for preventing hyperglycemia and maintaining glucose homeostasis .


3
Why does this patient have low blood pressure and a high heart rate?
Show answer

Low blood pressure combined with a high heart rate is suggestive of dehydration. Although the patient has been drinking abundant water, she has also had polyuria. Her low insulin levels (secondary to beta cell destruction) stimulate glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis resulting in hyperglycemia. The amount of glucose filtered by the renal tubule exceeds its capacity for reabsorption, and high urinary glucose pulls additional water into the urine (“osmotic diuresis”) and the patient becomes dehydrated.

4
Why does this patient have low serum sodium and high potassium?
Show answer

The osmotic diuresis caused by the patient’s high serum glucose also results in the increased excretion of sodium and chloride. Due to the acidosis, hydrogen ions enter cells in exchange for potassium ions, resulting in increased serum potassium.

5
What is the explanation for this patient’s low pH?
Show answer

Insulin deficiency stimulates hormone-sensitive lipase, with resultant breakdown of adipose stores and an increase in levels of free fatty acids. When these free fatty acids reach the liver, they are esterified to fatty acyl coenzyme A. Oxidation of fatty acyl coenzyme A molecules within hepatic mitochondria produces ketone bodies (acetoacetic acid and β-hydroxybutyric acid). The rate at which ketone bodies are formed may exceed the rate at which acetoacetic acid and β-hydroxybutyric acid can be utilized by peripheral tissues, leading to ketonemia and ketonuria. If the urinary excretion of ketones is compromised by dehydration, the result is a systemic metabolic ketoacidosis due to the low pKa of ketone bodies.


6
What is the cause of the patient’s slow, deep breathing?
Show answer

Kussmaul breathing is a compensatory mechanism of hyperventilation to induce a respiratory alkalosis to counteract the metabolic acidosis. Hyperventilation lowers the serum PCO2, which is reflected in this patient’s laboratory values.

What is the pathogenesis of type 1 diabetes? How does this differ from type 2 diabetes?
Show answer

Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune disease resulting in destruction of beta cells in the pancreatic islets. The autoimmune response against beta cells is triggered by some unidentified agent in genetically predisposed individuals. Although more than 30 genes have been shown to be associated with type 1 diabetes, at least half the genetic contribution comes from the HLA class II locus, consistent with a key role of CD4+ T cells in the pathogenesis. The importance of an environmental trigger is underscored by the fact that even in identical twins, the concordance rate of type 1 diabetes is only 50%. Type 2 diabetes is genetically and biochemically distinct from type 1 diabetes. Although there is a genetic predisposition for type 2 diabetes, there is no evidence of autoimmunity or HLA linkage. It typically starts in adults (>30 years old), and 80% of the patients are obese. The pathogenic mechanisms are (1) insulin resistance (i.e., the target tissues are resistant to the action of insulin) and (2) an undefined beta cell dysfunction which results in insulin deficiency later in the course of the disease.

What would be the histologic features seen on examination of this patient’s pancreas?
Show answer

There would be reduction in the number and size of the islets of Langerhans due to autoimmune destruction. Infiltration by T lymphocytes (insulitis) may be seen early in the disease process. In type 2 diabetes, amyloid deposition may be seen late in the disease process.

The patient was treated with insulin, intravenous fluids and electrolytes. Once she was stabilized, she was discharged on a regimen of twice-daily insulin therapy.

Twenty-three years later, at age 37, the patient presented to the Emergency Department with acute substernal chest pain that radiated to her neck and down her left arm. This pain lasted for 30 minutes and was associated with nausea and sweating. Investigations done at this time revealed that her serum troponin I was 9.4 ng/mL (reference range <0.5 ng/mL). An electrocardiogram revealed evidence of a myocardial infarction (ST segment changes and Q waves). After a 7-day stay in the hospital, she was discharged with continued insulin therapy.

9
How did this patient’s diabetes contribute to her current presentation?
Show answer

The patient developed coronary atherosclerosis (rare in a 37-year-old nondiabetic woman) with classic signs of myocardial infarction due to the macrovascular complications of hyperglycemia. Hyperglycemia greatly accelerates the formation of advanced glycation end products (AGEs). In large vessels, AGE-modified matrix components trap LDL, retarding its efflux from the vessel wall, and enhance the deposition of cholesterol in the intima, thus accelerating atherogenesis. Other clinical consequences of severe atherosclerosis include peripheral vascular disease, aortic aneurysm, gastrointestinal ischemia, and stroke.

She was lost to follow-up but presented 2 years later with generalized edema. On physical examination, there was no clinical evidence of cardiac failure. Neurologic examination revealed loss of sensation in both lower extremities. She also had bilateral visual impairment. Examination of her retinas with an ophthalmoscope revealed neovascularization.

Which histologic changes would you expect to see in the kidneys?
Show answer

Kidneys are often severely affected in diabetes due to vascular disease that affects the large blood vessels and glomerular capillaries and an increased predisposition to infections. Histologic findings include nodular glomerulosclerosis, hyaline thickening of the arteriolar wall and increased interstitial fibrous tissue secondary to tubular destruction. Nodular glomerulosclerosis (Kimmelstiel-Wilson lesion) is characterized by nodular deposition of pink-staining matrix material deposited in the mesangial core of the glomeruli at the periphery of the glomerular capillary tufts. Scattered glomeruli can become entirely replaced by scar and appear acellular and hyalinized. Glomerular changes also include diffuse thickening of the basement membrane and a diffuse increase in the mesangial matrix (nodular glomerulosclerosis) which are best appreciated on silver stain or PAS stain. Clinical consequences of glomerular changes include proteinuria and a progressive decrease in the glomerular filtration rate. Diabetic nephropathy is the leading cause of end stage renal disease in the US.

What are the major chronic complications of diabetes?
Show answer

The major complications are (1) macrovascular complications, such as myocardial infarction, renal vascular insufficiency, cerebrovascular accidents, and peripheral arterial insufficiency that can give rise to gangrene of the lower extremity; (2) diabetic nephropathy; (3) visual impairment due to retinopathy and premature cataracts; (4) diabetic neuropathy, most commonly distal symmetric polyneuropathy that affects both motor and sensory functions, particularly the latter (autonomic neuropathy can give rise to bowel and bladder dysfunction) and (5) increased susceptibility to fungal and bacterial infection.

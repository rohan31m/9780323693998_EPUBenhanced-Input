Objectives
  • • Describe the causes and clinical manifestations of hyper- and hypopituitarism.
  • • Describe the major types of pituitary tumors and how tumor type correlates with clinical presentation.

A 42-year-old man, previously in good health, presented to his physician for evaluation of headache and "problems seeing." The patient stated that over the past year, he had developed headaches that were generally worse while reclining. Although aspirin or acetaminophen provided symptomatic relief during the first 6 months, his pain became more persistent over the last 6 months. In addition, over the last 6 months, the patient noted "blind spots" in his lateral fields of vision, particularly on the right side. Review of systems is significant for decreased libido and erectile dysfunction.

1
What is the most likely underlying condition responsible for the symptoms in this patient?
Show answer

Headache that is worse on reclining suggests increased intracranial pressure, disturbance in visual fields is caused most likely by a mass compressing the optic nerves, and loss of libido and erectile dysfunction indicate that the lesion is affecting endocrine functions. Together these findings suggest a hormone-producing tumor in the region of the pituitary gland. The figure shows the ventral surface of the brain from a patient with similar symptoms who died.

Physical examination revealed slight blurring of the optic disks. An asymmetric bitemporal visual field deficit was present, involving the right temporal visual field more than the left. CT and MRI scans of the head revealed a large midline mass associated with erosion of the sella turcica. The mass extended into the suprasellar space and into the right cavernous sinus.

Laboratory evaluation revealed a prolactin level of 230 ng/mL (>20 ng/mL), luteinizing hormone (LH) of 4.5 mIU/mL (6–23 mIU/mL), follicle stimulating hormone (FSH) of 3.0 mIU/mL (4–25 mIU/mL) and testosterone of 150 ng/dL (normal 270–1070 ng/dL). The patient was taken to the operating room, where the sellar mass was exposed via frontotemporal craniotomy. The mass obliterated the diaphragma sella and the lateral walls of the sella turcica; it compressed the optic chiasm superiorly and extended bilaterally into the cavernous sinuses. A small biopsy specimen of the mass consisted of soft, almost gelatinous, tan tissue. Histologic examination of the specimen revealed a monomorphous population of cells with uniform, round-to-ovoid nuclei and amphophilic cytoplasm.

  • Sellar mass

2
How does the morphology in this case differ from that of the normal anterior pituitary?
Show answer

The normal pituitary gland contains a mixture of cell types, including red-staining acidophils, purple-staining basophils, and chromophobes, whose cytoplasm stains only weakly. These differences in staining reflect the presence of distinct hormones in the various cell types (prolactin or growth hormone in acidophils; thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH), adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH), or gonadotropins in basophils). The cells are grouped into small nests separated by the normal connective tissue framework of the anterior pituitary gland. By contrast, the resected lesion shows a monomorphic collection of cells, suggesting that they are all derived from one cell type and are producing the same hormone or set of hormones. The cells are growing in a diffuse pattern and lack the characteristic nesting architecture of the normal anterior pituitary gland. These features are diagnostic for a pituitary adenoma. Immunohistochemical (IHC) staining can be used to identify the hormone produced by a given cell. In the image to the right, lactotroph (prolactin-secreting) cells have been labeled with an antibody to prolactin. The antibody is linked to a brown chromogen. Notice that the lactotroph cells are scattered throughout the normal pituitary gland.

The mass was resected as completely as possible. The patient made an uneventful postoperative recovery and was discharged, to be followed in neurosurgery and endocrinology clinics.

How are pituitary adenomas classified?
Show answer

Pituitary adenomas are classified as functional (hormone producing), nonfunctioning (not producing hormone), or silent (hormone production can be identified at the tissue level by immunohistochemistry, but the patient is asymptomatic). Functional adenomas are further classified based on the hormone that is secreted.

4
Why does this patient have decreased libido and erectile dysfunction?
Show answer

Hyperprolactinemia inhibits the secretion of gonadotrophin-releasing hormone (GNRH) from the hypothalamus due to negative feedback inhibition. Decreased GNRH levels result in decreased pulsatile release of follicle stimulating hormone, luteinizing hormone and testosterone. Decreased levels of testosterone would account for this patient’s decreased libido and erectile dysfunction.

5
Which other endocrine studies might be useful in this patient?
Show answer

Evaluation of serum hormones is done for all patients with a sellar mass detected by MRI. Functional pituitary adenomas, as in this case, may secrete excessive amounts of a single hormone or multiple hormones, although usually there is one predominant hormone. When two hormones are secreted, the most common combination is prolactin and growth hormone. Furthermore, macroadenomas such as this one, even if nonfunctional, can encroach on and destroy the adjacent anterior pituitary, resulting in hypopituitarism. Definitive diagnosis is based on pathologic evaluation of the resected mass.

6
Why would nonfunctional pituitary adenomas (i.e., adenomas that do not secrete an active hormonal product) typically be larger than hormonally active tumors at the time of diagnosis?
Show answer

Functional adenomas tend to present earlier in their course than nonfunctional adenomas due to the symptoms caused by the excess hormones. For example, in premenopausal women, prolactinomas usually present at a much smaller size since they can cause amenorrhea, infertility, and galactorrhea. In men and postmenopausal women, the symptoms of hyperprolactinemia are more subtle and tumors are generally diagnosed when they have reached a large size and exert a mass effect or cause panhypopituitarism because of replacement of the normal gland. In this example, an advanced pituitary adenoma was found in an elderly woman who presented for evaluation of weakness and altered mental status. The adenoma had grown far beyond the confines of the sella turcica, markedly distorting the left lateral ventricle and encasing the internal carotid artery. Ancillary studies in this autopsy case revealed no evidence of hormone production by this adenoma. The cause of death was sudden hemorrhage into the tumor.

What is the pathogenesis of pituitary adenomas?
Show answer

G-protein mutations are one of the most common genetic abnormalities in pituitary adenomas. In particular, activating mutations of the alpha subunit of Gs, called Gsα (a stimulatory G-protein), which is encoded by the GNAS gene. Mutations are seen in approximately 40% of somatotroph adenomas and a minority of corticotroph adenomas. The constitutively activated G-protein causes persistent production of cAMP and unchecked cellular proliferation. GNAS mutations are not seen in adenomas that arise from cells whose hypothalamic release hormones do not signal via cAMP-dependent pathways; these include thyrotroph, lactotroph, and gonadotroph adenomas.

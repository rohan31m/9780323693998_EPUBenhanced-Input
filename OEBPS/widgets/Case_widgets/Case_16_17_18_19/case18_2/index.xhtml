I Feel it in My Bones

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • List the routes by which bacteria may seed bone and the organisms that are commonly responsible for pyogenic bone infection.
  2. • Describe the different organisms implicated in osteomyelitis and their associated clinical settings.
  3. • Compare and contrast the clinical and pathologic features of acute and chronic osteomyelitis.

A 4-year-old boy was brought to the pediatrician with a 3-day history of right knee pain. Ten days earlier, his mother noticed an infected scrape on his arm and treated it with topical over-the-counter antibiotic cream for several days. He had been having difficulty walking for the past two days and did not like to bear weight on that leg. She reported that he had no recent history of cough or dysuria.

Physical exam revealed erythema, warmth, swelling, and tenderness of the right lateral knee. Range of motion was reduced. Vital signs were significant for a temperature of 39° C. Laboratory analysis demonstrated a white blood cell (WBC) count of 18,500 cells/mm3 (4,500 –11,000/mm3) C-reactive protein of 15 mg/dL (<3.0 mg/dL) and an erythrocyte sedimentation rate of 100 mm/h (0–20 mm/h).

1
What is the most likely pathologic process in this patient?
Show answer

The increased WBC count, C-reactive protein, erythrocyte sedimentation rate, and low-grade fever all suggest an inflammatory process that is likely secondary to a bacterial infection.

A plain radiograph showed a lucent lesion in the right distal femoral metaphysis.

The patient was taken to surgery for open biopsy. Microscopic examination of the biopsy specimen showed a fragment of dead bone (sequestrum) surrounded by acute inflammatory cells and congested blood vessels.

2
What is the diagnosis in this case?
Show answer

The presence of a neutrophilic infiltrate is diagnostic of acute pyogenic osteomyelitis.

3
By which routes do bacteria infect bones?
Show answer

Organisms may reach the bone by (1) hematogenous spread; (2) extension from a contiguous site; and (3) direct implantation. In otherwise healthy children, most osteomyelitis is hematogenous in origin and develops in the long bones. The initiating bacteremia may stem from seemingly trivial mucosal injuries, such as may occur during defecation or vigorous chewing of hard foods, or from minor infections of the skin, as in this patient. In adults, however, osteomyelitis more often occurs as a complication of open fractures, surgical procedures, and diabetic infections of the feet.

4
Which organism would most likely grow in a culture of this specimen?
Show answer

In almost 50% of suspected cases of acute osteomyelitis, no organisms can be isolated; however, of culture-positive cases of pyogenic osteomyelitis, Staphylococcus aureus is responsible for 80% to 90% and is the most likely culprit in this case. These organisms express cell wall proteins that bind to bone matrix components such as collagen and facilitate adherence of the bacteria to bone. Escherichia coli, Pseudomonas, and Klebsiella are more frequently isolated from individuals with genitourinary tract infections or who are intravenous drug users. Mixed bacterial infections are seen in the setting of direct spread or inoculation of organisms during surgery or into open fractures. Individuals with hemoglobinopathies such as sickle cell disease and thalassemia are particularly susceptible to Salmonella infection, an organism that only rarely affects patients without a history of hemoglobinopathy.

5
Which areas of the bone are typically involved by bacterial infections in children and in adults?
Show answer

The location of the bone infections is influenced by the osseous vascular circulation, which varies with age. In the neonate, the metaphyseal vessels penetrate the growth plate, resulting in frequent infection of the metaphysis, epiphysis, or both. In children, localization of microorganisms in the metaphysis is typical. After growth plate closure, the metaphyseal vessels reunite with their epiphyseal counterparts and provide a route for the bacteria to seed the epiphyses and subchondral regions, which are common sites of infection in the adult.

The patient was treated for 6 weeks with intravenous antibiotics. Radiologic evaluation did not show resolution of the infection and a second biopsy was performed.

6
Which histologic features would be expected in chronic osteomyelitis?
Show answer

The sine qua non of chronic osteomyelitis is the presence of plasma cells, which are often associated with marrow fibrosis. Neutrophils may be present if there is an acute component to the infection.

  • Bone, histology
  • Plasma cells
  • Neutrophils

From Deyrup A., Siegal G.P., Practical Orthopedic Pathology: A Diagnostic Approach, Elsevier, 2015.

7
What is the significance of necrotic bone in chronic osteomyelitis?
Show answer

Necrotic bone may act as a nidus for infection. Although osteoclasts usually consume dead bone, they may not survive in the inflammatory milieu. Therefore, the necrotic bone must be removed surgically before the lesion can resolve.

8
What are the possible complications of osteomyelitis?
Show answer

In 5% to 25% of cases, acute osteomyelitis fails to resolve and persists as chronic infection. Chronic infections may develop when there is delay in diagnosis, extensive bone necrosis, inadequate antibiotic therapy or surgical debridement, or weakened host defenses. Complications of chronic osteomyelitis include pathologic fracture, secondary amyloidosis, endocarditis, sepsis, and development of squamous cell carcinoma in the draining sinus tracts and sarcoma in the infected bone.

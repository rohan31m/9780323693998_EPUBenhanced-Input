A State of Confusion

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  • • Describe the major clinical and pathologic features of Alzheimer disease.
  • • Describe the pathogenesis and genetic risk factors for developing Alzheimer disease.
  • • Compare and contrast the clinicopathologic features and underlying pathogenesis of Alzheimer disease with the other major neurodegenerative diseases (FTLD [frontotemporal lobar dementia], Parkinson, Huntington, ALS [amyotrophic lateral sclerosis]).

A 72-year-old woman was brought to her physician by her son because of increasing confusion and forgetfulness over the past several years. Several times during the past year she had lost her way returning from the grocery store. She had difficulty remembering names and had become increasingly depressed and uncommunicative.

Neurologic examination revealed a confused patient who was oriented to person, but not to place or time. She did not recognize her son. She could recall two of three items immediately after naming them, but could not remember them after 5 minutes. No focal neurologic abnormalities were identified. A head CT scan revealed generalized cortical atrophy and marked enlargement of the lateral ventricles (hydrocephalus ex vacuo) but no focal lesions. Her laboratory studies, including CSF examination, revealed no abnormalities.

Over the next 2 years, the patient’s cognitive status progressively deteriorated to the point that she required constant skilled nursing care and was transferred to a nursing home. Her course there was complicated by recurrent decubitus ulcers and several episodes of pneumonia; she died 3 years after admission following the development of severe bronchopneumonia.

At autopsy, the brain weighed 1000 gm (normal 1200 gm). There was generalized cerebral cortical atrophy with extensive widening of the sulci and narrowing of gyri in the patient's brain (compared to normal brain at right), indicative of substantial loss of brain parenchyma. The color difference is due to the meningeal covering of the normal brain.

Histologic examination revealed a dramatic loss of neurons in the pyramidal cell layer of the hippocampus. Well-developed neurofibrillary tangles and neuritic plaques were identified in the hippocampus and in multiple areas of the cerebral neocortex. Amyloid deposits were also identified in the walls of multiple blood vessels in the leptomeninges and brain parenchyma. Immunohistochemical stains for tau were positive in the neurofibrillary tangles.

1
What are neurofibrillary tangles and neuritic plaques?
Show answer

Tangles consist of paired helical filaments composed of an abnormally hyperphosphorylated and aggregated form of the protein tau. In Alzheimer disease (AD), tau accumulates within neuronal cell bodies to form neurofibrillary tangles and within swollen, dystrophic neuritic processes in the neuritic plaques. Plaques containing β-amyloid protein are another consistent feature of AD. β-amyloid is derived from a precursor molecule, amyloid precursor protein (APP). It is converted to β-amyloid (Aβ) by the action of the enzymes β-secretase and γ-secretase. In AD, Aβ deposits are found in the cores of neuritic plaques and in the walls of parenchymal and leptomeningeal blood vessels (amyloid angiopathy). These lesions, as well as those in amyloid angiopathy, can be identified with an immunohistochemical stain for β-amyloid.


2
What is this patient’s diagnosis?
Show answer

The pathologic findings (neurofibrillary tangles, neuritic plaques, amyloid angiopathy, and cerebral cortical atrophy) are diagnostic of Alzheimer disease, which is the most common cause of dementia in patients >65 years old. Approximately 85% of cases of AD are sporadic and of uncertain etiology, but familial forms of the disease have provided insights into disease pathogenesis.

3
What is the pathogenesis of AD?
Show answer

The fundamental abnormality in AD is the accumulation of Aβ and tau in specific brain regions, likely as a result of excessive production and defective removal, yielding plaques and tangles. Plaques are deposits of aggregated Aβ peptides in the neuropil, and tangles are aggregates of the microtubule binding protein tau, which develop intracellularly and then persist extracellularly after neuronal death. Both plaques and tangles appear to contribute to the neural dysfunction by mechanisms yet unknown.

4
What is the role of Aβ in the pathogenesis of Alzheimer disease?
Show answer

Abnormal β-amyloid deposition is one of the earliest pathologic findings in AD. Familial forms of the disease (which account for ~15% of cases) have provided supporting evidence. One important group of familial AD cases is associated with mutations in the gene encoding APP, located on chromosome 21. Similarly, individuals with Down syndrome (trisomy 21) have an extra copy of the APP gene and a tendency to develop early onset AD, perhaps due to the increase in APP gene dosage and APP protein levels. Another group of familial AD cases has been linked to mutations in genes encoding the cellular proteins presenilin-1 and presenilin-2, located on chromosomes 14 and 1, which are subunits of the γ-secretase enzyme that cuts APP to produce the pathogenic form of Aβ. These mutant forms of presenilin are associated with an increased production of Aβ.

5
What are additional genetic risk factors for AD?
Show answer

In addition to the mutations in the genes for APP and the genes that encode presenilin-1 and presenilin-2 (PSEN1 and PSEN2, respectively), the APOE locus on chromosome 19 that encodes apolipoprotein E (ApoE) has a strong influence on the risk of developing AD: there are three ApoE alleles (ε2, ε3, and ε4) that differ at two amino acid loci. The dosage of the ε4 allele increases the risk of AD and lowers the age of onset of the disease such that the incidence of the ε4 allele is overrepresented in AD patients.

6
Dementia is a feature of which other neurodegenerative diseases?
Show answer

Dementia can be a component of frontotemporal lobar degenerations (FTLDs), Parkinson disease (PD), and Huntington disease (HD). FTLDs are one of the more common causes of early onset dementia. This heterogeneous set of disorders is associated with focal degeneration of frontal and/or temporal lobes. In contrast to AD, alterations in personality, behavior, and language (aphasias) precede memory loss; global dementia occurs with progressive disease. Aβ-containing plaques are not seen; however, as with many neurodegenerative disease, FTLD is associated with cellular inclusions composed of various aggregated proteins, the two most common being tau (FTLD-tau) and TDP43 (FTLD-TDP).

Parkinson disease (PD) is associated with dementia in 10% to 15% of patients, although motor abnormalities predominate. Grossly, PD can be recognized by loss of dopaminergic neurons in the pars compacta of the substantia nigra (SN) in this patient with idiopathic Parkinson disease (right) compared to normal brain (left).

Progressive dementia is a component of Huntington disease (HD), an autosomal dominant disorder characterized by abnormal, involuntary, writhing, or jerking movements (choreoathetosis). Manifestations usually develop during adulthood. Morphologically, the major change in HD is loss of medium-sized spiny neurons in the neostriatum, seen grossly by severe atrophy of the caudate nuclei and, to a lesser degree, the putamen (normal brain left; patient brain, right). Cerebral cortical atrophy may also be evident, particularly in the frontal lobes. The disease is caused by expansion of a CAG repeat (an example of a triplet-repeat mutation) in the HD gene, which encodes a protein known as huntingtin. The greater the number of triplet repeats, the earlier the onset and the faster the progression of the disease.

Which major neurodegenerative disease is not associated with dementia?
Show answer

Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), also known as motor neuron disease, or Lou Gehrig disease (after the famous baseball player who succumbed to it), is a degenerative disorder characterized by a spontaneous, progressive loss of upper motor neurons in the cerebral cortex and lower motor neurons in the anterior horns of the spinal cord. Manifestations of upper motor neuron loss include spasticity, abnormally brisk deep tendon reflexes, and a Babinski sign (upgoing toes after application of painful pressure to the sole of the foot), while lower motor neuron loss leads to denervation atrophy of skeletal muscles, weakness, and fasciculations. These patients do not experience dementia.

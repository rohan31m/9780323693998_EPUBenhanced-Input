Sick and Tired

Objectives
  • • Describe how immune responses can damage the thyroid gland.
  • • Describe the causes and manifestations of hypothyroidism and the terms congenital hypothroidism and myxedema.
  • • Describe how disorders of thyroid function are diagnosed.
  • • Compare and contrast the different types of thyroiditis.

A 38-year-old attorney reported for her yearly physical examination with a 3-month history of fatigue, difficulty concentrating on her work, and a noticeable decline in her memory. She attributed many of these symptoms to the severe stress generated by her legal caseload. Further questioning revealed that the frequency of her bowel movements had decreased from once daily, 6 months ago, to once every 2 to 3 days. She had been slowly gaining weight, and despite warm weather, she felt chilled without a light sweater. Her only medication was an oral contraceptive.

Physical examination revealed a woman with a BMI of 25.2, sparse eyebrows (particularly at the lateral margins) and a diffusely, moderately enlarged thyroid gland with a prominent pyramidal lobe above the isthmus.

1
What do the patient’s serum hormone levels indicate?
Show answer

She has marked elevation of TSH and reduction in the free T4 level, which are diagnostic of hypothyroidism. TSH is the most sensitive and reliable test for thyroid function because TSH secretion responds in logarithmic fashion to small deviations of T4. This is part of a negative feedback loop that inhibits TSH release. The TSH level thus is a measure of how much T4 is “sensed” by the pituitary gland. Despite the wide reference range in the general population, an individual’s free T4 and total T4 are generally maintained within narrow limits. The total T4 reflects the majority bound to proteins and the small amount of active free T4. Birth control pills are one of many substances that can increase the quantity of binding proteins and drive up the level of total T4 without changing the free fraction. Of note, TSH levels are not elevated (and are typically decreased) in hypothyroidism caused by primary pituitary or hypothalamic disease.

2
What are the clinical manifestations of hypothyroidism in this case and how do they differ from hypothyroidism at an earlier age?
Show answer

The clinical presentation of hypothyroidism depends on the patient’s age: hypothyroidism developing in infancy or early childhood is called congenital hypothroidism while hypothyroidism in older adults results in a condition called myxedema. The patient in this case showed signs of myxedema, which is marked by a slowing of physical and mental activity. The initial symptoms include generalized fatigue, apathy, and mental sluggishness and may mimic depression. Patients with myxedema are listless, cold intolerant, and frequently overweight. Decreased sympathetic activity results in constipation and decreased sweating. The skin is cool and pale because of decreased blood flow. Accumulation of extracellular matrix molecules, such as glycosaminoglycans and hyaluronic acid, in the skin and subcutaneous tissue causes nonpitting edema, the hallmark of myxedema. Congenital hypothroidism was formerly widespread in areas of the world where dietary iodine deficiency was common; with increased supplementation of dietary salt with iodine, it has become rare. Clinical features include impaired development of the skeletal system and central nervous system, manifested by severe mental disability, short stature, coarse facial features, a protruding tongue, and umbilical hernia.

Fine needle aspiration of the pyramidal lobe revealed lymphocytes, plasma cells, and clusters of enlarged follicular epithelial cells with prominent nucleoli and abundant eosinophilic, granular cytoplasm (called Hurthle cells). The patient was placed on medication and asked to come for follow-up.

3
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

The symptoms and laboratory findings are indicative of hypothyroidism. The enlarged gland suggests a diffuse process, and the presence of antithyroid antibodies is consistent with an autoimmune reaction. The presence of lymphocytes, plasma cells and Hurthle cells supports a diagnosis of chronic lymphocytic (Hashimoto) thyroiditis, an autoimmune disease that destroys the thyroid gland. Although there are other causes of hypothyroidism, none of these is indicated in this patient.

What would histologic examination reveal if the thyroid gland were biopsied (not a typical practice in this disease)?
Show answer

In chronic lymphocytic (Hashimoto) thyroiditis, much of the thyroid parenchyma is replaced by a dense lymphocytic infiltrate that includes lymphoid follicles with germinal centers. Thyroid follicles are generally small, and they may undergo atrophy. A metaplastic change of some of the epithelial cells to Hürthle cells (cells with large amounts of granular eosinophilic cytoplasm and enlarged nuclei) is often observed. Interstitial fibrosis also may be prominent.

5
What is the significance of the antibodies against thyroglobulin and thyroid peroxidase that were detected in the serum?
Show answer

Thyroglobulin is secreted by thyroid follicular cells and stored in the follicular lumen as colloid. Thyroid peroxidase catalyzes the formation of monoiodotyrosine and diiodotyrosine, which then combine to form T4 and T3. These antibodies presumably reflect immune responses against self antigens. It is not established if these antibodies are merely markers for an antithyroid autoimmune reaction or if they contribute to the gland destruction.

6
What is the etiology of hypothyroidism in patients with this disease?
Show answer

It is not known why these patients develop immune responses against their own thyroid. The possible mechanisms of thyroid destruction include cell-mediated and antibody-mediated processes: presumably CD8+ CTLs react to some self-antigens, as yet unidentified, and kill thyroid epithelial cells and CD4+ T cells produce inflammatory cytokines such as interferon-γ in the thyroid gland, with resultant recruitment and activation of macrophages and damage to follicles.

7
What are the different types of thyroiditis and what are their clinical and microscopic features?
Show answer

There are four main types of thyroiditis: (1) chronic lymphocytic (Hashimoto) thyroiditis; (2) subacute granulomatous (de Quervain) thyroiditis; (3) subacute lymphocytic thyroiditis; and (4) Riedel thyroiditis. Subacute granulomatous (de Quervain) thyroiditis is believed to be caused by a viral infection or an inflammatory process triggered by viral infections and is not an autoimmune disease. Histologically, subacute granulomatous thyroiditis is characterized by granulomatous inflammation. Subacute lymphocytic thyroiditis, also known as silent or painless thyroiditis, may follow pregnancy and is most likely autoimmune in etiology, as circulating antithyroid antibodies are found in the majority of patients. Histologic examination of the thyroid reveals lymphocytic infiltration and hyperplastic germinal centers, similar to chronic lymphocytic (Hashimoto) thyroiditis. Riedel thyroiditis is a manifestation of IgG4-related disease and typically presents as a hard, fixed thyroid mass. Histologically, there is extensive fibrosis involving the thyroid and contiguous structures, simulating a thyroid malignancy. There may be idiopathic fibrosis in other sites of the body, such as the retroperitoneum.

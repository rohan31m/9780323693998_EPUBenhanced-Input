The Case of the Missing Myelin

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Describe the function of myelin and the consequences of its loss.
  2. • Describe the pathogenesis, clinical manifestations, spinal fluid changes, and pathology of multiple sclerosis.
  3. • Compare and contrast leukodystrophies and multiple sclerosis with regard to the distribution of lesions, genetics, and prognosis.

A 28-year-old woman presented to her primary care physician with a 1-week history of blurred vision in her left eye. She stated that shortly before these symptoms began, she had a flu-like illness that lasted a few days. Past medical history was significant for an episode of weakness and tingling in her right arm one year earlier that resolved after a few weeks, and a similar episode in her left leg three years earlier. Occasionally, she still felt tingling in her right arm. She had a 10-pack-year smoking history and drank two to three glasses of wine each week.

Physical examination revealed moderate leg weakness, with more subtle weakness in the left leg, and loss of vibratory and position sense in both legs. No other significant findings were noted.

1
What is the most likely diagnosis?
Show answer

The patient has experienced three neurologic events in three years. Transient, focal neurological deficits that are separated in time and that resolve with minimal residual effect are characteristic of multiple sclerosis. Multiple small ischemic infarcts due to arterial thromboembolism is another possibility, but quite unlikely in a young woman.

2
Which laboratory and imaging tests would be most appropriate?
Show answer

To support a diagnosis of multiple sclerosis, assessment of her CSF for total protein, pleocytosis (increased cell number) combined with a measurement of immunoglobulin level and the presence of oligoclonal bands is warranted. When present, these findings indicate local inflammation and immune activation. MRI of the brain and spinal cord is the best imaging choice. Inactive plaques are hyperintense on fluid-attenuated inversion recovery (FLAIR) and T2-weighted images. Gadolinium enhancement helps identify active lesions due to the increased vascular permeability and inflammation in these areas.

Lumbar puncture revealed an increased level of IgG and the presence of oligoclonal bands in the CSF. An MRI scan of the head revealed abnormal signal in the periventricular white matter. MRI of the spine revealed similar areas of signal abnormality within the spinal cord.

3
What is the diagnosis and how is it determined?
Show answer

This patient has multiple sclerosis (MS), which is primarily a clinical diagnosis based on a history of waxing and waning neurologic deficits involving multiple distinct areas of the CNS. Ancillary studies that can help make a diagnosis of MS include radiographic imaging studies and evaluation of the CSF for evidence of activation of an adaptive immune response (increased IgG levels, and oligoclonal IgG bands). Myelin basic protein is also occasionally present in the CSF during periods of active disease and indicates the presence of ongoing myelin breakdown. None of these ancillary studies is diagnostic of MS, but rather help support a diagnosis of MS in an appropriate clinical setting.

The patient gradually improved over the next several months and was fully ambulatory 8 months after the initial episode. Approximately 2 years later, after an uneventful pregnancy, the patient experienced an abrupt loss of vision in her right eye. She was treated with corticosteroids and her vision gradually improved over the next month although a small visual field deficit persisted. At age 34, the patient developed a tremor, most noticeable when she attempted to pick things up. She also stated that she felt "clumsy" and sometimes had difficulty maintaining her balance. Over the next year her tremor diminished, but she began to experience difficulty speaking (dysarthria) and experienced several transient episodes of double vision (diplopia).

4
What is the basis of the additional neurologic changes?
Show answer

MS can affect the brain stem, cerebellum, and spinal cord, resulting in palsies of cranial nerves (such as the optic nerve, causing vision loss), ataxia, nystagmus, and sensory and motor symptoms affecting the limbs and trunk. The symptoms typically resolve and recur (remitting relapsing MS).

At age 40 while riding as a passenger in a motor vehicle, she was involved in a collision and sustained massive chest trauma. She was transported by helicopter to a local trauma center but died en route and could not be resuscitated. An autopsy was performed.

Postmortem examination revealed evidence of extensive thoracic trauma with multiple rib fractures and cardiac contusion. Examination of the brain and spinal cord revealed multiple, irregular, sharply demarcated areas of gray, gelatinous discoloration that were distributed randomly. This distribution pattern accounts for the variable nature of the signs and symptoms that can be seen clinically in patients with multiple sclerosis.

Microscopic evaluation of H and E sections of the lesions showed areas of pallor consistent with areas of demyelination. There was a sharp demarcation between the lesion and the relatively normal myelinated white matter. This abrupt transition was also seen in a Luxol Fast Blue stain (which stains myelin) with dark blue staining of the unaffected white matter and complete absence of myelin staining in the plaque (higher power view). There is no significant cellular infiltration in the plaque, indicating that the demyelination occurred remotely. By contrast, active plaques are quite cellular due to the presence of inflammatory cells, most of which are macrophages actively engaged in phagocytosis of lipid-rich myelin debris. Active plaques are often centered on small vessels and demonstrate reactive astrocytic changes)

5
What is the pathogenesis of this disease?
Show answer

MS is an autoimmune disease. Environmental, genetic, and immunological factors have all been implicated and may share a role in its pathogenesis. It is possible that exposure to an environmental agent early in life plays a role in subsequent development of MS. The nature of the hypothetical environmental factor (possibly an infectious agent) is unknown. A role for genetic influences is suggested by (1) the observation that first-degree relatives of MS patients have a 15-fold increased risk of developing MS compared with the general population; (2) its association with certain HLA class II alleles; and (3) the observation that there is a significantly higher concordance rate for MS in monozygotic than in dizygotic twins.

6
How do leukodystrophies differ from MS?
Show answer

The leukodystrophies are a group of hereditary disorders (usually autosomal recessive, although some are X-linked) characterized by abnormal myelin synthesis or turnover or both. Although the biochemical basis of most forms of leukodystrophy remains incompletely understood, some are caused by well-characterized enzyme deficiencies. Examples include metachromatic leukodystrophy (arylsulfatase A deficiency), Krabbe disease (galactocerebroside β-galactosidase deficiency), and adrenoleukodystrophy (a defect in a peroxisomal membrane-associated protein). These diseases differ from MS in many important ways: in addition to being hereditary, the leukodystrophies progress relentlessly, usually from childhood, in contrast to the waxing and waning course typical of MS. Many leukodystrophies involve the peripheral nervous system and the CNS, in contrast to MS, which is limited to the CNS. Furthermore, the leukodystrophies involve the white matter in a widespread, symmetric pattern, whereas MS is characterized by an irregular distribution of lesions. There is typically sparing of the subcortical fibers, which can be seen on gross examination, while the central white matter appears discolored and shrunken due to widespread, symmetric myelin injury. Histologically, there is a generalized loss of myelin staining within the deep white matter, but relative sparing of the U fibers immediately beneath the cortex.

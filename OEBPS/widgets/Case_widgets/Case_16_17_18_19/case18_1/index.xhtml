Objectives
  1. • Review the regulation of bone formation, resorption, and remodeling.
  2. • Describe the pathogenesis of conditions associated with deficient bone formation and maintenance.
  3. • Compare and contrast the different types of bone fractures.

A 75-year-old woman was brought to a regional hospital by her son. She had fallen 6 weeks earlier and had been limping since the incident, but had resisted seeking medical attention. She was lucid and said that she had had no physical problems before her fall, except periodic back pain that waxed and waned. On physical examination, the physician noted prominent kyphosis of her thoracic spine and exaggerated cervical lordosis. She was unable to bear weight on her right leg and had marked pain on palpation of her upper right thigh.

Radiographs revealed a nonaligned fracture of the neck of the right femur, which was surrounded by a large, exaggerated callus. A chest X-ray showed generalized cortical thinning and radiographic lucency of her skeleton, as well as loss of height of multiple vertebrae.

1
What are the different types of fracture and which type does this patient have?
Show answer

This patient has two types of fracture: (1) a simple, nondisplaced fracture of the right femur and (2) vertebral compression fractures. In simple fractures, the overlying skin is intact, in contrast to compound fractures in which the bone communicates with the skin surface. Fractures in which the bone is shattered into small fragments are called comminuted fractures. When the ends of the bones of the fracture site are not aligned, the fracture is referred to as displaced. Stress fractures develop slowly due to increased physical activity in which the bone is subjected to repetitive loads. “Greenstick” fractures are most common in the soft bones of infants and young children; they extend only partially through the bone. This patient’s fractures would also be considered pathologic fractures since they involve bone weakened by an underlying disease process, such as a tumor or, in this case, osteoporosis.

2
Why is the patient’s fracture callus markedly enlarged?
Show answer

The patient has been walking with a femoral fracture for 6 weeks. Since the fracture was not immobilized, there has been movement of the callus, preventing normal maturation. Typically, after a fracture, a soft callus of predominantly uncalcified tissue forms by the end of the first week and anchors the ends of the fractured bone. After approximately two weeks, the deposition of woven bone transforms the soft callus into a bony callus. The stresses of weight bearing determine remodeling. In this patient, the continual movement of the fractured bones prevented development of a bony callus.

3
Which cells are involved in bone repair and in maintaining normal bone homeostasis?
Show answer

The three main cell types in bone are osteoblasts, osteoclasts, and osteocytes. Osteoblasts line the surface of bone where active synthesis of new bone is occurring (e.g., fracture callus) and produce osteoid, the organic matrix that becomes mineralized to form bone. The osteoclast is the cell responsible for bone resorption (note the “scalloped” appearance of the resorbed bone adjacent to the osteoclasts). Osteoclasts are multinucleated cells derived from monocyte precursors produced from hematopoietic stem cells in the marrow. This is in contrast to osteoblasts, which are also mesenchymal cells but are not derived from hematopoietic precursors. Osteocytes are cells derived from osteoblasts; they are created as osteoblasts are surrounded by bone, but send out processes that allow intercellular communication to occur. They are metabolically active and control serum calcium and phosphorus levels.

The patient underwent a surgical hip replacement with placement of a right femoral head prosthesis. At her follow-up visit, a dual energy x-ray absorptiometry (DXA) scan was scheduled. There was marked reduction in bone mineral density involving the opposite femur and spine. Her T score was −3.2.

4
What is a T score?
Show answer

The T score is used to assess a patient’s bone mineral density (BMD). The reading obtained by DXA scan is compared to the mean BMD of an average healthy young adult. Negative numbers indicate a BMD lower than the mean and the number of standard deviations (SD) from the mean is indicative of osteopenia (1 to 2.5 SD below the mean) and osteoporosis (greater than 2.5 SD below the mean).

5
What is the diagnosis in this case?
Show answer

This patient has severe osteoporosis, which is defined as decreased bone mass (osteopenia) of sufficient severity to significantly increase the risk of fracture. Osteoporosis is diagnosed when (1) the bone mass is at least 2.5 standard deviations below mean peak bone mass in young adults (as determined radiographically), or (2) there is an atraumatic or vertebral compression fracture. The patient’s diminished bone mass caused her vertebral compression fractures and increased her susceptibility to hip fracture following her fall.

6
What are the different types of osteoporosis?
Show answer

The most common types of osteoporosis are senile osteoporosis and post-menopausal osteoporosis. In addition, osteoporosis may arise secondary to endocrine disorders (e.g., hyperthyroidism), gastrointestinal disorders (e.g., malnutrition), or drugs (e.g., corticosteroids).

What is the etiology of post-menopausal osteoporosis?
Show answer

Decreased estrogen levels after menopause increase both bone resorption and bone formation, but the latter does not keep up with the former, leading to “high-turnover” osteoporosis. The decreased estrogen appears to increase secretion of inflammatory cytokines that stimulate osteoclast recruitment and activity by increasing the levels of RANKL (receptor activity of nuclear factor kappa-B ligand). The increase in osteoclast activity affects mainly bones or portions of bones with increased surface area, such as the cancellous compartment of vertebral bodies. Morphologically, this is manifested by loss of horizontal trabeculae and thickened vertical trabeculae (normal bone, left; osteoporotic bone, right). Cytokines such as IL-6, TNF-α, and IL-1 also have been implicated in postmenopausal osteoporosis. Their production may be increased secondary to or independent of reduced estrogen signaling. This patient’s severe osteoporosis is most likely due to factors related to her age (senile osteoporosis) as well as her hormone status (post-menopausal).

8
What is the pathophysiology of senile osteoporosis?
Show answer

Osteoblasts from older individuals have reduced proliferative and biosynthetic potential when compared with osteoblasts from younger individuals. Also, the cellular response to growth factors bound to the extracellular matrix becomes attenuated in older individuals. The net result is a diminished capacity to make bone (“low-turnover” osteoporosis). Furthermore, elderly patients may have poor nutritional status with insufficient calcium intake. Physical activity and time outdoors (exposure to sunlight increases vitamin D levels) are also typically decreased in this patient population. In senile osteoporosis, cortical bone is thinned by subperiosteal and endosteal resorption; in severe cases, cortical bone mimics cancellous bone.


9
What is the cause of this patient’s kyphosis and lordosis?
Show answer

In patients with osteoporosis, vertebral fractures frequently occur in the thoracic and lumbar regions, causing pain and, when multiple, significant loss of height and various deformities, including lumbar lordosis and kyphoscoliosis.

10
Why are vertebrae preferentially affected in osteoporosis?
Show answer

As mentioned above, the postmenopausal increase in osteoclast activity is most prominent in bones with increased surface area (e.g., the cancellous compartment of vertebral bodies). Thus, loss of bone in postmenopausal osteoporosis is more rapid in trabecular bone than in cortical bone; vertebrae, because of their prominent trabecular component, are often the most severely affected bones and are prone to fracture. In this example, note the variation in the width of the vertebrae, the presence of fractures, and the very porous appearance of the bone.

What is the typical histologic appearance of osteoporosis and how does this differ from osteomalacia?
Show answer

In osteoporosis, trabeculae would be reduced in number and width, but the matrix is normal and fully mineralized as in this case where there are decreased numbers of thinned trabeculae. The tissue seen consists mainly of intertrabecular fat. In osteomalacia, mineralization is defective; as a result, the trabeculae are rimmed by wide seams of osteoid (unmineralized bone matrix).

Objectives
  1. • Discuss the pathogenesis and long-term consequences of alcohol abuse.
  2. • Describe the pathophysiology, morphologic features, and natural history of acute and chronic pancreatitis.
  3. • Describe the effects of radiation on normal tissue.

A 58 year-old man presented to the Emergency Department with a 3-day history of weakness and fatigue. On questioning, he stated that he had recently lost his job and had been drinking up to a fifth of vodka per day. He also had smoked 1–2 packs of cigarettes per day for the past 30 years. He took ibuprofen occasionally for headaches, although he couldn’t remember the last time he did so. On physical examination he was thin, pale, and somewhat disheveled; his hair was uncombed and he had not shaved for several days. Stool was black and a test for occult blood was positive. Dermatologic inspection revealed multiple spider angiomas on his upper trunk and upper arms.

1
What is the significance of the positive fecal occult blood test? What is the likely cause?
Show answer

A positive test indicates gastrointestinal bleeding, which may explain his anemia. Alcoholics are prone to acute gastritis and ulceration. In chronic alcohol abuse, cirrhosis leads to portal hypertension, with consequent esophageal varices which can bleed. Furthermore, the damaged liver is unable to produce clotting factors, resulting in coagulopathies. However, in this instance there is no evidence of liver failure, coagulopathy or varices, suggesting that the bleeding likely stems from a direct toxic effect of alcohol on the gastric mucosa.

Colonoscopy revealed tar-like blood (melena) but no focal lesions. Endoscopy showed erythematous gastric mucosa with several pinpoint “petechial” sites of bleeding, but no frank ulceration. Over several days in the hospital, his stool no longer appeared black and testing was negative for occult blood. After 5 days, increased reticulocytes appeared in the peripheral blood. Social services learned that his wife had recently left him and that he lived alone. Arrangements were made for him to participate in an alcohol treatment program and he was discharged with a prescription for a nicotine patch.

2
How does alcohol contribute to gastritis? Which other inflammatory conditions of the upper aerodigestive tract are associated with alcohol abuse?
Show answer

Alcohol has a direct, dose-dependent toxic effect on the gastric mucosa. This effect may be exacerbated by nonsteroidal antiinflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen, which are also toxic to the surface epithelium of the stomach, in part by inhibiting the synthesis of prostaglandins that have protective effects against gastric acid. Alcohol, particularly when consumed in drinks with a high alcohol content (>10% alcohol), is also toxic to the oropharyngeal and esophageal mucosa, although the resilient squamous epithelium covering these surfaces is much more resistant to the effects of alcohol than the glandular surface lining the stomach. Alcohol is also associated with esophagitis due to the link between alcohol consumption and esophageal reflux.

The patient was lost to follow-up, but returned two years later with severe persistent epigastric abdominal pain associated with nausea and vomiting. He stated that he continued to smoke and drank one to two fifths of whiskey daily and a 12-pack of beer on the weekends. He had experienced several similar episodes over the last 9 months, but this time his symptoms were much worse. His abdomen was diffusely tender to palpation. Stool was negative for occult blood. Vital signs showed a temperature of 99.6° C, heart rate of 95 bpm, and blood pressure 107/60 mm Hg. Laboratory studies were notable for leukocytosis, WBC 21,100/µL, (4,500–11,000/µL) ) consisting mainly of neutrophils, normal hematocrit and platelet count, normal PT and PTT, serum amylase of 437 U/L (25–125 U/L), a serum lipase of 102 U/L (23–85 U/L), and a fasting glucose level of 160 mg/dL (70–110 mg/dL).

3
What is the most likely diagnosis?
Show answer

In this clinical setting, the elevated serum amylase and lipase are highly suggestive of acute pancreatitis. Since he has had multiple episodes in the past, it is likely that he also has chronic pancreatitis.

4
What are the major risk factors for acute pancreatitis and how do they contribute to its development?
Show answer

Acute pancreatitis can stem from duct obstruction, acinar cell injury, or more subtle effects on acinar cell function that lead to the premature activation of proteolytic enzymes. The major risk factors are alcohol abuse, which may result in acinar cell injury and also contribute to defects in acinar cell function that lead to activation of proteases, and pancreatic duct obstruction, most often due to gallstones. Other risk factors include inherited variants of trypsin, an important pancreatic protease; various drugs; and hypercalcemia.


The patient was admitted to the hospital and treated with intravenous fluids and analgesics. His pain resolved after 2 days and he was hemodynamically stable throughout his course. His serum lipase, amylase, and glucose levels returned to normal. However, on day 4 he experienced mild epigastric pain and his lipase and amylase levels rose slightly. A CT scan revealed multiple calcifications in the head of the pancreas and a 6 cm round, well encapsulated, fluid-filled cyst at the head of the pancreas. What is the explanation for the patient’s clinical progression and imaging findings?
Show answer

The CT scan reveals a pancreatic pseudocyst and dystrophic calcifications in the setting of acute and chronic pancreatitis. Alcohol abuse is nearly always the cause of chronic pancreatitis, which is characterized by perilobular fibrosis, destruction of the endocrine and exocrine pancreas, and eventually pancreatic insufficiency and diabetes. Necrosis of peripancreatic fat may give rise to dystrophic calcifications and digestion of the pancreatic tissue may give rise to pseudocysts, localized collections of liquefied necrotic and hemorrhagic material rich in pancreatic enzymes. The walls of these collections lack an epithelial lining, hence the term pseudocyst.


Because of its large size, the pseudocyst was drained endoscopically. The patient remained afebrile and his epigastric pain remitted. He was again strongly encouraged to quit drinking and smoking and referred to an alcohol treatment program at discharge.

The patient returned to the Emergency Department two years later after noticing a mass in his right upper neck. Questioning revealed that he had continued to smoke and drink to excess. Physical examination revealed several firm, fixed 2-cm submandibular lymph nodes and a 1-cm reddish area of ulceration on the underside of the tongue.

A biopsy of the oral lesion revealed dysplastic squamous epithelium, nests of invading squamous cells, and areas with keratin pearls diagnostic of invasive squamous cell carcinoma.

What are the major environmental risk factors for oropharyngeal carcinoma? How do they contribute to the development of cancer?
Show answer

Oropharyngeal carcinoma is associated with high risk HPV infection (e.g., types 16 and 18), tobacco use (smoked > smokeless), and alcohol abuse. High-risk HPV strains express E6 and E7 oncoproteins that inhibit p53 and Rb, respectively. Tobacco smoke contains many carcinogenic factors, including benz[a]pyrene, a procarcinogen that is converted by the P-450 system into a carcinogenic epoxide that directly targets DNA. Alcohol appears to synergize with smoking to increase the risk multiplicatively. How alcohol increases risk is not certain; chronic injury associated with mucosal damage and repair and the toxic effects of certain metabolites, particularly acetaldehyde, may contribute.

Tumors caused by HPV frequently start in the tonsils, whereas tumors caused by smoking and alcohol often originate elsewhere within a field of “cancerized” (mutated) cells. Carcinogen-associated cancers may be preceded by recognizable precursor lesions such as leukoplakia and are more likely to be multifocal.

7
Examination of the tumor by whole exome sequencing would most likely reveal which types of mutations?
Show answer

This tumor is likely to be related to carcinogen exposure and, therefore TP53 mutations, the presence of a tobacco-exposure mutational signature, and a high mutational burden would be expected. By contrast, in HPV-related cancers TP53 is generally wild type (and the protein is inactivated by HPV E6).


The primary tumor and metastatic lesions were treated with chemotherapy and radiation. His lymph nodes returned to normal size and the ulcerated sites of the primary tumor healed; however, he developed a dry mouth and reported that he had lost his sense of taste.

8
What is the basis of the patient’s dry mouth and loss of taste sensation? Which types of morphologic changes would be expected in his salivary glands?
Show answer

Although attempts are made to limit exposure of normal tissues, delivery of therapeutic radiation to areas like the neck and the mouth inevitably damages normal tissues in the field. Acutely, ionizing radiation is most toxic to rapidly proliferating cells, which is the basis for its use in treating cancers. However, slowly dividing normal cells are also damaged, particularly endothelial cells. This vascular damage often leads to endothelial proliferation, intimal thickening, and eventual narrowing or obliteration of vascular lumens, with secondary atrophy of exposed tissues, such as salivary glands. Consequently, there is fibrosis and fatty infiltration of the gland (middle, compare to normal salivary gland, far left). Loss of taste due to damage of taste receptor cells by radiation is another recognized complication.

9
In addition to head and neck cancer, which other cancers are associated with alcohol abuse? Why?
Show answer

Squamous cell carcinoma of the esophagus, hepatocellular carcinoma, and breast carcinoma are associated with alcohol consumption. Squamous cell carcinoma of the esophagus is related to direct damage to the mucosa by alcohol and associated chronic inflammation and repair; risk is greatly increased by concomitant smoking.

Hepatocellular carcinoma occurs in the setting of alcoholic liver injury, particularly in a background of alcoholic cirrhosis, possibly related to growth factors produced during chronic inflammation and scarring. Alcohol consumption is associated with a small increase in the risk of breast cancer in women, possibly related to effects on estrogen levels.

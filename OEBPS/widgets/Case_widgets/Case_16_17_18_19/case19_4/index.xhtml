Objectives
  1. • Describe the causes, pathophysiology and presentation of malnutrition
  2. • Compare and contrast the features of kwashiorkor and marasmus
  3. • Compare the features of eating disorders
Summary of case:

A 1-year-old male child was brought to the emergency room with severe edema, and generalized rash, and abdominal distension. The history revealed the baby had poor weight gain and had been exclusively fed a diet of rice-milk. Upon examination, the baby looked lethargic and had bilateral pedal edema, palpable liver and skin desquamation. Anthropometric examination demonstrated that a mid-upper-arm-circumference (MUAC) was 95 mm and a weight-for-height ratio was 3 standard deviations below normal. Laboratory investigation revealed Hb 6.5gm/dL with normocytic normochromic red cell morphology, serum albumin 1.8 gm/dL, signs of coagulopathy and vitamin deficiency. Abdominal imaging showed enlarged fatty liver.

1
What is the significance of such presentation in the baby?
Show answer

Poor weight gain and edema in the baby can be associated with a number of conditions, such as insufficient dietary intake, medical illnesses, and socio-environmental factors. Excessive feeding with rice-milk, decreased serum albumin, anemia with normocytic normochromic red cells and development of edema point towards markedly reduced intake of proteins and lesser reduction of caloric intake. Deranged anthropometric measurements, such as decreased mid-upper-arm-circumference (MUAC) and lower weight-for-height ratio are suggestive of a severe acute malnutrition.

2
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

The child has kwashiorkor. The diagnosis in this patient is based on 1) clinical signs and symptoms consistent with kwashiorkor (lethargic baby with edema, generalized rash, abdominal distension resulting from enlarged fatty liver and edema fluid collection, and skin desquamation) and 2) anthropometric measurements indicating a severe acute malnutrition (mid-upper-arm-circumference <115 mm and weight-for-height is 3 standard deviations below normal)

3
What is severe acute malnutrition?
Show answer

Malnutrition results from inadequate intake of proteins and calories (primary malnutrition) or results from malabsorption, impaired utilization or storage, and excess loss or increased need for nutrients (secondary malnutrition). UNICEF/ WHO define malnutrition as a life-threatening wasting disorder of children caused by poor nutrient intake and/ or recurrent illnesses. Severe acute malnutrition (SAM) is a critical health condition with severe wasting defined by a weight-for-height ratio being 3 SD below normal, a mid-upper arm circumference (MUAC) <115 mm or presence of bilateral pitting edema.

4
What are the causes of malnutrition?
Show answer

Several conditions can lead to malnutrition such as poverty, acute and chronic illness - wasting diseases, such as advanced cancers and AIDS, failure to recognize nutritional needs, and excessive alcohol intake. Malnutrition, more specifically vitamin deficiencies (thiamine, folate, pyridoxine, vitamin A) are caused by poor diet, defective gastrointestinal absorption, abnormal nutrient utilization and storage and increased metabolic demands. Ignorance about increased nutritional needs in infants, and pregnant females is also a contributing factor. Other causes of malnutrition include self-imposed dietary restriction (like anorexia nervosa, bulimia), malabsorption syndromes, drugs, and genetic diseases.

5
How does kwashiorkor baby present with?
Show answer

A baby with kwashiorkor typically presents with edema in the leg and abdomen, muscle wasting, growth retardation, skin and hair change and lethargy. Protein deficiency (hypoalbuminemia) leads to fluid retention usually in leg, feet and abdomen. Inadequate protein and calorie intake results in stunted growth. In the face of severe protein deficiency, body’s muscle mass (mainly limbs) breaks down to supply amino acids for vital functions, eventually leading to muscle wasting. Children with kwashiorkor have characteristic skin lesions with alternating zones of hyperpigmentation, desquamation, and hypopigmentation imparting a ‘flaky paint’ appearance. Hair looks thin, brittle or loses its color. Baby may present with hepatomegaly with fatty liver. If untreated, affected children may have long term complication like impaired cognitive disorders.

6
How is marasmus different from kwashiorkor?
Show answer

Marasmus and kwashiorkor represent two ends of a disease spectrum with considerable overlap. Marasmus develops when the diet is severely lacking in calories. Weight of the marasmus child usually falls to 60% of normal weight for sex, height and age. Child suffers from growth retardation, and loss of muscle mass because of catabolism and depletion of somatic protein compartment. Although both marasmus and kwashiorkor share some common features, a number of differences exist between them.

7
What is the malnutrition pattern in high-resource countries?
Show answer

Elderly, chronically ill and bedridden patients often develop secondary malnutrition. They have an increased risk of infection, impaired wound healing, sepsis and postoperative mortality. There are two main pathophysiological factors – 1) energy and nutrient deficiency: dysphagia following a stroke or short bowl syndrome may result in inadequate nutrient intake or absorption, 2) inflammation-related malnutrition: release of cytokines and prostaglandins in chronic diseases (cancer, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, inflammatory bowel disease and renal failure) causes an increase in resting energy expenditure leading to protein catabolism in skeletal muscle.

With antibiotic therapy, diet supplementation and supportive case, the patient was discharged 2 weeks after admission.

8
What is the leading cause of death in severe acute malnutrition?
Show answer

The leading cause of death in severe acute malnutrition (SAM) is bacterial infection. SAM weakens the immune system making the malnourished children susceptible to life-threating infection, such as pneumonia often leading to death if untreated.

9
What are the two most common eating disorders and how do they present?
Show answer

Two most common eating disorders are anorexia nervosa and bulimia. Anorexia nervosa is an eating disorder characterized by a severe restriction of food intake, fear of gaining weight and body image distortion. This self-starvation leads to significant weight loss. Patients with anorexia nervosa have characteristic features, such as amenorrhea, osteoporosis, and gelatinous transformation of bone marrow. Conversely, bulimia involves episodes of uncontrollable eating (binging) followed by purging behaviors, such as vomiting, or laxative misuse to prevent weight loss. Despite some overlapping features, there are a number of key differences between anorexia nervosa and bulimia.

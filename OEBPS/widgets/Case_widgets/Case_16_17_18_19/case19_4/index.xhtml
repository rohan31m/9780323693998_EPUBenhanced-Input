Objectives
  1. • Relate the clinical features of fragile X syndrome to underlying molecular abnormalities
  2. • Describe the mechanisms that lead to the causative genetic abnormalities
  3. • Discuss the spectrum of fragile X syndrome
Summary of case:

A 3-year-old male child was brought to a pediatrician by his parents with concerns of global developmental delay. The child was anxious, hyperactive with tantrums and typically hypotonic. His mother first recognized that rolling and crawling developmental milestones were delayed. The parents also noted that the child was nonverbal and uninterested in communication with other children. Physical examination was unremarkable except the child had slightly large forehead. There was no history of prenatal illness, teratogenic drug intake, and alcohol use. The history revealed that the child’s mother struggled academically in school and attained only an eighth-grade education. A maternal uncle also had academic difficulties and significant intellectual disability.

1
What is fragile X syndrome?
Show answer

Fragile X syndrome, a common genetic cause of intellectual disability, results from a trinucleotide (CGG-repeat) expansion in FMR1 gene. Originally, its name comes from the observation that when metaphase chromosomes are prepared from cells of affected individuals cultured in folate-deficient media or in the presence of inhibitors of DNA replication, a gap or a break appears in multiple chromosomes, including a “fragile site” in the X chromosome in band q27.3.

2
What physical and behavioral features are suggestive of fragile X syndrome?
Show answer

Fragile X syndrome is associated with characteristic physical features. Affected males have large head circumference, prominent forehead, large face, prominent jaw and large ears and abnormally large testes; however, these are not always present in young children until puberty. As in this case, the child had only prominent forehead. Behavioral features in fragile X syndrome are hyperactivity, inattentiveness and autistic features, such as poor eye contact, hypersensitivity to stimuli like light or sound, social anxiety, hand biting or flapping. This child showed some of these features (hyperactivity with tantrums, poor social interaction and anxiety).

3
What is the significance of family history in fragile X syndrome?
Show answer

Fragile X syndrome is inherited as an X-linked disorder. Generally, most cases have an unremarkable family history. In some families, the males on the maternal side, such as a maternal uncle or maternal cousin, have a history of mental disability. In contrast, affected females show a milder phenotype including developmental disability. In this case, the child’s maternal uncle had intellectual disability and the mother had a history of learning difficulties. However, since fragile X syndrome is the most common form of inherited developmental delay, a genetic counselling including fragile X testing should be considered even in the absence of a family history.

Fragile X syndrome has a unique pedigree (mode of inheritance). Around 20% of carrier males are clinically normal. Carrier males transmit their trait to affected grandchildren, generally through phenotypically normal daughters. However, in contrast to many X-linked traits, about 30 to 50% of the carrier females are affected. Clinical features of the disease worsen with the successive generations (anticipation).

4
What is trinucleotide repeat expansion?
Show answer

Trinucleotide repeat expansion is an important genetic cause of human genetic disease, particularly neurodegenerative disorders. Most of these expansions usually share the nucleotides G and C. Repeat expansion above a certain threshold level impairs gene function, leading to disease, and is susceptible to further repeat expansion with each generation, explaining to phenomenon known as anticipation. These repeat expansions can cause diseases through several mechanisms, including: 1) loss of function of the affected gene, typically by transcriptional silencing, such as in fragile X syndrome (CGG repeats involving noncoding regions); 2) toxic gain of function caused by an altered protein, as in Huntington disease (CAG repeats involving the coding region) and 3) toxic gain of function caused by an abnormal RNA, as in fragile X-associated tremor-ataxia syndrome (CGG repeats involving noncoding region)

5
Describe the spectrum of fragile X disorders.
Show answer

Fragile X syndrome is caused by a CGG repeat expansion of greater than 200 repeats (termed a ‘full’ mutation) in the 5´ noncoding region of FMR1 gene. Generally, the promoter region is fully methylated resulting in transcriptional silencing (leading to loss of function). In contrast, another group of affected individuals carry fragile X premutation, characterized by smaller repeat expansion of 55 to 200 CGG repeats. Unlike fragile X syndrome, this alteration leads to increased transcription of CGG repeats affecting mRNA (a gain of function mutation). Diseases caused by these abnormalities include fragile X-associated tremor-ataxia syndrome (FXTAS), fragile X-associated primary ovarian insufficiency (FXPOI) and other neurodegenerative disorders.

6
What is the molecular basis of intellectual disability in fragile X syndrome?
Show answer

The normal FMR1 gene contain up to 55 CGG repeats in its 5´ UTR. When the trinucleotide repeats exceed greater than 200 repeats, the DNA of the 5´ region of the gene becomes abnormally methylated resulting in transcriptional suppression of the FMR1 gene, and loss of function of FMRP (fragile X messenger ribonucleoprotein), the protein product of the FMR1gene.

Normally, FMRP protein is abundant in brain and testes. It is thought to act in two ways – 1) FMRP selectively binds to mRNAs and regulates their intracellular transport to dendrites and, 2) FMRP is a translation regulator that suppresses protein synthesis from mRNAs in response to signals generated by a metabotropic glutamate receptor that maintains synaptic plasticity, which is essential for learning and memory. Thus, a reduction in FMRP in fragile X syndrome results in loss of synaptic plasticity and intellectual disability.

7
What is size mosaicism in fragile X syndrome?
Show answer

Variable CGG repeat expansion in the 5´ UTR of FMR1 gene can lead to size mosaicism. Mosaicism is the presence of two or more genetically distinct populations of cells derived from a single zygote. For example, an individual might have one population of cells with a full mutation, and a second with a premutation. The number and type of cells bearing these two abnormalities will determine the disease penetrance and phenotype.

Considering the presentation and family history, a genetic counseling with fragile X testing was suggested and showed FMR1 mutation in the child.

8
Which genetic testing is done for fragile X syndrome?
Show answer

Genetic testing is primarily done to detect the number of CGG repeats in the FMR1 gene. Available tests include chromosomal microarray analysis, fragile X testing, and next generation sequencing. PCR based assays are commonly used to identify premutation and full mutation status.

9
Does carrier testing have a role in the mother?
Show answer

Transmission through males and females have different impacts on changes in CGG repeat expansions. When a carrier male transmits the repeats to their progeny there is generally only a minimal change in repeat number, but if the premutation is passed on by a carrier female, the repeat numbers are likely to be significantly increased. Thus, it is believed that the premutation is converted to full mutation only during the process of oogenesis. In this case, the child’s mother is likely to be a carrier of a premutation or full mutation.

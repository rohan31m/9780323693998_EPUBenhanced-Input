A Weak Spot

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • List the histologic features that are evaluated when grading an astrocytic neoplasm.
  2. • Describe the clinical course and prognosis of astrocytic neoplasms.
  3. • Compare the molecular genetics of the different types of astrocytic neoplasms.

A 40-year-old man presented to the Emergency Department following an episode of uncontrollable shaking in his left arm and leg, followed by weakness in his left leg. He reported that he had experienced headaches and weakness in his left arm during the previous three weeks. His medical history was significant for a diagnosis of diffuse astrocytoma three years earlier. Although radiation therapy was recommended, he declined treatment and had not sought further medical care.

On physical examination, he was found to have mild papilledema, decreased strength in his left arm and leg, and brisk deep tendon reflexes on the left side of his body compared with the right. A post-contrast T1-weighted MRI of his head was performed and revealed a large, ring-enhancing, right frontal lobe mass with associated mass effects, including midline shift to the left, sulcal effacement, and compression of lateral ventricle on the same side.

  • Ring-enhancing lesion
  • Midline shift
  • Lateral ventricle

Dr. Arie Perry, UCSF.

1
Which types of lesions have a ring-enhancing appearance on imaging?
Show answer

The ring-enhancing appearance of this lesion is characteristic of glioblastoma and other high-grade gliomas. Typically, the dark center corresponds to necrosis, the ring-like enhancement to foci of microvascular proliferation, and the surrounding “edema” to infiltrative tumor. Other processes that can produce a similar radiographic appearance include other types of neoplasms (e.g., lymphoma, metastatic carcinoma), abscesses, CNS toxoplasmosis, and organizing infarcts.

2
How did the lesion shown in the MRI cause this patient’s neurological symptoms?
Show answer

The shaking in this case was a primary motor seizure caused by involuntary firing of neurons in the right motor cortex, triggered by the adjacent right frontal lobe lesion. Because most axons from these cortical motor neurons cross in the medulla oblongata before descending in the spinal cord, their stimulation causes abnormal involuntary movement in the left arm and leg. The hyperreflexia is caused by an overall decrease in upper motor neuron input to anterior motor neurons in the spinal cord during periods between seizures. The patient has headaches because of a generalized increase in intracranial pressure. The presence of papilledema, noted during his initial evaluation, is also an indicator of increased intracranial pressure.

He was referred to a neurosurgeon, who admitted him to the hospital. Surgical exploration revealed a soft, partially necrotic mass expanding the right frontal lobe; a biopsy specimen was obtained. The biopsy showed a tumor composed of moderately pleomorphic nuclei with fibrillar cytoplasm. There are foci of necrosis with adjacent pseudo-palisading (lining up) of nuclei of malignant cells and endothelial cell proliferation. An immunohistochemical stain for glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP) was positive.

  • Tumor, histology
  • Necrosis
  • Pseudopalisading
  • Tumor, histology
  • Microvascular proliferation
  • Higher power
3
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

Based on the astrocytic differentiation, cellular pleomorphism, and infiltrative growth, this is a high-grade astrocytic neoplasm. The additional presence of necrosis and endothelial (microvascular) proliferation are diagnostic of glioblastoma, formerly called glioblastoma multiforme (GBM). These tumors typically have brisk mitotic activity that can be assessed using an immunohistochemical stain for proliferation markers. Glioblastoma may arise de novo (so-called primary glioblastoma) or may evolve from better differentiated infiltrating astrocytic neoplasms (secondary glioblastoma), as in this case.

4
What are the clinical characteristics of this tumor?
Show answer

Glioblastoma is the most common and most aggressive of the primary CNS parenchymal tumors. Although usually encountered in older individuals, it can occur at any age, including infancy and childhood. These tumors may occur anywhere in the brain or spinal cord, but in adults they arise most commonly in the cerebral hemispheres. By contrast, many pediatric glioblastomas involve the brainstem, thalamus, and/or spinal cord, where they are sometimes referred to as diffuse midline gliomas.

After histologic diagnosis, the patient began radiation therapy. Despite therapy, the mass continued to grow and was associated with progression of left-sided weakness and seizure activity. He died approximately 6 months after his initial diagnosis.

Postmortem examination revealed diffuse flattening of gyral crests and narrowing of sulci, more pronounced on the left side. Cut sections of the brain revealed that the right frontal lobe was expanded by a hemorrhagic, ill-defined mass. The cut surface of the tumor showed areas of yellow discoloration corresponding to areas of necrosis. Although the lesion appeared to be circumscribed grossly, this tumor widely infiltrated the adjacent brain. The microscopic features of the lesion were similar to the features noted in the biopsy specimen obtained 6 months earlier.

  • Brain, gross
  • Glioblastoma
  • Corpus callosum
  • Right frontal lobe
  • Left frontal lobe
5
Which genes are most likely mutated in this tumor?
Show answer

Low grade gliomas (grades II and III astrocytomas) frequently have mutations in TP53 and in the genes for isocitrate dehydrogenase (IDH1 and IDH2), a molecular signature that is carried forward as the neoplasm evolves to the higher grade, secondary glioblastoma. Secondary glioblastomas also often overexpress the receptor for platelet-derived growth factor receptor α (PDGFRA). By contrast, the majority of primary glioblastomas are characterized by mutations of the PTEN tumor suppressor gene, deletions of chromosome 10, and amplification of the EGFR oncogene.

6
How are infiltrating astrocytic neoplasms classified?
Show answer

On the basis of histologic features (i.e., cellularity, nuclear pleomorphism, mitotic figures, necrosis, microvascular proliferation), astrocytomas are stratified into three groups: (1) diffuse astrocytoma (grade II); (2) anaplastic astrocytoma (grade III); and (3) glioblastoma (grade IV), with increasingly grim prognosis as the grade increases.

  • Tumor, histology
  • Mitotic figures
  • Nuclear pleomorphism

From Dr. Arie Perry, UCSF

7
Which type of astrocytic neoplasm typically occurs in children and young adults?
Show answer

Pilocytic astrocytomas typically occur in children and young adults and are usually located in the cerebellum but may also appear in the floor and walls of the third ventricle, the optic nerves, and occasionally the cerebral hemispheres. They are often cystic; if solid, they may be well circumscribed or, less frequently, infiltrative. The tumor is composed of bipolar cells with long, thin, “hairlike” processes that are GFAP-positive and form dense fibrillary meshworks. Rosenthal fibers and eosinophilic granular bodies are also characteristic. Pilocytic astrocytomas generally have a much more favorable prognosis than infiltrating astrocytomas; when well circumscribed and completely resectable, survival rates are >90%.

  • Brain, gross
  • Medulla
  • Pilocytic astrocytoma
  • Tumor, histology
  • Rosenthal fibers

8
What are the molecular genetics of pilocytic astrocytoma?
Show answer

In contrast to infiltrating astrocytomas, TP53 is rarely mutated in pilocytic astrocytomas. Instead, two types of alterations in the BRAF signaling pathway have been found in pilocytic astrocytoma: (1) translocations that separate the coding sequence for the kinase domain from that for a flanking inhibitory domain and (2) an activating point mutation (V600E) that is also found in many other tumor types

9
What are the other types of gliomas?
Show answer

Gliomas, the most common group of primary brain tumors, include astrocytoma (described previously), oligodendroglioma, and ependymoma.

10
How do oligodendrogliomas typically present?
Show answer

Oligodendrogliomas present in the fourth and fifth decades, often with a several-year history of neurologic deficits, including seizures.

11
What are the characteristic morphologic features of oligodendrogliomas?
Show answer

Grossly, oligodendrogliomas are well-circumscribed, gelatinous masses, often with cysts, focal hemorrhage, and calcification. The tumors are composed of sheets of regular cells with spherical nuclei surrounded by a clear halo of vacuolated cytoplasm.

  • Oligodendroglioma, histology
  • Nuclei
12
Which genetic abnormalities are seen in oligodendroglioma?
Show answer

The most common genetic alterations in oligodendroglioma are mutations of the isocitrate dehydrogenase genes (IDH1 and IDH2), which occur in up to 90% of cases and portend a better prognosis, as they do for astrocytic tumors. Deletions of portions of chromosomes 1p and 19q, typically occurring together as a co-deletion, are also seen in up to 80% of cases.

13
What are ependymomas and how do they present?
Show answer

Ependymomas arise from ependymal cells and originate next to the ependyma-lined ventricular system, including the oft-obliterated central canal of the spinal cord. In the first two decades of life they typically occur near the fourth ventricle and symptoms may be related to obstructive hydrocephalus. In adults, the spinal cord is the most common location and tumors present clinically as progressive neurologic deficit and pain.

14
What are the morphologic features of ependymomas?
Show answer

Ependymomas are solid or papillary masses composed of cells with regular, round to oval nuclei and abundant granular cytoplasm. A variably dense fibrillary background separates the nuclei. The tumor cells may form round or elongated structures (rosettes, canals) that resemble the embryologic ependymal canal with delicate processes extending into a lumen. Perivascular rosettes are more common and consist of tumor cells arranged around vessels with an intervening zone of thin ependymal processes

  • Ependymoma, histology
  • Fibrillary background
  • Rosette
15
Which is most commonly mutated gene in ependymomas?
Show answer

Ependymomas of the spinal cord are particularly frequent in the setting of neurofibromatosis type 2, a disorder caused by loss-of-function mutations in the NF2 tumor suppressor gene. The NF2 gene is also commonly mutated in sporadic spinal ependymoma. Ependymomas do not share the genetic alterations that are found in infiltrating gliomas, such as mutations in TP53 or IDH genes.

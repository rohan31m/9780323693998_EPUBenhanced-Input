Objectives
  • • Describe the normal functions of thyroid hormones, and the clinical manifestations of hyperthyroidism.
  • • Describe how autoimmunity may cause excessive function of the affected organ.
  • • Compare and contrast the different autoantibodies seen in Graves disease.

A 35-year-old woman presented to her primary care physician for her annual checkup. She mentioned that she had noticed that buttoning her shirt collar was challenging because her neck felt thicker. Despite a voracious appetite, she had lost ten pounds during the previous 2 months. Review of systems revealed that although she formerly had been constipated, she now noted that she had bowel movements twice daily. On physical examination, her pulse was 86 bpm, and her blood pressure 125/70 mm Hg. She had a wide-eyed stare, prominent bulging eyes, and slight lagging of the upper lid on downward gaze. Her thyroid gland was symmetrically enlarged.

What is the normal function of thyroid hormone and how does it affect the body?
Show answer

In response to trophic factors from the hypothalamus, TSH (thyroid stimulating hormone, also called thyrotropin) is released from the anterior pituitary into the circulation. TSH binds to its receptor on thyroid follicular epithelial cells and stimulates thyroid hormone synthesis and release. Thyroid follicular epithelial cells convert thyroglobulin into thyroxine (T4) and lesser amounts of triiodothyronine (T3). T4 and T3 are released into the systemic circulation, where most of these peptides are bound to circulating plasma proteins. In the periphery, the majority of free T4 is deiodinated to T3; the latter binds to thyroid hormone nuclear receptors with 10-fold greater affinity than T4. Binding of T3 to its receptor regulates the transcription of a subset of cellular genes, many of which encode proteins that are involved in intermediary metabolism. Changes in these proteins have diverse effects, including increased carbohydrate and lipid catabolism and protein synthesis. The net result of these processes is an increase in the basal metabolic rate.

2
What is the most likely diagnosis based on the clinical presentation and laboratory findings?
Show answer

The symptoms (increased appetite, weight loss, diarrhea, tachycardia) are strongly suggestive of hyperthyroidism. Increased serum T4 and reduced TSH indicate excessive production of T4. The neck mass suggests enlargement of the thyroid, which could be caused by a hormone-producing adenoma or diffuse hyperplasia of the gland. The radioiodine scan shows diffuse uptake, consistent with diffuse hyperplasia and not a solitary adenoma (“hot nodule”). The exophthalmos is strongly suggestive of Graves disease.

3
What is the etiology of the patient’s ocular findings?
Show answer

Hyperthyroidism with exophthalmos is characteristic of Graves disease. A wide, staring gaze and lid lag are present because of sympathetic overstimulation of muscles that raise the upper eyelid. Autoimmunity plays a role in the development of an infiltrative ophthalmopathy. The protrusion of the eyeball (exophthalmos) is associated with increased volume of the retroorbital connective tissues and extraocular muscles, for several reasons. These include (1) marked infiltration of the retroorbital space by mononuclear cells, predominantly T cells; (2) inflammation with edema and swelling of extraocular muscles; (3) accumulation of extracellular matrix components; and (4) increased numbers of adipocytes (fatty infiltration). These changes displace the eyeball forward and can interfere with the function of the extraocular muscles.

She was treated initially with drugs that suppress thyroid hormone production and beta-adrenergic blockers to control peripheral cardiovascular effects. However, the symptoms persisted and surgical resection of most of the gland was recommended. Histologic examination of the thyroidectomy specimen showed tall columnar follicular epithelium due to cellular hypertrophy and papillary infoldings into the follicular lumens secondary to epithelial proliferation and overcrowding (hyperplasia). Peripheral scalloping of the colloid within follicular lumens was identified and represents active pinocytosis by the hyperfunctioning epithelium.

Which autoantibodies are implicated in Graves disease?
Show answer

There are three autoantibodies that can be seen in Graves disease: thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulin (TSI), thyroid growth-stimulating immunoglobulins, and TSH-binding inhibitor immunoglobulins. TSI is an IgG antibody that binds to the TSH receptor and mimics the action of TSH, stimulating adenyl cyclase, with a resultant increased production of thyroid hormone. Almost all individuals with Graves disease have detectable amounts of this autoantibody, which is relatively specific for the disease. Thyroid growth-stimulating immunoglobulins are also directed against the TSH receptor and have been implicated in the proliferation of thyroid follicular epithelium. TSH-binding inhibitor immunoglobulins are also anti-TSH receptor antibodies; however, they prevent TSH from binding to its receptor on thyroid epithelial cells, and in so doing, may inhibit thyroid cell function. The coexistence of stimulating and inhibiting immunoglobulins in the serum of the same patient is not unusual, and may explain why some patients develop episodes of hypothyroidism.

5
What is the etiology of thyroid hyperfunction in Graves disease?
Show answer

Autoantibodies bind to the same leucine-rich region of the TSH receptor as does the physiologic ligand, TSH; therefore, the antibodies mimic the action of TSH. Normally, increased plasma levels of T4 and T3 inhibit production of TSH and thus reduce thyroid hormone synthesis; however, when the stimulus is provided by an antibody, thyroid hormone production becomes “autonomous” (independent of the amount of TSH). Other antibodies, specific for thyroglobulin (the precursor protein from which thyroid hormone is made) and thyroid peroxidase (the key enzyme in thyroid hormone synthesis) are often detected in patients but these are not specific for Graves disease and do not have functional effects.

6
What are the other causes of hyperthyroidism?
Show answer

In addition to Graves disease, there are two other causes that are clinically important. (1) Toxic multinodular goiter: In long standing multinodular goiters, one of the nodules may become autonomous and start to secrete large amounts of thyroxine, independent of TSH. (2) Toxic adenoma: While the vast majority of benign tumors (follicular adenomas) of the thyroid are nonfunctional (and thus appear cold in radioiodine scans), a small subset secrete thyroid hormones and cause hyperthyroidism.

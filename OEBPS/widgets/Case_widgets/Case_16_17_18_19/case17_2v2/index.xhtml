Objectives
  1. • Compare the pathogenesis and morphology of focal infarcts, global ischemia, and “watershed” infarcts.
  2. • Describe the process of organization of an infarct in the CNS.
  3. • List the common risk factors for infarction and intraparenchymal hemorrhage.

A 75-year old, right-handed man was eating breakfast when his wife noticed he suddenly had difficulty speaking and trouble using his right arm and leg. His wife immediately called an ambulance. The patient was taken to the Emergency Department, where right-sided paralysis and aphasia (inability to speak) were documented. His previous medical history included a transmural myocardial infarct at age 69 and two episodes of transient, right-sided weakness and speech difficulty during the past year. In addition, he had a history of hypertension that was well controlled medically.

1
Which test should be performed next?
Show answer

The patient is demonstrating “localizing” symptoms, which is to say that his right-sided weakness and aphasia are consistent with a neurological defect in a particular area of the brain; in this case, the left side of the brain. By contrast, “non-localizing” symptoms include headache, papilledema, nausea and vomiting, and seizures. In this patient, his symptoms are highly suggestive of a cerebrovascular event (hemorrhage or infarct), also known as a stroke. A non-contrast head CT is the initial imaging modality in evaluating such patients.

A non-contrast head CT showed no mass lesions or hemorrhage and, following evaluation of additional risk factors, the patient was treated with tissue plasminogen activator (t-PA). Continued work up for suspected stroke included an MRI that showed changes in the left middle cerebral artery territory consistent with acute ischemia.

2
How are cerebral infarcts classified?
Show answer

Infarcts are subdivided into two broad groups based on the presence of hemorrhage. As infarcts begin with loss of blood supply, they are often initially nonhemorrhagic. Secondary hemorrhage can occur from ischemia-reperfusion injury, either through collaterals or following dissolution or fragmentation of the intravascular occlusive material. The hemorrhages are petechial in nature, and may be multiple or even confluent. The clinical management of patients with these two types of infarcts differs greatly as thrombolytic therapy is contraindicated in hemorrhagic infarcts.

3
What is the likely etiology of the middle cerebral artery thrombus?
Show answer

There are two major possibilities to consider in this case: (1) embolic occlusion or (2) local thrombotic occlusion of a branch of the left middle cerebral artery. Embolic occlusion is more common than local thrombosis in the middle cerebral artery territory (in contrast to the vertebrobasilar system, where thrombi are a more common cause of infarcts).

4
How does this patient’s previous myocardial infarction relate to his current presentation?
Show answer

Cardiac mural thrombi are a frequent source of emboli: myocardial dysfunction and atrial fibrillation, both of which are associated with myocardial infarction, are important predisposing factors for the formation of these thrombi due to stasis and turbulent flow of intercavitary blood. Ventricular wall hypokinesis and ventricular aneurysm at the site of a previous myocardial infarction are other possible sources of mural thrombus and consequent emboli.

5
What is the role of tissue plasminogen activator in the setting of acute ischemic stroke?
Show answer

In nonhemorrhagic stroke, thrombolytic therapy is effective at limiting the extent of infarction if it can be administered within 3 to 4.5 hours of the onset of the acute event. After 4.5 hours, mortality increases, probably due to stroke evolution and conversion to hemorrhagic stroke. t-PA is normally synthesized by endothelial cells and acts to limit clotting at the site of injury by activating plasminogen to form plasmin. Plasmin breaks down fibrin and interferes with its polymerization. t-PA is most active when bound to fibrin, which makes it a useful therapeutic agent, since its fibrinolytic activity is largely confined to sites of recent thrombosis.

What are the microscopic findings in acute ischemic injury in the brain?
Show answer

The infarcted area contains shrunken neurons with eosinophilic cytoplasm and pyknotic nuclei (“red” neurons), a classic feature of acute necrosis in the CNS. The red staining is caused by a combination of protein denaturation and loss of RNA within the cytoplasm of the affected neuron. Edema is recognized by the clear spaces surrounding the neurons. 7
How can stroke cause increased intracranial pressure?
Show answer

In ischemic stroke, the necrotic tissue is swollen because of edema due to leakage of fluid into the affected tissue from damaged blood vessels. In severe cases, this may increase the intracranial pressure to life-threatening levels. In hemorrhagic stroke, intracranial pressure may also be increased due to the space-filling effects of hematoma.

8
How does global cerebral ischemia differ from a focal infarct?
Show answer

In the setting of severe systemic hypotension, usually when systolic pressures fall below 50 mm Hg, as in cardiac arrest and shock, widespread ischemic-hypoxic injury can occur in the CNS due to global cerebral ischemia, which is characterized by generalized swelling and irregular discoloration of the grey matter. By contrast, focal infarcts are located in the distribution of a specific arterial branch. However, localized injury involving particularly vulnerable regions of the CNS can also occur in the setting of global cerebral ischemia; arterial border zones (i.e., the junctions between two vascular territories) are especially prone to decreases in perfusion pressure, and may be the only site of permanent tissue injury after a hypotensive episode. The term Brain, gross is sometimes used to describe such lesions. Global hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy appears acutely as necrotic neurons; with organization, there is segmental neuronal dropout with gliosis. Susceptible areas include the pyramidal layer of the hippocampus and the Purkinje cells of the cerebellum. If hypoxia-ischemia is severe, there is also diffuse necrosis of layers of cortical neurons.

9
Which areas of the brain are typically affected by Brain, grosss?
Show answer

Border-zone (watershed) infarcts are located at the distal borders of major cerebral arteries and are usually found in the parasagittal frontoparietal region (the border of the regions supplied by the middle and anterior cerebral arteries) or, less commonly, in the temporo-occipital region (the border of the regions supplied by the middle and posterior cerebral arteries) or parieto-occipital region (the border of the regions supplied by the anterior and posterior cerebral arteries). In this example from a patient with a hemorrhagic watershed infarct, the hemorrhagic lesion in the right cerebral hemisphere lies at the boundary between the anterior and middle cerebral artery territories. The hemorrhagic appearance of this infarct is caused by reperfusion of nonviable tissue through still-patent arterial branches.

  • Brain, gross
  • Border-zone infarct

If the patient’s brain were biopsied at the site of his infarct, which histologic features would be seen?
Show answer

At this point, histologic features would be consistent with a subacute infarct. Subacute changes occur from 24 hours to 2 weeks and include tissue necrosis, macrophage infiltration, and reactive gliosis. Reactive astrocytes tend to become swollen with bright pink cytoplasm.

11
Why is this lesion cystic?
Show answer

Following ischemic infarct, the affected tissue undergoes liquefactive necrosis. Macrophages migrate into the infarct during the first week and ingest the necrotic, lipid-rich tissue. Once this material is removed, a cystic space is left. Evolution of an infarct to a cystic defect is usually complete by about 6 months. This is a remote infarct, and is therefore completely cystic.

  • Infarct, histology
  • Macrophages

12
What are the major risk factors for the development of cerebral atherosclerosis?
Show answer

The major factors that predispose to atherosclerosis in the CNS are the same as those associated with atherosclerosis elsewhere in the body: hypertension, smoking, hyperlipidemia, diabetes, family history, and aging.

13
Why did the patient have transient episodes of right-sided weakness and speech difficulty during the year before the development of the infarct?
Show answer

The episodes of transient weakness and speech difficulty likely are caused by brief episodes of vascular occlusion by small embolic fragments, originating either from the heart or from atheromatous plaques in more proximal arterial segments. Such episodes are termed transient ischemic attacks (TIAs). This term denotes a self-limited episode of neurologic dysfunction lasting a few seconds to 24 hours (but usually < 30 minutes). About 20% to 25% of patients with TIAs develop a CNS infarct during the next 5 years.

14
Which form of stroke is caused by hypertension?
Show answer

Hypertension is associated with cerebral hemorrhage and includes lacunar infarcts, slit hemorrhages, and hypertensive encephalopathy, as well as massive hypertensive intracerebral hemorrhage, as in this figure that demonstrates a hematoma compressing and displacing adjacent brain parenchyma in the region of the right basal ganglia. Hypertensive changes (arteriolar sclerosis) of the deep penetrating vessels of the arteries and arterioles that supply the basal ganglia and hemispheric white matter as well as the brainstem can result in lacunar infarcts in the lenticular nucleus, thalamus, internal capsule, deep white matter, caudate nucleus, and pons, in descending order of frequency. The term lacunar is used to describe small infarcts; by convention, infarcts measuring less than 1.5 cm in greatest dimension.

  • Hematoma
  • Basal ganglia, gross

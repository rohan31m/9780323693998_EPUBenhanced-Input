Bad to the Bone

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast the primary malignant tumors of bone.
  2. • Describe the clinical, radiographic, and morphologic characteristics of osteosarcoma.
  3. • Describe the appropriate therapy of osteosarcoma in terms of its natural history.

A 17-year-old boy presented to his physician with a 6-month history of intermittent pain in the left knee. He reported that he had been kicked in the knee while playing soccer and thought the injury was simply slow to heal. Over the last several weeks, the pain had become worse and more persistent and he detected swelling just above the knee.

On physical examination, there was a firm mass of the lateral distal femur. The mobility of the knee joint was normal.

An X-ray of the patient’s knee was performed. There was a lesion centered on the metaphysis of the distal femur. There is a “moth-eaten” appearance suggestive of an aggressive process. In addition, there is a Codman triangle, a triangular area of new subperiosteal bone created when a lesion, usually a tumor, pushes the periosteum away from the bone (arrow) and mineralized matrix in the adjacent soft tissue.

1
Based on the radiologic findings, what is the most likely diagnosis?
Show answer

Radiologic studies provide information regarding not only the location of the lesion (epiphysis, metaphysis, diaphysis, and cortex vs. intramedullary) but also the reaction of the host bone to the pathologic process. Bone tumors typically arise in characteristic locations in the long bones: osteosarcoma in the metaphysis, giant cell tumor of bone in the epiphysis, and Ewing sarcoma in the diaphysis. This metaphyseal lesion shows “aggressive” radiologic features with a “moth-eaten” appearance: there is no distinct demarcation between the lesion and normal bone, which is characteristic of fast-moving processes such as malignancy or infection. By contrast, slow-growing, benign processes frequently display sclerosis as the adjacent, normal bone thickens to support the defect. The metaphyseal location and radiologic features are highly suggestive of osteosarcoma.

2
Which primary tumors of bone should be considered in the differential diagnosis?
Show answer

Osteosarcoma is the most common malignant bone tumor in adolescents and young adults. Ewing sarcoma also affects this patient population: approximately 80% are younger than 20 years. Both osteosarcoma and Ewing sarcoma predominantly affect males. Giant cell tumors of bone typically arise in patients in their 20s to 40s (after growth of the skeleton is completed) and chondrosarcoma is a tumor of patients older than 40.

A biopsy of the bone lesion was performed and revealed sheets of malignant cells that are producing spicules of bone. Mitotic figures were present. A staging chest CT scan revealed multiple mineralized nodules, consistent with metastatic malignancy. A biopsy of one of the pulmonary nodules demonstrated pleomorphic cells embedded in dense bands of osteoid (unmineralized bone that is always pathologic in adults).

3
What is the diagnosis in this case?
Show answer
The patient has an osteosarcoma, based on the histologic findings and supporting clinical and radiologic findings.

The boy’s mother was concerned that her younger children might develop osteosarcoma. She related that her father had developed an osteosarcoma in his 70’s.

4
Are the patient’s siblings at increased risk for developing this tumor?
Show answer

Although there is a familial syndrome in which a germline RB mutation predisposes patients to both retinoblastoma and osteosarcoma, the vast majority of osteosarcomas are sporadic. There would be no increased risk for this patient’s siblings. Osteosarcoma has a bimodal age distribution, most commonly affecting male patients under 20, but with a second spike in incidence in patients older than 65 years. In this second population, patients frequently have an associated condition that is known to predispose to osteosarcoma such as Paget disease, bone infarcts, and prior radiation. This is the most likely the explanation for the disease in this patient’s grandfather.

5
Why is a chest CT part of the screening protocol?
Show answer

Sarcomas, including osteosarcoma, usually spread hematogenously and therefore most commonly spread to the lungs. At diagnosis only 10% to 20% of affected individuals have demonstrable pulmonary metastases, but all patients receive neoadjuvant therapy under the assumption that occult metastases are present, followed by surgery. The prognosis of osteosarcoma has improved substantially since the advent of chemotherapy, with 5-year survival rates reaching 60% to 70% in patients without overt metastases at initial diagnosis. Of those who die of the neoplasm, 90% have metastases to the lungs, bones, brain, and elsewhere.

In view of the multiple pulmonary metastases seen on the screening CT scan, the patient was started on neoadjuvant chemotherapy. Following appropriate treatment, the lung lesions were stable (i.e., they did not increase in size or number) and the patient underwent limb-salvage surgery. The resected specimen showed tumor arising in the metaphysis, and extending into the soft tissue. Where the tumor invaded through the cortex, it raised the periosteum, causing new bone to be laid down, an appearance referred to as the Codman triangle. This feature was also noted in the patient’s initial plain radiograph.

6
Which genes are commonly mutated in osteosarcoma?
Show answer

Osteosarcomas usually have mutations in well-known tumor suppressors and oncogenes. As mentioned previously, germline mutations of RB are associated with a markedly increased (1000-fold!) risk of developing osteosarcoma. RB mutations are present in approximately 70% of sporadic osteosarcomas. Patients with germline mutations of TP53 (Li-Fraumeni syndrome) are also at greatly increased risk for osteosarcoma; p53 dysfunction is common in sporadic tumors as well.

7
What is the significance of antecedent trauma in this case?
Show answer

The majority of tumors occur in young, active patients who may have concurrent trauma to bones and joints, and patients may not seek prompt medical attention. There is no indication that trauma is an etiologic factor in the development of osteosarcoma.

8
What would this patient’s X-ray look like if this tumor were an Ewing sarcoma? What would a biopsy of the lesion show?
Show answer

Ewing sarcomas usually arise in the diaphysis of long tubular bones, especially the femur and the flat bones of the pelvis. The tumor is usually destructive and lytic, with permeative margins. As tumor cells extend through cortical bone, they raise the periosteum, producing layers of reactive bone that are deposited in an onion-skin fashion. A biopsy would show sheets of uniform, small, round cells with scant cytoplasm that may appear clear due to the presence of abundant glycogen.

9
What is the molecular abnormality that is characteristic of Ewing sarcoma?
Show answer

The vast majority of Ewing tumors carry a recurrent chromosomal translocation, t(11;22)(q24;q12), that fuses the EWSR1 gene on chromosome 22 to the FLI1 gene on chromosome 11. This can be detected using fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH). The fusion protein is a transcriptional regulator that affects multiple genes involved in cell proliferation and apoptosis and can also activate expression of telomerase.

10
What would the plain radiograph look like if the patient had a giant cell tumor? What would a biopsy of that lesion show?
Show answer

Giant cell tumors arise in the epiphyses of long bone and are lytic, creating a cystic appearance on plain radiograph, as in this example: while the cortex is thinned, it is not destroyed; radiologists describe this feature as a "soap bubble" lesion. Histologically, the tumor consists of sheets of small mononuclear cells admixed with numerous giant cells. The mononuclear cells are the neoplastic cell population; they express high levels of RANKL (receptor activator of nuclear factor kappa-Β ligand) which binds to RANK (receptor activator of nuclear factor kappa-Β) expressed by osteoclast precursors, stimulating their proliferation and differentiation into mature osteoclasts.

11
What would the radiograph look like if this were a chondrosarcoma? What would the biopsy show?
Show answer

Chondrosarcomas are rare tumors composed of malignant chondrocytes. It is very unlikely that this patient has a chondrosarcoma because (1) patients with chondrosarcomas are usually older than 40 and (2) the tumors arise most commonly in the axial, not the appendicular skeleton. Those that arise in the appendicular skeleton are associated with a pre-existing enchondroma; radiographically, these appear as intramedullary lesions with endosteal scalloping, remodeling of the cortical bone, and “popcorn” calcifications. A variety of genetic aberrations are associated with chondrosarcoma, including IDH1 and IDH2 mutations and silencing of the CDKN2A tumor suppressor locus by DNA methylation. Histologically, chondrosarcomas consist of atypical chondrocytes that permeate through existing lamellar bone. The tumors vary in cellularity, cytologic atypia, and mitotic activity and are assigned a grade from 1 to 3.

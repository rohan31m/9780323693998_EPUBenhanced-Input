Going Viral

Objectives
  1. • Describe the role of human papillomaviruses in the pathology of benign and malignant cervical tumors.
  2. • Recognize the morphologic and biologic spectrum of squamous intraepithelial lesions (SIL).
  3. • Describe the progression and natural history of invasive squamous carcinoma.

A 32-year-old woman presented to her gynecologist to discuss options for birth control. She had been fitted with a copper intrauterine device (IUD) 8 years earlier, but now was considering oral contraceptives. Pertinent history includes menarche at age 12; sexually active from age 15; eight lifetime sexual partners; and cyclic menses regularly every 28 days. Her last pelvic exam occurred at the time of her IUD fitting.

Pelvic exam revealed two 0.4 cm papillary lesions on the patient’s vulva. The vaginal walls and uterine cervix were unremarkable. There was no cervical motion tenderness and both ovaries were palpable and non-tender. A Pap test was performed.

1
What is a Pap test and what is its clinical significance?
Show answer

The Papanicolaou (Pap) test was introduced in the 1940’s at a time when cervical carcinoma was the number one cause of cancer deaths in women. Since that time, deaths from cervical cancer have decreased by more than 70% and cervical cancer has fallen to the 12th most common cause of death, changes that are largely attributable to Pap test screening. In this test, cells are scraped from the cervical mucosa and transformation zone and examined microscopically. Cervical precancerous lesions are associated with abnormalities in cytologic preparations that can be detected long before any abnormality is visible on gross inspection. Dysplastic cells can be recognized by increased nuclear-to-cytoplasmic ratios, enlarged nuclei, and coarsely granular chromatin. The presence of endocervical cells confirms that the transformation zone was sampled.

  • Pap test
  • Normal squamous cell
  • Dysplastic squamous cells
  • Endocervical cells

Cytologic examination revealed high-grade squamous intraepithelial lesion (HSIL).

2
What is the major risk factor for the development of cervical squamous intraepithelial lesion (SIL)?
Show answer

SIL refers to precursor lesions of cervical cancer (discussed later). The major risk factor is infection by high-risk human papillomavirus (HPV) serotypes, such as serotypes 16, 18, 31, 33, and 35. The risk of such infections correlates with sexual behavior: multiple lifetime male sexual partners (five or more); high-risk sexual partners (partners with condyloma or penile cancer); and early coitarche.

3
Which type of additional testing can improve the efficacy of Pap screening?
Show answer

Analysis for HPV DNA in cervical scrapings is highly sensitive but has lower specificity than the Pap test. Since most women acquire HPV infections in their early 20s and these are cleared by the immune system and never progress to SIL, HPV DNA screening is only recommended for women aged 30 or older. A positive test at this age is more likely to identify an individual with a persistent infection that may lead to cervical neoplasia.

4
How does HPV transform cells?
Show answer

The transforming ability is linked to the viral oncoproteins E6 and E7. Oncoprotein E6 binds to the product of the tumor suppressor gene TP53 and inactivates it. With loss of TP53 function, cells with damaged DNA are allowed to progress through the cell cycle, and ultimately enough mutations accumulate to give rise to cancer. E7 binds to the retinoblastoma (RB) protein and, in doing so, displaces certain transcription factors that are normally sequestered by RB; the released transcription factors drive the cell cycle without restraint. Development of carcinoma is usually associated with integration of HPV into the host genome, a process that further increases the expression of the E6 and E7 oncoproteins.


5
Is the cancer risk associated with HPV infection limited to women?
Show answer

No, men who have sex with men or men who participate in oral sex are prone to infections involving the squamous mucosa of the anus and the tonsillar crypts, respectively. High risk HPV is associated with both anal carcinoma and carcinoma of the oropharynx, particularly tonsillar carcinoma. Less commonly, HPV infection can be associated with penile carcinoma in men and vulvar and vaginal carcinoma in women.

Colposcopic examination with biopsy of two areas was performed. Histologic examination revealed LSIL and HSIL.

6
How are cervical precursor lesions classified and what is their natural history?
Show answer

Squamous intraepithelial lesion (SIL) is divided into low-grade squamous intraepithelial lesion (LSIL) and high-grade squamous intraepithelial lesion (HSIL) with different natural histories. LSIL is associated with productive HPV infection and does not progress directly to invasive carcinoma. Most regress and only a small percentage progress to HSIL. HSIL demonstrates increased proliferation, arrested epithelial maturation, and lower levels of viral replication. LSIL is not treated as a premalignant lesion, whereas HSIL is considered to have a high risk of progression to carcinoma. Immunosuppression, particularly due to untreated HIV infection, and smoking are both associated with an increased risk of chronic HPV infection leading to SIL.

7
Which interventions reduce the impact of HPV infection?
Show answer

A quadrivalent HPV vaccine for types 6, 11, 16, and 18 and a more recently introduced 9-valent vaccine are very effective in preventing HPV infections and are expected to greatly lower the frequency of genital warts and cervical cancers associated with these HPV genotypes. Despite its efficacy, vaccination does not supplant the need for routine cervical cancer screening—many at-risk women are already infected, and current vaccines protect against only some of the many oncogenic HPV genotypes.

8
What are the characteristic histologic features of LSIL?
Show answer

Human papillomavirus (HPV) infection is characterized by the presence of koilocytes, which are squamous cells with enlarged, wrinkled, dark (“raisinoid”) nuclei surrounded by a halo. As with all in situ lesions, the basement membrane is intact.

  • LSIL, histology
  • Koilocytes
  • Basement membrane
9
What are the characteristic histologic findings of HSIL?
Show answer

In contrast to LSIL, dysplastic changes involve nearly the entire thickness of the cervical epithelium. There are cellular and nuclear pleomorphism, failure of normal and orderly differentiation, hyperchromatic nuclei, and mitotic figures above the basal layer. Only the very superficial cells appear to differentiate and flatten. The basement membrane is intact.

  • HSIL, histology
  • Mitotic figures
  • Basement membrane
  • Nuclear pleomorphism
  • Hyperchromasia

The vulvar lesions were also excised at the time of cervical biopsy. Microscopic examination at low power revealed exophytic, papillary architecture. On higher power, koilocytic change (atypical, enlarged, hyperchromatic nuclei with a perinuclear halo) is present.

  • Vulvar lesions
  • Koilocytic change
10
What is the diagnosis of the vulvar lesions?
Show answer

In the vulva, these histologic features, which are identical to cervical LSIL, are diagnostic of condyloma acuminatum.

11
What is the etiologic agent of condyloma acuminatum?
Show answer

In contrast to cervical SIL and carcinoma which are caused primarily by HPV types 16 and 18 (though more than 15 high risk HPVs have been identified), condyloma acuminatum is caused by low risk oncogenic HPVs, mainly types 6 and 11. Condyloma acuminatum is not a premalignant lesion.

Based on the diagnosis of cervical HSIL, a cervical cone excision was recommended, but the patient was lost to follow up. Ten years later, she presented to her gynecologist with a 1-month history of a blood-tinged vaginal discharge. A pelvic exam revealed a fungating lesion of the anterior cervical lip. The patient elected to undergo a hysterectomy.

  • Lesion
12
What is the difference between HSIL and invasive carcinoma?
Show answer

HSIL is confined to the epithelium, whereas invasive carcinoma breaks through the basement membrane and infiltrates the underlying stroma. Such invasion frequently causes a desmoplastic response.

  • Cervix, histology
  • Basement membrane
  • HSIL
  • Invasive carcinoma
  • Invasive carcinoma, histology
  • Desmoplastic response
13
How does untreated cervical carcinoma progress?
Show answer

Cervical carcinoma spreads by direct extension to contiguous tissues, including paracervical soft tissue, urinary bladder, ureters (resulting in hydronephrosis), rectum and vagina. Lymphatic and vascular invasion results in local and distant lymph node metastases, and distant metastases may be found in the liver, lungs, bone marrow and other organs.

Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast the different types of anemias in terms of pathogenesis, clinical features and morphologic findings.
  2. • Discuss the natural history of sickle cell disease.
  3. • Compare and contrast the two main categories of clinically relevant hemoglobin abnormalities (hemoglobinopathies and thalassemias).

The patient is a 6-year-old boy who presented to the Emergency Department with severe abdominal pain. He had an upper respiratory infection about a week ago. His mother stated that these painful episodes began when he was about 1 year old and occurred once or twice a year, often after a mild illness. She also tells you that the patient, his brother, her husband, and she have been told they have the “sickle gene.” However, only her husband has experienced similar painful episodes.

1
What is sickle cell disease?
Show answer

Sickle cell disease is a common hereditary hemoglobinopathy caused by a point mutation in β-globin that promotes the polymerization of deoxygenated hemoglobin, leading to red cell distortion, hemolytic anemia, microvascular obstruction, and ischemic tissue damage.

2
In which patient population in the United States is this disease most prevalent?
Show answer

About 8% to 10% of Americans of African descent, or roughly 2 million individuals, are heterozygous for HbS, a largely asymptomatic condition known as sickle cell trait. The offspring of two heterozygotes has a 1 in 4 chance of being homozygous for the sickle mutation, a state that produces symptomatic sickle cell disease.

3
What is the pathophysiology of sickle cell disease?
Show answer

Hemoglobin is a tetrameric protein composed of two pairs of globin chains, each with its own heme group. Normal adult red cells contain mainly HbA (α2β2), along with small amounts of HbA2 (α2δ2) and fetal hemoglobin (HbF; α2γ2). Sickle cell disease is caused by a point mutation in the sixth codon of β-globin that leads to the replacement of a glutamate residue with a valine residue. The abnormal physiochemical properties of the resulting sickle hemoglobin (HbS) are responsible for the disease: the HbS molecules tend to stack into polymers when deoxygenated. Initially, this process converts the red cell cytosol from a freely flowing liquid into a viscous gel. With continued deoxygenation, HbS molecules assemble into long, needle-like fibers within red cells, producing a distorted sickle or holly-leaf shape. The high prevalence of sickle cell trait in certain African populations stems from its protective effects against Plasmodium falciparum, the parasite that causes malaria. The intracellular organisms consume oxygen and decrease intracellular pH, promoting sickling of red cells in patients with this mutation. The distorted, stiff cells may be cleared more rapidly by splenic and hepatic phagocytes, keeping parasite loads low.

Physical examination reveals a soft abdomen with splenomegaly and intact bowel sounds. The patient’s pulse is rapid, and his blood pressure is 125/90 mm Hg. His temperature is 98.8° F. Complete blood count (CBC) is significant for hemoglobin 8 g/dL (normal, 11–13 g/dL), white blood cell count (WBC) 13,500/mm3 (4,500–11,000/mm3) and platelets 350,000/mm3 (normal, 150,000–450,000/mm3)

How are anemias classified?
Show answer

Anemias can be classified by their underlying cause (bleeding, increased red cell destruction, or decreased red cell production, see table) as well as according to alterations in red cell morphology, which often point to particular causes. Morphologic characteristics providing etiologic clues include red cell size (normocytic, microcytic, or macrocytic); degree of hemoglobinization, reflected in the color of red cells (normochromic or hypochromic); and shape.

How are hemolytic anemias classified and what is the clinical significance of the classification?
Show answer

Hemolytic anemias can be classified based on whether hemolysis is primarily extravascular or intravascular. Extravascular hemolysis is caused by defects that increase the destruction of red cells by phagocytes, particularly in the spleen. Clinical findings suggestive of extravascular hemolysis include hyperbilirubinemia, jaundice, splenomegaly, and bilirubin-rich gallstones. Examples of diseases characterized by extravascular hemolysis include sickle cell anemia, hereditary spherocytosis, and most autoimmune hemolytic anemias. Intravascular hemolysis, by contrast, is characterized by injuries so severe that red cells literally burst within the circulation. Clinical findings consistent with intravascular hemolysis include hemoglobinemia, hemoglobinuria, hemosiderinuria, and iron deficiency. Causes of intravascular hemolysis include mechanical trauma (e g., microangiopathic hemolytic anemias), glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency, intracellular parasites, and rare disorders such as Clostridial sepsis.


Microscopic examination of the peripheral blood smear revealed sickle cells, anisocytosis, poikilocytosis, and target cells. In addition, there was polychromasia and an increased reticulocyte count.

6
What is the basis for reticulocytosis in patients with hemolytic anemia?
Show answer

Hemolysis leads to a decrease in hematocrit and delivery of oxygen to oxygen-sensing cells in the kidney. These cells respond by increasing their production of erythropoietin, the key growth factor for erythroid progenitors in the marrow. This increased production can be identified as reticulocytosis, since reticulocytes represent newly released red cells in the peripheral blood. These cells have a bluish tinge (polychromasia) due to the presence of RNA.

7
What does an anemia with a low reticulocyte count signify?
Show answer

Anemia with a low reticulocyte response signifies a production problem within the marrow (an anemia of underproduction).

8
Why does this patient have splenomegaly and abdominal pain?
Show answer

The red pulp of the spleen is a low flow, relatively hypoxic environment that renders HbS-containing cells prone to sickling and obstruction. In young children, the spleen may be enlarged by a combination of increased numbers of macrophages (a response to increased clearance of sickled cells) and chronic congestion due to obstruction of the red pulp by sickled cells. Inevitably the spleen undergoes auto infarction because of chronic hypoxia stemming from congestion and vascular obstruction by sickled red cells and is reduced to a fibrotic nubbin, usually by the end of childhood. Even before this occurs, splenic function is abnormal. The vascular occlusions caused by sickled cells may be extremely painful.

9
What is the pathophysiology of vasoocclusive crises in these patients?
Show answer

The pathophysiology of vascular occlusions and resulting organ damage is not fully understood. Recurrent sickling causes red cells to become loaded with calcium, lose water and potassium, and become stiffer and stickier due to irreversible membrane changes. Such cells, even if not sickled, tend to adhere to endothelium and retard blood flow in the microcirculation. In tissues with sluggish blood flow, there is greater deoxygenation of HbS, and sickling is facilitated. The abnormal cells accumulate and obstruct blood flow. Such a scenario is most likely in tissues with naturally sluggish blood flow, such as the spleen and the bone marrow.

10
The splenic pathology in sickle cell patients increases the risk of which type of infections?
Show answer

Patients with sickle cell disease are at high risk of sepsis caused by encapsulated microorganisms (such as Streptococcus pneumoniae and H. influenzae) and require vaccination and prophylactic treatment with antibiotics.

11
How is the severity of clinical manifestations affected by the red cell concentration of HbS and the presence of other types of hemoglobin?
Show answer

One of the most important factors determining the severity of sickle cell disease is the intracorpuscular concentration of HbS. Homozygotes with HbSS have nearly 100% HbS and full-blown sickle cell disease; heterozygotes with HbAS have a condition called sickle cell trait, which is asymptomatic except under unusual circumstances (e.g., dehydration at high altitude) and causes no hematologic abnormalities. Increased hemoglobin F in HbSS disease inhibits the polymerization of HbS by decreasing its concentration within the cell. The presence of an α-thalassemia allele also decreases the severity of HbSS disease by decreasing the concentration of HbS in the red cell. By contrast, HbSC disease (inheritance of an S and a C hemoglobin allele) is symptomatic because HbC provokes loss of water and potassium, increasing the intracellular concentration of HbS.

12
Why did this patient have no noticeable clinical problems before age 1?
Show answer

High levels of HbF present in red cells during infancy inhibit polymerization of HbS.

13
Why do the patient and his father have pain crises, but his mother and brother do not?
Show answer

His mother and brother probably are HbAS heterozygotes (i.e., they have sickle cell trait), while he and his father probably are HbSS homozygotes.

14
Which laboratory test results may be used to document increased red cell destruction?
Show answer

Increased serum indirect bilirubin, increased serum lactate dehydrogenase, and decreased serum haptoglobin may be used to document increased red cell destruction.

16
What are some other examples of hemolytic anemias and what are their causes?
Show answer

Hemolytic anemias can be caused by a variety of intrinsic red cell abnormalities including hemoglobin disorders, membrane disorders, and enzyme deficiencies. Hemoglobin disorders include sickle cell anemia and the thalassemias. Hereditary spherocytosis is a membrane disorder that is caused by diverse mutations that lead to an insufficiency of membrane skeletal components. Abnormalities in the hexose monophosphate shunt or glutathione metabolism resulting from deficient or impaired enzyme function reduce the ability of red cells to protect themselves against oxidative injuries and lead to hemolysis; hereditary deficiency of glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) activity is the most common of these disorders.

17
What are the inheritance pattern, red cell defect, mechanism of hemolysis, and treatment in hereditary spherocytosis?
Show answer

Hereditary spherocytosis is caused by a genetic defect (most commonly autosomal dominant) in one of the proteins that makes up the red cell membrane cytoskeleton. This leads to loss of the red cell membrane, converting flexible, biconcave discs to non-deformable spherocytes. These get trapped within the cords of the splenic red pulp and are destroyed by macrophages. Splenectomy has no effect on the formation of spherocytes, but in the absence of the spleen these cells survive longer, ameliorating the anemia. Due to hemolysis, there are increased numbers of reticulocytes. In addition, there is a population of normal-appearing red cells and platelets.

How do the thalassemias differ from structural hemoglobin disorders such as sickle cell disease?
Show answer

Thalassemias result from underproduction of normal globin chains, whereas structural hemoglobin disorders result from relatively normal production of abnormal globin chains. States in which alpha globin chains are underproduced are called α-thalassemias, whereas states with underproduction of beta globin chains are called β-thalassemias.

What is the cause of the anemia in the thalassemias?
Show answer

Although underproduction of hemoglobin contributes to the microcytosis and hypochromia seen in these disorders, the severity of anemia is directly related to the degree of imbalance between the production of alpha and beta globin chains. Excess normal globin chains of one type or the other (either alpha or beta, depending on the type of thalassemia) precipitate and damage the cell membrane. Precipitated beta-chains are particularly damaging and result in apoptosis of red cell progenitors in the marrow (ineffective erythropoiesis). Thus, both underproduction and hemolysis contribute to anemia in thalassemia.

21
In which areas of the world do patient populations have the greatest prevalence of thalassemia syndromes?
Show answer

Thalassemia syndromes are endemic in the Mediterranean basin as well as the Middle East, tropical Africa, the Indian subcontinent, and Asia, and in aggregate are among the most common inherited disorders of humans.

22
How is β-thalassemia minor distinguished from iron deficiency anemia?
Show answer

Hemoglobin A2 (a minor adult hemoglobin composed of two alpha chains and two delta chains) is elevated in β-thalassemia minor (trait) and normal in the other microcytic anemias. By contrast, iron studies are normal in β-thalassemia minor and show decreased serum iron and increased total iron binding capacity in iron deficiency.

23
Malaria is the most common cause of infection-induced hemolytic anemia. How does it cause hemolysis and anemia?
Show answer

This blood smear, taken from a patient with Plasmodium falciparum malaria, shows two ring-form parasites in the red cell in the center of the field. Malaria leads to hemolysis and anemia through several mechanisms: (1) Red cells infected by malaria are prone to recognition and destruction by sinusoidal macrophages during passage through the red pulp of the spleen, leading to splenomegaly that further increases extravascular hemolysis; (2) release of parasites from red cells may lead to lysis of the cells, and patients sometimes also develop blackwater fever, a sudden exacerbation of intravascular hemolysis of uncertain etiology (“blackwater” referring to hemoglobin in the urine); and (3) inflammation associated with infection suppresses erythropoiesis and, thereby, reticulocyte production.

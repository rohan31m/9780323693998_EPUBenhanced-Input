Take My Breath Away

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Describe acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) and describe its unifying pathogenic mechanisms.
  2. • Describe the morphologic changes of ARDS and how they evolve.
  3. • List the most common causes of ARDS and explain how these lead to the development of ARDS.

A 30-year-old woman presented to the Emergency Department with fever and chills for 24 hours. She was well until 4 days earlier, when she noted pain on urination, followed shortly thereafter by pain in her left flank. She had diabetes treated with insulin.


1
What is the most likely cause of this patient’s symptoms?
Show answer

The history of painful urination followed by left flank pain and onset of fever and chills is consistent with an ascending urinary tract infection.

While the patient was waiting in the Emergency Department, she developed acute shortness of breath and became cyanotic and was admitted to the hospital. Her blood pressure was 70/40 mm Hg, pulse was 120 beats/min, respiratory rate was 24 breaths/min, and temperature was 39 °C. On examination, wet rales were present in both lungs, and there was tenderness in the left flank. A chest X-ray was performed and showed extensive, diffuse, bilateral pulmonary infiltrates.

PTT 80 sec (23–32.5 sec)
Creatinine 4.8 mg/dL (0.6–1.2 mg/dL)
Serum glucose 600 mg/dL (70–110 mg/dL)

The peripheral blood smear showed fragmented red blood cells. Arterial blood gases were pH 7.25 (7.35–7.45), PCO2 36 mm Hg (33–45 mm Hg), PO2 28 mm Hg (.75–105 mm Hg). Urinalysis showed cloudy urine with abundant neutrophils.

2
Based on the clinical presentation and plain radiograph, what is this patient’s diagnosis?
Show answer

The patient’s laboratory values are diagnostic of sepsis with disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC, marked by a prolonged PT, PTT, thrombocytopenia, and hemolytic anemia with fragmented red blood cells) and acute renal failure (increased serum creatinine). Sepsis is the second most common trigger for acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), which is defined as respiratory failure occurring within 1 week of a known clinical insult with bilateral opacities on chest imaging, as in this patient. The correct diagnosis is ARDS arising in the setting of sepsis and DIC.

3
What are the causes of ARDS?
Show answer

Causes of ARDS can be divided into (1) disorders that cause severe diffuse lung injury directly, and (2) systemic disorders that secondarily damage endothelial cells throughout the lung. Causes of direct, severe diffuse lung injury that may lead to ARDS include pulmonary infections, aspiration (e.g., gastric contents, drowning), inhalation of toxins and irritants (e.g., chlorine, smoke), and direct trauma to the lung. Systemic conditions that may lead to ARDS include sepsis, severe trauma and burns, pancreatitis, DIC, transfusion reactions, and fat embolism.

4
What is the pathogenesis of ARDS?
Show answer

ARDS occurs when widespread injury to alveolar endothelial cells (diffuse alveolar damage) leads to leakage of edema fluid into the alveolar interstitium and the alveolar space. The edema fluid impairs gas exchange, leading to systemic hypoxemia and respiratory distress. Depending on the underlying cause, there also may be damage to alveolar epithelial cells, and formation of hyaline membranes within the alveolar space. Endothelial injury may stem from agents such as infection or aspiration that damage alveoli directly, leading to release of cytokines from resident macrophages, activation of endothelial cells, and recruitment of neutrophils; or from systemic proinflammatory insults such as sepsis, which produce cytokine-mediated endothelial damage and alveolar edema.


5
How does ARDS account for the patient’s hypoxemia and lactic acidosis?
Show answer

The presence of fluid in alveoli impairs gas exchange and also leads to ventilation/perfusion mismatch (continued perfusion of poorly aerated alveoli), producing hypoxemia, as seen in this patient. If severe, systemic hypoxemia may lead to lactic acidosis by impairing oxidative phosphorylation; the pH of 7.25 in this patient suggests that lactic acidosis is present.

The patient was intubated and placed on dopamine, 100% O2 , and intravenous antibiotics. She experienced progressive hypotension, upper gastrointestinal hemorrhage, and intractable acidosis; blood cultures grew Gram-negative rods. She died on the second hospital day. At autopsy, her lungs were heavy, firm, and oozed fluid from the cut surface}.


6
How did bacterial sepsis cause ARDS in this patient?
Show answer

By causing widespread activation of innate immune cells (in response to bacterial products) and massive production of cytokines and other inflammatory mediators, sepsis often leads to the systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS). SIRS is marked by endothelial activation and injury throughout the body, leading to edema formation in many organs, including the lung; if severe, this produces ARDS.

7
Why are the lungs heavy?
Show answer

There is a significant fluid accumulation in the lungs, leading to an increase in weight.

8
What would be the histologic findings in a patient who survives the initial damage of acute ARDS?
Show answer

The healing stage is marked by proliferation of type II pneumocytes that attempt to regenerate the alveolar lining. Sometimes this leads to complete resolution, but more commonly the fibrin-rich exudates organize into intraalveolar fibrosis with marked interstitial thickening of alveolar septa due to inflammatory cells, fibroblasts, and collagen. Residual hyaline membranes often remain. These persistent defects are often recognized clinically as a reduction in diffusion capacity (due to thickening of alveolar walls), sometimes accompanied by evidence of restrictive lung disease caused by decreased lung compliance.

9
How does the pathogenesis of ARDS differ from infant respiratory distress syndrome (formerly known as hyaline membrane disease of newborns)?
Show answer

The mechanism of infant respiratory distress syndrome is a deficiency of surfactant, which increases alveolar surface tension and causes alveoli to collapse under atmospheric pressures. The resulting mismatch between alveolar ventilation and perfusion produces hypoxemia and respiratory distress. If this situation is not corrected, the deficiency eventually produces alveolar epithelial injury, leading to inflammation, edema, alveolar cell death, and formation of hyaline membranes. In ARDS, hyaline membrane formation is initiated by alveolar damage rather than surfactant deficiency.

Objectives
  1. • Describe the pathogenesis of asthma, with specific reference to immune reactions, and how elucidating disease mechanisms has led to novel therapies.
  2. • Describe the morphologic changes in chronic asthma and discuss the clinical course.
  3. • Compare and contrast bronchiectasis, asthma, and hypersensitivity pneumonitis.

An 18-year-old man presented to the Emergency Department with wheezing and difficulty breathing. Similar attacks had occurred intermittently since childhood. He stated that he usually experienced these symptoms early in the morning or late at night and after running in cold weather. Laboratory tests were within normal limits except for eosinophilia (5.5%, normal 1%–3%). Pulmonary function tests showed a markedly decreased FEV1.

1
What is the most likely diagnosis?
Show answer

Recurrent episodes of wheezing, breathlessness, chest tightness, and cough are suggestive of asthma.

2
What are some of the common triggers of an asthma attack?
Show answer

Triggers of asthma attacks include (1) environmental allergens, including dusts, pollens, dander, and foods; (2) respiratory irritants, including smoke, occupational exposures to fumes, and chemicals; (3) respiratory infections, especially viral; (4) medications, especially aspirin; and (5) diverse other triggers, including exercise, stress, cold, and menstruation.

3
What are the two major clinical forms of asthma? What differentiates them in terms of the underlying immune mechanisms?
Show answer

Asthma has been divided into atopic asthma and nonatopic asthma. Atopy, a genetically determined tendency to develop Th2 type T cell responses to environmental allergens, is a strong risk factor for the development of atopic asthma. Atopic asthma is associated with the production of IgE antibodies that recognize allergens and lead to a type I hypersensitivity response. Non-atopic asthma refers to forms that arise in the absence of atopy. Mechanisms that lead to the development of non-atopic allergy are not well understood.

4
Which type of asthma does this patient likely have?
Show answer

Atopic asthma is more common in young individuals, and the patient had eosinophilia (a high peripheral blood eosinophil count), which supports the diagnosis of atopic asthma.

5
How does atopy set the stage for the development of asthma?
Show answer

Atopic individuals develop Th2 type T cell responses to environmental antigens. Activated Th2 cells release the cytokines IL-4, IL-5, and IL-13, which stimulate the production of eosinophils (particularly IL-5) and the production of IgE antibodies from antigen-stimulated B cells. The IgE antibodies bind to Fc receptors on mast cells located in the bronchial mucosa, priming these cells to respond to inhaled antigens.

6
What role do eosinophils play in causing tissue damage?
Show answer

Eosinophils release several substances that contribute to tissue damage and inflammation. Major basic protein appears to be directly toxic to epithelial cells, leading to their damage and shedding. Galectin-10 is a protein that forms characteristic extracellular crystals (Charcot-Leyden crystals) that are potent stimulants of allergic inflammation.

An x-ray of the chest was unremarkable. The patient’s FEV1 improved significantly after he inhaled a few puffs of a β-adrenergic agonist. Skin prick testing failed to identify hyperresponsiveness to commonly implicated environmental allergens. The patient was prescribed a β-adrenergic inhaler.

7
What is the mechanism of the wheezing and airway obstruction that characterizes patients in the midst of an asthma attack?
Show answer

Obstruction is caused by the constriction of bronchial smooth muscle, leading to the narrowing of airways, and is exacerbated by hypersecretion of mucus from bronchial glands. Bronchial smooth muscle in patients with asthma is hyperresponsive, a cardinal feature of asthma defined by exaggerated bronchoconstrictive responses to a variety of stimuli, including allergens, inflammatory mediators, neural stimulation, and physical and chemical exposures. The basis for the airway hyperresponsiveness in asthma is not understood. Therapy for patients having asthma attacks includes drugs that cause smooth muscle relaxation (β-adrenergic agonists, delivered by inhaler) and antiinflammatory agents such as glucocorticoids, which are believed to indirectly lower airway responsiveness by eliminating airway inflammation.

8
Which mechanism produces the early symptoms of an atopic asthma attack? What causes the late effects that appear several hours after exposure to an allergen?
Show answer

The acute early reaction of immediate (type I) hypersensitivity is characterized by binding of antigen to IgE-coated presensitized mast cells giving rise to mast cell activation. The activated mast cells release multiple mediators, including histamine (which causes bronchospasm) and leukotrienes (which cause bronchospasm; attract leukocytes, eosinophils, and prostaglandins; and stimulate bronchial glands to release mucus). In the late-phase reaction, cytokines released by leukocytes, eosinophils, and basophils recruited during the early phase contribute to the late-phase effects.


9
Which histologic changes are typically seen in asthma?
Show answer

The most striking finding is occlusion of bronchi and bronchioles by thick, tenacious mucous plugs containing whorls of shed epithelium (Curschmann spirals). Numerous eosinophils and Charcot-Leyden crystals (crystalloids made up of the eosinophil protein galectin-10) are also present. Other characteristic morphologic changes in asthma collectively called airway remodeling, include thickening of the airway wall, sub-basement membrane fibrosis, increased submucosal vascularity, an increase in size of the submucosal glands, goblet cell metaplasia of the airway epithelium, and hypertrophy and/or hyperplasia of the bronchial muscle.

Which new therapies have been developed based on the understanding of disease pathogenesis?
Show answer

Since inflammation and tissue injury in atopic asthma are driven mainly by the Th2 cytokines IL-4 and IL-5, antibodies against IL-5 and the IL-4 receptors are now approved for treatment of atopic asthma. These cytokine antagonists work best in patients with markers of strong Th2 activation (so-called Th2-high asthma). Antibodies that block the binding of IgE to mast cells also have some efficacy, particularly in patients with positive skin prick tests.

How does the inflammatory infiltrate in bronchiectasis differ from that seen in asthma?
Show answer

Acute inflammatory cells, especially neutrophils, are present in bronchiectasis, although evidence of chronic inflammation is also likely to be present. By comparison, in asthma, the inflammatory cells are composed of CD4+ lymphocytes, eosinophils, mast cells, and a few neutrophils.

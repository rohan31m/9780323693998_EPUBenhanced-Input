The Walls Came Down

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast obstructive versus restrictive lung disease.
  2. • Discuss chronic obstructive pulmonary disease in terms of its pathogenesis, morphologic findings, and clinical progression.
  3. • Describe cor pulmonale and its relationship to COPD.

A 58-year-old man presented to his primary care physician with a several-year history of a persistent cough that had worsened recently and produced thick, white mucus. The cough was particularly severe in the morning on awakening. He had a 40-pack year smoking history and no other significant social, medical, or family history. Pulmonary exam revealed coarse rhonchi and wheezes. The remainder of his physical exam was unremarkable. His physician recommended smoking cessation, and the patient was lost to follow-up.

1
Which diseases are associated with smoking?
Show answer

In addition to a wide range of malignancies, particularly carcinomas of the lung, oropharynx, kidney, and bladder, smoking is also an etiologic factor in chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), consisting of chronic bronchitis and emphysema), cardiovascular disease, and interstitial lung disease.

2
Which aspects in this patient’s history are suggestive of chronic bronchitis? Which histologic features would support the diagnosis?
Show answer

Chronic bronchitis is defined by the presence of a persistent cough with sputum production for at least 3 months in 2 consecutive years. Histologic examination reveals mucous gland hyperplasia, which contributes to increased sputum production. This is often accompanied by varying degrees of chronic inflammation, superimposed infection, and acute inflammation.

  • Lung, chronic bronchitis
  • Bronchial epithelium
  • Mucous glands
3
How do the changes in chronic bronchitis differ from those seen in bronchiectasis?
Show answer

The characteristic feature of bronchiectasis is infection-related destruction and permanent dilation of the bronchial wall, often associated with exuberant inflammation.

4
What is the basis for wheezing in the patient?
Show answer

Patients with chronic bronchitis sometimes develop a component of reactive airway disease characterized by hyperresponsiveness of bronchial smooth muscle to a variety of stimuli, including viral and bacterial infection, leading to intermittent bronchospasm. As in asthma, the pathogenesis of smooth muscle hyperresponsiveness is unknown, but this component of COPD responds to therapy directed at bronchospasm, such as β-adrenergic agonists, and anti-inflammatory agents, such as glucocorticoids.

5
How do the morphologic changes in chronic bronchitis differ from the changes seen in a typical case of atopic asthma?
Show answer

In typical atopic asthma, which also has mucous gland hyperplasia, the bronchial wall has an inflammatory infiltrate in which eosinophils are prominent. Asthma is also associated with hypertrophy and hyperplasia of bronchial smooth muscle cells, called airway remodeling.

6
What is the difference between obstructive and restrictive airway disease?
Show answer

Obstructive pulmonary diseases are characterized by an increase in resistance to airflow due to partial or complete obstruction at any level from the trachea and larger bronchi to the terminal and respiratory bronchioles. By contrast, restrictive diseases are characterized by a reduced ability of the lung parenchyma to expand and a decreased total lung capacity.

7
How can pulmonary function tests help distinguish between these two types of airway disease?
Show answer

Obstructive lung diseases have increased resistance to airflow. In patients with diffuse obstructive disorders, pulmonary function tests show decreased maximal airflow rates during forced expiration, usually expressed as the forced expiratory volume at 1 second (FEV1) over the forced ventilatory capacity (FVC). An FEV1:FVC ratio of less than 0.7 generally indicates airway obstruction. Restrictive lung diseases have reduced total lung capacity, usually measured by the forced vital capacity (FVC) test. There are proportionate decreases in both FEV1 and FCV, such that the FEV1/FVC ratio remains normal.

Ten years later, the patient presented to the Emergency Department in obvious distress, coughing and wheezing and reporting “tightness” in his chest. Two weeks earlier he had experienced an upper respiratory tract infection. The patient was treated with bronchodilators and antibiotics and responded satisfactorily. Physical exam revealed a thin man in some distress. The anterior to posterior diameter of his chest was increased (“barrel chest”) and breath sounds were diffusely diminished. His chest was hyperresonant to percussion. Pulmonary function tests were ordered. They showed a decrease in FEV1 and an FEV1:FVC of 0.65.

8
Which disease process is occurring in this patient? Which diseases are in the differential diagnosis based on the clinical presentation and pulmonary function tests?
Show answer

The pulmonary function test findings are consistent with obstructive airway disease.

A chest X ray was performed.

9
Based on the chest x-ray and clinical history, what is the most likely diagnosis?
Show answer

The lungs in the chest x-ray appear hyperlucent due to overexpansion, which also has led to depression of the diaphragm. These features, combined with the clinical history and pulmonary function tests, support a diagnosis of emphysema. Grossly, there is marked expansion of alveolar spaces due to dropout of septa, without fibrosis. Histologically, emphysema is characterized by permanent enlargement of the airspaces distal to the terminal bronchiole, accompanied by destruction of their walls, without obvious fibrosis.

  • Gross, lung
  • Alveolar spaces
  • Emphysema, histology
  • Expanded alveolar spaces
10
How does emphysema cause obstructive airway disease?
Show answer

In emphysema, there is a functional airflow obstruction caused by destruction of elastic fibers in the septal walls; this diminishes the elastic recoil that is necessary to push air out of the lungs. Eventually, the septal walls are destroyed, producing the enlarged airspaces that characterize emphysema. As a result of these defects the FEV1 is decreased, which is the clinical measure used to define obstructive lung disease.

11
What causes septal wall destruction in emphysema?
Show answer

The most plausible explanation is protease-antiprotease imbalance. The proteases (e.g., elastase) are derived from neutrophils and macrophages that accumulate in the lungs of smokers. The principal antiprotease is α1-antitrypsin. Inflammation caused by smoking-related lung damage leads to increased release of proteases and overwhelms the protective effects of antiproteases, leading to destruction of extracellular matrix components in the alveolar wall, such as elastin. This effect is accelerated in individuals with inherited deficiencies of α1-antitrypsin.

12
What are the different types of emphysema?
Show answer

Emphysema is subclassified as centriacinar (centrilobular), panacinar (panlobular), distal acinar (paraseptal), and irregular. In centriacinar, the central or proximal parts of the acini, formed by respiratory bronchioles, are affected, whereas the distal alveoli are spared. This is the usual form associated with COPD, and it usually is more severe in the upper lobes. Panacinar emphysema is characterized by uniform enlargement of the acini from the level of the respiratory bronchioles to the terminal blind alveoli. It affects the lower lobes more severely and is associated with α1-antitrypsin deficiency. Distal acinar emphysema occurs adjacent to areas of fibrosis, scarring, or atelectasis and is usually more severe in the upper half of the lungs. Irregular emphysema is almost invariably associated with scarring. In most instances it occurs in small foci and is clinically insignificant.

13
What is this patient’s diagnosis?
Show answer

Emphysema and chronic bronchitis are considered to be two ends of the spectrum of abnormalities that are associated with COPD, and most patients with COPD have features of both. This patient has both forms of COPD: chronic bronchitis and emphysema.

Over the next several years, the patient developed increasing somnolence, exertional dyspnea and mild cyanosis. He was given home oxygen. Laboratory values repeatedly showed an elevated hematocrit and blood gases showed low O2 and high CO2 levels.

14
Why is this patient’s hematocrit increased?
Show answer

Chronic hypoxia (as evidenced by his low O2 levels) causes the kidney to release increased amounts of erythropoietin, leading to increased erythropoiesis and compensatory polycythemia.

15
Why does this patient have increased somnolence and cyanosis?
Show answer

Increased sleepiness reflects CO2 narcosis and the cyanosis reflects very poor oxygenation.

Finally, the patient was admitted to the hospital with cyanosis, distended neck veins, an enlarged tender liver, and increasingly severe edema in his lower legs and feet.

16
What is the most likely etiology of the patient’s distended neck veins, tender liver, and peripheral edema?
Show answer

These findings are consistent with cor pulmonale, right-sided heart failure caused by pulmonary hypertension. Both chronic alveolar hypoxia and chronic arterial hypoxemia cause vasoconstriction of pulmonary vessels, raising pulmonary artery pressures and right-sided pressures in the heart, resulting in hypertrophy and eventual dilation of the right ventricle. Right-sided heart failure leads to passive liver congestion (causing tenderness) and peripheral edema.

  • Heart, cor pulmonale
  • Right ventricular wall
  • Right ventricular cavity
17
What are the pulmonary vascular alterations that are associated with cor pulmonale?
Show answer

In severe, long-standing pulmonary hypertension, the media of the pulmonary artery undergoes hypertrophy and the intima is thickened by myofibroblasts, extracellular matrix, and macrophages. Full-blown features of atherosclerosis are rarely seen. One extreme in the spectrum of pathologic changes is the plexiform lesion, so called because a tuft of anastomosing capillaries is present, producing a network, or web, that spans the lumens of dilated thin-walled, small arteries and may extend outside the vessel.

  • Lung, histology
  • Thickened media
  • Plexiform lesion
18
What are some other causes of pulmonary hypertension?
Show answer

Most cases are secondary, caused either by increased pulmonary blood flow (left-to-right shunts) or increased resistance to blood-flow in the pulmonary vasculature due to obstruction (e.g., recurrent thromboemboli), left-sided heart dysfunction (e.g., mitral stenosis), or persistent pulmonary vasoconstriction (e.g., COPD). In primary or idiopathic pulmonary hypertension, hypertension stems from an intrinsic abnormality or a toxic exposure that affects the pulmonary vasculature directly.

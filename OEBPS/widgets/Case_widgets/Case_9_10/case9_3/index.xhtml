Big, Bad Bumps

Objectives
  1. • Define the subtypes of hematopoietic neoplasms.
  2. • Compare and contrast the clinical and morphologic features, pathophysiology, and natural history of follicular lymphoma, chronic lymphocytic leukemia/small lymphocytic lymphoma, and mantle cell lymphoma.

A 66-year-old man presented to his primary care physician with a 5-month history of painless enlarged lymph nodes in his neck and inguinal region and a 5 to 7 lb weight loss over the last 2 months. He had had neither fever nor night sweats. Physical exam revealed multiple nontender, firm, 2 to 3 cm cervical, supraclavicular, and inguinal lymph nodes.



Hematocrit 43% (41%–53%)
White blood cell count 8,200/µL (4,500–11,000/µL)
Lymphocytes 26% (25%–30%)
Platelet 225,000/µL (150,000 – 400,000/µL)


1
What is the clinical significance of the patient’s laboratory values?
Show answer

Lymphadenopathy may be due to multiple causes, including infection, immune/inflammatory disorders, hematopoietic neoplasms, and metastatic cancer. The patient's normal white blood cell (WBC) count argues against a systemic infectious or inflammatory process, and lymphadenopathy involving multiple widely distributed lymph nodes would be unusual for metastatic cancer (e.g., carcinoma or melanoma).

A 2.5-cm lymph node from the neck region was biopsied. The lymph node showed effacement of the normal nodal architecture by a nodular proliferation. The nodules were composed of a mixture of small, irregular cells with angular “cleaved” nuclei with prominent indentations and linear infoldings and large cells with round to oval nuclei with several nucleoli. The neoplastic cells were positive for CD20, CD10, BCL2, and BCL6 and negative for CD5.

  • Lymph node
  • Nodular proliferations
  • Lymph node biopsy
  • Large cells
  • Small cleaved cells
2
Which type of cells are the neoplastic cells?
Show answer

These are germinal center B cells. CD20 is a marker of mature B cells and CD10 and BCL6 are positive in germinal-center B cells.

3
Based on the morphology and phenotyping results, what is the diagnosis?
Show answer

This patient has follicular lymphoma.

4
What is the natural history of follicular lymphoma?
Show answer

Follicular lymphoma tends to produce widespread, painless, lymphadenopathy that may wax and wane. It is generally disseminated at presentation and has a high frequency of bone marrow involvement. Although indolent (median survival is 7–9 years), it is considered incurable. The natural history is not modified by therapy, so a common approach to treatment is observation until symptoms necessitate chemotherapy.

5
Which chromosomal translocation is commonly seen in this disease?
Show answer

Up to 90% of follicular lymphomas are positive for a translocation that juxtaposes the immunoglobulin heavy chain (IGH) locus on chromosome 14 and the BCL2 locus on chromosome 18. This leads to overexpression of BCL2 in follicular center cells in more than 90% of cases, in contrast to normal follicular center B cells, which are BCL2-negative.

  • Lymph node, BCL2 IHC
  • Normal BCL2 expression
  • BCL2 overexpression
6
How does BCL2 overexpression relate to the pathogenesis of follicular lymphoma and the clinical features of this disease?
Show answer

BCL2 is an antiapoptotic protein that controls the intrinsic (mitochondrial) pathway of apoptosis. Follicular lymphomas are neoplasms with decreased apoptosis rather than increased proliferation, resulting in a slow accumulation of long-lived cells. Because most chemotherapeutic agents are effective against rapidly proliferating cells, the slowly proliferating cells of follicular lymphoma are difficult to eradicate fully.

7
Does follicular lymphoma undergo transformation to an aggressive disease?
Show answer

In 30%–50% of cases, follicular lymphoma eventually transforms to an aggressive neoplasm resembling diffuse large B cell lymphoma (DLBCL) or Burkitt lymphoma. In some instances, this transformation event is associated with acquisition of translocations involving the gene MYC, which encodes a transcription factor that promotes Warburg metabolism and rapid cell growth. Such tumors adopt a diffuse growth pattern, spread widely, and often are resistant to therapy.

8
What is the prognosis of transformed follicular lymphoma?
Show answer

The prognosis is worse than those of de novo DLBCL or Burkitt lymphoma. Even if the transformed tumor is successfully treated, the underlying follicular lymphoma commonly recurs.

9
How does de novo DLBCL differ from follicular lymphoma in its clinical presentation, morphology, molecular changes, and prognosis?
Show answer

DLBCL is more common (35% of adult non-Hodgkin lymphomas [NHLs]) than follicular lymphoma, and about one-third have rearrangements of the BCL6  gene; an even higher fraction of tumors have activating point mutations in the BCL6  promoter. In contrast to follicular lymphoma, extranodal presentations are common, with the gastrointestinal tract being the most common extranodal site. Although DLBCL is aggressive, with intensive combination chemotherapy and anti-CD20 immunotherapy, complete remissions are achieved in 60% to 80% of the patients, and about half of patients are cured.

10
What are the broad categories of hematopoietic neoplasia?
Show answer

There are three broad categories of hematopoietic neoplasms based on the origin and differentiation state of the tumor cells: (1) lymphoid neoplasms, including certain leukemias, non-Hodgkin lymphoma, and Hodgkin lymphomas, as well as plasma cell neoplasms and related entities; (2) myeloid neoplasms, including certain myeloid leukemias, myelodysplastic syndromes (MDSs), and myeloproliferative neoplasms; and (3) histiocytic neoplasms, including proliferative lesions of macrophages and dendritic cells

11
What are the different types of lymphoid neoplasms?
Show answer

Lymphoid neoplasms are classified based on their cell of origin. These tumors may be comprised of immature or mature B cells or T cells; plasma cells; or (rarely) natural killer cells. Some characteristically manifest as leukemias, with involvement of the bone marrow and the peripheral blood. Others tend to present as lymphomas and produce masses in lymph nodes or other tissues. Plasma cell tumors usually arise within the bones and manifest as discrete masses, causing systemic symptoms related to the production of a complete or partial monoclonal immunoglobulin. Although these tendencies are reflected in the names given to particular entities, in reality all lymphoid neoplasms have the potential to spread to lymph nodes and other tissues, especially the liver, spleen, bone marrow, and peripheral blood.



BLB, Pre-B lymphoblast; CLP, common lymphoid precursor; DN, CD4/CD8 double-negative pro-T cell; DP, CD4/CD8 double-positive pre-T cell; GC, germinal center B cell; MC, mantle B cell; MZ, marginal zone B cell; NBC, naive B cell; PTC, peripheral T cell.

12
What are the two different groups of lymphomas?
Show answer

Two groups of lymphomas are recognized: Hodgkin lymphomas and non-Hodgkin lymphomas (NHLs). Although both arise most commonly in lymphoid tissues, Hodgkin lymphoma is set apart by the presence of distinctive neoplastic Reed-Sternberg giant cells, which usually are greatly outnumbered by nonneoplastic inflammatory cells. Reed-Sternberg cells have a characteristic immunophenotype: positive for CD15 and CD30, negative for most B-cell and T-cell markers.

13
In addition to follicular lymphoma, what are the other mature B cell neoplasms?
Show answer

Mature B-cell neoplasms include chronic lymphocytic leukemia/small lymphocytic lymphoma (CLL/SLL), mantle cell lymphoma, diffuse large B cell lymphoma, marginal zone lymphoma, Burkitt lymphoma, hairy cell leukemia, lymphoplasmacytic lymphoma and plasma cell neoplasms (multiple myeloma, plasmacytoma).

14
How does the growth pattern of CLL/SLL in lymph nodes compare to follicular lymphoma?
Show answer

In contrast to follicular lymphoma, which typically shows nodular architecture, CLL/SLL has a diffuse growth pattern. The tumor is mainly composed of small, round lymphocytes with round to slightly irregular nuclei, condensed chromatin, and scant cytoplasm.

  • Diffuse growth pattern
  • Small, round cells
15
What is the relationship between chronic lymphocytic leukemia and small lymphocytic lymphoma?
Show answer

CLL/SLL differ in the extent of peripheral blood lymphocytosis, but otherwise are identical and should be considered a single disease with a range of manifestations. Most cases of CLL/SLL have peripheral blood involvement sufficient to cause leukocytosis. While this is distinctive, it is not unique: all B-cell lymphomas have some propensity to involve the peripheral blood and may produce leukocytosis due to circulating tumor cells, particularly in cases of advanced disease.

16
How do patients with CLL/SLL commonly present clinically?
Show answer

CLL/SLL is often an incidental finding in an asymptomatic older adult. When symptoms are present, they include generalized lymphadenopathy, fatigue, anorexia, and weight loss.

17
What is the primary determinant of prognosis in CLL/SLL?
Show answer

Clinical stage remains an important determinant. The staging of CLL depends on such factors as the presence of lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, and cytopenias. Certain biologic features of CLL/SLL have been discovered that also have prognostic implications. Among these, one of the most important is a deletion of chromosome 17p that involves the TP53 tumor suppressor gene.

18
Which molecular pathways promote the growth and survival of CLL/SLL cells?
Show answer

CLL/SLL cells often harbor molecular abnormalities such as deletions of chromosome 13q that appear to upregulate expression of the antiapoptotic gene BCL2, as in follicular lymphoma. CLL/SLL cells also depend on signals triggered by the B-cell receptor (BCR, surface immunoglobulin) for their growth and survival. These insights have led to recent FDA approval of BCL2 and BCR signaling antagonists for treatment of symptomatic CLL/SLL.

19
What is the distinctive immunophenotype of CLL/SLL?
Show answer

In addition to the expression of typical B-cell markers such as CD19, CD20, and surface immunoglobulin, the cells also express the antigen CD5. This antigen is expressed on most T cells and a small fraction of normal B-cells in adults. Among mature B-cell neoplasms, CD5 is expressed primarily in CLL/SLL and mantle cell lymphoma (MCL).

20
Which translocation is commonly seen in mantle cell lymphoma (MCL), and what role does this play in the pathogenesis of this disease?
Show answer

Virtually all MCL are positive for the t(11;14), which results in the juxtaposition of the transcriptionally active immunoglobulin heavy chain gene on chromosome 14 and the cyclin D1 gene on chromosome 11. As a consequence, there is overexpression of cyclin D1, a cell cycle regulator that stimulates growth by promoting the progression of cells from the G1 phase to the S phase of the cell cycle.

21
What are the morphologic features of MCL?
Show answer

Like CLL/SLL, MCL is a tumor of small mature lymphocytes. The tumor cells are usually small and slightly irregular and arranged in a diffuse or mantle zone (surrounding reactive germinal centers) pattern. Similar to follicular lymphoma and CLL/SLL, it is usually disseminated at presentation, with frequent involvement of blood and bone marrow.


22
What is the typical presentation and prognosis of MCL?
Show answer

MCL usually presents in the fifth to sixth decades of life and shows a male predominance. The most common presentation is painless lymphadenopathy. Symptoms related to involvement of the spleen (present in ~50% of cases) and gastrointestinal tract are also common. The prognosis is variable; with recent changes in therapy, the median survival is now 8 to 10 years. This lymphoma is not curable with conventional chemotherapy, and most patients eventually succumb to organ dysfunction caused by tumor infiltration.

23
How common are lymphomas arising from T cells?
Show answer

T-cell neoplasms account for about 5% of all lymphomas in the US, but they are more common in Eastern Asian countries. Examples include adult T-cell leukemia/lymphoma, which is related to infection with human T-cell leukemia virus-1 (HTLV-1). HTLV-1 is a retrovirus (like HIV) that transforms infected cells by unknown mechanisms. It is endemic in Japan, West Africa, parts of South America, and the Caribbean. Mycosis fungoides and Sezary syndrome are tumors of CD4+ T cells that home to the skin; as a result, they often are referred to as cutaneous T cell lymphoma. Mycosis fungoides usually manifests as a nonspecific, erythrodermic rash that progresses with time to a plaque phase and then to a tumor phase. Sézary syndrome is a clinical variant characterized by (1) a generalized exfoliative erythroderma and (2) tumor cells (Sézary cells) in the peripheral blood.

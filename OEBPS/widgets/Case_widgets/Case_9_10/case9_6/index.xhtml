Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast the clinical features of Hodgkin lymphoma (HL) and non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL).
  2. • Compare and contrast the different types of Hodgkin lymphoma.
  3. • Describe the clinical and laboratory findings in anemia of chronic inflammation.

A 22-year-old woman presented to her primary care physician with a two-month history of slowly increasing fatigue. She initially attributed her symptoms to stress and lack of sleep, but recently noted difficulty keeping up with friends while jogging. She had not traveled out of the country and was a non-smoker. Physical examination revealed a mobile left supraclavicular lymph node, 1.5 to 2.0 cm in size.

Laboratory values

Hematocrit 30.6% (37%–43%)
Mean cell volume 81 fL (82–96 fL)
White blood cell count 7.8 × 103/mm3 (4–10 ×103/mm3)
Platelet count 505 × 103/mm3 (150–450 × 103/mm3)
Differential Notable for 9% eosinophils (<3%)
1
What are possible causes for the patient’s anemia?
Show answer

The patient has a microcytic anemia. Causes of microcytic anemia include iron deficiency, globin abnormalities (thalassemia), defects in heme synthesis (e.g., lead poisoning), and anemia of chronic inflammation.

2
Which additional tests would be helpful in clarifying the diagnosis?
Show answer

For patients with microcytic anemia, additional laboratory studies to examine iron metabolism would be helpful:

Serum iron 35 μg/dL (60–170 μg/dL)
Serum total iron binding capacity (transferrin) 120 μg/dL (240–450 μg/dL)
Transferrin saturation 30% (25%–35%)
Ferritin 750 ng/mL (up to 200 ng/mL)
Hemoglobin electrophoresis Normal amount of HbA2, no abnormal species detected
3
Which type of anemia does the patient have?
Show answer

The low serum iron, low transferrin, and high ferritin levels are consistent with the anemia of chronic inflammation (also known as the anemia of chronic disease). The low transferrin and normal transferrin saturation effectively rule out iron deficiency, while the normal hemoglobin electrophoresis rules out thalassemia.

4
What is the pathophysiology of this disorder?
Show answer

Systemic inflammation is associated with the production of cytokines (such as tumor necrosis factor [TNF], IL-6, and IL-1). IL-6 is particularly important in stimulating hepatic production of hepcidin, a regulator of iron metabolism. Hepcidin blocks iron uptake from the gut and the release of iron from macrophages. Iron trapping in macrophages “starves” red cell progenitors of iron, diminishing erythropoiesis. Inflammation also decreases erythropoietin production in the kidney, which also contributes to anemia.

5
What might explain the eosinophilia?
Show answer

Entities commonly associated with eosinophilia include neoplasia (hematopoietic and nonhematopoietic), parasitic infection, allergy, asthma, and certain forms of vasculitis.

6
What might explain the high platelet counts?
Show answer

Inflammation increases the platelet count because hepatocytes respond to inflammatory cytokines such as IL-6 by releasing thrombopoietin, the major growth factor for megakaryocytes.

Based on the observed blood abnormalities, a systemic inflammatory disorder was suspected. The patient reported no history of weight loss, night sweats, rash, or pruritis, but on further questioning remarked that she felt that her face had become progressively “puffier” and swollen over the past month, particularly in the morning. A chest X-ray was obtained and showed a mediastinal mass.

7
What is the likely cause of the patient’s face swelling?
Show answer

Facial swelling in a patient with a mediastinal mass is consistent with development of superior vena cava syndrome, in which venous flow to the heart from the upper body is impeded by compression of mediastinal vessels by a mass. Such processes may also impinge on airways in the lungs.

Biopsy of a mediastinal mass in a patient with superior vena cava syndrome may be dangerous because swelling caused by the procedure may exacerbate vascular or airway compromise. Therefore, the supraclavicular lymph node was biopsied and showed well-defined bands of pink, acellular collagen that subdivided the lymph node into cellular nodules.

Higher magnification of the cellular areas revealed a polymorphous population of T cells, eosinophils, plasma cells, and macrophages with scattered large cells. These large cells had multiple nuclei or a single nucleus with multiple nuclear lobes, each with a large, inclusion-like nucleolus about the size of a small lymphocyte. The large cells were positive for CD15 and CD30.

8
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

The patient has classic Hodgkin lymphoma, nodular sclerosis subtype. This is the most common form of HL (65%–70% of cases). The sexes are affected equally and, most commonly, those with the disease are young adults.

9
Which of these various cells is neoplastic?
Show answer

The large and/or multinucleated cells, known as Hodgkin/Reed-Sternberg (HRS) cells, are neoplastic. The characteristic associated inflammatory cell infiltrate is induced by a number of cytokines. Some of these are secreted by HRS cells, including IL-5, a chemoattractant for eosinophils, and transforming growth factor-β, a fibrogenic factor. Furthermore, the responding inflammatory cells, rather than being innocent bystanders, produce additional factors that aid the growth and survival of HRS cells and contribute further to the tissue reaction (although they are not part of the neoplastic clone itself). Release of proinflammatory cytokines, either by HRS cells or stromal cells and reactive immune cells, causes the local host response and the systemic inflammation. HRS cells also express high levels of PD-L1 and PD-L2, ligands for the PD-1 receptor, which transmits signals that suppress the activity of cytotoxic T cells.

10
Which type of cells are HRS cells?
Show answer

In the vast majority of HLs, the neoplastic Hodgkin/Reed-Sternberg cells are derived from germinal center or post-germinal center B cells. This has been established by molecular studies showing that HRS cells have clonal immunoglobulin gene rearrangements with evidence of somatic hypermutation, a process that is believed to only occur in germinal center B cells.

11
How should the patient be worked up further?
Show answer

Whereas most forms of non-Hodgkin lymphomas spread widely early in their course in an unpredictable fashion, Hodgkin lymphoma spreads to contiguous lymph nodes in a stepwise fashion, followed by hematogenous spread to the spleen, liver, bone marrow, and other sites. Hence, while lymphoma staging provides generally useful prognostic information, it is of most utility in guiding therapy in Hodgkin lymphoma. Therefore, radiologic evaluation to determine the tumor stage is the next step in work up of the patient.

A magnetic resonance image (MRI) revealed a mass involving the thymus that impinged on the superior vena cava and several adjacent, enlarged mediastinal lymph nodes. The spleen was not enlarged, liver lesions were absent, and there was no infradiaphragmatic lymphadenopathy.

12
What is the stage of disease?
Show answer

The disease appears to be confined to several lymph node groups above the diaphragm (stage II).

Table 9.6.1 Clinical Staging of Hodgkin and Non-Hodgkin Lymphomas (Ann Arbor Classification)a

Stage Distribution of Disease
I Involvement of a single lymph node region (I) or involvement of a single extralymphatic organ or tissue (IE)
II Involvement of two or more lymph node regions on the same side of the diaphragm alone (II) or with involvement of limited contiguous extralymphatic organs or tissue (IIE)
III Involvement of lymph node regions on both sides of the diaphragm (III), which may include the spleen (IIIS), limited contiguous extralymphatic organ or site (IIIE), or both (IIIES)
IV Multiple or disseminated foci of involvement of one or more extralymphatic organs or tissues with or without lymphatic involvement

aAll stages are further divided based on the absence (A) or presence (B) of the following systemic symptoms and signs: significant fever, night sweats, unexplained loss of more than 10% of normal body weight.

From Carbone PT, et al: Symposium (Ann Arbor): staging in Hodgkin disease. Cancer Res 31:1707, 1971.

13
What is the treatment and what is the prognosis?
Show answer

The treatment of choice is combination chemotherapy. The overall prognosis is excellent. HL was the first human cancer to be successfully treated with radiation therapy and chemotherapy, and is curable in most cases.

14

15
What are the other types of Hodgkin lymphoma?
Show answer

The World Health Organization classification recognizes five subtypes of HL: (1) nodular sclerosis; (2) mixed cellularity; (3) lymphocyte-rich; (4) lymphocyte-depleted; and (5) nodular lymphocyte–predominant. Nodular sclerosis and mixed cellularity are the most common subtypes, while the others are relatively uncommon. The nodular sclerosis, mixed cellularity, lymphocyte-rich, and lymphocyte-depleted types are classified together as classic Hodgkin lymphoma, since the Reed-Sternberg cells have a similar immunophenotype. By contrast, the nodular lymphocyte–predominant type is set apart by the distinct morphology of the large cells (often with convoluted or "popcorn-like" nuclei rather than the bilobed nuclei of classic HL) and by the expression of mature B-cell markers (including CD20) and germinal center B cell markers by the large cells.

16
Which virus is commonly associated with HL?
Show answer

Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) is present in the HRS cells of classic Hodgkin lymphoma in virtually all cases of the lymphocyte-depleted type, approximately 70% of cases of the mixed-cellularity subtype, and in a smaller fraction of other subtypes. EBV is rarely if ever seen in the tumor cells of nodular lymphocyte predominant Hodgkin lymphoma.

17
How is EBV related to the development of classic HL?
Show answer

Activation of the transcription factor NF-κB is a common event in classic HL. NF-κB may be activated by EBV infection. EBV+ tumor cells express latent membrane protein-1 (LMP-1), a protein encoded by the EBV genome that transmits signals that upregulate NF-κB. In some cases without EBV infection, the tumor cells contain mutations in proteins that normally regulate NF-κB, again leading to unregulated NF-κB activity. NF-κB stimulates production of many proteins associated with lymphocyte cell survival, growth, and proliferation.

18
Which other lymphomas are associated with EBV infection?
Show answer

EBV infection is seen in extranodal NK/T-cell lymphoma, Burkitt lymphoma, and immunodeficiency-associated large B-cell lymphoma.

19
What is the typical presentation of mixed cellularity HL?
Show answer

Mixed cellularity HL is the most common type of HL in patients older than 50. There is a male predominance. This subtype is more likely to be disseminated and to be associated with systemic manifestations than the nodular sclerosis subtype. Association with EBV is common.

20
Which features distinguish nodular lymphocyte predominant HL (NLPHL) from nodular sclerosis HL?
Show answer

Both NLPHL and nodular sclerosis HL display nodular architecture; however, fibrous bands are not seen in the former. Scattered in a background of small lymphocytes, the large neoplastic cells in NLPHL typically have a delicate, multilobed, puffy nucleus that resembles popped corn (“popcorn cell”); these are known as lymphocytic and histiocytic (L and H) cells. In contrast to the HRS cells of classic HL, these cells express B-cell markers typical of mature germinal-center B cells, such as CD20 and BCL6, and are usually negative for CD15 and CD30.

21
What is the clinical presentation of nodular lymphocyte predominance HL?
Show answer

A majority of the patients are males, usually younger than 35 years of age. They typically present with cervical or axillary lymphadenopathy. Mediastinal and bone marrow involvement is rare. The prognosis is excellent.

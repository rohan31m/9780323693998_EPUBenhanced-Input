Objectives
  1. • Discuss the pathogenesis and morphology of chronic myeloid leukemia
  2. • Describe the classification of myeloproliferative neoplasms
  3. • Compare and contrast the different types of myeloproliferative neoplasms
Summary of case:

A 68-year-old male patient presented to his physician with a 6-month history of increasing fatigue and weakness and 5 lb weight loss over the last 4 months. Physical examination revealed splenomegaly. Lab testing demonstrated a Hb of 9.5 g/dL with normal red cell morphology. Peripheral smear showed leukocytosis with 45% neutrophils including band forms, 22% metamyelocytes, 10% myelocytes, 7% eosinophils, 4% basophils and 3% blasts. Bone marrow was hypercellular with increased granulocytic and megakaryocytic precursors. FISH study revealed BCR:ABL translocation.

1
What explains his clinical presentation?
Show answer

Hypercellular bone marrow with increased numbers of maturing granolocytic and megakaryocytic precursors indicates a bone marrow proliferation. Leukocytosis with predominance of neutrophils, band forms, metamyelocytes, myelocytes, eosinophils, and basophils, also suggests a granulocytic bone marrow proliferation and their subsequent release into the blood stream. Weakness can be explained by anemia. Splenomegaly results from extensive extramedullary hematopoiesis. An older male patient with an abnormal proliferation of myeloid cells in the bone marrow and leukocytosis is consistent with a myeloproliferative neoplasm.

3
What is a myeloproliferative neoplasm?
Show answer

Myeloproliferative neoplasms are characterized by the presence of mutated, constitutively active tyrosine kinases or other acquired aberrations in signaling pathways that lead to growth factor independence of marrow progenitors. The resulting uncontrolled proliferation commonly leads to an increase in the production of one or more mature blood elements. Important myeloproliferative neoplasms include chronic myeloid leukemia (CML), polycythemia vera (PV), essential thrombocythemia (ET), and primary myelofibrosis. Myeloproliferative neoplasms share certain common features, albeit to varying degrees, such as extramedullary hematopoiesis, transformation to a spent phase characterized by marrow fibrosis and transformation to acute leukemia.

4
How is CML genetically distinct from other myeloproliferative neoplasms?
Show answer

CML is distinguished from other myeloproliferative neoplasms by the presence of a chimeric BCR::ABL fusion gene derived from portions of the BCR gene on chromosome 22 and the ABL gene on chromosome 9. In more than 90% of cases, this fusion gene is created by a balanced reciprocal translocation t(9;22) (q34;q11). In such instances, the BCR::ABL fusion gene is located on the derivative 22 chromosome, which is also called the Philadelphia chromosome (Ph) after the city in which it was discovered. In more than 90% cases, the fusion gene directs the synthesis of a constitutively active BCR::ABL tyrosine kinase with a molecular weight of 210 kDa. Other myeloproliferative neoplasms are frequently associated with mutations involving JAK2, CALR, and MPL genes. Through several different mechanisms, these mutations also lead to constitutively active tyrosine kinase signaling.

5
What is the pathogenesis of CML?
Show answer

Normally, tyrosine kinases are regulated by ligand-mediated dimerization and phosphorylation that creates an activated kinase capable of phosphorylating other protein substrates. The BCR moiety of the BCR::ABL kinase contains a dimerization domain that self-associates, resulting in the activation of the ABL tyrosine kinase moiety. In turn, the ABL kinase phosphorylates proteins that induce signaling through the same progrowth and prosurvival pathways that are normally activated by hematopoietic growth factors, including the RAS and JAK/STAT pathways. For unknown reasons, BCR::ABL signaling preferentially drives the proliferation of granulocytic and megakaryocytic progenitors. The cell of origin in CML is a pluripotent hematopoietic stem cell.

6
What is the typical presentation of chronic myeloid leukemia?
Show answer

CML is mainly a disease of adults with insidious onset and a peak incidence in the fifth to sixth decades of life, although with frequent screening of peripheral blood as part routine check-ups it is increasing recognized in asymptomatic patients. Those who are symptomatic present with weakness, weight loss, anorexia, and dragging sensation in the abdomen caused by splenomegaly. The natural history of the disease is one of steady progression; before the introduction of tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKI), untreated CML cases inevitably progressed to a blast crisis resembling acute leukemia. Prior to blast crisis, about 50% of untreated patients enter an ‘accelerated phase’ marked by increasing anemia and thrombocytopenia, sometimes accompanied by a rise in the number of basophils in the blood. Additional clonal cytogenetic abnormalities often appear during the accelerated phase. Within 6 to 12 months, the accelerated phase terminates in blast crisis. In the remaining 50% cases, the blast crisis occurs abruptly without an accelerated phase. In 70% of cases, the blasts are of myeloid origin. Due to disease progression, median survival without any treatment is 3-5 years. Fortunately, TKI therapy results in hematologic remission in >90% of patients and also suppresses disease progression.

7
What is the treatment for CML?
Show answer

Treatment of choice is tyrosine kinase inhibitors. BCR::ABL inhibitors result in sustained hematologic remission in >90% of patients with generally tolerable side effects. Remarkably, due to this targeted therapy the life expectancy of CML patients is now close to normal, and some patients appear to be cured. For healthy and relatively young patients with suboptimal response to TKI therapy due to disease resistance, or those who cannot tolerate TKIs due to side effects, hematopoietic stem cell transplantation is the preferred option.

8
What is polycythemia vera (PV)?
Show answer

Polycythemia vera (PV) is characterized by panmyelosis, the increased bone marrow production of red cells, granulocytes, and platelets. Most of the disease symptoms are related to increased red cell production, which leads to polycythemia. Activating mutations in the tyrosine kinase JAK2 markedly decrease the requirement for erythropoietin and other hematopoietic growth factors, explaining abnormally high red cell numbers in the face of low serum erythropoietin levels. In contrast, virtually all other causes of absolute polycythemia are associated with high serum erythropoietin levels, save rare patients who inherit activating mutations in the erythropoietin receptor.

9
What is the pathogenesis of polycythemia vera?
Show answer

The activating mutation in JAK2 results in a valine-to-phenylalanine substitution at amino acid residue 617. JAK2 participates in the JAK/STAT pathway, which lies downstream of multiple hematopoietic growth factor receptors, including the erythropoietin receptor. Constitutive activation of JAK2 in polycythemia vera renders hematopoietic cell lines growth factor independent.

10
What is the pathogenesis of essential thrombocythemia?
Show answer

Essential thrombocythemia (ET) is associated with increased JAK-STAT signaling stemming from diverse mutations that mimic constitutive growth factor signaling. Over 90% cases have activating mutations in JAK2, CALR, or MPL. MPL is a receptor tyrosine kinase that is normally activated by thrombopoietin and that signals through JAK2. Mutated forms of CALR are secreted and then feedback to bind and activate MPL. Constitutive JAK2 or MPL signaling renders megakaryocytic progenitors thrombopoietin-independent. The JAK2 mutation in ET is same one that is seen in polycythemia vera (PV); why some patients present with PV and others with ET is not known. Like PCV, ET can progress to a spent phase or to acute myeloid leukemia in a small fraction of cases.

11
What is primary myelofibrosis?
Show answer

Primary myelofibrosis is a myeloproliferative disorder associated with progressive marrow fibrosis, eventually leading to suppression of hematopoiesis, cytopenias and extensive extramedullary hematopoiesis.

12
What causes fibrosis in primary myelofibrosis?
Show answer

Neoplastic megakaryocytes release fibrogenic factors, such as platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF) and transforming growth factor-β (TGF-β). In addition to being a fibroblast mitogen, TGF-β also promotes collagen deposition and angiogenesis.

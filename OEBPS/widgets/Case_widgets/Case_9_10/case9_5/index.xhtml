Objectives
  1. • Describe the pathogenesis and pathophysiology of acute leukemias.
  2. • Compare and contrast the clinical and pathologic features of the B- and T-cell types of acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL).
  3. • Explain the role of cytogenetics and genetics in subclassification of ALL and its treatment.
  4. • Discuss the pathogenesis and pathophysiology of diffuse large B-cell lymphoma.
A 3-year-old boy was brought to his pediatrician with a 1-month history of increasing fatigue. His mother noted that sometimes his gums bled when he brushed his teeth. Physical exam was unremarkable.

Complete blood count (CBC):
Hemoglobin (Hb) 10.5 g/dL (normal, 11–14 g/dL)
White blood cells (WBC) 75,000/mm3 (normal, 4,000–10,000/mm3)
Neutrophils 600/mm3 (normal, 2,500–6,000/mm3)
Platelets (Plt) 25,000/mm3 (150,000–400,000/mm3)


The peripheral smear revealed leukocytes with high nuclear-cytoplasmic ratios, scant agranular cytoplasm, and irregular nuclei with fine chromatin and distinct nucleoli.

Which types of cells are these likely to be? Which tests would be helpful in confirming the diagnosis?
Show answer

The cells fit the description of blasts. Agranular cytoplasm suggests that the cells are lymphoblasts, as does the age of the patient (acute lymphoblastic leukemia is more common in children than acute myeloid leukemia, which predominates in adults). To confirm this impression, peripheral blood and/or marrow from the patient would be submitted for flow cytometry to assess the immunophenotype of the cells. In addition, cytogenetic and molecular studies would be performed to identify genetic abnormalities, such as the presence of a BCR-ABL fusion gene or rearrangement of the MLL gene, that might affect prognosis or guide treatment.

2
What would be the next step in making a diagnosis?
Show answer

Flow cytometry should be performed to determine the phenotype of the cells.

3
What is flow cytometry and what is its clinical utility?
Show answer

Flow cytometry is a method of cell analysis in which a laser is applied to stream of droplets, each containing a single cell, to assess cell morphology and immunophenotype. To assess morphology, cells are “interrogated” with a laser beam focused on the droplets. The scattering of laser light can be used to estimate the size and granularity of individual cells, and serves as the basis for automated differential leukocyte counts. In a second method, cells are stained with panels of antibodies linked to different fluorophores, which are detected on cells with a laser “tuned” to the excitation wavelength for particular fluorophores. Because of its ability to simultaneously and accurately measure expression of multiple cell-surface markers on individual cells, flow cytometry is the standard test for assessment of immunophenotype in hematologic malignancies generally and in acute leukemias in particular.

4
What are the two major types of acute lymphoblastic leukemia?
Show answer

Acute lymphoblastic leukemia/lymphomas (ALLs) are neoplasms composed of immature B or T cells, which are referred to as lymphoblasts. About 85% are B-ALLs, which typically manifest as childhood acute leukemias. B-ALL peaks in incidence at about the age of 3, perhaps because the number of normal bone marrow B cell progenitors (the cell of origin) is greatest. The less common T-ALLs tend to present as thymic lymphomas in adolescence, the age when the thymus (filled with T cell progenitors) reaches its maximum size.  

5
Which immunophenotypic features are characteristic of lymphoblasts?
Show answer

Immunophenotyping is very useful in subtyping ALLs and distinguishing them from acute myeloid leukemia (AML). Terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase (TdT), an enzyme expressed in immature B and T cells, is present in more than 95% of cases. Further subtyping of ALL into B and T cell types relies on stains for lineage-specific markers, such as the B cell marker CD19 and the T cell marker CD3.

Flow cytometry shows that the lymphoblasts are positive for terminal deoxynucleotidyl-transferase (TdT), the B-cell markers CD19 and CD22, and the marker CD10, all of which are commonly expressed on pre-B lymphoblasts.

6
What is the patient’s diagnosis?
Show answer

The flow cytometry confirms a diagnosis of B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (B-ALL).

7
How does B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (B-ALL) differ clinically from T-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (T-ALL)?
Show answer

B-ALL represents about 85% of acute lymphoblastic leukemia/lymphoma, while the remaining cases are comprised of T-ALL. T-ALLs tend to present in adolescent males as thymic masses. There is, however, considerable overlap in the clinical behavior of B-ALL and T-ALL; for example, B-ALL uncommonly presents as a mass in the skin or a bone, and many T-ALLs present with or evolve to a leukemic picture with extensive marrow and blood involvement.

8
What is the likely basis for the patient’s thrombocytopenia, neutropenia, and anemia? How does this contribute to the clinical features of acute leukemia?
Show answer

The proliferation of leukemic blasts in the bone marrow interferes with normal hematopoiesis. Thus, bone marrow examination typically shows numerous blasts and little normal hematopoiesis. The failure of the marrow to produce normal blood cells places the patient at high risk of infection and bleeding, and also produces fatigue due to diminished oxygen carrying capacity of the blood.

9
What is the pathogenesis of ALL?
Show answer

Many chromosomal aberrations seen in ALL dysregulate the expression and function of transcription factors that are required for the normal differentiation of B- and T-cell progenitors. Up to 70% of T-ALLs have gain-of-function mutations in NOTCH1, a gene that is essential for T-cell differentiation, whereas a high fraction of B-ALLs has loss-of-function mutations in genes that are required for B-cell differentiation, such as PAX5. These varied mutations appear to promote maturation arrest and increased self-renewal, a feature of immortalized cells and one of the hallmarks of cancers.

10
What is the prognosis for B-ALL? Which features are associated with a worse prognosis?
Show answer

With aggressive chemotherapy, about 95% of children with B-ALL obtain a remission, and 75% to 85% are cured. Several factors are associated with a worse prognosis: (1) age younger than 2 years, largely because these tumors are genetically distinct; (2) presentation in adolescence or adulthood; and (3) peripheral blood blast counts greater than 100,000. Favorable prognostic markers include (1) age between 2 and 10 years; (2) a low white cell count; and (3) hyperdiploidy.

11
What are some important cytogenetic abnormalities associated with prognosis in B-ALL?
Show answer

Outcomes in B-ALL and AML are strongly linked to genetic features, and these features sometimes also dictate therapy. Cytogenetic features associated with a favorable prognosis in B-ALL include the presence of a (12;21) translocation and hyperdiploidy. By contrast, a worse prognosis is seen in the setting of a (4;11) translocation or other rearrangements involving chromosome 11, genetic findings that are particularly common in B-ALL occurring in infants younger than 2 years of age. Another important cytogenetic abnormality in B-ALL is the (9;22) translocation (the Philadelphia chromosome), which creates a BCR-ABL fusion gene encoding a constitutively active tyrosine kinase. The presence of BCR-ABL was once a predictor of poor outcome, but with addition of BCR-ABL inhibitors to combination chemotherapy, outcomes have improved substantially.

Cytogenetic analysis confirmed the absence of the (9;22) translocation. The patient was treated with a multidrug regimen and entered complete remission.

12
How does diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL), a disease of mature cells, differ from B-ALL?
Show answer

In contrast to B-ALL, which typically presents as a “leukemia” with marrow and blood involvement, DLBCL is often identified as a mass lesion, either in a lymph node or an extranodal site. DLBCL may involve the marrow, but rarely to a degree that produces pancytopenia, and significant peripheral blood involvement is rare. Thus, DLBCL usually presents with symptoms related to mass effects or sometimes B symptoms (e.g., fever, weight loss), which stem from cytokines released from tumor cells or reactive immune cells. DLBCL is more common in older patients (i.e., over 60 years old), although approximately 15% occur in pediatric patients.

13
How is DLBCL distinguished diagnostically from B-ALL?
Show answer

The tumor cells of DLBCL tend to be larger, have more abundant cytoplasm, and more “open” (less condensed) chromatin than those of B-ALL. Immunophenotypically, DLBCL cells lack markers of immaturity (TdT, CD34) and have a mature B cell phenotype, often expressing surface immunoglobulin, which is not a feature of B-ALL.

14
What are two of the most important driver mutations in DLBCL? How do they contribute to lymphomagenesis?
Show answer

About 20% of DLBCLs have a (14;18) translocation, similar to the one seen in follicular lymphoma, that leads to overexpression of BCL2, a key antiapoptotic protein. About 30% of DLBCLs have a rearrangement of chromosome 3 that leads to overexpression of BCL6, a transcription factor with important pro-growth and survival functions in mature B cells.

15
What is the prognosis of DLBCL?
Show answer

Without treatment, DLBCL are aggressive and rapidly fatal. With intensive combination chemotherapy and anti-CD20 immunotherapy, complete remissions are achieved in 60% to 80% of patients; of these, approximately 50% remain free of disease and appear to be cured.

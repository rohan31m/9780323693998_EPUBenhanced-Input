Objectives
  1. • Describe the clinical findings and natural history of the acute myeloid leukemia.
  2. • Compare and contrast the morphologic and immunophenotypic features of myeloblasts and lymphoblasts.
  3. • Explain the clinical significance of the (15;17) translocation in acute promyelocytic leukemia.

A 40-year-old man presented to the Emergency Department with a 1-day history of abundant bright red blood in his stool, increasing fatigue over the last several weeks, and the appearance of numerous small red dots on his skin the day before. He stated he occasionally took aspirin for muscle aches, but did not smoke or drink. Physical examination revealed tachycardia (heart rate 104 beats/min), tachypnea (respiratory rate 27 breaths/min) and orthostatic hypotension. In addition, his skin and mucous membranes were pale, and he had numerous petechiae on his arms, legs, and torso.

1
Which conditions are associated with multiple petechiae?
Show answer

Petechiae are seen in bleeding disorders that result from vessel wall abnormalities (e.g., infections, drug reactions, Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, scurvy) or decreased platelet function, which usually stems from thrombocytopenia. The major causes of clinically significant thrombocytopenia include disorders associated with decreased platelet production (e.g., depressed marrow output secondary to aplastic anemia or leukemia) or decreased platelet survival (e.g., immune thrombocytopenia, disseminated intravascular coagulation, thrombotic microangiopathies). Splenomegaly also can produce thrombocytopenia by sequestration of platelets, but this is not of sufficient severity to produce a bleeding problem.

2
Which laboratory tests should be ordered?
Show answer

A complete blood count will be informative regarding the patient’s petechiae (platelet count) and fatigue/mucosal pallor (hematocrit, hemoglobin). Tests to query the possibility of disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) include prothrombin time (PT), partial thromboplastin time (aPTT), fibrinogen and D-dimer.

What is the clinical relevance of these laboratory values?
Show answer

The elevated PT, PTT, and D-dimers; decreased fibrinogen and platelet count; and clinical evidence of bleeding all indicate the presence of disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC). The decreased WBCs, hemoglobin, and hematocrit (pancytopenia) suggest a diffuse process involving bone marrow.

4
What is disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)?
Show answer

DIC is an acute, subacute, or chronic thrombohemorrhagic disorder characterized by the excessive activation of coagulation and the formation of thrombi in the microvasculature of the body. Particularly in acute DIC, as a consequence of the thrombotic diathesis there is consumption of platelets, fibrin, and coagulation factors and, secondarily, activation of fibrinolysis.

A peripheral blood smear revealed 11% abnormal immature cells with abundant cytoplasmic azurophilic granules and a high nuclear:cytoplasmic ratio.

5
What does the presence of immature cells in the peripheral smear suggest?
Show answer

Circulating immature cells suggest marrow involvement by acute leukemia. An alternative possibility is a marrow infiltrative process (metastatic cancer, granulomas) leading to release of WBC and RBC marrow progenitors due to distortion of the normal marrow architecture, a picture referred to as leukoerythroblastosis.

6
What is the primary criterion for a diagnosis of acute leukemia?
Show answer

The primary criterion for a diagnosis of acute leukemia is 20% blasts in blood or bone marrow. The proliferating cells of acute promyelocytic leukemia, although not strictly blasts, are considered as such for the purpose of diagnosis.

7
Is it possible to determine the type of leukemia purely on morphologic grounds?
Show answer

Compared to lymphoblasts (right), myeloblasts (left) tend to have more cytoplasm, finer chromatin, and more prominent nucleoli. In addition, depending on the type of AML, myeloblasts may display cytoplasmic granules and/or Auer rods. However, for certainty of diagnosis, immunophenotyping (flow cytometry) and molecular diagnostic techniques are always used.

  • Abundant cytoplasm
  • Peripheral blood smear
  • Auer rods
8
Why is it necessary to distinguish between AML and ALL?
Show answer

Different forms of chemotherapy are employed in these two diseases.

9
Which additional tests are helpful to differentiate AML from ALL?
Show answer

Flow cytometry is very useful, since myeloid and lymphoid blasts express different lineage-specific markers. Two such markers are the enzymes myeloperoxidase and terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase (TdT). Myeloperoxidase is an enzyme present in the primary granules of granulocyte precursors. A positive stain for myeloperoxidase in blasts is diagnostic of AML. TdT is a specialized DNA polymerase that plays a key role in the diversification of antigen receptor genes in immature B and T cells. TdT expression, usually assessed by flow cytometry in conjunction with absence of myeloperoxidase staining, suggests ALL. Typically, a panel of additional markers would be done, including markers specific for B cells (CD19, CD20, CD22), T cells (CD1a, CD3, CD5, CD4, CD8), and myeloid cells (CD13, CD33, CD64, CD117).

A bone marrow biopsy was performed and showed markedly hypercellular bone marrow (normally in a patient this age, approximately half of the marrow space should be occupied by fat cells). Evaluation of the aspirate smear showed a dominant population of large cells that comprised greater than 80% of the bone marrow. The cells had abundant purple cytoplasmic granules and some had bilobed nuclei. Occasional promyelocytes were present that contained multiple Auer rods.

  • Bone marrow biopsy
  • Hypercellular bone marrow
  • Bony trabeculae
10
What are Auer rods, and what is their significance?
Show answer

Auer rods appear to represent neutrophil primary granules. They are always abnormal and, except for occasional cases of myelodysplastic syndromes, are essentially pathognomonic of AML. Many cases of AML do not have Auer rods, however.

11
What are the three broad categories of myeloid neoplasia?
Show answer

The three broad categories are (1) acute myeloid leukemia, in which there is an accumulation of immature myeloid (blasts) forms in the bone marrow that suppresses normal hematopoiesis; (2) myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS), in which defective maturation of myeloid progenitors (ineffective hematopoiesis) leads to cytopenias; and (3) myeloproliferative neoplasms, in which there is increased production of one or more types of blood cells.

12
What is the natural history of MDS and what are some prognostic factors?
Show answer

The clinical course of MDS varies widely, from gradually worsening cytopenias to rapidly progressive marrow failure to transformation to AML. The prognosis, as well as the rate of transformation to AML, depends on multiple factors, including the bone marrow blast percentage, chromosomal abnormalities, and the severity of the cytopenias. Because of the propensity for MDS to transform to AML, these disorders in the past were called preleukemias.

13
Which types of blasts can be seen in AML?
Show answer

The term myeloid encompasses granulocytic, erythroid, megakaryocytic, and monocytic cells; however, AML can be composed of immature cells of any of these myeloid lineages (e.g., monoblasts, megakaryoblasts). Also, some AMLs retain the capacity for limited differentiation, and this forms the basis for part of the World Health Organization (WHO) classification system.

14
How are acute myeloid leukemias classified?
Show answer

AMLs are diverse in terms of genetics, cellular lineage, and degree of maturation. The World Health Organization (WHO) classification relies on all of these features to divide AML into four categories:(1) AMLs associated with specific genetic aberrations, which are important because they predict outcome and guide therapy; (2) AMLs with dysplasia, many of which arise from MDSs; (3) AMLs occurring after genotoxic chemotherapy; and (4) AMLs lacking any of the previous features. AMLs in the last category are subclassified based on the predominant line of differentiation that the tumor exhibits.

15
What is the diagnosis in this case?
Show answer

The (15;17) chromosomal translocation is diagnostic of acute promyelocytic leukemia (APML). The morphologic features and clinical presentation also support the diagnosis. APML is almost always associated with some degree of DIC at presentation, and this complication is responsible for much of the early morbidity and mortality in this disease. The coagulopathy results from the release of procoagulant substances by the tumor cells. DIC may also be seen in other forms of acute myeloid leukemia, but is uncommon.

16
What is the molecular abnormality associated with t(15;17), and how does it relate to disease pathogenesis and treatment?
Show answer

t(15;17) results in the fusion of the retinoic acid receptor alpha gene on chromosome 17 and the PML gene on chromosome 15, creating a fusion gene encoding a leukemogenic PML-RARA chimeric transcription factor. Retinoic acid binding to the normal RARA transcription factor is important for granulocyte maturation, and the PML-RARA protein disrupts that pathway. As a result, the neoplastic cells arrest at the promyelocyte stage of differentiation. Pharmacologic doses of all-trans-retinoic acid (ATRA) overcome this maturational block by binding to the PML-RARA fusion protein, both normalizing its function and also increasing its turnover, so immature cells are able to differentiate into mature leukocytes and are no longer hyperproliferative. This treatment also ameliorates DIC. Arsenic trioxide is a second agent that also targets the PML-RARA fusion protein by increasing its proteasomal degradation. Combined treatment of APL with ATRA + arsenic has been transformative: with chemotherapy, only 20% of patients were cured of APL, but the cure rate with ATRA + arsenic is close to 95%.

17
Is there any correlation between recurrent cytogenetic abnormalities and morphologic features in AML?
Show answer

Yes. As previously demonstrated, the t(15;17) is associated with a particular form of AML in which the cells are frozen at the promyelocyte stage of differentiation. AML with t(8;21) usually manifests as AML with some maturation and the presence of Auer rods; the inv(16) with AML accompanied by numerous abnormal eosinophils; and translocations or other rearrangements involving chromosome 11q23 with monocytic differentiation.

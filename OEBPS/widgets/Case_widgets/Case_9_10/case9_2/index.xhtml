Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast the anemias of diminished erythropoiesis.
  2. • Describe the pathophysiology, clinical presentation, and morphologic features of the megaloblastic anemias.

A 65-year-old man presented to his physician with a 3-month history of increasing fatigue and weakness. He recently began experiencing shortness of breath during routine physical activity. The patient had no personal or family history of anemia, unusual dietary habits, or bleeding. On physical examination, pallor and tachycardia were noted without other abnormal findings.

Hemoglobin 6.6 gm/dL (13.5–17.5. gm/dL)
Hct 27% (41%–53%)
RDW 21.5 (11.6–14.6)
MCV 68 µm3 (80–100 µm3)
MCHC 24.4 g/dk (31–36 g/dl)
Reticulocytes 0.7% (0.5%–1.5%)
Leukocytes 8,100/mm3 (4500–11,000/mm3)
Platelets 480,000/mm3 (150,000–400,000/mm3)
1
What is the clinical significance of these laboratory values?
Show answer

The patient has a microcytic hypochromic anemia. These cellular changes are the result of failure of the red cell precursors to produce adequate hemoglobin. Iron deficiency is the most common cause of hypochromic microcytic anemia; other causes include the thalassemias (α and β), severe anemia of chronic inflammation, and relatively rare inherited disorders of heme synthesis (sideroblastic anemia).

What are the features seen in the blood smear?
Show answer

The red cells are smaller than normal (microcytic). The central area of pallor is increased (hypochromia), an indication that the cells contain less than normal amounts of hemoglobin. In addition, there is considerable variation in the size (anisocytosis) and shape (poikilocytosis). A segmented neutrophil, and multiple platelets, all with normal morphology, are also present.

Additional laboratory work was ordered. The results of these studies are as follows:


Serum iron concentration 20 mg/dL (50–150 mg/dL)
Transferrin saturation 5% (20%–50%)
Serum ferritin 12 ng/mL (23–336 ng/mL)
TIBC 400 (250–370 μg/dL )

TIBC, total iron binding capacity

3
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

The laboratory values (reduced serum iron and ferritin concentration) and morphologic features (microcytosis, hypochromia) are diagnostic of iron deficiency anemia.


4
How should this patient be evaluated further?
Show answer

Iron deficiency can result from (1) chronic blood loss (by far the most common cause in the economically privileged world); (2) impaired absorption; (3) increased requirement; or (4) dietary lack. In general, iron deficiency anemia due to chronic blood loss should be considered the likely cause until proven otherwise, and the primary clinical imperative should be to identify the source. In an elderly patient such as this one, iron deficiency anemia is likely due to chronic blood loss from the gastrointestinal tract. Therefore, upper and lower endoscopy is recommended. In women, a history of bleeding from the gynecologic tract should be obtained. Urinary tract bleeding should also be excluded in both sexes.

Colonoscopy revealed a fungating, ulcerated mass in the cecum. The patient was referred to surgery for resection.

5
What is the pathophysiology of iron deficiency anemia and how would it manifest in the bone marrow?
Show answer

Iron deficiency anemia is an example of an anemia due to diminished erythropoiesis. The marrow has increased numbers of erythroid precursors because anemia causes decreased oxygen delivery to interstitial cells in the kidney, which increase their production of erythropoietin, the major growth factor for red cell progenitors. However, in the absence of adequate iron, hemoglobin cannot be made in normal amounts. This impairs the production of red cells and also explains the small size and low hemoglobin concentrations found in the circulating red cells of patients with iron deficiency anemia. A Prussian blue stain, which highlights hemosiderin, will fail to detect storage iron in a bone marrow aspirate from patients with iron deficiency anemia (right), normal bone marrow on left.

  • Hemosiderin
6
What are some other entities associated with diminished erythropoiesis?
Show answer

The most common and important anemias associated with red cell underproduction (besides iron deficiency) are those caused by other nutritional deficiencies (particularly of vitamin B12 and folate), followed by those that arise secondary to renal failure and chronic inflammation. Also included are less common disorders that lead to generalized bone marrow failure, such as aplastic anemia, primary hematopoietic neoplasms, and infiltrative disorders that lead to marrow replacement (e.g., metastatic cancer, disseminated granulomatous disease).

7
How is anemia of chronic inflammation distinguished from iron deficiency anemia?
Show answer

Morphologically, there is some overlap: anemia of chronic inflammation may be microcytic and hypochromic, like iron deficiency anemia, or may be normochromic and normocytic. However, in anemia of chronic inflammation, there is increased storage of iron in marrow macrophages, a high serum ferritin level, and a reduced total iron-binding capacity.

8
What is the etiology of anemia of chronic inflammation?
Show answer

Anemia of chronic inflammation results from the release of inflammatory cytokines (IL-1, tumor necrosis factor [TNF], IL-6) due to inflammatory or neoplastic processes. These cytokines suppress erythropoiesis through multiple mechanisms; most importantly, IL-6 increases production of hepcidin, a peptide hormone synthesized by the liver. Hepcidin inhibits ferroportin, an iron transporter found on duodenal enterocytes and macrophages. When hepcidin levels rise, iron is sequestered in macrophages, and uptake of iron from the gut is also antagonized; both actions decrease the levels of transferrin-bound iron in the serum, the major source of iron that red cell progenitors use to produce hemoglobin. The increased storage of iron in macrophages is reflected as increased serum ferritin; thus, increased serum ferritin in conjunction with decreased serum iron is characteristic of anemia of chronic inflammation.

9
What is a hypoproliferative anemia that is associated with decreased erythropoietin production?
Show answer

Anemia of renal failure results directly from a deficiency in erythropoietin production, making it unique among the many types of anemia. Because of the decreased erythropoietin, there is decreased proliferation of erythroid precursors in the marrow. Anemia of renal failure falls into the broad category of hypoproliferative anemias. The anemia is generally normochromic/normocytic, with little anisopoikilocytosis.

10
How is anemia of renal failure treated?
Show answer

Most patients respond well to exogenous erythropoietin administration, assuming there are no other factors interfering with erythropoiesis (e.g., iron deficiency).

11
How would the peripheral smear in a patient with megaloblastic anemia compare with one from a patient with iron deficiency anemia?
Show answer

The key feature of megaloblastic anemia on a peripheral blood smear is the presence of large, ovoid red cells, known as macro-ovalocytes, in contrast to the small and hypochromic red cells seen in iron deficiency anemia. In addition, the neutrophils in megaloblastic anemia are hypersegmented because of an acquired defect in DNA synthesis in marrow progenitors that leads to abnormal nuclear maturation, whereas neutrophils in iron deficiency anemia (and nonmegaloblastic anemias in general) are normally segmented (3-4 lobes). Neutrophil hypersegmentation is typical of megaloblastic anemia but can be seen in any condition in which there is an acquired defect in DNA synthesis in marrow progenitors. Because DNA synthesis is impaired in all proliferating cells, granulocytic precursors in the marrow also display dysmaturation in the form of giant metamyelocytes and band forms that exhibit nuclear-cytoplasmic asynchrony (maturation of cytoplasmic features proceed normally, while nuclear maturation is delayed).


  • Peripheral blood smear
  • Hypersegmented nucleus
  • Macro-ovalocytes
12
What are the major causes of megaloblastic anemia?
Show answer

Megaloblastic anemia can be caused by folate and B12 deficiency, or by drugs that interfere with folate metabolism, notably methotrexate.

13
What is the biochemical basis of delayed nuclear maturation in anemias due to folate and B12 deficiency?
Show answer

Folate is required for the synthesis of deoxythymidylate monophosphate, which is required for DNA synthesis. Vitamin B12 is essential for the synthesis of the biologically active form of folic acid, tetrahydrofolate. In vitamin B12 deficiency, the inability to synthesize tetrahydrofolate from other folate metabolites leads to inadequate deoxythymidylate monophosphate synthesis.



14
What is the functional classification of megaloblastic anemia?
Show answer

Megaloblastic anemia is another example of ineffective erythropoiesis, although in this disease, the maturation defect is not restricted to the erythroid lineage (i.e., this represents ineffective hematopoiesis).

15
What would be the morphologic features of a bone marrow biopsy from a patient with megaloblastic anemia?
Show answer

As with the other anemias, the decreased red cell mass results in the diminished delivery of oxygen to oxygen-sensing cells in the kidney. These cells respond by increasing their production of erythropoietin, the key growth factor for erythroid progenitors in the marrow. Hence the marrow would be hypercellular. At high power, abnormal myeloid and erythroid cells with nuclear-cytoplasmic asynchrony (i.e., cells with mature-appearing cytoplasm but nuclei of more immature precursors) would be seen. Normal bone marrow is about 50% fat and 50% hematopoietic precursors.



16
Can folate deficiency and vitamin B12 deficiency be distinguished purely by morphologic features?
Show answer

No, serum vitamin B12, folate, and red cell folate levels must be obtained to differentiate the two processes.

17
What are the similarities and differences between the effects of folate deficiency and vitamin B12 deficiency?
Show answer

Folate deficiency and vitamin B12 deficiency both produce megaloblastic anemias that result from abnormal nuclear maturation due to suppressed synthesis of DNA. In most cases, folate deficiency is related to inadequate dietary intake, whereas vitamin B12 deficiency involves failure to absorb vitamin B12 normally, as in pernicious anemia, with dietary lack being a less common cause (although strict vegans are at risk and may require B12 supplements). In addition to causing megaloblastic anemia, vitamin B12 deficiency leads to neurologic changes resulting from damage to the ascending and descending fibers of the posterolateral spinal tracts. Folate deficiency does not affect the nervous system.

18
What are the risk factors for folate deficiency?
Show answer

The three major causes of folic acid deficiency are (1) decreased intake; (2) increased requirements; and (3) impaired utilization. Decreased intake can result from either a nutritionally inadequate diet or impaired intestinal absorption. Dietary inadequacies are most frequently encountered in chronic alcoholics, the indigent, and the very old. Malabsorption syndromes such as celiac disease can lead to inadequate absorption of this nutrient, as can diffuse infiltrative diseases of the small intestine (e.g., lymphoma). In addition, certain drugs, particularly the anticonvulsant phenytoin and oral contraceptives, interfere with absorption. A relative deficiency can be encountered when requirements are increased, such as in pregnancy, infancy, hyperactive hematopoiesis (e.g., hemolytic anemias), and disseminated cancer. Folic acid antagonists, such as methotrexate, inhibit dihydrofolate reductase and lead to a deficiency of tetrahydrofolate (FH4).

19
Why is it important to distinguish folate deficiency from vitamin B12 deficiency?
Show answer

The neurologic complications associated with vitamin B12 deficiency are not improved (and may actually be worsened) by folate administration. Serum levels of both folate and vitamin B12 should be assessed in patients with megaloblastic anemia so that the appropriate supplement can be provided.

20
What are other causes of macrocytic anemia?
Show answer

Other causes of macrocytic (but not megaloblastic) anemia include reticulocytosis, alcoholism, liver disease, dyserythropoietic bone marrow disorders, and hypothyroidism, but the mean red cell volume is rarely >110 fL in these disorders, whereas it frequently exceeds this level in megaloblastic anemias. Also, macrocytosis in these disorders is not caused by defects in DNA synthesis.

21
What is the pathogenesis of aplastic anemia? How is it treated?
Show answer

Aplastic anemia is characterized by anemia, neutropenia, and thrombocytopenia (pancytopenia). It results from a primary failure or immunologically mediated suppression of multipotent hematopoietic stem cells. As a result, there is inadequate production of red cells, platelets, and granulocytes. Some cases follow exposure to chemicals (particularly organic solvents), drugs (particularly chloramphenicol), or viral infections (particularly hepatitis). Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation is often curative. In cases in which transplantation is not possible, immunosuppressive therapy (e.g., antithymocyte globulin, steroids, cyclosporine) is used and can produce remissions.

22
How does pure red cell aplasia differ from aplastic anemia? What are its major causes?
Show answer

Pure red cell aplasia differs from aplastic anemia in that it represents a selective loss of red cell precursors; the granulocytic and megakaryocytic lineages are unaffected, and so patients present with anemia only. Most cases are immune mediated. It is associated with a variety of conditions, including thymomas, unusual leukemias of large granular lymphocytes, and autoantibodies against erythropoietin. An important cause of transient pure red cell aplasia is infection with parvovirus B19, which can cause severe anemia in patients with hemolytic anemias (e.g., hereditary spherocytosis) associated with a shortened red cell lifespan. Parvovirus specifically infects and kills erythroid progenitors. In normal individuals in which red cells have a lifespan of 120 days, cessation of red cell production for 1–2 weeks before the body mounts a response to parvovirus has little effect, whereas for someone with a hemolytic anemia associated with a greatly shortened red cell lifespan, parvovirus infection can be life-threatening unless red cell transfusions are administered.

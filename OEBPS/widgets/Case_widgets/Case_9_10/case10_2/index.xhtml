For No Good Reason

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Describe the postulated pathogenesis, morphologic findings, and natural history of idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis.
  2. • Discuss the characteristic morphologic findings and proposed etiology of the pneumoconioses.
  3. • Describe the range of clinical presentations that are associated with sarcoidosis and its underlying pathology.

A 67-year-old man was admitted to the hospital with a 5-month history of dyspnea, dry cough, and gradual onset of weakness. Over this same period, he lost 15 pounds. The patient had a 20-pack-year smoking history but no exposure to irradiation, silica dust, coal dust, or asbestos. He was taking no medications.

On physical examination, bilateral basilar crackles were auscultated. Oral temperature was 37°C, blood pressure was 120/80 mm Hg, pulse was 80 beats/min, and respirations were 20 breaths/min. The remainder of the physical examination was unremarkable.

An arterial blood gas on room air showed a pH of 7.44 (7.35–7.45), a P02 of 92 mm Hg (75–105 mm Hg), and a PCO2 of 36 mm Hg (33–45 mm Hg). A chest x-ray revealed a diffuse increase in linear markings that were particularly prominent at the lung base. A chest computed tomography (CT) scan showed honeycombing and reticular opacities consistent with usual interstitial pneumonia.

1
Which entities are associated with an interstitial pneumonia pattern on CT?
Show answer

Connective tissue diseases, idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, chronic hypersensitivity pneumonitis, and asbestosis can all present with this radiologic pattern of honeycombing and reticular opacities. Clinical and laboratory data combined with histologic evaluation are necessary for diagnosis.

Laboratory analysis showed normal white blood cell (WBC) count, differential counts, hematocrit, hemoglobin, urinalysis, and serum chemistry profile. Evaluation for autoimmune disease, including antinuclear antibodies, anticitrullinated peptide, and rheumatoid factor was negative. Pulmonary function tests showed reduced forced vital capacity (FVC) and decreased diffusing capacity but a normal FEV1:FVC. A PPD test was negative.

2
Does this patient have restrictive or obstructive lung disease?
Show answer

This patient has restrictive lung disease. Restrictive diseases are associated with proportionate decreases in both total lung capacity and FEV1, leading to normal FEV1:FVC ratio.

3
What are the two major types of restrictive lung disease?
Show answer

Restrictive lung disorders fall into two general categories: (1) chronic interstitial and infiltrative diseases that are intrinsic to the lung, such as pneumoconioses and interstitial fibrosis of unknown etiology; and (2) chest wall disorders that are extrinsic to the lung, such as neuromuscular diseases (e.g., poliomyelitis, severe obesity, pleural diseases, and kyphoscoliosis).


An open lung biopsy of the right lower lobe was performed. It showed patchy interstitial fibrosis that was most prominent in subpleural regions and along the interlobular septa. There were markedly thickened alveolar septa containing chronic inflammatory cells. The pulmonary vessels showed sclerotic thickening as a result of hypoxia and subsequent pulmonary hypertension.

  • Lung histology, low power
  • Fibrosis
  • Lung histology, higher power
  • Chronic inflammation
  • Thickened septa
4
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

The combination of the usual interstitial pneumonia pattern on high resolution CT and patchy interstitial fibrosis in the absence of a particular etiology (e.g., asbestos, connective tissue disease, radiation exposure) is diagnostic of idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis.

5
How do the pathologic changes in the lung explain the patient's pulmonary function tests?
Show answer

The thickened septa restrict the ability of the lungs to expand (decreased FVC) and slow the diffusion of oxygen and CO2 between alveoli and capillaries. They do not impede airflow significantly, however (explaining the only mildly decreased FEV1).

6
Which pathogenic mechanisms are believed to contribute to fibrosis in IPF?
Show answer

The mechanism of pulmonary fibrosis is not understood. Clues come from genetic studies showing that some affected patients have inherited defects in telomerase, the enzyme that prevents cellular senescence. Other affected patients have mutations affecting surfactant genes or a gene that encodes a mucin that is only expressed in alveolar epithelial cells. All of these genetic variants are suspected to render alveolar epithelial cells susceptible to senescence or injury. Fibrosis may stem from profibrogenic factors released directly from abnormal epithelial cells or from factors released by macrophages drawn to areas of epithelial injury.

7
What is the definitive therapy for patients with IPF?
Show answer

Although there are antifibrotic drugs approved for the treatment of IPF, lung transplant provides the only opportunity for cure.

Over the next three years, the patient's lung function progressively deteriorated, and he eventually required continuous oxygen. He died and an autopsy was performed.

8
What would be the expected gross appearance of his lungs?
Show answer

The “honeycomb” lung seen on high resolution CT scan would also be seen grossly as numerous cystic spaces outlined by dense, yellow-white fibrosis, resulting from gradual destruction of the normal lung architecture. Typically, these changes are most severe in the lower lobes.

  • Lung, gross
  • Cytsic spaces
  • Fibrosis
9
How does the gross appearance of centriacinar emphysema differ from that of IPF?
Show answer

IPF is invariably associated with fibrotic lungs, whereas centriacinar emphysema (shown below) is not usually accompanied by grossly visible fibrosis. Centriacinar emphysema typically affects the upper zones of the lung, whereas IPF is more evident in the lower lung zones and periphery. The cyst-like spaces of emphysema (arrows) range in size from just visible to the naked eye to many centimeters across. The cystic spaces of honeycomb lung are more uniform, approximately 5 mm in size.


10
Both emphysema and idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) result from alveolar damage, but emphysema is an obstructive lung disorder, whereas IPF is a restrictive lung disorder. Which pathologic changes explain these functional differences?
Show answer

In pulmonary fibrosis, the fibrosis causes the dilated airspaces to become rigid, restricting lung expansion. By contrast, emphysema is marked by a loss of elastic fibers in the interstitium without significant fibrosis. As a result, the lungs become hyperexpanded due to air trapping caused by collapse of small airways upon expiration, producing obstruction.

11
What are the pneumoconioses and how do they mimic IPF?
Show answer

Pneumoconioses are lung disorders caused by inhalation of organic and inorganic particulates. The three most common mineral dust pneumonoconioses are due to inhalation of coal dust, silica, and asbestos. All of these are associated with pulmonary fibrosis and can show radiographic findings similar to IPF. Histologic examination is necessary for diagnosis.

12
How do the morphologic changes of IPF compare with those found in asbestosis?
Show answer

Histologically, the pulmonary fibrosis of asbestosis is nearly indistinguishable from IPF; both show diffuse fibrosis that distorts the pulmonary architecture, creating enlarged airspaces enclosed within thick fibrous walls, eventually causing honeycombing. Therefore, a detailed clinical history and careful gross and histologic examination is necessary to exclude asbestosis before a diagnosis of IPF can be made. Patients with asbestosis frequently have discrete, well circumscribed plaques on the pleura composed of dense collagen (pleural plaques). Histologic examination will reveal asbestos bodies, golden brown fusiform or beaded rods with a translucent center. They consist of asbestos fibers coated with an iron-containing proteinaceous material.

  • Diaphragm, gross
  • Plaques
  • Asbestosis, histology
  • Asbestos body
13
How do asbestos fibers cause fibrosis?
Show answer

Asbestos fibers are phagocytosed by macrophages and are among a number of indigestible substances that activate the inflammasome. This leads to release of IL-1 and other proinflammatory mediators, which recruit additional inflammatory cells and activate fibroblasts, leading to collagen deposition. This proinflammatory effect is believed to explain the pulmonary fibrosis and pleural plaques that are seen with exposure to asbestos.

14
Which granulomatous diseases of the lung cause pulmonary fibrosis?
Show answer

Sarcoidosis and hypersensitivity pneumonia are both associated with pulmonary fibrosis.

15
Which symptoms occur in patients with sarcoidosis?
Show answer

Symptoms of sarcoidosis are highly variable and may stem from involvement of many different organ systems. Frequently patients have no symptoms and are seen because of unexplained lymph node enlargement. Pulmonary involvement may present as cough and dyspnea. Nonspecific systemic symptoms related to inflammation such as fever, fatigue, and weight loss, may be present. Involvement of lacrimal and salivary glands may cause dryness of the eyes and mouth. Skin lesions in the form of plaques or nodules may be present.

16
What are the typical morphologic findings in sarcoidosis?
Show answer

The characteristic histology is noncaseating granulomatous inflammation. Granulomas are typically not appreciated grossly, except in advanced disease. Histologic examination reveals well-formed nonnecrotizing granulomas composed of aggregates of tightly clustered epithelioid macrophages, often with giant cells. Unlike in tuberculosis, central necrosis is uncommon.

  • Lung, sarcoidosis
  • Giant cell
  • Epithelioid macrophages
  • Granuloma
17
What does the presence of granulomas suggest regarding the possible pathogenesis of sarcoidosis?
Show answer

Sarcoidosis remains a disease of unknown etiology. The presence of granulomas suggests chronic exposure to some agent that persists, causing a delayed hypersensitivity reaction involving Th1 type CD4+ T helper cells and, consequently, granuloma formation. In support of this hypothesis, lungs involved by sarcoidosis contain numerous activated CD4+ T cells.

18
Which other organs are commonly involved in sarcoidosis?
Show answer

Although almost any organ may be involved, common sites include lymph nodes, eye, salivary glands, skin, spleen, and liver. Biopsy of involved tissues reveals noncaseating granulomas, as in involved lung.

Objectives
  1. • Describe the types, pathogenesis and presentation of hydatidiform mole.
  2. • Compare and contrast salient features of complete and partial moles.
Summary of case:

A 20-year-old female presented to Obstetrics and Gynecology clinic with painless vaginal bleeding, nausea, vomiting and abdominal cramping. On clinical examination, patient was alert, oriented with a body temperature of 98.8 F, and BP 122/70 mmHg. Her last menstrual period was 6 weeks ago. Past medical and family history was unremarkable. Pelvic examination demonstrated vaginal discharge, blood clots and abundant brown-colored grape-like materials. Bilateral adnexa were free and non-tender. Lab testing demonstrated a Hb of 9 g/dL with normal red cell morphology and elevated β-HCG of 310,450 mIU/mL (1,080 to 56,500 mIU/mL at 6 weeks of gestation). Ultrasound of the pelvis showed a large cloud-like mass in the uterine cavity giving a snowstorm appearance, without a fetus present. Based on the presentation and lab findings, a dilatation and curettage was performed and the samples were sent for histopathological examination.

1
What is the significance of such presentation in this case?
Show answer

Vaginal discharge in early pregnancy may be associated with a number of conditions, such as spontaneous abortion, miscarriage, ruptured ectopic pregnancy and molar pregnancy. In case of incomplete miscarriage, retained products of conception may result in vaginal bleeding which is generally associated with fever. Pelvic ultrasonography can detect ectopic pregnancy or ruptured ectopic pregnancy. Molar pregnancy (complete or partial hydatidiform moles) is indicated by β-HCG levels greatly exceed those of a normal pregnancy of similar gestational age, typical ultrasonography findings, such as cloud-like (snowstorm) appearance and absence of an intrauterine fetus.

2
What is the likely diagnosis?
Show answer

Patient has a molar pregnancy. Given the clinical and lab presentation of a young patient with increased β-HCG level much higher than that of normal gestational age, intrauterine cloud-like mass with no evidence of fetus, and abnormal vaginal discharge with large-amount of brown-colored grape-like materials, the most likely diagnosis is a complete hydatidiform mole.

3
What is hydatidiform mole?
Show answer

Hydatidiform mole is a gestational trophoblastic disease, characterized by abnormal trophoblastic proliferation. Hydatidiform moles are classified as complete and partial mole. Gestational trophoblastic disease encompasses a spectrum of disorders, such as hydatidiform mole, invasive mole, placental site trophoblastic tumor, epithelioid trophoblastic tumor and choriocarcinoma.

4
What is the pathogenesis of molar pregnancy?
Show answer

Hydatidiform mole can be of two types – complete or partial mole. Complete mole results from fertilization of an egg that has lost (or subsequently loses) its female chromosomes, and as a result its nuclear genetic material is completely paternally derived. Most of the cases result from duplication of the genetic material of one sperm, a phenomenon called androgenesis, creating a diploid 46,XX karyotype, also known as monospermic complete hydatidiform mole. Remaining cases occur from the fertilization of an empty egg by 2 sperm, resulting in a dispermic complete hydatidiform mole with 46,XX or 46,XY karyotype. Partial mole results from fertilization of an egg with 2 sperm. In these moles, karyotpe is triploid (e.g., 69,XXY) or occasionally tetraploid (e.g., 92,XXXY).

5
What are the clinical features of molar pregnancy?
Show answer

Patients with partial or early complete mole present with spontaneous miscarriage and undergo curettage because of ultrasound findings of abnormal villous enlargement. In case of complete mole, β-HCG levels greatly exceed compared to a normal pregnancy of similar gestational age. Additionally, the rate at which β-HCG levels rise over time in molar pregnancy exceeds what is seen with normal single or even multiple pregnancies. Most moles are successfully removed by curettage. Patients are subsequently monitored for 6 months to 1 year until the levels are undetectable. In complete mole, there is no embryonic development. Patients have 2.5% risk of subsequent chorocarcinoma and a 15% risk of persistent or invasive mole. In partial mole, fetal development may be seen, but the fetus is typically growth-retardesd and has lethal anomalies of central nervous system and skeletal abnormalities. Partial moles may have an increased risk of persistent molar disease, but are not typically associated with postmolar choriocarcinoma.

6
How do you differentiate complete moles from partial moles?
Show answer

Hydatidiform moles characteristically appear as a delicate, friable mass of thin walled, translucent, cystic grape-like structures consisting of swollen edematous (hydropic) villi. In complete mole, the microscopy involves all or most of the villous tissue. The chorionic villi are enlarged, smooth, and oval with central cavitation (cisterns) and are covered by a trophoblast proliferation that includes both cytotrophoblasts and syncytiotrophoblasts. In contrast, only a fraction of the villi is enlarged and edematous in partial hydatifdiform mole. Histologically, it shows trophoblastic hyperplasia that is focal and less marked and only syncytiotrophoblasts are hyperplastic. Unlike complete mole, partial moles have irregular villous outlines often with prominent trophoblastic inclusions.

7
When do you suspect an invasive mole?
Show answer

Continuous elevation of beta-human chorionic gonadotrophin (HCG) may be indicative of persistent or invasive mole. In molar pregnancy, the β-HCG levels greatly exceed those of a normal pregnancy of similar gestational age.

8
What is choriocarcinoma?
Show answer

Choriocarcinoma is a malignant neoplasm of trophoblastic cells. Gestational choriocarcinoma, an uncommon condition accounting for 1 in 20,000 to 30,000 pregnancies in the United States, originate from a previously normal or abnormal pregnancy, such as an extrauterine ectopic pregnancy or a complete hydatidiform mole. Choriocarcinoma is characterized by destructive and hemorrhagic invasion and is often widely metastatic (lungs, vagina, brain, liver, bone and kidney) at presentation. These are soft fleshy yellow-white tumor usually with large areas of necrosis and hemorrhage. Histologically, it shows proliferating syncytiotrophoblasts and cytotrophoblasts without chorionic villi, mitoses some with atypical forms, and invasion of underlying myometrium. β-HCG levels are typically elevated to levels above those encountered in hydatidiform moles. Treatment of gestational choriocarcinoma depends on the stage of the tumors and usually consists of evacuation of the uterine contents and chemotherapy with nearly 100% remission rate. Rarely, nongestational choriocarcinoma develops from germ cells in the ovaries or mediastinum. Unlike gestational counterpart, these tumors lack paternally derived DNA and have a poor prognosis.

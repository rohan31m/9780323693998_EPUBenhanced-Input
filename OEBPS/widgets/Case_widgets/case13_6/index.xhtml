A 62-year-old man presented to his physician with a 6-month history of loss of appetite and gradual weight loss of about 30 pounds. Review of systems revealed loose bowel movements that were greasy, clay colored, and malodorous. He had a 45 pack-year smoking history and a history of heavy alcohol use for many years, but no history of chemical or toxin exposure. Physical examination revealed a thin, frail man with prominent bitemporal muscle wasting. He was jaundiced and icteric.

The chest and cardiac examinations were unremarkable. His liver percussed to 13 cm (normal 10.5 cm), and a firm, irregular edge was palpable below the right costal margin. No spleen was felt.

What are the causes of jaundice and icterus?
Show answer

Jaundice and icterus, yellow discoloration of the skin and sclerae, respectively, occur when there is bilirubin overproduction, hepatitis, or obstruction of the flow of bile, any of which can disturb the equilibrium between bilirubin production and clearance.

What might be responsible for frequent greasy and foul-smelling stools?
Show answer

This constellation of symptoms, called steatorrhea, is the classic presentation of malabsorption. The disorders that are most commonly responsible for malabsorption in the United States are pancreatic insufficiency, celiac disease, and Crohn disease. Pancreatic insufficiency can be caused by chronic pancreatitis and cystic fibrosis. Alcohol can cause pancreatitis, as is likely in this patient.

Which of this patient’s symptoms are suggestive of chronic pancreatitis?
Show answer

Chronic pancreatitis is defined as prolonged inflammation of the pancreas associated with irreversible destruction of exocrine parenchyma, fibrosis, and, in the late stages, the destruction of endocrine parenchyma. With the loss of exocrine parenchyma, insufficient lipase secretion can result in steatorrhea as is seen in this patient. The combination of jaundice and malabsorption is strongly suggestive of chronic pancreatitis or pancreatic duct obstruction.

What is the best interpretation of this patient’s liver function tests?
Show answer

Liver function tests include aspartate aminotransferase (AST), alanine aminotransferase (ALT), alkaline phosphatase (AP), gamma-glutamyl transpeptidase (GGT), and total bilirubin. These tests are typically ordered as a panel since, in isolation, a single test is not informative. The results can be interpreted to determine whether the primary injury is to hepatocytes (hepatocellular), to bile ducts (cholestatic), or is infiltrative (replacement of the liver by nonhepatic tissues such as metastatic tumor). The patient’s elevated AST and ALT indicate hepatocyte injury. The ratio of AST:ALT is less than 2, suggesting that the liver damage is not likely to be due to acute alcoholic injury. Alkaline phosphatase is most concentrated near the canalicular membrane of the hepatocyte and is typically increased in obstructive diseases such as bile duct obstruction, primary sclerosing cholangitis, and primary biliary cholangitis. Gamma-glutamyl transpeptidase is also found in the canalicular face of hepatocytes and bile duct epithelium. It can be elevated in hepatocyte injury and in bile duct obstruction. Since alkaline phosphatase can also be found in bones, its level may be elevated in states of high bone turnover. Elevation of GGT and alkaline phosphatase localizes the disease to the hepatobiliary system. Elevated total serum bilirubin is seen in a variety of conditions, including obstruction of the biliary tract and hepatocyte injury, while conjugated hyperbilirubinemia is typically seen in obstructive disease. Clay-colored stool is seen when bile does not pass into the gastrointestinal system. Conjugated bilirubin is filtered by the kidney; hence, bilirubin can be found in the urine with any cause of conjugated hyperbilirubinemia. In sum, the patient has pancreatitis (elevated lipase/amylase), hepatitis (hepatocyte injury), and cholestasis, likely related to obstruction.

What is the significance of this patient’s amylase and lipase levels?
Show answer

Amylase and lipase are present in pancreatic acini and hence their serum levels are typically elevated in acute pancreatitis; however, in chronic pancreatitis, because of loss of acini, only mild to moderate elevation may be seen. When there is sufficient damage to the pancreas, lower than normal values may be seen.

6
What are some of the complications of chronic pancreatitis?
Show answer

Although chronic pancreatitis is usually not an immediately life-threatening condition, the long-term outlook for individuals with chronic pancreatitis is poor, with a 20- to 25-year mortality rate of 50%. Pancreatic exocrine insufficiency, chronic malabsorption, and diabetes can all lead to significant morbidity and contribute to mortality. In other patients, severe chronic pain is a dominant problem.

7
What are the histologic features of chronic pancreatitis?
Show answer

Chronic pancreatitis is characterized by fibrosis, atrophy, and dropout of acini, and variable dilation of pancreatic ducts. These changes are typically accompanied by a chronic inflammatory infiltrate around lobules and ducts. Acinar loss is a constant feature. There is usually relative sparing of the islets of Langerhans, but in advanced disease the islets are also lost.

What are some risk factors for chronic pancreatitis?

The most common cause of chronic pancreatitis by far is long-term alcohol abuse; long-standing obstruction of the pancreatic duct (due to calculi or neoplasms), autoimmune pancreatitis, and hereditary pancreatitis can also lead to chronic pancreatitis.

How do the radiologic and laboratory findings relate to the patient’s clinical presentation?
Show answer

All of these findings are consistent with a diagnosis of carcinoma of the head of the pancreas. Most carcinomas of the head of the pancreas obstruct the distal common bile duct as it courses through the head of the pancreas. In 50% of such cases, there is marked distention of the biliary tree, and patients typically exhibit jaundice, as in this case. By contrast, carcinomas of the body and tail of the pancreas do not impinge on the biliary tract. Increased serum levels of CA19-9 are neither specific nor sensitive for pancreatic carcinoma; however, in this clinical setting, a level of 800 U/mL supports the diagnosis.

Is the presence of pancreatitis and carcinoma in this patient coincidental or etiologically related? What are some of the causes of chronic pancreatitis?
Show answer

Although most cases of pancreatic adenocarcinoma are not associated with chronic pancreatitis, chronic pancreatitis is associated with an increased risk of cancer. Chronic pancreatitis results in destruction of the exocrine and, eventually, endocrine portions of the pancreas. It usually follows repeated bouts of acute pancreatitis that are due to alcohol ingestion or obstruction of the pancreatic duct. It is one of many chronic inflammatory conditions that are associated with an increased risk of various cancers.

What is the precursor lesion for pancreatic adenocarcinoma?
Show answer

The most common antecedent lesions of pancreatic cancer arise in small ducts and ductules and are called pancreatic intraepithelial neoplasias (PanINs). They are characterized by dysplastic epithelium with stratification and enlarged, hyperchromatic nuclei. These microscopic lesions often are found adjacent to infiltrating carcinomas and share a number of genetic alterations with pancreatic adenocarcinoma. The epithelial cells in PanINs show dramatic telomere shortening, potentially predisposing these lesions to pathogenic chromosomal abnormalities that may contribute to acquisition of the full spectrum of cancer hallmarks. This example of PanIN-3 is involving a small pancreatic duct.

  • Pancreas, histology
  • Dysplastic epithelium

Which are the most common somatic mutations in pancreatic adenocarcinoma?
Show answer

KRAS is the most frequently altered oncogene in pancreatic cancer; it is activated by a point mutation in greater than 90% of cases. These mutations impair the intrinsic GTPase activity of the KRAS protein so that it is constitutively active. In turn, KRAS activates a number of intracellular signaling pathways that promote carcinogenesis. The p16 (CDKN2A) gene is the most frequently inactivated tumor suppressor gene in pancreatic cancer, being turned off in 95% of cases. The p16 protein has a critical role in cell-cycle control. The SMAD4 tumor suppressor gene is inactivated in 55% of pancreatic cancers and only rarely in other tumors; it codes for a protein that plays an important role in signal transduction downstream of the transforming growth factor-β receptor. Inactivation of the TP53 tumor suppressor gene occurs in 50% to 70% of pancreatic cancers. Its gene product, p53, acts both to enforce cell-cycle checkpoints and as an inducer of apoptosis or senescence.

By which routes do malignant tumors metastasize to the liver?
Show answer

Most tumors metastasize to the liver hematogenously through the portal vein or hepatic artery. Biliary tract tumors directly and contiguously spread to the liver.

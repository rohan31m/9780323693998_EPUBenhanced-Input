Keeping Abreast

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast benign breast diseases with regard to clinical presentation, morphologic features, and subsequent risk of breast cancer.
  2. • Describe the major risk factors for breast cancer.
  3. • Compare and contrast the different types of breast cancer.
  4. • Discuss prognostic factors in breast cancer.

A 20-year-old woman presented to her primary care physician after detecting a lump in her left breast. She had not noticed nipple discharge, skin changes, or alteration in the lump’s size. Physical exam revealed a freely mobile, 3-cm, spherical mass in the upper outer quadrant of her breast. An ultrasound showed the mass to be solid, not cystic. A needle biopsy was performed and was benign; however, the patient requested that the mass be excised since her maternal aunt had recently been diagnosed with breast cancer.

1
Is it likely that this is a malignant tumor?
Show answer

Although breast cancer can present in young women, the clinical presentation is highly suggestive of a fibroadenoma. Fibroadenoma is common in younger women (>40 years of age) and usually presents as a well-circumscribed, mobile, rubbery nodule, indicating an encapsulated, non-invasive lesion. Fibroadenoma is the most common benign tumor of the female breast. Most occur in women in their 20s and 30s, and they are frequently multiple and bilateral. Even with a classic presentation, biopsy is necessary for definitive diagnosis.

  • Fibroadenoma, gross
2
What is the neoplastic component of fibroadenomas?
Show answer

Fibroadenoma consists of large, often compressed, and irregularly shaped glandular spaces with intervening fibrous stroma. Clonal cytogenetic aberrations are present in the stromal component of fibroadenomas; the epithelial cells are entrapped and are not neoplastic.

  • Fibroadenoma, histology
  • Compressed glands
  • Stromal proliferation

Twenty years later, at the age of 40, the patient presented to her primary care physician with a 3-month history of “lumpiness” in both breasts. The lumps were more prominent the week before her menstrual cycle and were mildly tender. A breast ultrasound showed thickened tissue and scattered small cysts without a definite mass. Mammography showed scattered calcifications. A needle biopsy was recommended; however, the patient desired an excisional biopsy.

3
What is the most likely diagnosis?
Show answer

This clinical presentation is most consistent with fibrocystic changes. These lesions are categorized as “nonproliferative” and consist of an admixture of fibrous tissue, cysts, and increased lobular acini (adenosis). The cysts are frequently lined by cells with abundant eosinophilic, granular cytoplasm and resemble the cells of apocrine sweat glands (so-called apocrine metaplasia). Calcifications are common. Despite the classic presentation, biopsy is necessary for definitive diagnosis.

  • Fibrocystic changes, gross
  • Areas of fibrosis
  • Blue-domed cyst
  • Fibrocystic changes, histology
  • Calcifications
  • Apopcrine metaplasia
4
Are calcifications in the breast parenchyma diagnostic of carcinoma?
Show answer

No, microcalcifications are most often associated with benign disorders such as fibrocystic changes. Radiologists examine the character of the microcalcifications to determine whether or not they suggest the presence of carcinoma.

The biopsy confirmed benign fibrocystic changes. In addition, however, there was a focus of ductal hyperplasia without atypia, characterized by increased numbers of luminal and myoepithelial cells that filled and distended ducts and lobules. Irregular lumens were discerned at the periphery

  • Biopsy, histology
  • Ductal hyperlasia
  • Irregular spaces
5
How are benign epithelial lesions classified? What is the clinical significance of this classification?
Show answer

Benign changes are divided into three groups: nonproliferative disease, proliferative disease without atypia (hyperplasia), and proliferative disease with atypia (atypical hyperplasia), each associated with a different degree of breast cancer risk. Nonproliferative disease (fibrocystic changes) is not associated with an increased risk of breast cancer. Proliferative disease without atypia encompasses polyclonal hyperplasias (ductal hyperplasia and lobular hyperplasia) that are associated with a slightly increased risk of breast cancer. Atypical hyperplasia includes monoclonal “precancers” that are associated with a modest increase in the risk of breast cancer in both breasts; overall, 13% to 17% of women with these lesions develop breast cancer.


6
Do this patient’s previous diagnoses increase her risk for breast carcinoma?
Show answer

Neither fibroadenomas nor fibrocystic changes increases her risk. However, ductal hyperplasia without atypia slightly increases her risk (1.5 to 2 times). Atypical ductal hyperplasia (ADH), marked by cytologic and architectural atypia, has a four to five times increased risk, but was not seen in this patient. ADH, along with atypical lobular hyperplasia (ALH), closely resemble ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) and lobular carcinoma in situ (LCIS), respectively. Both of these in situ lesions can progress to invasive carcinomas in up to 1/3 of the affected individuals.

7
What are the major risk factors for the development of breast carcinoma?
Show answer

Risk factors include family history (first-degree relatives with breast carcinoma), estrogen exposure unopposed by progesterone, and proliferative fibrocystic disease. Early menarche, nulliparity, late menopause, and obesity are all associated with an increased risk of breast cancer due to increased exposure of the breast to estrogen, which drives the proliferation and survival of breast epithelial cells. Five percent to 10% of breast cancers are related to the inheritance of mutations in the TP53 gene (Li-Fraumeni syndrome), BRCA1 gene, or BRCA2 gene. A high fraction of individuals who inherit a mutated copy of one of these tumor suppressor genes develops carcinoma, often at a younger age than is typical for sporadic breast carcinoma.

8
How does LCIS differ from DCIS with regard to the subsequent risk for invasive breast carcinoma?
Show answer

The terms lobular and ductular refer to the morphologic pattern of the in situ lesions. LCIS expands the lobules, whereas DCIS distorts the lobules to create duct like spaces. By definition, these in situ lesions do not breach the basement membrane. With long-term follow-up (approximately 24 years), 30% of patients with LCIS develop invasive carcinoma; however, the tumor may be ductal or lobular, and in the same or contralateral breast. For this reason, lobular carcinoma in situ is considered both a marker of increased risk for invasive breast carcinoma and a precursor to invasive breast carcinoma. By contrast, when DCIS progresses to invasive cancer, it usually is found in the same breast and quadrant as the earlier DCIS.

Fifteen years later, at the age of 55, the patient returned to her physician two weeks after detecting a lump in her right breast. She had not noticed any nipple discharge, skin changes, or alteration in the lump's size.

Physical examination revealed a nontender, slightly movable, 2-cm mass in the upper outer quadrant of her right breast. In addition, several smaller nodules and ill-defined firm areas were present in both breasts, consistent with fibrocystic changes. The nipple and skin appeared normal.


9
Is this likely to be in situ carcinoma?
Show answer

Ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) is usually asymptomatic and nonpalpable or only vaguely palpable, and is most commonly identified due to the presence of calcifications that are detected by mammography. Lobular carcinoma in situ is always an incidental biopsy finding. Since this patient has a palpable mass, it is more likely to be a ductal carcinoma.

10
How do high-grade and low-grade intraductal carcinomas differ in clinical behavior?
Show answer

Recurrence or invasion occurs in approximately 40% of patients with high-grade intraductal carcinoma after lumpectomy alone compared with only about 10% of patients with low-grade intraductal carcinoma after lumpectomy alone. High-grade tumor may have a central area of necrosis; such lesions are sometimes called comedocarcinoma. The necrotic areas can calcify and thereby appear in breast imaging studies.

A mammogram showed an irregular mass with stippled calcifications in the upper outer quadrant of the right breast. A core biopsy was performed that showed invasive carcinoma. A lumpectomy with sentinel node biopsy was scheduled.

11
What is a sentinel node biopsy and what is its clinical utility?
Show answer

Axillary lymph node status is the most important prognostic factor for invasive carcinoma in the absence of distant metastases. The clinical assessment of lymph node status is unreliable and biopsy is necessary for accurate assessment. Lymphatic vessels in most breast carcinomas drain first to several sentinel nodes and can spread from there to other lymph nodes. The sentinel lymph node(s) is identified during surgery using radiotracer and/or colored dye injected into the tumor. In some cases, a frozen section is performed, and if metastatic carcinoma is identified in three or more lymph nodes or if one or two lymph nodes are positive for metastatic carcinoma and other clinical criteria are met, an axillary dissection will be performed. However, if a biopsy is negative, it is unlikely that other, more distant nodes will be involved and the patient can be spared the morbidity of axillary dissection. Clinical algorithms are being increasingly used to try to predict the likelihood of having additional metastatic disease in the setting of a positive sentinel lymph node to limit axillary dissections to only those patients that would truly benefit from one.

The frozen section analysis of the lymph node showed a focus of metastatic carcinoma. Additional lymph nodes were biopsied with a total of three positive lymph nodes. An axillary dissection was performed at the time of lumpectomy.

Gross examination of the lumpectomy specimen showed a 2-cm, white, scirrhous (fibrotic) mass in the central portion of the specimen. The margins of the lumpectomy specimen were free of tumor.

  • Sentinel lymph node, histology
  • Lymph node capsule
  • Tumor in sinuses
  • Tumor in lymph node
  • Lumpectomy specimen, gross
  • Central mass
12
What is the basis for the scar-like characteristics of most invasive breast cancers?
Show answer

Infiltrating cancer causes local tissue damage, and this and the host immune response to cancer cells induce varying degrees of inflammation and attempts at tissue healing. As with normal healing responses, this includes the activation of fibroblasts and deposition of collagen (fibrosis), producing a scar-like lesion.

Histologically, the specimen showed irregular duct-like structures lined by atypical epithelial cells. There was an exuberant desmoplastic and inflammatory response.

Fifteen lymph nodes were isolated from the axillary dissection; two additional lymph nodes were positive for metastatic carcinoma.

  • Breast carcinoma, histology
  • Malignant glands
  • Desmoplasia
13
Which additional laboratory tests should be performed on the tissue and how would the results affect treatment?
Show answer

Breast cancers are classified clinically into three groups based on estrogen receptor (ER)/progesterone receptor (PR) status and HER2 amplification and protein expression. HER2 encodes a receptor tyrosine kinase that is a member of the epidermal growth factor receptor family. Usually, immunohistochemistry and FISH are used to determine receptor status and HER2 amplification, respectively. The three clinical groups are (1) ER positive, HER2 negative (50%–65% of cancers); (2) HER2 positive, ER positive or negative (10%–20% of cancers); and (3) triple negative: ER, PR, and HER2 negative (10%–20% of cancers). ER-positive tumors respond well to anti-estrogen hormone treatment, while HER2-positive tumors are treated with antibodies or small molecule inhibitors of HER2.

14
What are the two main morphologic types of carcinoma and which type does this patient have?
Show answer

Invasive carcinomas can be divided based on molecular and morphologic characteristics into several clinically important subgroups. The terms ductal and lobular are still used to describe subsets of both in situ and invasive carcinomas, but most evidence suggests all breast carcinomas actually arise from cells in the terminal duct lobular unit. Approximately 70%–80% of breast carcinomas are classified morphologically as “ductal” or “NST” (no special type). The remaining tumors include invasive lobular carcinoma (10%–15%), medullary carcinoma (5%) and other, rarer entities. Ductal carcinomas are characterized by infiltrating tubules and duct-like structures, as are seen in this patient. By contrast, lobular carcinoma shows single-file arrangement of invasive tumor cells. This growth pattern is due in most cases to loss of E-cadherin, an important cell adhesion molecule. Medullary carcinoma consists of sheets of large anaplastic cells associated with prominent lymphocytic infiltrates.

  • Lobular carcinoma, histology
  • Invasive cells
15
How does the prognosis of invasive lobular carcinoma compare with that of invasive ductal carcinoma?
Show answer

Although there are conflicting studies, there is probably no significant difference in survival of invasive ductal and lobular carcinoma.

16
Which pathologic features are important in determining the prognosis and treatment of invasive breast carcinoma?
Show answer

The most important characteristics are tumor stage; expression of estrogen receptor and progesterone receptor; and amplification or overexpression of the HER2 receptor. Also important are the grade of the tumor and the presence or absence of lymphatic and vascular invasion.

17
Which organs are commonly involved by metastatic breast carcinoma?
Show answer

In addition to lymph nodes, the lungs, liver, bone, and the CNS are frequent sites of metastasis.

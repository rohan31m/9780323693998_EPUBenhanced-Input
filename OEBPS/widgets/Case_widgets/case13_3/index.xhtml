Objectives
  1. • Compare the etiologies and pathogenesis of hereditary (primary) hemochromatosis versus acquired (secondary) hemochromatosis.
  2. • List the organs and cells in which iron deposition occurs in primary hemochromatosis.
  3. • Identify the most serious complications of hepatic involvement in hemochromatosis.

A 47-year-old, previously healthy man presented to his primary care physician with a 6-month history of fatigue, joint pain, and vague abdominal pain. He stated that he has been awakening frequently at night to urinate. His arthralgia was primarily in his hands.

Physical exam revealed metallic gray pigmentation of the sun-exposed skin on his face and bilateral swelling of the first and second metacarpophalangeal joints. His liver percussed to a span of 13 cm (normal 10.5 cm).

Laboratory tests were performed:


Hct 45% (41%–53%)
AST 240 U/L (8–20 U/L)
ALT 180 (8–20 U/L)
Alkaline phosphatase 202 U/L (20–70 U/L)
Fasting serum glucose 325 mg/dL (70–110 mg/dL)
1
Which additional laboratory tests should be ordered?
Show answer

This patient is demonstrating the classic triad of hemochromatosis: hepatomegaly, abnormal skin pigmentation, and arthritis. Hemochromatosis is caused by excessive absorption of iron, which is primarily deposited in parenchymal organs such as the liver, as well as in the heart, joints, and pancreatic islets. In order to confirm the diagnosis, serum iron quantitation is necessary and the serum ferritin and total iron binding capacity (TIBC) should be evaluated. Transferrin saturation can then be calculated from these data. In view of his elevated liver enzymes, infectious hepatitis should also be excluded.

Laboratory values:


Serum iron concentration 175 mg/dL (50–150 mg/dL)
Transferrin saturation 63% (20%–50%)
Serum ferritin 650 ng/mL (23–336 ng/mL)
TIBC 200 (250–370 μg/dL)
HBsAg Negative
Anti-HBc Total–negative
Anti-HCV Negative
2
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

The laboratory values are diagnostic of hemochromatosis.

3
What are the various forms of iron overload? How do the etiologies of primary and secondary hemochromatosis (secondary iron overload or hemosiderosis) differ?
Show answer

Primary hemochromatosis is an autosomal recessive inherited disorder, resulting in progressive iron absorption through the gastrointestinal tract with accumulation of iron in a variety of organs, including the liver. Secondary iron overload or secondary hemochromatosis that results in hemosiderosis of the liver can be due to a variety of causes, including chronic blood transfusion and disorders that are associated with ineffective hematopoiesis (e.g., thalassemia major), which also lead to increased uptake of iron from the gut.

4
How is iron regulated in the body?
Show answer

Because there is no regulated iron excretion from the body, the total body content of iron is tightly regulated by intestinal absorption. Duodenal epithelial cells take up heme and nonheme iron. When the storage sites of the body are replete with iron and erythropoietic activity is normal, plasma hepcidin balances iron uptake and loss to maintain iron hemostasis by downregulating ferroportin and limiting iron uptake (middle panel). Hepcidin rises in the setting of systemic inflammation or when iron levels are high, decreasing iron uptake and increasing iron loss through shedding of duodenocytes (right panel), and it falls in the settings of low plasma iron, primary hemochromatosis, or ineffective hematopoiesis, resulting in increased iron uptake (left panel). DMT1, Divalent metal transporter-1.


5
Which genetic test should be ordered? What is the function of the gene product?
Show answer

The most frequently mutated gene in patients with primary hemochromatosis is HFE. HFE encodes an HLA class I–like molecule that regulates the synthesis of hepcidin in hepatocytes. The most common HFE mutation is a cysteine-to-tyrosine substitution at amino acid 282 (C282Y) that inactivates HFE with a consequent decrease in hepcidin synthesis. With decreased hepcidin levels, there is increased iron absorption by enterocytes. Hemochromatosis typically manifests after 20 gm of iron have accumulated in liver iron stores.

6
What is transferrin and why is transferrin saturation increased in hemochromatosis?
Show answer

Transferrin is an iron-binding glycoprotein that transports iron in plasma. In normal individuals, transferrin is about one third saturated with iron. Transferrin saturation is calculated by dividing the serum iron concentration by the total iron-binding capacity (TIBC) and multiplying by 100. In hemochromatosis, the serum iron concentration is elevated while TIBC is decreased. An elevated transferrin saturation is one of the earliest signs of hereditary hemochromatosis.

A genetic test confirmed that the patient was homozygous for the HFE C282Y mutation. A biopsy of the patient’s liver was performed.

7
Which stain is used to confirm the presence of iron in a histologic section?
Show answer

Prussian blue stains iron blue. In this section, the parenchymal architecture is normal, despite the abundant iron.

8
How does iron cause liver injury?
Show answer

Excessive iron appears to be directly toxic to tissues. Mechanisms of liver injury include (1) lipid peroxidation via iron-catalyzed free radical reactions; (2) stimulation of collagen formation by activation of hepatic stellate cells; and (3) interaction of reactive oxygen species and iron itself with DNA, leading to lethal cell injury and predisposition to hepatocellular carcinoma.

9
What are the long-term consequences of primary and secondary hemochromatosis?
Show answer

Without treatment, primary hemochromatosis leads to cirrhosis and hepatic failure. With involvement of other organs, patients can also develop cardiac failure and various endocrine insufficiency states (e.g., diabetes). Patients with hemochromatosis and cirrhosis are at an increased risk for developing hepatocellular carcinoma. By contrast, secondary hemochromatosis typically does not cause severe liver injury or cirrhosis, but if untreated may cause cardiac damage of sufficient severity to lead to heart failure.

10
What is the gross appearance of the liver in primary hemochromatosis?
Show answer

Grossly, the liver is cirrhotic, with fibrous connective tissue bands separating regenerating nodules of chocolate-brown hepatic parenchyma. Histologically, brown hemosiderin (iron) deposits are noted in both periportal hepatocytes and bile duct epithelial cells.

  • Hemochromatosis, gross
  • Fibrous bands
  • Parenchymal nodules
  • Hemochromatosis, histology
  • Bile ductules
  • Periportal hepatocytes
11
Why do women typically present at an older age than men?
Show answer

Regular menstrual bleeding and loss of iron to the fetus during pregnancy limit the accumulation of iron.

12
What is the standard treatment for patients with hereditary hemochromatosis?
Show answer

Regular phlebotomy is used to remove iron found in hemoglobin and decrease red cell mass. The decreased red cell mass stimulates the marrow to make more red cells, a process that requires iron and in turn decreases tissue stores. With this simple treatment, life expectancy is normal.

The patient presented for regular phlebotomy and was followed with serial hepatic ultrasounds to monitor disease progression. Six years after diagnosis, a dominant nodule was noted in the left lobe of his liver. Laboratory tests showed an elevated serum α-fetoprotein level.

13
What is the most likely diagnosis?
Show answer

Primary hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) arising in the setting of cirrhosis is the likely diangnosis. Cirrhosis is not necessary for hepatocarcinogenesis, but rather, progression to cirrhosis and to hepatocellular cancer takes place in parallel over many years to decades.

A liver biopsy was performed that showed cells similar to normal hepatocytes arranged in widened trabeculae with pseudoacinar spaces, diagnostic of well differentiated HCC. There was background cirrhosis and iron deposition. The patient was treated with chemotherapy and surgical resection.

  • Hepatocellular carcinoma
  • Widened trabeculae
  • Pseudoacinar spaces
14
Which factors are most important in hepatocarcinogenesis?
Show answer

Chronic liver disease is the most common setting for HCC. Viral infections (HBV, HCV) and toxic injuries (alcohol, aflatoxin) are the most important underlying factors. HBV (and perhaps HCV) infection synergizes with aflatoxin exposure to further increase risk. Alcohol consumption also synergistically increases risk with HBV and HCV. In addition to hemochromatosis, inherited disorders such as α1-antitrypsin deficiency and Wilson disease also are associated with HCC, as is metabolic syndrome, with its attendant nonalcoholic fatty liver disease, diabetes, and obesity.

15
Which other disease of the liver is caused by excessive deposition of a metallic substance?
Show answer

Wilson disease is caused by deposition of copper due to recessively inherited loss-of-function mutations in the ATP7B gene. ATP7B encodes the transmembrane copper-transporting ATPase located on the canicular membranes of hepatocytes. The deficiency of the transmembrane copper-transporting ATPase leads to copper accumulation in the liver. Eventually, copper spills over into the circulation and causes damage in other tissues.

16
How does the clinical presentation of Wilson disease differ from hemochromatosis?
Show answer

Unlike hemochromatosis, which typically presents in older adults, the age at onset and the clinical presentation of Wilson disease are extremely variable. Symptoms usually appear between 6 and 40 years of age. Acute or chronic liver disease are common presenting features. Neuropsychiatric manifestations are the initial features in most of the remaining cases and stem from deposition of copper in the basal ganglia. Nearly all patients with neurologic involvement develop eye lesions called Kayser-Fleischer rings, which are green to brown deposits (arrows) of copper in the Descemet membrane in the limbus of the cornea.

Madame Ovary

Objectives
  1. • Describe and contrast the pathologic and clinical features of epithelial tumors of the ovary.
  2. • Describe the etiology of ovarian serous carcinoma.
  3. • Describe the usual clinical presentation and pathologic features of mature and immature teratomas.

A 52-year-old woman presented to her internist with a 6-month history of vague abdominal pain and a feeling of fullness. She experienced menopause at the age of 49; her medical and surgical history were otherwise noncontributory. Physical exam was unremarkable and she was referred to a gastroenterologist. The gastroenterologist ordered upper and lower GI endoscopy; however, the studies were negative.

Over the next 6 months, the patient returned multiple times to these physicians, without definitive diagnosis. However, at one of her visits, a third-year medical student noted abdominal fullness with a fluid wave, consistent with ascites. A pelvic examination revealed a mass in the right lower quadrant. A CT scan was performed and showed a multiloculated 10-cm left adnexal mass.

1
What is the differential diagnosis for a large multiloculated mass in a post-menopausal woman?
Show answer

The differential diagnosis includes tuboovarian complex from prior pelvic inflammatory disease and benign and malignant ovarian tumors. Tumors of the ovary are remarkably varied as they may arise from any of the three cell types in the normal ovary: multipotent surface (coelomic) epithelium, totipotent germ cells, and sex cord–stromal cells. Neoplasms of epithelial origin account for the great majority of ovarian tumors and, in their malignant forms, account for almost 90% of ovarian cancers. Germ cell and sex cord–stromal cell tumors are much less frequent; although they constitute 20% to 30% of ovarian tumors, they are collectively responsible for less than 10% of malignant tumors of the ovary.

An open laparotomy was performed and tumor was seen involving the contralateral ovary, omentum, and numerous peritoneal surfaces. Grossly, the mass had solid and cystic portions, which when bisected were found to be filled with friable, tan-pink papillary excrescences. Also noted were areas of tumor invasion associated with firm, white, desmoplastic stroma. Histologic exam revealed complex papillary structures with rare psammoma bodies. Invasion of the underlying stroma was present. The epithelium showed marked atypia that included nuclear pleomorphism and atypical mitotic figures.

2
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

The presence of papillary structures, malignant epithelium, stromal invasion, and psammoma bodies is diagnostic of an ovarian serous carcinoma. Malignant serous tumors (both high and low grade) account for the majority of malignant tumors of the ovary. Low grade serous carcinomas tend to have simpler branching patterns. High grade serous carcinoma, as in this case, shows complex branching patterns and destructive invasion of underlying stroma.

3
How does the pathogenesis differ between low-grade and high-grade serous ovarian carcinoma?
Show answer

Low-grade serous carcinomas arise from benign or borderline lesions and progress slowly in a stepwise manner to become invasive carcinoma. They are associated with somatic mutations in genes encoding signaling proteins, such as KRAS, a member of the RAS gene family. Many high-grade serous carcinomas appear to arise in the fimbriated end of the fallopian tube from a precursor lesion referred to as serous tubal intraepithelial carcinoma. TP53 mutations are virtually ubiquitous in high-grade serous cancers and precursor lesions in the fallopian tube, being present in more than 95% of cases. Other frequently mutated genes include the tumor suppressors NF1 and RB, as well as BRCA1 and BRCA2 in familial ovarian cancers.


STIC, serous tubal intraepithelial carcinoma.

4
Why is ovarian carcinoma often discovered at an advanced stage?
Show answer

The relatively deep location of the ovaries and the large size of the peritoneal cavity allow tumors to grow and spread without causing symptoms until late in their course. Patients with early stage disease have a more favorable prognosis: 5-year survival for patients with high grade serous carcinoma limited to the ovary is 70%, but this drops to 25% for patients (like this one) with peritoneal spread.

5
What are the three clinical categories of ovarian epithelial neoplasms?
Show answer

Ovarian epithelial neoplasms are categorized as benign, borderline, and malignant. Benign lesions usually are cystic (cystadenoma) and may have an accompanying stromal component (cystadenofibroma). Malignant tumors also may be cystic (cystadenocarcinoma) or solid (carcinoma). Some ovarian epithelial tumors fall into an intermediate category currently referred to as borderline tumors. Although the majority of borderline tumors behave in a benign manner, they can recur and some progress to carcinoma.

6
How do serous cystadenomas differ from serous carcinomas?
Show answer

Ovarian serous cystadenomas are typically unilocular and have a smooth inner surface. Occasionally, serous cystadenomas may have several locules. The friable papillae seen in serous cystadenocarcinomas or serous tumors of low malignant potential are not present. Instead, the cyst is lined by bland epithelial cells that may be ciliated, similar to fallopian tube epithelium. These tumors are benign and are typically cured by simple excision.

7
What is a borderline serous tumor?
Show answer

The category “borderline” (also known as low malignant potential) refers to tumors that are neither clearly malignant nor clearly benign. Both serous and mucinous borderline lesions exist. They exhibit less cytologic atypia than carcinomas and typically lack stromal invasion. They have complex papillary structures covered by epithelium with nuclear atypia, stratification, and mitotic activity. They may seed the peritoneum, but the tumor implants are usually limited to the surface and do not invade.

8
What is the prognosis for a completely excised ovarian serous borderline tumor that is confined to the ovary?
Show answer

The prognosis is excellent; 100% are cured.

9
How does a typical mucinous cystadenoma differ in gross appearance from a typical serous cystadenoma?
Show answer

Serous cystadenomas are typically unilocular, whereas mucinous cystadenomas are typically multilocular. In mucinous tumors, the fluid within the cyst is sticky and gelatinous due to the presence of abundant glycoproteins, in contrast to the watery fluid of serous cystadenomas.

10
How do mucinous carcinomas differ from serous carcinomas?
Show answer

Primary mucinous carcinomas are uncommon (approximately 3% of all ovarian cancers) and, unlike serous carcinomas, are rarely bilateral (<5% vs. 65%). The surface of the ovary is very infrequently involved in mucinous tumors, in contrast to serous carcinomas. Mutation of the KRAS oncogene is a consistent genetic alteration seen in all types of mucinous ovarian neoplasms (i.e., cystadenomas, borderline tumors and mucinous carcinomas), whereas KRAS alterations are seen primarily in low-grade serous carcinomas, not in high-grade serous carcinomas.

11
What should be considered in the differential diagnosis of a bilateral mucinous tumor?
Show answer

Metastatic tumor to the ovary should always be excluded in cases of bilateral mucinous carcinoma of the ovary. A gastrointestinal carcinoma metastatic to the ovary is called a Krukenberg tumor. Krukenberg tumors are typically bilateral and composed of mucin-producing, signet-ring cancer cells, most often of gastric origin.

12
Which other tumors commonly metastasize to the ovaries?
Show answer

Endometrial carcinoma, breast carcinoma, and other gastrointestinal carcinomas commonly metastasize to the ovaries.

13
Which type of epithelial ovarian tumor is commonly associated with endometriosis?
Show answer

About 15% to 20% of cases of endometrioid carcinoma coexist with endometriosis. They are distinguished from serous and mucinous tumors by the presence of tubular glands resembling benign or malignant endometrium. They typically present with solid and cystic areas of growth. Forty percent are bilateral.

14
In addition to mucinous and serous tumors, which other ovarian tumor is usually cystic?
Show answer

Mature teratoma, a type of germ cell tumor, is usually cystic. Instead of serous or mucinous fluid, however, the cystic space is typically filled with hair and sebaceous material that is shed from an epidermal lining complete with skin appendages, that is found in most mature teratomas. Mature teratomas are characterized by the presence of mature tissues derived from all three germ cell layers: ectoderm, endoderm, and mesoderm. In addition to hair and skin, bone and teeth are commonly seen, as well as any other type of mature tissue (e.g., neural tissue resembling brain). Mature teratomas usually arise in the first few decades of life.

15
What is an immature teratoma? How does it differ from a mature teratoma?
Show answer

Approximately 10% of teratomas have components that resemble embryonal or immature fetal tissue. The amount of immature neuroepithelium correlates with the risk of disease spread and therefore prognosis. The tumor is found chiefly in prepubertal adolescents and young women (mean age, 18 years).

  • Immature teratoma, histology
  • Immature neuroepithelium
16
Where else in the body do teratomas arise?
Show answer

They occur in the testis and, rarely, in the mediastinum, pineal gland, and sacrococcygeal region.

17
What are some other types of germ cell tumors?
Show answer

Germ cell tumors of the ovary bear a remarkable similarity to germ cell tumors in the testis and arise in a similar manner. In addition to mature and immature teratomas, germ cell tumors of the ovary include dysgerminoma (analogous to seminoma in the testis), embryonal carcinoma, yolk sac tumor, and non-gestational choriocarcinoma. As in the testis, ovarian germ cell tumors are often associated with isochromosome 12q.

18
What are sex-cord stromal tumors?
Show answer

Sex-cord stromal tumors are derived from the ovarian stroma, which in turn is derived from the sex cords of the embryonic gonad. The undifferentiated gonadal mesenchyme eventually produces specific types of cells in both male (Sertoli and Leydig) and female (granulosa and theca) gonads, and tumors resembling all of these cell types can be identified in the ovary. Moreover, because some of these cells normally secrete estrogens (granulosa and theca cells) or androgens (Leydig cells), their corresponding tumors may be either feminizing (granulosa/theca cell tumors) or masculinizing (Leydig cell tumors).

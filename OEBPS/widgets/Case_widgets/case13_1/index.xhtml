It’s Not Easy Being Yellow

Objectives
  1. • Discuss the major similarities and differences in the diseases caused by the various hepatitis viruses.
  2. • Discuss the role of the immune response in causing acute liver damage in viral hepatitis.
  3. • Discuss the use of serologic tests in differentiating between the various forms of viral hepatitis and in determining the state of the disease (i.e., asymptomatic and symptomatic acute hepatitis and carrier and chronic states of infection).
  4. • Describe the pathology of acute hepatitis and chronic hepatitis.

A 59-year-old man presented to the local clinic with a one-week history of anorexia and nausea. Although he had at first attributed his symptoms to the flu, during the last two to three days, he developed vomiting and was unable to keep down food or liquids, resulting in a 8-pound weight loss. His social history was significant for multiple sexual partners, social alcohol use and a remote history of occasional intravenous drug use.

Physical examination revealed a thin man who looked ill. His blood pressure was 110/80 mm Hg lying down and decreased to 90/60 mm Hg upon sitting. His pulse changed from 84 bpm lying down to 108 bpm when sitting. His temperature was 37.5° C, and his respiratory rate was 20 breaths/min. He was icteric. There was mild right upper quadrant abdominal tenderness. The liver percussed to 3 cm below the costal margin with a total span of 13 cm (normal 10.5 cm). The remainder of his physical examination was unremarkable.

The patient was admitted to the hospital for volume repletion and antiemetics.

1
What do the patient’s blood pressure and heart rate reveal about his condition?
Show answer

The patient’s orthostatic hypotension and positional increase in heart rate are indicative of hypovolemia, which is to be expected considering three days of vomiting.

2
In addition to standard admission tests (i.e., basic metabolic panel, complete blood count with differential), which laboratory tests should be ordered?
Show answer

In this clinical setting (icterus and right upper quadrant abdominal pain), hepatitis should be excluded by liver function tests, including alanine aminotransferase (ALT), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), and alkaline phosphatase, all of which are elevated in patients with hepatocyte damage, and serum bilirubin, which is elevated in cholestasis, a frequent consequence of hepatocyte injury.

Based on these studies, which additional tests should be ordered?
Show answer

The patient’s lab results indicate acute hepatitis (elevated AST, ALT, alkaline phosphatase, and total and direct bilirubin). The AST:ALT ratio is less than 2, which makes alcoholic hepatitis less likely. The most common causes of acute hepatitis include drugs and toxins (including alcohol), autoimmune disease, and viral infection. Therefore, a screen for hepatitis viruses is warranted. Although autoimmune hepatitis is a consideration, this disease is uncommon in men and, in view of the clinical history, tests for autoantibodies would be low yield. However, if other studies were negative, tests for anti-nuclear, anti–smooth muscle actin, anti-mitochondrial, and anti-soluble liver antigen/liver-pancreas antigen would be warranted.

4
What is this patient’s diagnosis?
Show answer

The increased titers of antibodies against the HBV core antigen (HBc) and the positive result for HBV surface antigen (HBs, the viral envelope glycoprotein) are diagnostic for hepatitis B virus (HBV) infection. Since the HBc antibody is the IgM antibody, this is a recent infection, and the positive HBs test indicates that the infection is active. Therefore, the diagnosis is recent hepatitis B infection. In addition, the positive test for anti-HAV antibody IgG and negative test for IgM is consistent with past infection or vaccination. Serologic tests for hepatitis B antigens are helpful for defining the stage of infection and the course (see figure below):

•  HBsAg appears before the onset of symptoms, peaks during symptomatic disease, and then usually declines to undetectable levels in 12 weeks (although it may occasionally persist as long as 24 weeks).

•  Anti-HBs antibody appears after the acute disease is over and usually is not detected until a few weeks to several months after HBsAg disappears. Anti-HBs may persist for life and confers protection.

•  IgM anti-HBc becomes detectable in serum shortly before the onset of symptoms, concurrent with the onset of elevated serum aminotransferase levels (indicative of hepatocyte destruction). Over a period of months, the IgM anti-HBc antibody is replaced by IgG anti-HBc.

If a biopsy were performed, which is not standard practice, which histologic features would be seen?
Show answer

The morphologic changes in acute and chronic viral hepatitis are shared among the hepatotropic viruses and can be mimicked by drug reactions or autoimmune hepatitis; both apoptosis and necrosis can be seen. Characteristic findings in acute hepatitis include absent to marked portal inflammation that may obscure the liver architecture and scattered hepatocyte injury (“spotty necrosis” or lobular hepatitis). Regenerating hepatocytes are large and frequently contain multiple nuclei.

6
What are the differences between routes of transmission of hepatitis B (HBV) and hepatitis C (HCV) viruses and hepatitis A virus (HAV)?
Show answer

Hepatitis A is an RNA virus that is spread through a fecal-oral route, because the virus is shed predominantly in the stool. Cases of HAV are commonly seen as sporadic outbreaks in restaurants where a food-handler has a subclinical or early infection, or in other close-contact settings. In less developed countries, where sanitation and poor hygiene are problems, HAV is usually contracted as a childhood disease, and most adults show serologic evidence of prior exposure. By contrast, HBV, which is a DNA virus, is spread parenterally and through intimate contact, whereby the virus is transferred to mucosal or cutaneous surfaces, as may occur during sexual activities. HBV in the United States is mainly confined to specific populations, including intravenous drug users, medical personnel, and individuals who have received multiple blood products. It is endemic in areas such as Africa and Southeast Asia where neonatal infections are common due to vertical transmission from an infected mother to her fetus. HCV, which is an RNA virus, is a major cause of liver disease worldwide. HCV is primarily spread parenterally (e.g., intravenous drug use or, much less commonly, in health care settings). It is only rarely spread through contact-associated mechanisms such as during sexual intercourse and it is even less frequently spread through vertical transmission from infected mothers to their fetuses.

7
What are the potential clinical outcomes of acute infection with hepatitis B virus? How does this differ from infections with hepatitis A and hepatitis C viruses?
Show answer

Most patients with hepatitis B have either subclinical or minimally symptomatic acute infections, from which they recover completely. Very few become carriers. Approximately 5% of infected adults develop chronic hepatitis. A higher percentage of acutely infected neonates and small children go on to develop carrier states/chronic hepatitis B infections. About one third of patients with chronic hepatitis B infections eventually develop cirrhosis. Patients with hepatitis C infections have a much greater chance of developing chronic hepatitis (greater than 50% of infected adults), and of these individuals, a significant percentage go on to develop cirrhosis. Patients with hepatitis A viral infections never develop a carrier or chronic state after their acute illness.

8
What do the histologic features in this biopsy suggest in terms of liver damage and the natural history of the patient's disease?
Show answer

There is chronic inflammation limited to the portal area with no extension into the adjacent lobule without active liver injury (i.e., interface hepatitis [piecemeal necrosis] or fibrosis). Therefore, this patient has chronic hepatitis without significant chronic liver injury. Patients with chronic viral hepatitis may completely recover at some point, or they may have chronic hepatitis indefinitely. Of the latter group, some will go on to develop cirrhosis. This is more common in patients with chronic hepatitis due to hepatitis C virus than in patients with hepatitis due to hepatitis B virus. In hepatitis C infection, prominent lymphoid aggregates or fully formed lymphoid follicles in the portal tracts are common, which is not seen here.

Objectives
  1. • Describe the clinical presentation and morphologic findings of renal cell carcinoma.
  2. • Compare and contrast the gross and microscopic appearances of renal tumors.
  3. • Recognize the paraneoplastic syndromes associated with clear cell renal cell carcinoma.

A 63-year-old man presented to his primary care physician with a 6-month history of right flank discomfort and a 1-week history of lower extremity edema that was worse on the right. His past medical history was significant for hypertension that was well controlled by medication. He had a 40-pack-year smoking history and drank alcohol two to three times each week.

Physical exam revealed normal vital signs and left upper quadrant pain on deep palpation. Cardiovascular and respiratory exams were within normal limits. Urine dipstick revealed 3+ blood.

Laboratory results showed the following:

Electrolytes normal
Hct 57% (41%–53%)
Hb 19 g/dL (13.5–17.5 g/dL)
BUN 12 mg/dL (7–18 mg/dL)
Creatinine 0.7 mg/dL (0.6–1.2 mg/dL)
WBC 7,450/mm3 (4500–11,000/ mm3)
1
What are the potential causes of this patient’s increased hematocrit and hemoglobin?
Show answer

Erythrocytosis (increased hematocrit) and increased hemoglobin concentration can be seen in polycythemia vera and in the setting of increased erythropoietin secretion. Polycythemia vera is a myeloproliferative neoplasm associated with acquired mutations in JAK2, a gene that encodes a signaling molecule that lies downstream of multiple hematopoietic growth factor receptors, including the erythropoietin receptor. The JAK2 mutations seen in polycythemia vera are activating and lead to constitutive signaling and increased production of red cells, white cells, and platelets, whereas only increased numbers of red cells are seen here. “Pure” polycythemia may also be seen if there is an increase in erythropoietin from exogenous (e.g., in athletes who are “blood doping”) or endogenous sources (e.g., an erythropoietin-producing malignancy).

A computed tomography (CT) scan revealed a 5 cm mass in the upper pole of the left kidney. A nephrectomy was performed. There was a lobulated mass in the upper pole of the kidney and tumor in the dilated, thrombosed renal vein.

Based on the gross appearance, what is the most likely diagnosis?
Show answer

The yellow, hemorrhagic appearance is very suggestive of the clear cell variant of renal cell carcinoma. The yellow color is due to the accumulation of lipid within the tumor cells. Other tumors of the kidney include papillary renal cell carcinoma, which is frequently multifocal and hemorrhagic; chromophobe renal cell carcinoma, typically tan to light brown; and Wilms tumor, a malignant tumor of children, usually large and tan-gray with foci of hemorrhage, necrosis, or cysts.

3
What is the typical presentation for renal cell carcinoma?
Show answer

The classic triad of presenting symptoms is (1) costovertebral angle pain; (2) palpable mass; and (3) hematuria. These symptoms are seen together in only 10% of patients, however. The most reliable symptom is hematuria, which is seen in 90% of patients. Because of the widespread use of imaging studies for unrelated conditions, small tumors may be detected incidentally before they become symptomatic.

4
What would histologic examination of this specimen most likely show?
Show answer

The tumor cells in clear cell renal carcinoma (RCC) are rounded to polygonal and have abundant clear or granular cytoplasm that contains glycogen and lipids. The cells are typically arranged in nests separated by septa with a delicate branching vasculature. Cystic and solid areas may be seen. Foci of necrosis and hemorrhage are common. Most tumors are well differentiated with bland nuclei although bizarre nuclei and giant cells may be present.

  • RCC, histology
  • Nests of cells
  • Septa
  • Nuclei

Based on the histologic features, a diagnosis of clear cell renal cell carcinoma was rendered.

5
Which gene is most commonly mutated in this tumor?
Show answer

Loss or inactivation of both copies of the VHL gene appears to be the common underlying molecular abnormality in both sporadic and familial forms of clear cell carcinomas. The VHL protein causes the degradation of hypoxia-induced factors (HIFs), and in the absence of VHL, HIFs are stabilized. HIFs are transcription factors that contribute to carcinogenesis by stimulating the expression of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), an important angiogenic factor, as well as a number of other genes that drive tumor cell growth.

6
Which hereditary syndrome is associated with an increased incidence of this tumor?
Show answer

Although most clear cell renal cell carcinomas are sporadic, they also occur in familial forms or in association with von Hippel-Lindau (VHL) disease. VHL disease is inherited as an autosomal dominant trait and is characterized by predisposition to a variety of neoplasms, but particularly to hemangioblastomas of the cerebellum and retina. Hundreds of bilateral renal cysts and bilateral, often multiple, clear cell carcinomas develop in 40% to 60% of affected individuals.

7
What are the risk factors for developing this tumor?
Show answer

Tobacco use is the most significant risk factor. Cigarette smokers have double the incidence of renal cell carcinoma, and pipe and cigar smokers are also more susceptible. An international study has identified additional risk factors, including obesity (particularly in women); hypertension; unopposed estrogen therapy; and exposure to asbestos, petroleum products, and heavy metals. There is also an increased risk in patients with end-stage renal disease, chronic kidney disease, acquired cystic disease and tuberous sclerosis. Half to two thirds of individuals with VHL syndrome (nearly all, if they live long enough) develop renal cysts and bilateral, often multiple, renal cell carcinomas.

8
Which paraneoplastic syndromes are associated with renal cell carcinoma?
Show answer

Renal cell carcinoma can have a number of unexpected effects. Polycythemia affects 5% to 10% of affected individuals and results from production of erythropoietin by the cancer cells. Hypercalcemia can also be seen due to tumor production of parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP). Uncommonly, these tumors produce other hormone-like substances, resulting in hypertension, Cushing syndrome, or feminization or masculinization.

9
How does the clinical presentation of Wilms tumor differ from that of renal cell carcinoma?
Show answer

Wilms tumor, or nephroblastoma, is the most common primary tumor of the kidney in children, with most cases occurring in children between 2 and 5 years of age. In unilateral Wilms tumor, the sex ratio is essentially equal; however, in bilateral cases, girls are affected nearly twice as often as boys. By contrast, renal cell carcinoma typically affects patients in their sixth to seventh decades, with a 2:1 male:female ratio.

10
What is the histologic appearance of Wilms tumor?
Show answer

Microscopically, Wilms tumors are characterized by a triphasic combination of blastemal, stromal, and epithelial cell types. Sheets of small blue cells with few distinctive features characterize the blastemal component. Epithelial differentiation is usually in the form of abortive tubules or glomeruli. Stromal cells are usually fibrocytic or myxoid in nature, although skeletal muscle differentiation is not uncommon. Approximately 5% of tumors reveal anaplasia, defined as the presence of cells with large, hyperchromatic, pleomorphic nuclei and abnormal mitoses. The presence of anaplasia correlates with the presence of TP53 mutations and the emergence of resistance to chemotherapy.

  • Epithelial differentiation
  • Small blue cells
  • Stromal component

What is the pathogenesis of Wilms tumor?
Show answer

Wilms tumor arises from persistent mesenchymal rests that fail to differentiate into tubules and glomeruli, a manifestation of aberrant renal development. About 10% of cases are associated with rare congenital syndromes, whose genetic basis has provided important insights into the pathogenesis of the tumor. Of patients with the WAGR (Wilms tumor, aniridia, genital abnormalities, and mental retardation) syndrome, about a third develop the tumor. The gene that is mutated in this disease, called WT1, encodes a nuclear protein that regulates the expression of genes that are required for renal and gonadal development. The genetic abnormalities in sporadic Wilms tumors are much less well understood. They include gain of function mutations in the β-catenin genes and several genes involved in micro-RNA processing. One effect of such mutations is impairment of mesenchymal to epithelial transformation during renal morphogenesis, with persistence of nephrogenic rests that undergo malignant transformation with accumulation of other mutations, including in the TP53  tumor suppressor gene. Such nephrogenic rests can be found in up to 40% of Wilms tumors adjacent to tumor cells.

Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast nephritic syndrome and nephrotic syndrome.
  2. • Describe the formation of immune complexes in the glomerulus and how they may cause disease.
  3. • Describe the distinctive features of the major primary renal diseases that cause nephrotic syndrome in adults.

A previously healthy, 35-year-old man presented to his primary care physician with a 2-week history of fatigue. During the last few days, he also noticed swelling of his feet and ankles and puffiness around his eyes. Physical examination revealed an afebrile man with lower extremity pitting edema extending to the knee and periorbital edema. Blood pressure was 112/76 mm Hg.

Laboratory results were:

Urinalysis: protein 4+
glucose neg
blood neg
bilirubin neg

Creatinine 0.8 mg/dL (0.6–1.2 mg/dL)
BUN 18 mg/dL (7–18 mg/dL)
Albumin 2.8 g/dL (3.5–5.5 g/dL)
Hematocrit 40% (41%–53%)
Liver function tests normal
Triglycerides 400 mg/dL (35–160 mg/dL)
Cholesterol 375 mg/dL (<200 mg/dL)
24-hr urine protein 11.2 g/24 hr (<0.15 g/24 hr)

Drug history was negative. Antinuclear antibodies (ANA), HIV, and hepatitis serologies were negative.

1
Based on the physical examination and the urinalysis results, does this patient have nephrotic syndrome or nephritic syndrome?
Show answer

This patient shows all four of the features of nephrotic syndrome: (1) proteinuria with a daily protein loss in the urine of 3.5 g or more in adults; (2) hypoalbuminemia with plasma levels less than 3 g/dL; (3) generalized edema; and (4) hyperlipidemia and lipiduria. By contrast, nephritic syndrome is characterized by (1) hematuria (RBCs and RBC casts in urine); (2) proteinuria (usually mild, in the subnephrotic range) with or without edema; (3) azotemia; and (4) hypertension.

A renal biopsy was performed to determine the cause of his symptoms. A silver stain, which highlights the glomerular basement membrane, clearly demonstrated uniform diffuse thickening. The capillary lumens were open (no inflammation), and the mesangial areas and cellularity were normal.

Immunofluorescent staining for IgG and C3 and electron microscopic evaluation were performed. Immunofluorescent staining (IF) for IgG revealed the granular pattern associated with immune complex deposition.

Electron microscopy showed electron-dense deposits (“spikes”) located in the outer aspect of the glomerular basement membrane (GBM) under the visceral epithelium of the Bowman capsule (subepithelial deposits).

Based on the results of light microscopy, immunofluorescence, and electron microscopy of the renal biopsy specimen, what is the patient's diagnosis?
Show answer

By light microscopy, the silver stain shows uniformly thickened GBM in the absence of increased cellularity while electron microscopy demonstrates subepithelial deposits of electron-dense, immune complex–type material. These features, combined with the granular, peripheral capillary loop immunofluorescent staining with IgG and C3, are diagnostic of membranous nephropathy.

3
How does this disease differ from the other major causes of nephrotic syndrome in adults?
Show answer

The most common causes of nephrotic syndrome in adults are (1) membranous nephropathy; (2) focal segmental glomerulosclerosis; (3) membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis; and (4) systemic diseases such as diabetes and amyloidosis. Membranous nephropathy is characterized by immune complex deposition in the GBM, resulting in a thickened GBM but without inflammation.

Primary focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS) is the most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in adults in the United States. FSGS may be primary (idiopathic) or occur in association with HIV infection, heroin abuse, or IgA nephropathy. Morphologically, there is focal glomerular sclerosis (i.e., normal and abnormal glomeruli are present) that affects only parts of the capillary tufts (i.e., the lesion is segmental). There is diffuse effacement of foot processes. FSGS is not an immune complex disease. The permeability defect in FSGS is likely a result of podocyte injury, the mechanism of which is unknown.

Membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis (MPGN) is best considered a pattern of immune-mediated injury rather than a specific disease. The glomeruli in MPGN are large and hypercellular due to a proliferation of mesangial and endothelial cells and infiltration of neutrophils and monocytes into the glomerulus. PAS and silver stains demonstrate the “tram-track” appearance caused by intercalation of mesangial cell processes into the peripheral capillary loops. Immunofluorescent staining reveals granular deposits of IgG and C3 in the subendothelial region of the GBM and prominently in the mesangium. MPGN may be primary or can occur in association with systemic lupus erythematosus or hepatitis B or C infection.

Nephrotic syndrome is also seen in systemic diseases that involve the kidney, notably systemic lupus erythematosus and diabetes, as well as amyloidosis from any cause.

How are immune complexes formed in the glomerulus?
Show answer

Immune complexes may form and deposit in the GBM by two different mechanisms: (1) they are formed in the blood against antigens in the circulation and deposit in the GBM due to the structure of the glomerular capillary wall, the high pressure flow, and the charge of the GBM, all of which favor deposition of antibodies and immune complexes; and (2) antibodies react in situ within the glomerulus, either with fixed (intrinsic) glomerular antigens or with molecules planted within the glomerulus. These pathways are not mutually exclusive, and all may contribute to injury. Experts believe that the pathway of in situ complex formation is more common in human diseases, but it is difficult to definitively exclude the presence of circulating complexes in any patient. Many different antigens are thought to be “planted” in the GBM, including nucleoproteins and viral proteins, but these may also be present in the circulation. In membranous nephropathy, there is evidence that complexes are formed in situ, without a need for circulating immune complexes. One known antigen is the phospholipase A2 receptor (PLA2R), which is actually expressed in the membrane of podocytes. It has been postulated that complexes first form in the podocyte membrane and are then shed and deposited in the subepithelial layer of the GBM.


5
How does immune complex deposition disrupt glomerular permeability?
Show answer

Regardless of how complexes form in the GBM, they disrupt glomerular permeability in at least two ways: (1) they activate complement and recruit leukocytes, and the products of these inflammatory cells damage the GBM (as in proliferative lesions in poststreptococcal glomerulonephritis and systemic lupus erythematosus); and (2) they may cause a mechanical disruption of the GBM barrier in the absence of inflammation (as in membranous nephropathy). It is also suspected that in some cases, there is damage to epithelial cells (podocytes), perhaps by antibodies or immune complexes, and this contributes to the permeability defect.

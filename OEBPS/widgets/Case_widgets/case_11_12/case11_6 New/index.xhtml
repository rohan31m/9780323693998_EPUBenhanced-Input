Objectives
  1. • Describe the types, genetics and pathogenesis of cystic diseases of kidney
  2. • Compare and contrast salient features of important kidney cysts
Summary of case:

A 45-year-old male patient presented to his primary care physician with a history of insidious-onset hematuria. On clinical examination, there was no abdominal tenderness. His blood pressure was 160/120 mmHg. Lab tests revealed a Hb of 12 g/dL, creatinine 4 mg/dL (0.6-1.2 mg/dL), BUN 30 mg/dL (7-18 mg/dL). Urine cytology was negative for malignant cells. Abdominal ultrasound demonstrated bilateral enlarged kidneys along with multiple cysts of varying size in both kidneys and the liver.

1
What is the likely diagnosis?
Show answer

Adult polycystic kidney disease. Autosomal dominant (adult) polycystic kidney disease (ADPKD) is a hereditary disorder characterized by multiple expanding cysts of both kidneys that ultimately destroy the renal parenchyma and cause renal failure. The disease is always bilateral. The cysts initially involve a minority of nephrons, so renal function is retained until about the fourth or fifth decades of life. The patient is in renal failure as evidenced by elevated BUN and creatinine.

2
What are the renal cystic diseases?
Show answer

Renal cystic diseases are heterogenous, encompassing a range of hereditary, developmental and acquired disorders. Common renal cystic diseases include autosomal dominant (adult) polycystic kidney disease, autosomal recessive (childhood) polycystic kidney disease, medullary sponge kidney, nephronophthisis, multicystic renal dysplasia, acquired cystic disease and simple cysts.

3
What is the genetics and pathogenesis of ADPKD?
Show answer

Adult polycystic kidney diseases are mostly caused by mutation in one of two genes, PKD1 or PKD2. Mutation of the PKD1 gene accounts for about 85% of cases. PKD1 gene encodes a membrane protein, named polycystin-1 that is expressed in tubular epithelial cells, mainly distal nephrons. Its precise function is not known, but it contains domains that are usually involved in cell-cell and cell-matrix interactions. Remaining cases are due to mutations of the PKD2 gene. PKD2 encodes polycystin-2, a membrane protein expressed in all segments of renal tubules and in many extrarenal tissues. Polycystin-2 functions as a Ca2+-permeable cation channel.

The pathogenesis of ADPKD is yet to be elucidated, but the currently favored hypothesis places the cilia-centrosome complex of tubular epithelial cells at the center of the disorder. Polycystin-1 and polycystin-2 localize in the kidney tubules to apical cilia, structures that sense fluid flow and sheer stress. In polycystic kidney disease (PKD), normal calcium signaling is profoundly disrupted by mutations in the PKD1 or PKD2 genes, which encode the proteins polycystin-1 (PC1) and polycystin-2 (PC2). This dysregulation of calcium flux is a primary event in the formation and growth of kidney cysts. Ca2+-induced signals also alter the interaction of epithelial cells with ECM, and this too is thought to contribute to the cyst formation and interstitial fibrosis that are characteristic of progressive polycystic kidney disease.

4
What are the clinical features of ADPKD?
Show answer

Patients are initially asymptomatic until about 4th to 5th decades of life. Progressive dilation and hemorrhage in the cysts may produce pain. The disease occasionally begins with insidious onset of hematuria, followed by other features of progressive chronic disease, such as proteinuria, polyuria and hypertension. Patients with polycystic kidney disease also frequently have extrarenal anomalies, such as polycystic liver diseases (40%), mitral valve prolapse (20% to 25%), and intracranial berry aneurysm arising in circle of Willis (4% to 10%). This form of chronic kidney disease is remarkable in that patients may survive for many years with azotemia.

5
How is autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease different from the ADPKD?
Show answer

Autosomal recessive (childhood) polycystic kidney disease (ARPKD) is genetically distinct from adult polycystic kidney disease. The disease is caused by mutation in the PKHD1 gene that encodes the integral membrane protein fibrocystin. Like polycystin 1 and 2, fibrocystin localizes to the primary cilium of tubular cells. The function of fibrocystin is unknown, but its structure suggests a role in collecting duct and biliary epithelium differentiation. Unlike ADPKD, serious manifestations are usually present at birth and affected infants may succumb to renal failure. Patients who survive infancy may develop a peculiar hepatic injury, termed congenital hepatic fibrosis. In older children, hepatic disease predominates and often leads to portal hypertension and splenomegaly.

6
Describe the gross and microscopic features of polycystic kidney disease?
Show answer

ADPKD: Grossly, both kidneys are enlarged with bumpy external surface composed solely of a mass of cysts, up to 3 to 4 cm in diameter. The cysts may be filled with clear serous or turbid hemorrhagic fluid. Microscopically, the cysts have variable lining epithelia from simple flattened to cuboidal. ARPKD: In gross appearance, the kidneys are enlarged with a smooth external surface. Dilated elongated channels are present at right angles to the cortical surface. On microscopic examination, the cysts have a uniform lining of cuboidal cells.

7
Describe the salient features of various cystic diseases of kidney.
Show answer

Cystic diseases of the kidney are heterogenous. They are important for several reasons: 1) They are reasonably common and often represent diagnostic problems for clinicians, radiologists, and pathologists; 2) some forms, such as adult polycystic kidney disease, are major cause of chronic kidney disease; and 3) they can occasionally be confused with malignant tumors. Renal cystic diseases can be differentiated by a number of features, such as inheritance pattern, clinicopathological characteristics, and typical outcomes. Medullary sponge kidney is characterized by multiple cystic dilations of the collecting ducts in the medulla. It usually presents with normal renal function. Nephronophthisis, a group of progressive renal disorders, is characterized by variable number of cysts in the medulla, usually concentrated at the corticomedullary junction. Mutations in sixteen different genes, NPHP1 to NPHP11 (that encode proteins called nephrocystins), JBTS2, JBTS3, JBTS9, and JBTS11, are implicated in the various forms of nephronophthisis. Renal dysplasia is a sporadic disorder that can be unilateral or bilateral and the kidney is usually enlarged, extremely irregular and multicystic.

8
What is the common cause of death in ADPKD?
Show answer

The most common cause of death in ADPKD is cardiovascular heart disease. About 40% of adult patients die of coronary or hypertensive heart disease, 25% of infection, and 15% of a ruptured berry aneurysm or hypertensive intracerebral hemorrhage.

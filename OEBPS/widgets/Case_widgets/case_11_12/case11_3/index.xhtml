Crescent Wrenched

Objectives
  1. • Describe the features of nephritic syndrome.
  2. • Discuss the features of acute renal failure.
  3. • Explain the usual clinical presentations and major histologic glomerular findings in rapidly progressive (crescentic) glomerulonephritis (RPGN).
  4. • List the diseases that may be associated with the clinicopathologic presentation of RPGN.

A 27-year-old man who was previously in good health presented to his family physician with a 1-week history of increasing fatigue and a 1-day history of red urine. Review of systems was negative. Physical examination was negative except for hypertension (165/110 mm Hg). Urinalysis revealed 2+ protein and 2+ blood; his serum creatinine was 1.8 mg/dL (0.6–1.2 mg/dL). An appointment was made with a nephrologist in two weeks’ time.

A week later, however, the patient continued to have intermittent bouts of hematuria and began to notice ankle swelling and generalized malaise, nausea and vomiting, and decreasing urine output. He presented to a hospital Emergency Department and was noted to be almost anuric. On physical examination, his blood pressure was 170/110 mm Hg. An abdominal sonogram showed kidneys of normal size with no evidence of hydronephrosis.

Laboratory examination revealed:

Urinalysis: protein 2+
blood 4+
glucose negative
Microscopy: >40 RBCs/HPF (0–2 RBCs/HPF)
10 WBCs/HPF (0–2 WBCs/HPF)
5–10 RBC casts/LPF (0 casts/LPF)
Hematocrit 38% (41%–53%)
Creatinine 3.9 mg/dL (0.6–1.2 mg/dL)
BUN 102 mg/dL (7–18 mg/dL)
Liver serology normal
ANCA, ANA, HIV negative
1
With which renal syndrome did this patient present? What is the most common cause of this presentation?
Show answer

This patient presented initially with symptoms associated with acute nephritic syndrome: (1) hypertension; (2) hematuria; (3) oliguria; and (4) evidence of renal failure (i.e., increased serum creatinine). The most common cause of acute nephritic syndrome worldwide is poststreptococcal (postinfectious) glomerulonephritis. Other causes include various forms of rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RPGN). The presence of RBC casts is pathognomonic of glomerular disease: when RBCs leak from damaged glomeruli, fluid is resorbed in tubules, and casts are formed.

This patient also is showing features of acute renal failure: oliguria/anuria (by history and observation), uremia/azotemia (by laboratory tests), nausea and vomiting (by history), fluid retention or volume depletion (by history and physical examination), and ankle edema and hypertension (by physical examination).

The patient was admitted to the intensive care unit with acute renal failure and fluid retention. A renal biopsy was performed. Two glomeruli are seen in this biopsy specimen. The one on the right is essentially normal; however, the glomerulus on the left shows a crescent that distorts and compresses the glomerulus. Crescents are accumulations of proliferating glomerular epithelial cells (predominately parietal but including some visceral cells) and infiltrating leukocytes. Crescent formation occurs following an immune/inflammatory injury involving the capillary walls. Plasma proteins leak into the urinary space, where it is believed that exposure to procoagulants such as tissue factor leads to fibrin deposition.

A silver stain to visualize the GBM highlights a normal tuft away from the crescent. The tuft closer to the glomerulus has collapsed and is no longer functional. Crescent formation indicates a severe injury that can lead to collapse of the entire glomerulus.

What does the presence of crescents reveal about the pathogenesis of this patient’s disease?
Show answer

The formation of crescents is a result of severe glomerular injury, but it tells little about the pathogenesis of the injury. Immunofluorescence (IF), electron microscopy (EM), and serologic tests are required to determine the cause of injury.

Additional studies, including immunofluorescence (IF) and electron microscopy (EM), were performed. An immunofluorescent stain for IgG showed a smooth, ribbon-like linear deposition of IgG in the GBM. Compare this to the immunofluorescent stain for Case 11.2.

3
What would the EM show? Will there be immune complexes in the basement membrane?
Show answer

Electron microscopy revealed no electron-dense immune complex deposits. Rupture of the glomerular basement membrane (GBM) was noted, indicating marked glomerular damage.

4
What is the pathological diagnosis?
Show answer

This is an example of rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RPGN), also called crescentic glomerulonephritis. RPGN is a clinical syndrome and crescentic GN refers to the pattern of glomerular pathology; neither denotes a specific disease entity.

5
What are the causes of this type of lesion and how can they be distinguished?
Show answer

There are three types of RPGN, which can be distinguished by IF staining patterns and serologic testing for antibodies (ANCA). Each of the three patterns results from specific diseases, all of which cause severe glomerular injury. Type I RPGN shows a linear pattern of IgG and C3 deposition, and is an autoimmune disease caused by anti-GBM antibodies. These antibodies are most often reactive with type IV collagen, a component of the GBM. Type II RPGN shows a granular pattern of IgG and C3 deposition, reflecting immune complex deposition. It may be associated with systemic lupus erythematosus or IgA nephropathy. Type III RPGN shows no immune deposits (hence the designation pauci-immune). It may be a renal manifestation of a systemic vasculitis, such as the vasculitis caused by antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA).

6
Based on the light microscopic and IF findings, what is the appropriate treatment for this patient?
Show answer

The IF-staining pattern in this patient indicates anti-GBM antibody–mediated disease. Plasmapheresis can reduce the level of these circulating antibodies and is therefore the treatment of choice. Plasmapheresis is not effective in treating types II and III RPGN, which tend to respond well to steroids and cytotoxic agents.

Based on the light microscopic and immunofluorescence findings, plasmapheresis was begun. Over the ensuing 2 weeks, the patient's creatinine decreased to 1.7 mg/dL, with a reduction in peripheral edema and resolution of hematuria.

7
If this patient were to develop lung hemorrhage, what would be the likely diagnosis?
Show answer

The combination of renal dysfunction and pulmonary hemorrhage is seen in Goodpasture syndrome. In this disease, the anti-GBM antibodies cross-react with basement membranes in the lung alveoli, resulting in simultaneous lung and renal lesions.

Quit Bugging Me

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Describe the pathogenesis, clinical presentation, and complications of chronic peptic ulcer disease.
  2. • Compare and contrast chronic peptic ulcers with acute stress ulcers of the stomach.
  3. • Describe the differences between chronic peptic ulcer and gastric carcinoma in terms of clinical presentation, morphology, and outcomes.

A 45-year-old man presented to his primary care physician with a 6-month history of intermittent upper abdominal pain that was worse shortly after meals. Antacids did not help his symptoms. He also said that he had been feeling fatigued and was easily tired. He reported that he drank socially and had never smoked.

On physical examination, the patient was in no distress and appeared fit. His conjunctivae appeared slightly pale. His blood pressure was 96/72 mm Hg, pulse was 90 beats/min, respiratory rate was 18 breaths/min, and temperature was 98.9℉. Pulmonary and cardiac examinations were unremarkable. The abdominal examination revealed mild epigastric pain on palpation. A test for fecal occult blood was positive. On admission, the following laboratory results were noted:

TEST RESULT
Hemoglobin 11.0 g/dL (12.0–16.0 g/dL)
Hematocrit 32% (41%–53%)
MCV 65 µm3 (80–100 µm3)
WBC 11,900/mm3 (4,500–11000 mm3)

An electrocardiogram (ECG) was normal; likewise, chest x-ray and x-rays of kidneys, ureters, and bladder (KUB film) were unremarkable.

1
What is the differential diagnosis and what tests should be ordered?
Show answer

The presence of fecal occult blood and documented microcytic anemia are consistent with acute and chronic gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding. Although the postprandial epigastric pain is suggestive of a peptic ulcer, a colorectal source must be excluded, so upper and lower endoscopy should be performed. Gastric carcinoma is also in the differential diagnosis.

A colonoscopy was negative, and esophagogastroduodenoscopy revealed a 2-cm gastric ulcer in the antrum along the lesser curvature. The lesion had a “punched out” appearance and there was fibrinoid debris in the smooth ulcer base. Biopsy specimens were taken of the ulcer and surrounding mucosa.

2
What are the gross and microscopic features of peptic ulcers?
Show answer

Peptic ulcers arise in the context of chronic gastritis but are most common in the proximal duodenum. Gastric peptic ulcers are predominantly located along the lesser curvature near the interface of the body and the antrum. Peptic ulcers are usually solitary, round to oval, sharply punched-out defects. The mucosal margin may overhang the base slightly but is usually level with the surrounding mucosa; by contrast, heaped-up margins are more characteristic of cancers. The base of peptic ulcers is smooth and clean. Active ulcers may be lined by a thin layer of fibrinous debris with a subjacent predominantly neutrophilic inflammatory infiltrate. Beneath this, granulation tissue infiltrated by mononuclear leukocytes and a fibrous scar form the ulcer base. The glands adjacent to the ulcer often show a neutrophilic infiltrate and associated reactive changes.

3
What is a likely etiologic agent for gastritis and peptic ulcer?
Show answer

Helicobacter pylori plays an important role in the pathogenesis of chronic gastric ulcers. The spiral-shaped organisms can be seen adherent to the mucosal surface using Warthin-Starry silver stain, Giemsa staining and by performing immunohistochemistry with antibodies specific for H. pylori. H. pylori infection of gastric mucosa is present in 100% of patients with duodenal ulcer and 70% of patients with gastric ulcer. The eradication of H. pylori decreases the risk of recurrence.

  • Biopsy, silver stain
  • Helicobacter pylori
4
Which region of the stomach is most commonly affected by H. pylori?
Show answer

In most cases, H. pylori gastritis is limited to the antrum; however, rarely the gastric body and fundus can become involved.

5
What is the relationship between chronic gastritis, H. pylori, and chronic peptic ulcer?
Show answer

Chronic infection, especially with H. pylori, is the most common cause of chronic gastritis. Patients secrete normal or excess acid and have a low intragastric pH. H. pylori is also associated with gastric and duodenal ulcer disease. H. pylori induces an intense inflammatory and immune response and increased production of proinflammatory cytokines. It secretes a urease that breaks down urea to form toxic compounds such as ammonium chloride and monochloramine. The chronically inflamed mucosa is more susceptible to chemical injury by acid and peptic ulceration.

6
Which malignancies are associated with H. pylori gastritis?
Show answer

Patients with H. pylori gastritis are at increased risk for both intestinal-type adenocarcinoma and extranodal marginal zone lymphoma, a low-grade tumor also known as mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT)oma. The former is more common when there is extension of the gastritis to the gastric body and fundus, resulting in intestinal metaplasia, which increases the risk of gastric adenocarcinoma. Chronic inflammation of the mucosa is important in the pathogenesis of both gastric adenocarcinoma and MALToma. Chronic H. pylori gastritis is characterized by lymphoid aggregates with germinal centers and abundant subepithelial plasma cells within the superficial lamina propria.

  • Chronic H. pylori gastritis
  • Lymphoid aggregate

The patient was treated with acid-suppressive therapy and antibiotics and instructed not to take aspirin or drink alcohol.

7
What are the different types of acute gastric ulcers and how do they differ from peptic ulcers?
Show answer

Acute (stress) ulcers are multiple, small, circular mucosal defects that usually are less than 1 cm in size. The ulcer bases are stained dark brown by digestion of extravasated red cells and denaturation of hemoglobin by acid. Microscopically, necrosis involves the full thickness of the mucosa. Healing with complete re-epithelialization occurs with minimal fibrosis. Chronic peptic ulcers typically have a smooth base due to peptic digestion of the exudate.

As described earlier, peptic ulcers are typically single, deep, and have a base of granulation tissue and associated fibrosis. Acute ulcers are most commonly encountered in patients with shock, burns, sepsis, intracranial conditions, or aspirin use; they are not associated with H. pylori infection. In the hospital setting, 1% to 4% of patients admitted to intensive care units acutely develop superficial gastric erosion or ulcers. Although these ulcers are small and superficial, they nevertheless can cause severe upper GI bleeding.

  • Acute ulcers
  • Stomach, acute gastric ulcer
  • Normal musclaris mucosae
  • Necrosis

What are the two major forms of gastric adenocarcinomas and how is a peptic ulcer distinguished from a gastric adenocarcinoma?
Show answer

Gastric adenocarcinomas are classified as intestinal-type or diffuse. Intestinal-type adenocarcinomas tend to form bulky exophytic and sometimes ulcerated tumors composed of malignant glandular structures. In contrast to benign gastric ulcers, intestinal-type gastric adenocarcinomas typically have irregular, heaped up borders around areas of ulceration. These features are sufficiently different that, in most cases, these two entities can be distinguished radiologically. However, the diagnosis must be confirmed by endoscopy and multiple biopsy specimens.

In contrast to the intestinal type, diffuse adenocarcinomas have an infiltrative growth pattern and generally are composed of discohesive signet-ring cells. A mass may be difficult to appreciate; however, these infiltrative tumors provoke a desmoplastic response that stiffens the gastric wall. When there are large areas of infiltration, widespread rugal flattening and a rigid thickened wall may impart a leather bottle appearance termed linitis plastica.

  • Desmoplasia
  • Malignant glands
  • Intestinal-type, gross

  • Diffuse-type, histology
  • Signet ring cells
  • Diffuse-type, gross
  • Thickened gastic wall
  • Loss of rugal folds

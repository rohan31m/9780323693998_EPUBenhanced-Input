Objectives
  • • Describe the features of the nephrotic syndrome and how it differs from nephritis.
  • • List the causes of the nephrotic syndrome in children and adults.
  • • Describe the different methods of pathologic evaluation of kidney biopsies and their value in diagnosis and understanding pathogenesis of renal diseases.

A 4-year-old boy was brought to the pediatrician by his parents with a 2-day history of facial swelling. One week earlier, he had a mild upper respiratory tract infection and since then has been fatigued. There was no history of joint pains or rash. There were no rheumatologic or kidney diseases in his family. Physical exam revealed blood pressure 136/72 mm Hg, pulse 62 beats/min and respiratory rate 20 breaths/minute. He had periorbital edema and 3+ pitting edema of the legs and mild scrotal edema. Lungs were clear, heart rate was regular with no gallops or murmurs, and there was no jugular venous distension. Urinalysis by dipstick showed 3+ proteinuria, without hematuria or RBC/WBC casts.

K 4.7 mEq/L (3.5–5.0 mEq/L)

Creatinine 0.8 mg/dL (0.6–1.2 mg/dL)
BUN 17 mg/dL (7–18 mg/dL)
Ca 9.3 mEq/L (8.4–10.2 mEq/L)
Phosphate 4.2 (3–4.5 mg/dL)
Albumin 2.5 g/dL (normal 3.5–5.5 g/dL)

24 hr urine showed 1.5 gm protein (mostly albumin) (normal <30 mg).

1
Which clinical syndrome do the presentation and laboratory findings suggest?
Show answer

This patient presented with marked proteinuria, low serum albumin, increased albumin in 24 hr urine, and generalized edema, all features of nephrotic syndrome. No RBCs are seen in this patient's urine, a characteristic finding in nephritic syndrome, which is due to inflammation (hence, nephritis) and tends to be more destructive. Patients with nephritic syndrome often present with hematuria, hypertension, and signs of acute renal failure; proteinuria may be present but is typically not a prominent feature.

2
What are the expected findings on a kidney biopsy?
Show answer

The most common causes of nephrotic syndrome in children and adolescents are minimal change disease (80% of cases) and focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS; 20%). If the patient has minimal change disease (MCD), the renal biopsy should be essentially normal by light microscopy. In focal segmental glomerulosclerosis, the lesions are focal (affecting some glomeruli and not others) and segmental (affecting some tufts in the glomerulus but not all). A biopsy of FSGS would show increased mesangial matrix, obliterated capillary lumina, deposition of hyaline (hyalinosis) and foamy (lipid-laden) macrophages.

The patient’s kidney was biopsied. The glomerulus had normal mesangial cellularity with normal open tufts. The basement membrane was of uniform thickness. No inflammation was seen. The tubules were of normal shape and size. The interstitium was also unremarkable.

3
What would electron microscopic analysis reveal?
Show answer

Effacement of foot processes is seen in most diseases that cause the nephrotic syndrome, and by itself it does not suggest a specific diagnosis. In minimal change disease, there is diffuse effacement of the visceral podocyte foot processes which are reduced to a rim of cytoplasm with loss of recognizable intervening slit diaphragms (often incorrectly called “fusion” of the foot processes). Electron microscopy revealed diffuse effacement of visceral podocyte foot processes and immunofluorescent analysis revealed no immune complex deposition or anti-glomerular basement membrane (GBM) antibodies.

4
What is this patient’s diagnosis? How does it differ from other causes of nephrotic syndrome?
Show answer

This is a case of minimal change disease, so named because of the pathological findings in renal biopsies. Selective proteinuria (mostly albumin) is also typical of this disease. Other causes of the nephrotic syndrome show abnormalities in the glomerular basement membrane and often deposits of antibodies and complement in the glomerulus (see Case 11.2 as an example).

The patient was immunosuppressed with steroids and cyclosporine. Steroids were tapered and he eventually achieved remission. Cyclosporine was discontinued.

5
What is the pathogenesis of this disease?
Show answer

The pathogenesis of minimal change disease is not known. There is no evidence of anti-GBM antibodies or immune complexes. It has been suggested that systemic immune dysfunction results in the elaboration of a cytokine that damages the epithelial cells, leading to effacement of foot processes and secondary alterations in the GBM resulting in proteinuria, but no such cytokine has been identified. The circumstantial evidence supporting an immune abnormality is the association with known immunologic disorders and therapeutic response to antiinflammatory and immunosuppressive drugs (steroids, cyclosporine).

6
What are the most common etiologies of nephrotic syndrome in children versus adults?
Show answer

In children, primary glomerular disease accounts for 95% of the cases of nephrotic syndrome, the most common cause being minimal change disease. By comparison, primary glomerular diseases are responsible for only 60% of cases in adults, and nephrotic syndrome secondary to diabetes and lupus is much more frequent in this population.

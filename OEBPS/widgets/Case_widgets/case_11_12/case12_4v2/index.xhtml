Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast the two main forms of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) with respect to pathology, clinical features, and complications.
  2. • Describe the pathogenesis of IBD.

A 27-year-old man with an 8-year history of inflammatory bowel disease presented to his gastroenterologist with an acute exacerbation of his disease. He reported more than 20 bloody bowel movements per day, diffuse abdominal pain, nausea, and vomiting. He had experienced a similar episode 3 months earlier with a 20-lb weight loss and bloody diarrhea. He stated that previous flare-ups resolved after a short course of prednisone and that, in general, his medications controlled the disease well, although he did not like the side effects. He had no family history of inflammatory bowel disease.

On physical examination, the patient was a cachectic man. His abdomen had bowel sounds and was nondistended, soft, and mildly tender. There was no hepatosplenomegaly or masses. Rectal examination revealed black stool that was positive for fecal blood, but no masses were felt. The remainder of the physical examination was unremarkable. Laboratory tests showed a hematocrit of 23.3% (41%–53%)

1
What is inflammatory bowel disease? What are the two major categories of inflammatory bowel disease?
Show answer

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) is a chronic condition resulting from complex interactions between intestinal microbiota and host immunity in genetically predisposed individuals resulting in an inappropriate mucosal immune activation. IBD encompasses two entities, Crohn disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC). The distinction between ulcerative colitis and Crohn disease is based, in large part, on the distribution of affected sites and the morphologic expression of disease at those sites.

2
What is the proposed pathogenesis of IBD?
Show answer

Evidence suggests that IBD is due to the combined effects of alterations in (1) host interactions with intestinal microbiota; (2) intestinal epithelial dysfunction; (3) aberrant mucosal immune responses; and (4) altered composition of the gut microbiome. A variety of epithelial defects have been described in CD and UC. Defects in intestinal epithelial tight junction barrier function occur in patients with Crohn disease. Defects in regulatory T cells, especially the IL-10-producing subset, are believed to underlie the inflammation, especially in Crohn disease.

The patient was admitted to the hospital and treated with steroids without symptomatic improvement. He also received 2 units of packed RBCs to improve his anemia. After 8 days of therapy, the patient was made NPO (nothing by mouth) and placed on total parenteral nutrition (TPN). Colonoscopy revealed friable mucosa with a finely granular appearance, ulcerations, and spontaneous bleeding in the distal ascending, transverse, descending and sigmoid colon and rectum. The full circumference of the colorectum was involved, and pseudopolyps were present. Areas of normal mucosa in the rectum, sigmoid, and descending colon were not seen. Furthermore, no fissures or strictures were identified.

3
How do these colonoscopic findings aid in distinguishing UC from CD?
Show answer

UC affects the colon and rectum, as in this patient, and progresses in a contiguous fashion. Ileum is not involved. By contrast, CD frequently has ileal involvement. It may involve any area of the gastrointestinal tract and areas of normal bowel may separate lesions (referred to as skip lesions). It is also known as regional enteritis. Crohn disease usually spares the rectum. Both UC and CD can have ulcerations; however, in UC, the ulcers are superficial and broad-based whereas in CD they are deep and knifelike. Pseudopolyps can be seen in both UC and CD, but are more common in UC. Based on the colonoscopic findings, UC is the more likely diagnosis in this patient.

Several biopsies were performed. The patient’s biopsy showed acute inflammation limited to the mucosa and submucosa. There was architectural distortion of crypts and crypt abscesses (clusters of neutrophils within a crypt). No granulomas were identified. Proximally, an area of normal mucosa was seen.

Do the histologic features support a diagnosis of UC or CD?
Show answer

Both UC and CD are characterized by acute inflammation with abundant neutrophils and damage to crypt epithelium. In both diseases, repeated cycles of crypt destruction and regeneration distort the mucosal architecture: the normally straight and parallel crypts take on bizarre branching shapes and unusual orientation to each other. Crypt abscesses are more common in UC. UC typically affects only the mucosa and superficial submucosa while CD involves the full thickness of the bowel wall, a finding best appreciated in resected specimens. In CD, the bowel wall is thickened due to transmural edema, inflammation, submucosal fibrosis, and hypertrophy of the muscularis propria. Microscopically, there are bowel wall thickening and mucosal and subserosal lymphoid aggregates in CD. Granulomas are also seen in about 50% of cases of CD. Based on the superficial involvement (mucosa and submucosa), absence of granulomas and presence of crypt abscesses, the histologic findings support a diagnosis of ulcerative colitis.

  • Distorted crypts
  • Lymphoid aggregate
  • Crohn disease, biopsy
  • Granulomas

In addition to the mucosal and submucosal inflammation in the patient’s biopsies, two foci of high-grade dysplasia were seen.

5
What is the significance of the focal high-grade dysplasia?
Show answer

The most serious complication of IBD is the development of colonic adenocarcinoma. Cancers are preceded by dysplasia, which tends to arise in multiple sites. The risk of developing dysplasia correlates with the duration of disease, its extent, and the nature of the inflammatory response. Risk increases sharply 8 to 10 years after disease onset, and patients with pancolitis are at greater risk than those with only left-sided disease. Greater frequency and severity of active inflammation (characterized by the presence of neutrophils) confers increased risk. To facilitate early detection of neoplasia, patients are typically enrolled in surveillance programs approximately 8 years after diagnosis of IBD.

In view of the high-grade dysplasia identified on biopsy, the patient elected to undergo total colectomy. The colonic mucosa was diffusely and continuously inflamed from the rectum to the ascending colon near the cecum (appendix visible on the left side of the image).

What would be the appearance of a colon resection from a patient with CD?
Show answer

The transmural inflammation characteristic of CD ultimately results in fibrosis and thickening of the bowel wall seen in a specimen of right colon and ileum. Aphthous ulcers often coalesce into elongated, serpentine ulcers oriented along the long axis of the bowel. Fissures frequently develop between mucosal folds and may extend deeply to become fistula tracts or sites of perforation. In cases with extensive transmural disease, mesenteric fat frequently extends around the serosal surface (creeping fat). In this example, there is a section of normal small bowel and normal large intestine (so-called “skip lesions”) separated by the diseased segment.

  • Thickened bowel wall
  • Creeping fat
  • Ileum
  • Normal colon
  • Diseased segment
7
What are the complications of Crohn disease?
Show answer

Iron-deficiency anemia may develop in individuals with colonic disease (both in UC and CD), whereas extensive small bowel disease in CD may result in protein loss (protein-losing enteropathy) and hypoalbuminemia, generalized nutrient malabsorption, or malabsorption of vitamin B12 and bile salts. Fibrosing strictures, particularly of the terminal ileum, are common and require surgical resection. Disease often recurs at the site of anastomosis, and as many as 40% of patients require additional resections within 10 years. Fistulae develop between loops of bowel and may also involve the urinary bladder, vagina, and abdominal or perianal skin. Perforations and peritoneal abscesses are common.

8
Which other organs are affected in CD and UC?
Show answer

Crohn disease and ulcerative colitis are systemic diseases, associated with various extraintestinal manifestations of immunologic origin. Both diseases are associated with uveitis, migratory polyarthritis, sacroiliitis, ankylosing spondylitis, and erythema nodosum. Pericholangitis and primary sclerosing cholangitis may occur in CD but are more common in UC.

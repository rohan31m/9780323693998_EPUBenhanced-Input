Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast the major causes of tubulointerstitial nephritis.
  2. • Describe the pathogenesis of pyelonephritis, specifically causative organisms, predisposing conditions, and outcomes.
  3. • Discuss the composition and pathogenesis of renal stones (calculi).

A 53-year-old woman presented to her physician with a 2-day history of bilateral flank pain and fever. She noted burning on urination for the past week without discoloration of the urine. She reduced her fluid intake due to painful urination. Her past history was significant for diabetes for the last 13 years, first controlled with oral medications, but now requiring daily insulin injections. In addition, she has had chronic low back pain for the last 10 years that she treated with daily nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs). She has been treated before for bladder infections. On physical examination, she is a moderately obese woman with a temperature of 41° C, blood pressure 155/95 mm Hg, pulse 84 beats/min (60–100 beats/min), and respirations 26 breaths/min (8–16 breaths/min). Her skin was warm and dry, and she had moderate bilateral costovertebral angle tenderness. No abdominal masses were palpated.

What are WBC casts and what does their presence indicate?
Show answer

The presence of white blood cells (usually mostly neutrophils) in casts indicates that acute tubular inflammation of the kidney is present. They may also be seen in acute tubulointerstitial nephritis and acute tubular necrosis. Casts form when material in the tubules becomes concentrated because of fluid resorption. They are seen in tubular diseases but not with lesions involving the lower urinary tract (ureters, bladder).

2
What is the most likely cause of the patient’s symptoms?
Show answer

This patient is febrile with an elevated WBC, both suggestive of an infection. Urinalysis revealed large numbers of WBCs (pyuria), leukocyte esterase (elevated when there are significant numbers of WBCs in the urine) and nitrite (Gram-negative bacteria convert nitrate in the urine to nitrite) as well as bacteria. The presence of WBC casts confirms that the infection is not limited to the lower urinary tract. Therefore, the correct diagnosis is acute pyelonephritis.

3
Which organism is most likely responsible for her urinary tract infection (UTI)?
Show answer

The principal causative organisms in acute pyelonephritis are enteric Gram-negative bacilli. Escherichia coli is by far the most common. Other important organisms are Proteus, Klebsiella, Enterobacter, and Pseudomonas; these usually are associated with recurrent infections, especially in individuals who undergo urinary tract manipulations or have congenital or acquired anomalies of the lower urinary tract (see later). Staphylococci and Streptococcus faecalis also may cause pyelonephritis, but they are uncommon pathogens in this setting.

An ultrasound revealed mild hydronephrosis of the both kidneys with hydroureter. At this point, the patient developed severe intermittent pain of the left flank and developed chills and frank hematuria. A KUB (kidney, ureters, bladder) plain radiograph revealed a small, incidental calculus in the right kidney; however, a computed tomography (CT) scan also revealed a filling defect in the left renal pelvis and bilateral hydronephrosis and hydroureter. She was placed on broad-spectrum antibiotics and admitted to the hospital. Urine cultures taken grew Escherichia coli, confirming a diagnosis of acute pyelonephritis, and her antibiotic regimen was adjusted.

What are the different types of renal calculi and what are the risk factors for developing them?
Show answer

There are three major types of renal stones: (1) the vast majority (~80%) are made of calcium oxalate or calcium oxalate mixed with calcium phosphate; (2) about 10% are made of magnesium ammonium phosphate; and (3) about 6%–7% are uric acid stones. The most important determinant of stone formation is an increased urinary concentration of the stones’ constituents, such that it exceeds their solubility (supersaturation). Approximately 50% of patients who develop calcium stones have hypercalciuria that is not associated with hypercalcemia. Most patients in this group absorb calcium from the gut in excessive amounts (absorptive hypercalciuria) and promptly excrete it in the urine, and some have a primary renal defect of calcium reabsorption (renal hypercalciuria). Magnesium ammonium phosphate (struvite) stones almost always occur in individuals with persistently alkaline urine resulting from UTIs; typically infections with urea-splitting bacteria, such as Proteus vulgaris and Staphylococci, predispose individuals to urolithiasis. Gout and diseases involving rapid cell turnover, such as leukemias, lead to high uric acid levels in the urine and the possibility of uric acid stones.

What is the pathogenesis of acute pyelonephritis? What are the risk factors?
Show answer

Acute pyelonephritis may be caused by ascending infection or by hematogenous spread. Ascending infection is due most commonly to enteric Gram-negative bacilli that reach the kidney by ascending infection. This type of infection is facilitated by instrumentation (e.g., catheters) and by obstruction in the lower urinary tract (e.g., prostate enlargement). In the absence of instrumentation, UTI most commonly affects females due to the close proximity of the female urethra to the rectum, facilitating colonizationby enteric bacteria. Furthermore, the short urethra and trauma to the urethra during sexual intercourse enable entry of bacteria into the urinary bladder. Incompetence of the vesicoureteral valve is also important because reflux of urine from the bladder allows the infection to move upward to the kidney. Diabetes is another significant risk factor due to the increased risk of infections and bladder dysfunction due to neurogenic bladder. Hematogenous spread is far less common than ascending infection, but may result from seeding of the kidneys by bacteria in the course of septicemia or infective endocarditis.



Three days later, she developed hypotension (blood pressure 70/40) and was transferred to the intensive care unit for septic shock. She developed hypoxic respiratory failure and lactic acidosis (serum lactate 6.5 mmol/L [0.5–1 mmol/L]). Despite antibiotics and vasopressor therapy, she developed multiorgan failure followed by cardiac arrest. Attempts at CPR were not successful. An autopsy was performed.

Gross examination of the bisected surface of the left kidney showed a mildly dilated pelvicalyceal system. There were discrete hemorrhagic foci in many of the renal papillae indicating foci of papillary necrosis that resembled infarcts. A portion of sloughed renal papillary tissue was found in the left renal pelvis. Histologic examination of the kidney showed coagulative necrosis in the papilla and a surrounding neutrophilic infiltrate. The leukocytic infiltrate was limited to the junction between viable and necrotic tissue.

What are predisposing factors for developing renal papillary necrosis? What is the likely cause in this patient?
Show answer

Papillary necrosis can be seen in diabetes, analgesic abuse, urinary tract obstruction, sickle cell disease or trait (described later), and focally in renal tuberculosis. In all cases, it is caused by ischemia resulting from compression or obstruction of small blood vessels in the medulla. Such compression may be caused by interstitial edema (e.g., inflammatory reactions and urinary tract obstruction) or microvascular disease (e.g., diabetes). On occasion, necrotic papillae are excreted, and may cause gross hematuria or renal colic due to ureteric obstruction, as in this patient. This patient has a history of diabetes, urinary tract infection and long-term analgesic use, all of which are risk factors for papillary necrosis.

This patient’s tubulointerstitial nephritis was due to bacterial infection. What is another cause of tubulointerstitial nephritis?
Show answer

Another important cause of tubulointerstitial nephritis (TIN) is an idiosyncratic reaction to a therapeutic drug; the antibiotic ampicillin is known to be associated with this lesion (a rare reaction to a widely used drug). Drug-induced tubulointerstitial nephritis is characterized by mononuclear inflammation in the interstitium. Eosinophils are often present as well. By contrast, acute pyelonephritis is characterized by a neutrophilic exudate within tubules and interstitial inflammation.

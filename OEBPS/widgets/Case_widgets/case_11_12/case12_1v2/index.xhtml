Which histologic features seen in the biopsy specimen would confirm the diagnosis of reflux esophagitis?

In mild reflux esophagitis, the mucosal histology is often unremarkable. With more significant disease, eosinophils are recruited into the squamous mucosa, followed by neutrophils, which are usually associated with more severe injury. Basal zone hyperplasia exceeding 20% of the total epithelial thickness and elongation of lamina propria papillae, such that they extend into the upper third of the epithelium, may also be present.

What are the major causes of reflux esophagitis?
Show answer

The most common cause of gastroesophageal reflux is transient lower esophageal sphincter relaxation. This is thought to be mediated via vagal pathways and can be triggered by gastric distention, by gas or food, and by stress. Gastroesophageal reflux can also occur following swallow-induced lower esophageal sphincter relaxations or due to forceful opening of a relatively hypotensive lower esophageal sphincter by an abrupt increase in intraabdominal pressure, such as that due to coughing, straining, or bending. Other conditions that decrease lower esophageal sphincter tone or increase abdominal pressure and contribute to gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) include alcohol and tobacco use, obesity, central nervous system depressants, pregnancy, hiatal hernia, delayed gastric emptying, and increased gastric volume.

4
What are the major complications of reflux esophagitis?
Show answer

The potential complications of severe reflux esophagitis are (1) ulcer; (2) bleeding; (3) development of stricture; and (4) development of Barrett esophagus.

5
What is Barrett esophagus and what is its clinical significance?
Show answer

Barrett esophagus is a complication of chronic GERD that is characterized by intestinal columnar metaplasia within the esophageal squamous mucosa. The major risk of Barrett esophagus is the development of epithelial dysplasia, a precursor to adenocarcinoma. Dysplasia is recognized by the presence of cytologic and architectural abnormalities. The dysplastic glands resemble adenomatous glands typically seen in adenomatous polyps of the colon. The recognition of associated dysplasia is crucial to the evaluation of patients with Barrett esophagus.

The patient was prescribed proton-pump inhibitors and given a follow-up appointment in one year; however, he did not return for three years. He presented with heartburn and epigastric pain.

What is the diagnosis, and what is the etiology of this lesion?
Show answer

This histology is diagnostic of Barrett esophagus. The columnar epithelium of Barrett esophagus is a complication of long-standing gastroesophageal reflux, occurring in approximately 10% of patients with reflux esophagitis. The metaplasia is a response of the epithelium in the distal esophagus to prolonged injury, but it is not known why the more rugged squamous epithelium is replaced by the more delicate columnar epithelium. Endoscopic features and histologic identification of metaplastic columnar epithelium are both necessary for a diagnosis of Barrett esophagus.

Which type of malignancy typically develops in Barrett esophagus?
Show answer

Adenocarcinoma is the most commonly diagnosed malignancy arising in a background of Barrett esophagus.

The patient was scheduled to meet with a surgeon but did not return for his appointment and was lost to follow up. Four years later, he presented to his primary care physician with worsening dysphagia and a 10 pound-weight loss over the previous 4 months. A barium swallow showed distal narrowing of the esophagus. Endoscopic examination of the esophagus revealed an ulcerating mass in the distal esophagus. A biopsy specimen was obtained and showed a glandular tumor composed of irregular back-to-back glands, diagnostic of adenocarcinoma. Note the presence of squamous epithelium adjacent to the adenocarcinoma.

Radiologic studies (FDG-PET) showed no obvious metastatic disease and endoscopic ultrasound suggested that the tumor was limited to the submucosa. Neoadjuvant chemoradiation was given; however, the tumor did not respond. An esophagogastrectomy was performed. Grossly, the specimen was an ulcerated mass at the gastroesophageal junction. The tumor involved the cardia, which is often the case.

Histologic examination revealed intralymphatic carcinoma; one lymph node was positive for a microscopic focus of metastatic carcinoma. The patient was scheduled for adjuvant chemoradiotherapy.

What is the pathogenesis of adenocarcinoma arising in Barrett esophagus?
Show answer

Molecular studies suggest that the progression of Barrett esophagus to adenocarcinoma occurs over an extended period through the stepwise acquisition of genetic and epigenetic changes. This model is supported by the observation that epithelial clones identified in nondysplastic Barrett metaplasia persist and accumulate mutations in tumor suppressor genes and oncogenes during progression to dysplasia and invasive carcinoma. Chromosomal abnormalities, mutation of the TP53 tumor suppressor gene, and downregulation of the CDKN2A tumor suppressor gene (also known as p16/INK4a), which encodes a cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitor, are detected at early stages. In the case of CDKN2A, both allelic loss and hypermethylation-induced epigenetic silencing have been described. Later during progression there is amplification of the EGFR, ERBB2, MET, cyclin D1, and cyclin E oncogenes.

10
Which is the other major type of carcinoma that arises in the esophagus?
Show answer

Two malignancies account for a majority of esophageal cancers: adenocarcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma. Worldwide, squamous cell carcinoma is more common, but adenocarcinoma rates are increasing. In contrast to adenocarcinomas, which commonly arise at the gastroesophageal junction, squamous carcinomas typically originate in the midesophagus. Squamous cell carcinomas (SCC) begin as in situ lesions in the form of squamous dysplasia, and grossly appear as gray-white plaques on the mucosa. Advanced lesions can be polypoid and protrude into the lumen, ulcerate, or infiltrate diffusely. Microscopy shows nests of malignant cells that partially recapitulate the stratified organization of squamous epithelium.

What are the risk factors for the development of squamous cell carcinoma of the esophagus?
Show answer

A majority of esophageal squamous cell carcinomas in Europe and the United States are at least partially related to the use of alcohol and tobacco, the effects of which synergize to increase risk. Additional risk factors include poverty, caustic esophageal injury, achalasia, Plummer-Vinson syndrome, frequent consumption of very hot beverages, and previous radiation therapy to the mediastinum. Esophageal squamous cell carcinoma incidence varies up to 180-fold between and within countries. The regions with highest incidence are Iran, central China, Hong Kong, Brazil, and South Africa. HPV infection also has been implicated in esophageal squamous cell carcinoma in high-risk but not in low-risk regions.

Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast the different types of polyps.
  2. • Describe the differences between polyps, adenoma, and carcinoma.
  3. • Discuss the hereditary syndromes associated with colon cancer and the relevant genes.
  4. • Describe the pathogenesis, morphology, and clinical presentation of colon cancer.
  5. • Compare and contrast the APC/β-catenin pathway and the microsatellite instability pathway in the development of colon cancer.

A previously healthy 65-year-old woman presented to her primary care physician with a 3-month history of fatigue. She experienced menopause at the age of 51. She reported that she drank socially but did not smoke. Her family history was noncontributory.

On physical examination, the patient was a thin woman in no acute distress. Her conjunctivae were pale. Abdominal, cardiovascular, and respiratory examinations were unremarkable. A rectal exam was positive for fecal occult blood.

What is the most likely diagnosis for this patient?
Show answer

The patient has a microcytic hypochromic anemia which, combined with the low serum ferritin, is diagnostic of an iron-deficiency anemia. The positive test for fecal occult blood confirms a GI source of the blood loss and raises the possibility of a gastrointestinal malignancy.

The patient was started on iron replacement therapy and she was scheduled for upper and lower endoscopy. Esophagogastroduodenoscopy was within normal limits. Colonoscopy showed a 5-cm polypoid, exophytic cecal mass with focal surface ulceration. There was a 1.5 cm pedunculated polyp a few centimeters away from the ulcerated lesion and two additional 1 cm polyps in the transverse colon.

2
Histologic examination of the 1.5 cm cecal polyp and two 1 cm transverse colon polyps would most likely reveal which type of polyp?
Show answer

There are many different types of intestinal polyps. In general, they can be classified as nonneoplastic or neoplastic. The most common neoplastic polyp is the adenoma, which has the potential to progress to cancer. The nonneoplastic polyps can be further classified as inflammatory, hamartomatous, or hyperplastic. In this clinical setting, the two most likely tumor types would be adenomas and hyperplastic polyps. Most hyperplastic polyps are <5 mm in diameter and located in the left colon, in contrast to the patient’s polyps which are 1 cm and in the transverse colon. Adenomas are characterized histologically by epithelial dysplasia (nuclear hyperchromasia, elongation, and stratification). These changes are most easily appreciated at the surface of the adenoma because the epithelium fails to mature as the cells migrate out of the crypt. Crypt dilation and rupture with associated reactive inflammation can sometimes be seen.

Which variables increase the risk of malignant change in a polyp?
Show answer

Although all polyps do not progress to carcinoma, it is thought that most colonic carcinomas start as polyps. Three interrelated features determine the risk of cancerous transformation: (1) polyp size; (2) histologic architecture; and (3) severity of dysplasia. Cancer is rare in tubular adenomas less than 1 cm in diameter. The likelihood of cancer is high (about 50%) in sessile villous adenomas that are greater than 4 cm in diameter. Adenomas with severe dysplasia are more likely to progress to cancer than those without. Such dysplasias are often found in villous areas. Of all these, size is the most important factor.

Based on histologic findings of the cecal mass biopsy, a partial colon resection was performed that included the two 1 cm polyps of the transverse colon and an ulcerated fungating cecal mass with associated polyp.

What is the diagnosis of the cecal mass? Which features are most important in determining prognosis?
Show answer

There are malignant glands invading into the muscularis propria; therefore, this is a colonic adenocarcinoma. The two most important prognostic factors are depth of invasion and the presence of lymph node metastases. Invasion into the muscularis propria confers significantly reduced survival that is decreased further by the presence of lymph node metastases. In this case, 28 lymph nodes were negative for metastasis.

5
Which features are evaluated in staging colon cancer?
Show answer

Tumors are staged by the TNM method, in which T refers to extent of local invasion by the tumor; N, the number of regional lymph nodes involved; M, the extent of distant metastatic involvement. In colon cancer, the extent of invasion is based on depth: T1 is tumor restricted to the submucosa, T2 is tumor invading the muscularis propria, and T3 is tumor invading through the muscularis propria into the subserosa. This tumor was classified as a T3N0MX (T3, invasion through the muscularis propria; N0, no lymph node metastases; MX, metastatic status unknown).

6
Is it likely that this patient has a hereditary cancer syndrome?
Show answer

The two hereditary cancer syndromes most strongly associated with carcinoma of the colon are familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP) and hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer (HNPCC), also known as Lynch syndrome. FAP is an autosomal dominant disorder marked by the appearance of numerous colorectal adenomas by the teenage years. It is caused by mutations of the adenomatous polyposis coli (APC) gene. A count of at least 100 polyps is necessary for a diagnosis of classic FAP and as many as several thousand may be present. Since this patient only has four lesions, she does not have FAP. HNPCC is associated with colorectal carcinomas, typically on the right side (as in this case). However, colon cancers in patients with HNPCC tend to occur at younger ages than do sporadic colon cancers. Adenomas are present in HNPCC, but excessive numbers (i.e., polyposis) is not. HNPCC is caused by germ line mutations in genes that encode proteins responsible for the detection, excision, and repair of errors that occur during DNA replication (so-called mismatch repair or MMR genes). It is unlikely that the patient has HNPCC due to the late age of onset of her tumor. In HNPCC, cancers may also occur at sites other than the colorectum, including endometrium, stomach, ovary, ureters, brain, small bowel, hepatobiliary tract, and skin. The absence of other tumors in this patient also argues against a diagnosis of HNPCC. Thus, it is likely that this, like most cases, is an example of a sporadic colon cancer.

7
What is the most common molecular genetic pathway involved in the progression of adenomas to carcinomas?
Show answer

The classic adenoma-carcinoma sequence, which accounts for as much as 80% of sporadic colon tumors, typically involves mutation of the APC tumor suppressor early in the neoplastic process. For adenomas to develop, both copies of the APC gene must be functionally inactivated, either by mutation or epigenetic events. APC is a key negative regulator of β-catenin, a component of the WNT signaling pathway. Other mutations involving KRAS, SMAD2, and SMAD4, and the tumor suppressor gene TP53, lead to the emergence of carcinoma, in which additional mutations occur. Although there may be a preferred temporal sequence for these changes, it is the aggregate effect of the mutations, rather than their order of occurrence, that appears most critical.


8
What is another common genetic pathway that results in sporadic colorectal carcinomas?
Show answer

Loss of mismatch repair (MMR) gene function is seen in approximately 15% of sporadic colorectal carcinomas. In contrast to HNPCC, in which there is a germline mutation in one of the MMR genes (see previous questions), in sporadic colorectal carcinomas, there is epigenetic silencing of MMR function, typically by biallelic hypermethylation of the MLH1 gene. This deactivation allows mutations to occur in microsatellite repeats, which is referred to as microsatellite instability (MSI). Some microsatellite sequences are located in the coding or promoter regions of genes involved in regulation of cell growth, such as those encoding the type II TGF-β receptor and the pro-apoptotic protein Bax. Because TGF-β inhibits colonic epithelial cell proliferation, mutation of type II TGF-β receptor can contribute to uncontrolled cell growth, while loss of Bax may enhance the survival of genetically abnormal clones.

What is carcinoembryonic antigen and why is this being evaluated in this patient?
Show answer

Colon carcinomas produce various proteins (e.g., CEA) that can be detected in the blood. CEA is not specific for colon cancer; CEA levels may be elevated in patients with carcinomas of the lung, breast, ovary, urinary bladder, and prostate, and in patients with numerous non-neoplastic disorders, including alcoholic cirrhosis, pancreatitis, and ulcerative colitis. Because elevated levels have little specificity, they are not of diagnostic value, but they are useful for assessing possible recurrence after resection of a primary tumor. If total removal has been accomplished, the serum CEA level declines to normal. Reappearance of an elevated CEA following resection is a reliable indicator of tumor recurrence.

What is the significance of these laboratory values?
Show answer

The elevated CEA suggests a recurrence of the patient’s colonic adenocarcinoma. Her liver function tests are consistent with liver damage. The common sites of metastatic disease in colon cancer are regional lymph nodes, liver, and lungs. Because of the portal drainage, the liver is the most common site of metastases. Based on these laboratory values, it is likely that the patient has hepatic metastases of her colon cancer.

A screening CT showed multiple liver nodules.

11
If the patient’s tumor revealed a mismatch repair defect (microsatellite instability), which therapeutic modality might be considered?
Show answer

Patients with genetically unstable recurrent tumors are now treated with immunotherapy, specifically checkpoint blockade with antibodies against the inhibitory receptor PD-1 or its ligand PD-L1. These antibodies release the brakes (checkpoints) on anti-tumor T cells and promote strong antitumor immunity. Because of genetic instability, tumors due to mismatch repair (MMR) typically contain many mutations, which greatly increase the possibility of production of large numbers of “neoantigens” (new protein antigens that are not normally present and are viewed as foreign by the patient’s immune system).

Despite an initial response to immunotherapy, the tumor persisted. A liver-spleen scan revealed multiple masses in the liver. The patient refused additional treatment and died 6 weeks later. An autopsy was performed and the liver showed multiple mass lesions with areas of central necrosis, a typical finding in colon cancer.

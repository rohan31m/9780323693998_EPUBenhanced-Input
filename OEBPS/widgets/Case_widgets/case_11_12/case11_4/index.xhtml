Objectives
  1. • Describe the main causes of chronic renal failure.
  2. • Explain the clinical features of chronic renal failure.

A 67-year-old man presented to the Emergency Department with a two-week history of nausea, a three-day history of chest pain, and a two-day history of vomiting. He also noticed that he was having difficulty putting on his shoes. He described the chest pain as retrosternal, burning, and worse on inspiration. His past medical history was significant for untreated hypertension diagnosed 14 years earlier.

Physical examination revealed a thin man with ecchymoses on his trunk and extremities. He had 3+ pitting edema of his lower extremities. His blood pressure was 160/120 mm Hg, his temperature 36.7°C, and pulse 100 beats/min (60–100 beats/min). Lung exam revealed bilateral rales to the mid-lung fields, and cardiac exam revealed muffled heart sounds and a friction rub.

Laboratory analysis:

Urinalysis: protein 1+

blood 1+

glucose neg

bacteria neg
WBC 6,700/mm3 (4,500 – 11,000/mm3)
Platelets 250,000/mm3 (150,000–400,000/mm3)
Hematocrit 26% (41%–53%)
Creatinine 15 mg/dL (0.6–1.2 mg/dL)
BUN 215 mg/dL (7–18 mg/dL)
Calcium 7.2 mg/dL (8.4–10.4 mg/dL)
Potassium

Cardiac markers of MI		 7.0 mEq/L (3.5–5.0 mEq/L)

Negative

Chest x-ray showed moderate cardiomegaly with increased pulmonary vascular markings and hazy obliteration of the lower lung bases. An electrocardiogram (ECG) showed no evidence of myocardial infarction; however, there was complete heart block with a wide QRS complex. An echocardiogram revealed a moderate amount of fluid around the heart. A pericardiocentesis was performed and 250 cc of serosanguineous fluid was removed.

1
What was the cause of this patient’s cardiac symptoms?
Show answer

This patient is in renal failure, as evidenced by his significantly elevated BUN and creatinine. Hyperkalemia is common in end stage renal disease due to decreased urinary excretion of potassium; in this setting, the cardiac myocytes cease to function normally which, in this patient, has led to complete heart block and widened QRS. Pericarditis is a frequent manifestation of uremia. Retrosternal chest pain, worse on inspiration, is a classic finding in patients with pericarditis. The muffled heart tones also support this diagnosis. The negative serum cardiac markers would make myocardial infarction an unlikely possibility.

2
Why does this patient have ecchymoses?
Show answer

In addition to causing pericarditis, uremia is associated with increased risk of bleeding. The cause of the bleeding is not well understood; both platelet dysfunction and abnormal platelet-endothelial interactions are observed. Platelet dysfunction likely plays the greater role and is manifested by decreased platelet aggregation in response to standard stimuli and decreased platelet adhesiveness.

3
Why is this patient anemic?
Show answer

Chronic renal failure is associated with an anemia that roughly correlates with the degree of uremia. The damaged kidneys are unable to produce sufficient erythropoietin to maintain adequate red blood cell production. In addition, patients may be iron deficient due to increased bleeding (see the previous question).


4
Why does this patient have hypocalcemia?
Show answer

In end stage renal disease, the kidneys are no longer able to generate sufficient 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D, the most active form of vitamin D. When 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D is deficient, intestinal calcium absorption and calcium resorption in the kidney decrease. In addition, 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D interacts with parathyroid hormone to regulate blood calcium and contributes to the mineralization of bone. In the setting of vitamin D deficiency, parathyroid hormone increases (secondary hyperparathyroidism) and tends to correct serum calcium levels.

5
What accounts for this patient’s pulmonary findings and pitting edema?
Show answer

This patient is in congestive heart failure due to the pericardial effusion combined with his longstanding hypertension.

The patient was urgently admitted for hemodialysis; however, while in the dialysis suite, he experienced a fatal arrhythmia and died. An autopsy was performed.

The kidneys had a finely granular appearance, likely reflecting areas of atrophy and scarring with retraction alternating with normal areas. The retracted scar tissue (sunken areas) accentuates the preserved areas (protruding bumps), creating the granularity.

Histologic evaluation of the patient's kidney showed prominent damage to all four components (glomeruli, tubules, interstitium, and vasculature) typical of an end-stage kidney. Obsolescent (sclerotic) glomeruli were easily noted, and the dilated atrophic tubules, normally back-to-back, were separated by interstitial fibrosis with mild inflammation. Thick-walled blood vessels were also present.

Focally, there were arterioles and small arteries that showed thickening and hyalinization of the walls with consequent narrowing of the lumens. This is a frequent finding in kidneys in patients with longstanding hypertension.

  • Kidney, low-power
  • Sclerotic glomeruli
  • Thick-walled arteries
  • Atrophic tubules
  • Interstitial fibrosis
  • Arterioles
  • Hyalinized vessel wall
6
What was the most likely cause of chronic renal failure in this patient? Which pathological findings would support this diagnosis?
Show answer

The patient is an older man with a history of untreated hypertension, and the kidneys show severe damage with diffuse tubular atrophy (due to ischemia and glomerular loss) and interstitial fibrosis (likely due to ischemia and loss of normal tubules). Some tubules become enlarged and hypertrophic as a compensatory mechanism. Atrophic tubules often have thickened basement membranes. All of these changes are nonspecific and could result from various causes (e.g., hypertension, chronic glomerular disease). However, the clinical history and pathological findings together point to hypertension-associated nephrosclerosis as the most likely cause of chronic renal failure in this case. Hypertension causes significant endothelial damage, often allowing plasma proteins to leak into the vessel walls, compromising the lumina. Smooth muscle cells may also die and be replaced by collagen, and the vessel wall appears hyalinized. These small vessel (arteriolar) changes are characteristically seen in chronic hypertension and in the kidney are responsible for the constellation of abnormalities (tubule loss, interstitial fibrosis) termed benign nephrosclerosis.

Objectives
  1. • Describe the pathogenesis, histopathology, and clinical features of celiac disease.
  2. • Compare and contrast the major categories of diarrheal disease and representative causes of each.
  3. • Describe the malabsorption syndromes and the major organ systems affected by malabsorption.

A 44-year-old man presented with a 7-month history of worsening diarrhea and chronic fatigue and a 2-month history of a pruritic rash on his arms and legs. The frequency of his bowel movements had increased to five to seven per day, and his stools were yellow and malodorous. He had occasional abdominal cramping, but no melena or bleeding. When he fasted, his bowel movement frequency decreased. He had not traveled outside of the country or been camping during the last two years. His brother also had a history of chronic diarrhea.

Physical exam revealed a maculopapular rash on the extensor surfaces of his arms and legs and scattered ecchymoses. His abdomen was protuberant with increased bowel sounds. Cardiovascular and pulmonary exams were within normal limits.

What are the types of diarrheal disease and their causes?
Show answer

There are four main categories of diarrheal disease, summarized in the table.

Type of Diarrhea Features Representative Causes
Secretory diarrhea Watery, isotonic stool; not relieved by fasting Cholera
Osmotic diarrhea Unabsorbed luminal solutes increase osmotic pressure, drawing water into the lumen Lactase deficiency
Malabsorptive diarrhea Usually chronic, associated with defective nutrient absorption, steatorrhea; relieved by fasting Pancreatic insufficiency, celiac disease, Crohn disease
Exudative diarrhea Purulent, sometimes bloody, stools; not relieved by fasting Infections, (e.g., Shigella), ulcerative colitis
2
What does the character of his stool suggest about the etiology of his diarrhea?
Show answer

Stool that is yellow and malodorous (steatorrhea) is typically seen when there is fat malabsorption. Excess dietary fat is therefore delivered to the large bowel, where colonic bacteria produce hydroxy fatty acids that draw in fluid osmotically, resulting in diarrhea. The differential diagnosis includes pancreatic insufficiency, celiac disease and Crohn disease.

Stool tests revealed a stool output of 4128 g/day (normal 100–200 g/day) and a fat excretion of 17 g/day (normal 5 g/day). Microscopic examination for ova and parasites and cultures for bacterial pathogens and acid-fast bacilli were negative. Blood testing showed mild anemia (Hct 33%; 41%–53%), hypoproteinemia (4.9 mg/dL; 6–7.8 mg/dL), and hypoalbuminemia (3.2 mg/dL; 3.5–5.5 mg/dL).

What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

Based on the histologic findings (villous atrophy) and serology findings, this patient has celiac disease.


5
What is the pathogenesis of this disease?
Show answer

Celiac disease is an intestinal immune reaction to gluten, the major storage protein of wheat, rye, barley, and similar grains. Gluten is digested by luminal and brush border enzymes into amino acids and peptides, including a 33–amino acid gliadin peptide that is resistant to degradation by gastric, pancreatic, and small-intestinal proteases. Gliadin is deamidated by tissue transglutaminase and is then able to interact with HLA-DQ2 or HLA-DQ8 on antigen-presenting cells and be presented to CD4+ T cells. These T cells and other inflammatory cells in lamina propria produce cytokines that likely contribute to the tissue damage and characteristic mucosal histopathology. The symptoms and histologic changes resolve with exclusion of gluten from the diet.


The morphologic alterations that may be present in celiac disease, including varying degrees of villous atrophy, increased numbers of intraepithelial lymphocytes (IELs), and epithelial proliferation with crypt elongation (left). A model for the pathogenesis of celiac disease is shown at right. Note that both innate and adaptive immune mechanisms are involved in the tissue responses to gliadin. CD4 T cells (producing IFNγ) are shown in the lamina propria, and CD8 T cells, expressing the NKG2D receptor, are shown in between epithelial cells.

The patient was discharged with appropriate dietary instructions. His diarrhea resolved, and his weight began to increase.

6
What accounts for the patient’s ecchymoses?
Show answer

Flattening of the villi greatly decreases the surface area available for nutrient absorption. Patients with celiac disease may have increased bruising due to decreased absorption of fat-soluble vitamin K. They may also present with osteopenia, osteoporosis, and neurologic symptoms secondary to decreased calcium transport and binding of calcium to unabsorbed dietary fatty acids.

7
What is the etiology of anemia in patients with celiac disease?
Show answer

Iron and folate, two critical components of hematopoiesis, are absorbed in the small intestine. In cases of severe disease that involves the ileum, vitamin B12 absorption can be impaired. Folate and vitamin B12 deficiency cause megaloblastic anemia, while insufficient iron leads to a hypochromic, microcytic anemia.

8
What is the pathogenesis of the patient’s maculopapular rash?
Show answer

Ten percent to 20% of patients with celiac disease develop a rash on the extensor surfaces of their extremities and on the trunk, buttocks, scalp, and neck. Lesions consist of intact and eroded (usually scratched) erythematous blisters, often in groups. The blisters are associated with the accumulation of neutrophils (microabscesses) at the tips of dermal papillae. This rash is called dermatitis herpetiformis. Immunofluorescent stains revealing selective deposition of IgA autoantibody at the tips of dermal papillae is characteristic. Antigliadin antibodies cross-react with endomysium and tissue transglutaminases, including epidermal transglutaminase expressed by keratinocytes. The resultant injury and inflammation produce a subepidermal blister. Dermatitis herpetiformis can also be seen in patients who do not have celiac disease.

9
Which other diseases are associated with celiac disease?
Show answer

The most common diseases associated with celiac disease are other immune diseases, including type 1 diabetes, thyroiditis, Sjögren syndrome, and IgA nephropathy. In addition, celiac disease may be associated with neurologic disorders, such as ataxia, autism, depression, epilepsy, Down syndrome, and Turner syndrome.

10
Which etiology should be suspected in a patient who has malabsorption and has has drunk untreated water?
Show answer

Giardia lambia, a flagellated protozoan, is the most common pathogenic parasite in humans and is spread by fecally contaminated water or food. Because cysts are resistant to chlorine, Giardia organisms are endemic in unfiltered public and rural water supplies. Infection may occur after ingestion of as few as 10 cysts and is characterized by acute or chronic malabsorptive diarrhea. Giardia trophozoites can be identified in duodenal biopsies based on their characteristic pear shape and the presence of two equally sized nuclei. Despite large numbers of trophozoites, which are tightly bound to the brush border of villous enterocytes, there is no invasion and small intestinal morphology may be normal.

  • Giardia trophozoites

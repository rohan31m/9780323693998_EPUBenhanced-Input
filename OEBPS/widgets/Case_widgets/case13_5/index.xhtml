Objectives
  1. • Compare the various types of gallstones, including how they are formed, and the risk factors for their development.
  2. • Describe the causes and clinical consequences of acute cholecystitis.
  3. • Describe the acute consequences of bile duct obstruction.
  4. • Discuss the causes and clinicopathologic findings in acute pancreatitis.

A 45-year-old woman presented with a 2-day history of abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and fever. During the past six months, she had experienced mid-epigastric pain four or five times. She described this pain as steady but cramping and lasting several hours, sometimes with radiation to the right subscapular area. A few episodes occurred shortly after eating fried food. The most recent episode of pain, while similar in character to prior episodes, was more severe, had persisted for more than one day, and was accompanied by chills, fever, nausea, and vomiting. The patient was on no medications, and her past medical history was notable for prior delivery of five children.

Physical examination revealed an obese woman in moderate discomfort. Her vital signs were blood pressure 110/80 mm Hg; temperature 39° C, and pulse 110 bpm. Her eyes were icteric, and her abdomen was tender in the right upper quadrant, especially on deep inspiration. Her liver was slightly enlarged on percussion. Bowel sounds were heard. The remainder of her exam was within normal limits.

1
What is the most likely etiology of this patient’s pain?
Show answer

Postprandial epigastric pain in an obese woman of child-bearing age is highly suggestive of cholelithiasis (gallstones). Pain from cholelithiasis localizes to the right upper quadrant or epigastrium and may radiate to the right shoulder or back. Biliary colic may be excruciating and, despite its characterization as “colic” (implying sharp and intermittent), it is usually constant, not colicky. The pain often follows a fatty meal that stimulates bile secretion, forcing a stone against the gallbladder outlet. Distention of the gallbladder leads to increased luminal pressure, which compromises blood flow to the wall, thus contributing to mucosal injury.

What are the different types of gallstones?
Show answer

There are two general classes of gallstones: cholesterol stones, consisting of more than 50% crystalline cholesterol monohydrate, and pigment stones, composed predominantly of bilirubin calcium salts.

An abdominal ultrasound exam showed stones in the gallbladder and dilation of the hepatic and common bile ducts and pancreatic duct. Laboratory tests were sent.

3
What do the laboratory tests indicate?
Show answer

The marked elevation of serum amylase and lipase is consistent with a diagnosis of acute pancreatitis. An elevated alkaline phosphatase with hyperbilirubinemia is suggestive of obstructive jaundice due to choledocholithiasis. Her elevated WBC with the presence of immature neutrophils (called band forms because nuclei have not yet segmented) indicates that she has acute inflammation. The most likely diagnosis, based on the clinical and laboratory findings, is acute calculous cholecystitis and acute pancreatitis.

The patient was admitted to the hospital. After blood for cultures were obtained, parenteral antibiotic therapy was started. Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) demonstrated obstruction of the distal portion of the common bile duct near the ampulla of Vater by an impacted stone. Attempts to remove the stone via the endoscope were unsuccessful. A cholecystectomy was performed. Gross examination revealed a round, yellow, pure cholesterol gallstone and geographic, yellow mucosal gallbladder surface due to cholesterolosis. Microscopically, cholesterolosis manifests as numerous triglyceride- and cholesterol-laden macrophages in the lamina propria of the tips of the villi in the mucosal layer.

4
What are the risk factors for developing this type of gallstone?
Show answer

Risk factors for cholesterol (and mixed) gallstones include ethnic and geographic factors; increasing age and female sex (both related to increase in biliary cholesterol secretion); environmental factors (oral contraceptive use and multiparity; both increase cholesterol synthesis and uptake in the liver by stimulating HMG-COA reductase activity and enhanced expression of LDL receptors, respectively); acquired disorders, most of which affect cholesterol synthesis and secretion (e.g., diabetes, obesity, rapid weight loss, diseases of the distal small intestine such as Crohn disease); and hereditary disorders (hyperlipidemias) (Table 13.5.1).

What is the pathogenesis of this type of gallstone?
Show answer

Normally, cholesterol is rendered soluble in bile by forming micelles with water-soluble bile salts and water-insoluble lecithins. When cholesterol concentrations increase, supersaturation of bile and nucleation of cholesterol crystals occur. Gallbladder stasis, increased mucin content of the bile, and biliary sludge all act to promote nucleation of cholesterol crystals, which grow by accretion into stones.

6
What is the pathogenesis of pigment gallstones and what are the risk factors for developing them?
Show answer

Pigment gallstones are complex mixtures of insoluble calcium salts of unconjugated bilirubin along with inorganic calcium salts. Disorders that are associated with elevated levels of unconjugated bilirubin in bile, such as chronic hemolytic anemias, severe ileal dysfunction (e.g., in Crohn disease or following ileal resection or bypass), bacterial infection of the biliary tree, and cirrhosis increase the risk of developing pigment stones. In hemolytic anemias, the secretion of conjugated bilirubin into the bile increases. About 1% of bilirubin glucuronides are deconjugated in the biliary tree, and in the setting of chronically increased secretion of conjugated bilirubin, there is sufficiently large amount of deconjugated bilirubin left to allow pigment stones to form.
Gastrointestinal disorders: ileal disease (e.g., Crohn disease), ileal resection or bypass, cystic fibrosis with pancreatic insufficiency.

Which features are typically seen in gallbladders removed for cholelithiasis?
Show answer

Usually, both acute and chronic inflammatory changes are present. Grossly, ulcerations, erythema and acute inflammation due to ischemic necrosis secondary to pressure from gallstones, and wall thickening from chronic inflammation can be seen. Microscopically, Rokitansky-Aschoff sinuses (outpouchings of the mucosal epithelium through the muscular wall of the gallbladder) are found in cases of chronic cholecystitis.

  • Thickened wall
  • Ulceration
  • Gallstone
  • Gallbladder, histology
  • Rokitansky-Aschoff sinus
8
In addition to cholecystitis, what are the complications arising from cholelithiasis?
Show answer

Although 70% to 80% of patients with gallstones are asymptomatic, in the remaining population, stones can cause pain, acute and chronic cholecystitis, empyema of the gallbladder, gallbladder perforation or fistulae, gallstone ileus, choledocholithiasis (stones in the bile duct, sometimes complicated by ascending cholangitis and liver abscesses), obstructive cholestasis, and pancreatitis.

9
What are the causes and postulated mechanisms leading to the development of acute pancreatitis?
Show answer

Alcoholism and biliary tract disease (cholelithiasis) account for the majority (80%) of cases of acute pancreatitis. Ten to twenty percent of cases are idiopathic; there is increasing evidence of genetic defects in such cases. The shared feature in most of these inherited cases is a defect that increases the activity of trypsin, a pivotal pancreatic enzyme. Acute pancreatitis results from intrapancreatic activation of digestive enzymes such as trypsin, causing proteolysis, and hemorrhage with necrosis and acute inflammation. Elevation of serum amylase (first 24 hr) and lipase (72–96 hr) is diagnostic of acute pancreatitis. Within the pancreas, lipase and phospholipase degrade lipids and membrane phospholipids, which can manifest as fat necrosis. Released elastase degrades elastic tissue in vessel walls. Fibrinoid necrosis of the vessel wall can be seen. Proposed mechanisms of pancreatic injury and subsequent enzyme release include: (1) duct obstruction; (2) direct acinar cell injury; and (3) deranged intracellular transport of pancreatic enzymes.

  • Acute pancreatitis
  • Necrosis/inflammation
  • Blood vessel
  • Fibrinoid necrosis
  • Fat necrosis
10
What are the major acute complications of acute pancreatitis?
Show answer

About 5% of patients with pancreatitis die during the acute illness due to systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS), marked by widespread activation of components of the innate immune system, vascular changes leading to diminished perfusion of tissues and edema, and possible multiorgan failure. The mortality is higher in patients with other concomitant diseases. Possible sequelae in patients with acute pancreatitis include development of pancreatic pseudocysts, pancreatic abscesses, duodenal obstruction, and, with repeated episodes of pancreatitis, development of chronic pancreatitis.

Objectives
  1. • Relate the molecular defect in the CFTR protein to the abnormal sweat chloride test and pathologic changes in the lung and other tissues.
  2. • Give examples of genetic and environmental modifiers.
  3. • Describe the characteristic pathologic changes (gross and microscopic) that occur in the lungs, pancreas, intestines, liver, nasal sinuses, and male genital tract and how they relate to the clinical features of this disease.

A 9-month-old boy was brought to the pediatrician with a 1-month history of poor weight gain and recurrent respiratory infections. He was born to a 22-year-old mother by spontaneous vaginal delivery after an uncomplicated pregnancy with no prenatal care. There was no family history of significant illnesses. Past medical history was significant for meconium ileus at birth treated with enemas. She stated that additional testing was done, but that she had never received the results. For the last several months, he had four to eight bulky, foul-smelling bowel movements each day.

1
What is the significance of the clinical presentation?
Show answer

Poor weight gain (“failure to thrive”) can be associated with a number of conditions including feeding difficulties, malabsorption syndromes, and heart and lung problems that lead to insufficient oxygenation. The history of recurrent respiratory infections could indicate an inherited immune deficiency. Meconium ileus is strongly associated with cystic fibrosis: 90% of patients with meconium ileus have this disease. Frequent bulky, foul-smelling bowel movements are suggestive of a malabsorption syndrome.

Physical examination disclosed a pale, small infant who appeared malnourished, with scant subcutaneous fat, pitting edema of the extremities, and a protuberant abdomen, within which the liver could be palpated 4 cm below the right costal margin. There were coarse rhonchi and scattered rales in the lungs. The cardiac examination was normal. A chest x-ray showed streaky markings in all lung fields.

The infant’s laboratory values were as follows:

White blood cell count 7,500/mm3 (nL 4–10,000/mm3)
Hemoglobin 8.5 g/dL (nL 9.5–13 g/dL)
Platelets 350,000/mm3 (150–450,000/mm3)
Prothrombin time 14.5 sec (nL 9.5–13.5 sec)
Serum albumin 1.8 g/dL (nL 3.5–5.5 g/dL)
Sweat chloride 110 mEq/L (nL <60 mEq/L)
Stool smear positive for fat
2
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

This patient has cystic fibrosis. The diagnosis in this patient is based on (1) clinical symptoms consistent with cystic fibrosis (e.g., meconium ileus, pancreatic insufficiency) and (2) evidence of cystic fibrosis conductance regulator (CFTR) dysfunction (e.g., elevated sweat chloride). Both conditions must be met for diagnosis. The second criterion can also be met by the detection of CFTR mutations.

3
Which laboratory findings of this patient are consistent with the diagnosis of cystic fibrosis?
Show answer

The patient’s prolonged prothrombin time is due to malabsorption of vitamin K, a fat-soluble vitamin; vitamin K is required for the synthesis of active forms of coagulation factors II (thrombin), VII, IX, and X. The presence of fat in the stool smear confirms fat malabsorption. Protein malnutrition is reflected by low serum albumin. Sweat chloride is characteristically increased because of impaired reabsorption in the sweat duct.

4
What is the pathophysiology of cystic fibrosis?
Show answer

The primary defect in cystic fibrosis is reduced production or abnormal function of an epithelial chloride channel protein encoded by the CFTR gene. Ion transport defects lead to abnormally viscid mucous secretions in the lung and the pancreas that block the airways and the pancreatic ducts, which in turn are responsible for the two most important clinical manifestations: recurrent and chronic pulmonary infections and pancreatic insufficiency. Pancreatic insufficiency is exacerbated by increased acidity of pancreatic secretions due to a failure of bicarbonate ion secretion, which also depends on normal CFTR function. Acidic secretions diminish the activity of pancreatic enzymes such as trypsin, which function best at alkaline pH, and also increased mucin precipitation and plugging of ducts by inspissated secretions causing cystic dilation. Bacteria bind to plugged mucins, and the resultant inflammation destroys acini, which are replaced by fibrous tissue. The islets are usually preserved until late in the course of the disease; extensive destruction of islets may lead to diabetes mellitus, which occurs in about 10% of patients with cystic fibrosis.

Genetic analysis showed biallelic deletion of three nucleotides coding for phenylalanine at amino acid position 508 (ΔF508).

5
What is the pattern of inheritance of cystic fibrosis?
Show answer

Cystic fibrosis follows simple autosomal recessive transmission, although even heterozygous carriers have a predisposition toward pulmonary and pancreatic disease at higher rates than the general population.

6
Which population is most frequently affected by cystic fibrosis?
Show answer

Cystic fibrosis is most common in individuals of northern European descent (1 in 2500 to 3000 births), and is less common in people of African or Asian descent.

7
What is the significance of the ΔF508 mutation?
Show answer

The ΔF508 mutation causes misfolding and accelerated degradation of CFTR, leading to loss of function. Worldwide, this mutation is found in approximately 70% of cystic fibrosis patients. It is considered a “severe” mutation.

8
How does inactivation of CFTR cause increased sweat salinity?
Show answer

The major function of the CFTR protein in the sweat gland ducts is to reabsorb luminal chloride ions and augment sodium reabsorption through the epithelial sodium channel. Therefore, in the sweat ducts, loss of CFTR function leads to decreased reabsorption of sodium chloride and production of hypertonic (“salty”) sweat.


9
How does the molecular defect in cystic fibrosis lead to lung disease?
Show answer

In respiratory (and intestinal) epithelium, CFTR is one of the most important avenues for active luminal secretion of chloride. At these sites, CFTR mutations result in loss or reduction of chloride secretion into the lumen. Active luminal sodium absorption is increased (due to loss of inhibition of epithelial sodium channel activity), and both of these ion changes increase passive water reabsorption from the lumen, lowering the water content of the surface fluid layer coating mucosal cells. In the lungs, this dehydration leads to defective mucociliary action and the accumulation of hyperconcentrated, viscid secretions that obstruct the air passages and predispose to recurrent pulmonary infections, parenchymal destruction, bronchiectasis and fibrosis.


  • Lung, gross
  • Bronchiectatic airways
10
Which histologic features would be expected in a lung from a cystic fibrosis patient?
Show answer

The bronchioles become permanently dilated (cystic) because of repeated plugging by viscid secretions and secondary infection. An acute inflammatory infiltrate is typically seen in the plug of secretions and in the surrounding lung parenchyma. Extensive dense fibrosis around the bronchiole and extending into the adjacent pulmonary parenchyma, the result of repeated bouts of obstruction and inflammation, is the second hallmark of this disease. Chronic inflammation is also present.

11
Which pathogens are commonly isolated from the infected lungs of patients with cystic fibrosis?
Show answer

Early infections are often caused by Haemophilus influenza or Staphylococcus aureus, but with time patients tend to become chronically infected with Pseudomonas aeruginosa and/or Burkholderia cepacia.

On the day after admission, the infant became febrile with increasing respiratory distress and tachypnea. Physical findings indicated progressive consolidation of the right lung, suggesting worsening pneumonia. This was confirmed radiographically. The white blood cell count rose to 24,000/mm3, with an increase in band forms (immature neutrophils) to 20%. Sputum culture grew Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Staphylococcus aureus. Aggressive antibiotic therapy and supportive care were able to resolve the infection and the patient was discharged 6 weeks after admission with a follow-up appointment with a cystic fibrosis specialist.

12
Which therapies exist for cystic fibrosis patients?
Show answer

Treatment for cystic fibrosis includes antimicrobial therapies, pancreatic enzyme replacement, bilateral lung transplantation (when the pulmonary disease is advanced), and most recently the use of drugs that improve the function of particular mutant forms of CFTR. The latter include drugs that selectively improve the folding and transport of the Δ508 mutant and that enhance the opening of the CFTR chloride channels. Due to these new treatments and improvements in the management of cystic fibrosis, the median life expectancy has risen to close to 40 years, and increasingly a lethal disease of childhood is changing into a chronic disease of adults.

13
Would two patients with the same CFTR mutations be expected to have a similar disease course?
Show answer

No. Although cystic fibrosis remains one of the best-known examples of the “one gene, one disease” axiom, there is now considerable evidence that genes other than CFTR modify the frequency and severity of certain organ-specific manifestations, especially pulmonary manifestations and neonatal meconium ileus. Polymorphisms in genes whose products modulate neutrophil function in response to bacterial infections act as modifier loci for the severity of pulmonary disease in cystic fibrosis. Environmental modifiers (i.e., virulence of organisms, efficacy of therapy, intercurrent and concurrent infections by other organisms, exposure to tobacco and allergens) may also explain the significant phenotypic differences between individuals who share the same CFTR genotype.

14
What are the long-term sequelae of cystic fibrosis?
Show answer

Patients typically have multiple pulmonary infections that lead to bronchiectasis, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease and pulmonary dysfunction that may require oxygen therapy. Destruction of the pancreatic acini results in exocrine pancreas insufficiency and consequent nutritional challenges. Diabetes can arise due to destruction of the islets late in the disease. Liver failure can also occur secondary to plugging of bile canaliculi by hyperviscous secretions, leading to bile ductular proliferation, fibrosis, and (eventually) biliary cirrhosis. Infertility is seen in over 95% of male patients who survive to adulthood due to congenital bilateral absence of the vas deferens.

15
What is the leading cause of death in cystic fibrosis patients?
Show answer

Repeated respiratory infections cause 80%–90% of deaths of cystic fibrosis patients.

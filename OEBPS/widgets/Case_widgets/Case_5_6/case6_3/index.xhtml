Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast the different types of structural and numerical chromosomal abnormalities.
  2. • Discuss the risk factors for genetic diseases.
  3. • Describe the mechanisms that cause genetic abnormalities.

A 2300-gm male infant was born at 37 weeks’ gestational age to a 37-year-old mother. This was her sixth pregnancy; she had had two previous children and three spontaneous abortions. At birth, multiple congenital anomalies were noted, including bilateral cleft lip and palate; microcephaly; polydactyly; low-set, abnormally shaped ears; undescended testes; and rocker-bottom feet.

  • Clinical presentation
  • Bilateral cleft lip and palate
  • Polydactyly
  • Clinical presentation
  • Rocker-bottom feet
1
What is the most probable diagnosis for this patient, based on the phenotype alone?
Show answer

The extent of the abnormalities suggests a significant aberration, such as a trisomy. Although not all infants with trisomy 13 have polydactyly and a cleft lip, their presence together is almost always associated with trisomy 13. Based on the history of multiple spontaneous abortions, it is unlikely that the trisomy is sporadic; there is probably an unsuspected robertsonian translocation in one of the parents.

2
Is cytogenetic confirmation necessary?
Show answer

Yes, none of the features of trisomy 13 is entirely specific, and cytogenetic confirmation is essential.

Cytogenetic evaluation of the infant showed 46,XY,+13,der(13;14)(q10;q10). The robertsonian translocation chromosome is derived (der) from the long arms (q) of two acrocentric chromosomes fused to each other at the centromere(s) (centromere = band 10).

  • Karyotype
  • #13 chromosomes
  • Robertsonian translocation chromosome
3
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

The patient has trisomy 13, also known as Patau syndrome.

4
What are the types of genetic aberrations that affect chromosomal structure and what are their clinical consequences?
Show answer

Deletion refers to loss of a portion of a chromosome. Most deletions are interstitial, but rarely, terminal deletions may occur. The consequences of deletions depend on the genetic material that is lost. Ring chromosomes are produced when a break occurs at both ends of a chromosome and the damaged ends fuse; these typically result in deletion of some terminal genomic sequences. Ring chromosomes do not behave normally in meiosis or mitosis and usually have serious consequences for daughter cells. Inversion refers to a rearrangement that involves two breaks within a single chromosome with reincorporation of the inverted, intervening segment. Inversions are often compatible with normal development. Isochromosomes result when one arm of a chromosome is lost and the remaining arm is duplicated, yielding a chromosome consisting of two short arms or two long arms. In a translocation, a segment of one chromosome is transferred to another. In one form, called a balanced reciprocal translocation, there are single breaks in each of two chromosomes, with exchange of material. A balanced translocation carrier is at increased risk for producing abnormal gametes. The other important translocation is called a robertsonian translocation (or centric fusion), a translocation between two acrocentric chromosomes. Typically the breaks occur close to the centromeres of each chromosome. Transfer of the segments then leads to one very large chromosome and one extremely small one, which is often lost. The significance of this form of translocation lies in the production of abnormal gametes, which in turn leads to abnormal progeny.


5
Given the infant’s karyotype, what should be done next?
Show answer

Karyotypes should be prepared from the peripheral blood lymphocytes of both parents to determine if one carries a der(13;14). This abnormal chromosome carries the designation “der” (for derivative) instead of “t” (for translocation) because the other derivative chromosome, consisting of just the short arms of chromosome 13 and 14, is lost. Thus, technically, the cells do not contain a balanced translocation product.

6
If the patient’s father is a carrier, what will his karyotype be? Why doesn’t he show any phenotypic abnormalities?
Show answer

His karyotype will be 45,XY,(13;14)(q10;q10). He is phenotypically normal because no essential genetic information is carried on the short arms of acrocentric chromosomes. Acrocentric chromosomes include chromosomes 13, 14, 15, 21, and 22, any of which may be involved by robertsonian translocations in phenotypically normal individuals. (Not all robertsonian translocations are inherited. Many of them arise de novo in the individuals who carry them, so it may be that both parents will have normal karyotypes.)

7
Are infants with this karyotype phenotypically different from infants with 47,XY,+13?
Show answer

No, because at the level-of-function regions of the genome, both have triploidy for essential portions of chromosome 13 and no other defects. Most patients with trisomy 13 have this karyotype.

The patient was treated with supportive care but died 9 days after birth.

8
How common are carriers of a robertsonian translocation? If this infant has inherited his robertsonian translocation from one of his parents, what significance does this have for the parents’ reproductive future?
Show answer

About 1 in 1000 individuals carries a robertsonian translocation, most commonly der(13;14). The product of conception of this individual and a second, unaffected, individual has a high risk of spontaneous abortion or demonstrating the trisomy 13 phenotype. This karyotype, in contrast to the previous karyotype, has three separate #13 chromosomes. This is the karyotype of most infants with trisomy 13.

  • #13 chromosomes

9
What is the mechanism (process) that results in karyotypes such as this one?
Show answer

Trisomy 13 with this karyotype (47,XY,+13) results from nondisjunction of chromosome 13 during meiosis.

10
Is this karyotype usually associated with advanced maternal age (>34 years)?
Show answer

Yes, there is a direct correlation between nondisjunction and advanced (>34 years) maternal age.

11
Which chromosomal disorder is most common?
Show answer

Down syndrome is the most common chromosomal disorder and is a major cause of intellectual disability. In the United States, the incidence in newborns is about 1 in 700. Approximately 95% of affected individuals have trisomy 21, so their chromosome count is 47.

12
What is the relationship between Down syndrome incidence and maternal age?
Show answer

Maternal age has a strong influence on the incidence of trisomy 21. It occurs once in 1550 live births in women under age 20, in contrast to 1 in 25 live births for mothers older than age 45. There is a direct correlation between nondisjunction (the most common cause of trisomy) and advanced (>34 years) maternal age. The correlation with maternal age suggests that in most cases meiotic nondisjunction of chromosome 21 occurs in the ovum.

13
How could cases of Down syndrome that are not due to trisomy 21 arise?
Show answer

Most other individuals with trisomy 21 phenotypes have normal chromosome numbers, but the extra chromosome 21 material is present as a translocation. In about 4% of cases of Down syndrome, the extra chromosomal material derives from the presence of a robertsonian translocation of the long arm of chromosome 21 to another acrocentric chromosome (e.g., 22 or 14), similar to the patient in this case.

14
What would be the expected phenotype of a patient who was a mosaic for Down syndrome?
Show answer

Approximately 1% of Down syndrome patients are mosaics, having a mixture of cells with 46 or 47 chromosomes. This mosaicism results from mitotic nondisjunction of chromosome 21 during an early stage of embryogenesis. Symptoms in such cases are variable and milder, depending on the proportion of abnormal cells. In cases of translocation or mosaic Down syndrome, maternal age is of no importance.

15
How does Turner syndrome compare to Down syndrome?
Show answer

Turner syndrome is due to monosomy of the X chromosome whereas Down syndrome is a trisomy of an autosome (chromosome 21). Turner patients are phenotypically female. The mental status of these patients is usually normal, but subtle defects in nonverbal, visual-spatial information processing have been noted, in contrast to the intellectual disability seen in patients with Down syndrome. Both Turner syndrome and Down syndrome are associated with cardiac abnormalities.

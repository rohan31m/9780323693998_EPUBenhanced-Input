Trouble Lumen

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Describe the dysplasia-to-carcinoma in situ sequence in the colon and its molecular basis.
  2. • Compare and contrast benign and malignant tumors with respect to invasiveness and encapsulation, degree of differentiation, and metastatic potential.
  3. • Compare and contrast clinical features of cancer in patients with an inherited predisposition versus sporadic cancer.
  4. • Discuss the pathophysiology of metastasis.

A 28-year-old man presented to his primary care physician with a 6-month history of increasing fatigue and a 3-week history of abdominal pain. Over the last three months he has lost fifteen pounds. Family history includes a maternal uncle and maternal grandfather who died of colon cancer, both in their 30s. Physical exam revealed a thin man with pale conjunctiva and mucous membranes. A test for fecal occult blood was positive.

Colonoscopy revealed the presence of hundreds of polyps (see gross specimen from a patient with the same disease) distributed throughout the colon and a 2 × 2 cm ulcerated mass in the descending colon. Screening computed tomography (CT) scans showed multiple nodules in his lungs and liver.

  • Gross specimen
  • Polyps
  • Normal mucosa
1
Do the clinical features of this patient suggest that he has a hereditary cancer syndrome?
Show answer

Yes. The patient’s young age, positive family history and multiple lesions are suggestive of a hereditary cancer syndrome. Most sporadic colon cancers arise in patients older than 50 years, and most cancer deaths occur between 55 and 75 years of age. The rising incidence with age may be explained by the accumulation of somatic mutations that drive the emergence of malignant neoplasms (discussed later). The decline in immune competence that accompanies aging also may be a factor.

2
What is the most likely site of origin of the original malignancy in this patient with multiple liver and lung lesions?
Show answer

The multiplicity of lesions in the lungs and liver suggests hematogenous spread from a different primary site: the ulcerated mass in the colon. Primary tumors of the lung and liver tend to be solitary until late in the disease course, when localized metastases may be present.

3
What is the most probable diagnosis?
Show answer

In a patient with hundreds of polyps, familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP) is likely. The presence of an ulcerated colonic mass with multiple nodules in the liver and lungs suggests metastatic colonic adenocarcinoma.


4
What is a polyp?
Show answer

When a neoplasm—benign or malignant—produces a macroscopically visible projection above a mucosal surface and projects, for example, into the gastric or colonic lumen, it is termed a polyp. Histologically, there is often a stalk attached to normal mucosa. If the polyp has dysplastic glandular tissue, it is called an adenomatous polyp.

  • Polyp
  • Stalk
  • Normal mucosa
  • Muscularis
  • Lumen
5
What would histologic examination of one of the polyps most likely show?
Show answer

Adenomatous polyps are covered by dysplastic epithelium characterized by nuclear hyperchromasia. Unlike normal intestinal epithelium that produces mucin, dysplastic cells usually lose this specialized function.


  • Adenomatous polyp
  • Mucin
  • Dysplastic epithelium
  • Normal epithelium
6
Which genetic alterations are seen in patients with this syndrome?
Show answer

Patients with familial adenomatous polyposis are born with loss or mutation of one copy of the APC gene, which is on the long arm of chromosome 5 (5q), and as a result their cells have only one functional copy of APC. Cancer develops when the wild-type allele of APC is mutated or lost in the colonic epithelium.

7
What is the role of APC in the development of this disease?
Show answer

APC is a negative regulator of the transcription factor β-catenin, a component of the WNT signaling pathway. Normally, APC forms a complex with β-catenin and causes its destruction. In the absence of APC, destruction of β-catenin cannot occur and it translocates to the nucleus where it causes unregulated transcription of growth promoting genes such as MYC. leading to excessive proliferation and adenoma formation.


8
How does the genotype of the polyps differ from the germline genotype in this patient?
Show answer

In this patient, one copy of the APC gene was lost in the germline and both copies were lost in the polyp. APC is a classic tumor suppressor gene: loss of function of both alleles is necessary for adenomas to form. Although biallelic loss can happen sporadically, the risk of this occurring in any individual cell is much higher in carriers with only one normal APC allele. These acquired mutations take the form of sporadic mutations or deletions that completely abolish APC function.

9
Which test would prove that each polyp arose independently (e.g., is genetically distinct)?
Show answer

DNA sequencing of multiple adenomas would show that each has a unique acquired mutation that abolishes the function of the single normal germline APC gene.

10
What features distinguish an adenoma from colon carcinoma?
Show answer

Adenomas are well circumscribed, slow growing, and well differentiated. Although there is dysplasia, there is no invasion. Colon carcinomas can manifest as large, ulcerated lesions or masses. Because of invasion, their margins are irregular and often elevated. Histologically, they may be well or poorly differentiated. There is typically a stromal reaction, which often takes the form of fibrosis, and variable degrees of immune/inflammatory response composed of infiltrates of macrophages and lymphocytes. Abundant fibrosis due to collagen deposition is called desmoplasia.

  • Colon with adenomas
  • Polyps
  • Colon with carcinoma
  • Normal mucosa
  • Elevated margins
  • Ulcerated carcinoma
  • Carcinoma, histology
11
What is the term for a malignant tumor that forms neoplastic glands?
Show answer

Adenocarcinoma.

12
Which term is used to describe cells and nuclei with irregular sizes and shapes?
Show answer

The term pleomorphism describes cells and nuclei with irregular sizes and shapes.

13
What is the proposed model for the development of colorectal carcinoma from an adenoma (the adenoma-carcinoma sequence)?
Show answer

Given that malignant tumors must acquire multiple “hallmarks” of cancer, it follows that cancers result from the stepwise accumulation of multiple mutations that act in complementary ways to produce a fully malignant tumor. A classic example of incremental acquisition of the malignant phenotype is found in colon carcinoma. Many of these cancers evolve through a series of morphologically identifiable stages: colon epithelial hyperplasia followed by formation of adenomas that progressively enlarge and ultimately undergo malignant transformation. While multiple mutations, including gain of oncogenes and loss of tumor suppressors, are required for carcinogenesis, the precise temporal sequence of mutations does not appear to be fixed and may be different in each organ and tumor type.


14
Based on this model, which alteration is associated with tumor initiation?
Show answer

Mutation of the APC gene is considered the initiating event.

15
Which alterations are associated with tumor progression?
Show answer

The subsequent mutations in RAS, SMAD4 (caused by LOH of chromosome 18q), and TP53 result in tumor progression (e.g., the tumor cells acquire new properties that make the cells more abnormal in behavior).

16
How does genomic instability influence neoplastic transformation?
Show answer

Genomic instability contributes to neoplastic transformation indirectly by predisposing to mutations in proto-oncogenes and tumor suppressor genes.

17
How does hereditary nonpolyposis colon cancer (HNPCC) differ from FAP?
Show answer

HNPCC results from inherited mutations in genes that are required for repair of a particular type of DNA damage; the process by which this is carried out is called mismatch repair. Loss of function mutations in DNA damage repair genes contribute to carcinogenesis by impairing the ability of cells to repair nonlethal genetic damage in other genes, some of which may be proto-oncogenes or tumor suppressor genes. Cancers in patients with HNPCC often arise from adenomatous polyps, but polyps are not dramatically increased in number, suggesting that in HNPCC the transition from adenoma to carcinoma is accelerated. HNPCC cancers are also associated with a different set of complementary driver mutations in genes such as BAX (a regulator of apoptosis) and TGFBR1 (encodes the TGF-beta receptor). A subset of sporadic colon cancers also arises through this alternative colon carcinogenesis pathway.

18
Colon carcinomas arising through the mismatch repair pathway are genomically unstable and accumulate enormous numbers of passenger mutations. How might this influence the host response to the developing tumor and the treatment of such cancers?
Show answer

By chance, some of these passenger mutations will fall in the coding sequences of genes, producing neoantigens that can be recognized by the host immune system. Mismatch repair–defective cancers are characterized by having large numbers of infiltrating T cells and a high probability of response to immune checkpoint therapies, both of which appear to reflect the high burden of neoantigens that are present in these tumors.

The patient was offered chemotherapy, but he opted for palliative care. Over the next four weeks, he became increasingly thin, despite a good appetite, with bitemporal muscle wasting before he died, 5 weeks after diagnosis.

19
What is the cause of this patient’s extreme weight loss?
Show answer

Individuals with cancer commonly suffer progressive loss of body fat and lean body mass accompanied by profound weakness, anorexia, and anemia, referred to as cachexia. Cachexia is associated with (1) loss of fat and lean muscle; (2) elevated basal metabolic rate; and (3) evidence of systemic inflammation (i.e., an increase in acute-phase reactants). Tumor necrosis factor (formerly known as cachectin) is a leading suspect among several mediators released from immune cells that may contribute to cachexia. Humoral factors released from tumor cells have been implicated in the loss of muscle mass, but its pathogenesis remains uncertain.

20
What is the tumor marker in the serum associated with this tumor? What is its main clinical use?
Show answer

Carcinoma of the colon is associated with the presence of carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) in the serum. This marker is not specific for colon carcinoma, however, because it also may be present in the serum of patients with gastric cancer or breast cancer. Some non-neoplastic conditions may also be associated with elevations of serum CEA. Therefore, CEA is of limited utility as a diagnostic marker. However, once a diagnosis of colon cancer is established, it is useful in following the response of the tumor to therapy.

An autopsy was performed. His liver showed multiple nodules of various sizes, consistent with the CT findings.


  • Liver, gross
  • Metastatic colon cancer
21
How does the spread of carcinomas differ from the metastatic pattern of sarcomas?
Show answer

Transport through the lymphatic system is the most common pathway for the initial dissemination of carcinomas, and regional lymph nodes are involved early. Sarcomas may also use this route, although lymph node involvement is less common in this tumor type. Hematogenous spread of sarcomas frequently results in liver and lung metastases. The emphasis on lymphatic spread for carcinomas and hematogenous spread for sarcomas is somewhat misleading because ultimately, there are numerous interconnections between the vascular and the lymphatic systems. This explains the propensity of colon carcinoma to spread to the liver via the portal vein.

22
Why are larger tumors more prone to metastasis?
Show answer

Large tumors have likely gone unnoticed for a long period of time, during which time individual tumor cells can accumulate additional mutations. This evolution is referred to as tumor progression. Although some of these mutations may be lethal, others may affect the function of cancer genes, thereby making this subclone of cells more adept at growth, survival, invasion, metastasis, or immune evasion.

23
In addition to size, which tumor characteristics increase the probability of distant metastasis?
Show answer

The less well differentiated a tumor is, the greater the probability that it will behave aggressively (i.e., anaplastic tumors generally behave aggressively). The presence of lymphatic and vascular invasion (tumor cells within vascular channels at the site of the primary tumor) also correlates with the increased risk of metastasis.

24
Which steps are necessary for tumor cells to invade the underlying stroma?
Show answer

A carcinoma first must breach the underlying basement membrane, then traverse the interstitial connective tissue, and ultimately gain access to the circulation by penetrating the vascular basement membrane. First, there must be loosening or loss of intercellular connections, for example, by downregulation of adhesion molecules such as E-cadherin. Next, the tumor cells must increase production of proteolytic enzymes and decrease production of protease inhibitors to degrade the basement membrane and interstitial connective tissue. Chemotactic factors in the microenvironment allow the tumor cell to migrate into the stroma. This process is repeated in reverse when tumor cell emboli extravasate at a distant site.


25
Once cancers invade locally, what is the rate-limiting step in the establishment of metastatic disease?
Show answer

Sensitive analyses have shown that most clinically relevant cancers shed large numbers of tumor cells into the bloodstream every day. Therefore, the rate-limiting, inefficient step is the extravasation and growth of circulating tumor cells at metastatic sites.

26
Which two factors determine the site(s) at which metastatic disease tends to occur in particular tumors?
Show answer

Metastatic site is determined by anatomy and tumor tropism. Tissues that are immediately downstream of cancers receive tumor cells first and are therefore at higher risk of developing metastatic disease. However, this cannot explain certain characteristic patterns of spread. For example, melanoma of the eye (uveal melanoma) virtually always spreads to the liver. Presumably this is because some aspect of the liver microenvironment preferentially supports the growth of this type of cancer cell. Conversely, other tissue sites (e.g., spleen, heart, kidney) are relatively resistant to metastases despite receiving a substantial fraction of cardiac output.

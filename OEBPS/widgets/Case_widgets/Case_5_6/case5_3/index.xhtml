Objectives
  1. • Describe the role of ultraviolet rays in carcinogenesis.
  2. • Discuss the importance of the host immunologic response and tumor immunoevasion in cancer.
  3. • Analyze the role of p53, cyclin dependent kinases/cyclin dependent kinase inhibitors, telomerase, and BRAF in carcinogenesis.
  4. • Explain the importance of tumor heterogeneity in evolving resistance to therapy.

A 35-year-old woman presented to her primary care physician with a 1-week history of a mass in her left axilla. Although she does not drink alcohol or smoke, she stated that she has been going to tanning salons once or twice per month since college. Her family history was noncontributory.

1
Which malignancies are associated with increased exposure to ultraviolet (UV) rays?
Show answer

Exposure to UV rays derived from the sun or a tanning bed, particularly in fair-skinned individuals, is associated with an increased incidence of squamous cell carcinoma, basal cell carcinoma, and melanoma of the skin. The degree of risk depends on the type of UV rays, the intensity of exposure, and the quantity of the light absorbing “protective mantle” of melanin in the skin.

2
How are UV rays carcinogenic?
Show answer

The UV portion of the solar spectrum can be divided into three types based on wavelength: UVA, UVB, and UVC. Of these, UVB is believed to be responsible for the induction of cutaneous cancers. The carcinogenicity of UVB light is caused by formation of pyrimidine dimers in DNA. When the energy in a photon of UV light is absorbed by DNA, it produces a chemical reaction that leads to covalent crosslinking of pyrimidine bases, particularly adjacent thymidine residues in the same strand of DNA. This distorts the DNA helix and prevents proper pairing of the dimer with bases in the opposite DNA strand. Pyrimidine dimers are repaired by the nucleotide excision repair pathway, which is imprecise and prone to making errors that result in mutations.

Physical examination revealed a deeply tanned woman with a left axillary mass approximately 3 cm in size that was firm and fixed. Breast examination was unremarkable, but examination of the skin revealed a 1.1 cm pigmented lesion on the back of her left forearm.

4
Which pathologic feature of cutaneous melanoma is most closely correlated with metastasis?
Show answer

The risk of metastasis correlates with the depth of invasion. Increasing depth likely reflects the tumor’s growth rate, age, and invasiveness, all of which could contribute to a higher risk of metastasis.

  • Skin, histology
  • Lymphocytic response
5
Invasive melanomas are often associated with an intense dermal lymphocytic response. What is the most likely reason for this?
Show answer

Some of the numerous mutations in UV-induced melanoma occur in the coding regions of genes, thereby altering the sequence of the encoded proteins. These represent potential tumor neoantigens, some of which bear sequences that bind class I MHC molecules, enabling their recognition by the adaptive immune system and generation of a host immune response. Most of the tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes are T cells, which may be responding to such antigens.

6
Occasionally patients with melanoma develop vitiligo, the loss of skin pigmentation. What might explain this phenomenon?
Show answer

T cells in patients with melanoma sometimes develop immune responses to normal melanocyte antigens, such as the enzyme tyrosinase, which is required for melanin synthesis. Although tyrosinase is expressed in normal melanocytes, it is normally produced in such small amounts and in so few normal cells that it is not recognized by the immune system and fails to induce tolerance.

7
What is the likely cause of this patient’s melanoma and how could this be proven?
Show answer

As mentioned previously, cutaneous melanomas that arise in sun-exposed skin (or skin exposed in tanning beds) are strongly associated with UV-induced DNA damage. The nucleotide excision repair pathway is not completely accurate and creates mutations that bear a sequence signature identifiable by DNA sequencing. Finding this signature can assign a likely cause to any individual melanoma. This has even been done in cases of metastatic melanoma where no primary tumor is found, yet the metastasis has a UV signature, proving that it must have come from a cutaneous tumor that regressed, possibly because of the host immune response.

8
What is considered a precursor lesion for this malignancy?
Show answer

Dysplastic nevi may be direct precursors of melanoma. Multiple dysplastic nevi are a marker of an increased risk for melanoma. Although dysplastic nevi can give rise to melanoma, the vast majority of such lesions are clinically stable and never progress.

9
Patients with dysplastic nevus syndrome frequently have loss of function mutations in CDKN2A. How might this contribute to the pathogenesis of these lesions?
Show answer

CDKN2A encodes two tumor suppressor genes: p16/INK4a and p14ARF. p16/INK4a is a negative regulator of cyclin-dependent kinase 4 (CDK4) and cyclin-dependent kinase 6 (CDK6). Defects in cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitors interfere with the function of the G1/S checkpoint, allowing cells with damaged DNA to divide, resulting in mutated daughter cells with the potential to develop into malignant tumors. p14ARF stabilizes the p53 tumor suppressor protein by inhibiting MDM2, which promotes p53 degradation; hence, loss of p14-ARF interferes with p53 function.


10
What is the role of p53 in the normal cell?
Show answer

p53 is a transcription factor that thwarts neoplastic transformation by three interlocking mechanisms: (1) activation of temporary cell cycle arrest (termed quiescence); (2) induction of permanent cell cycle arrest (termed senescence); and (3) triggering of programmed cell death (apoptosis). p53-mediated cell cycle arrest may be considered the primordial response to DNA damage. p53 induces expression of DNA damage repair genes and, if DNA damage is repaired successfully, p53 upregulates transcription of MDM2, leading to its own destruction and relief of the cell cycle block. If the damage cannot be repaired, the cell may enter p53-induced senescence or undergo p53-directed apoptosis.

Core biopsies of the left axillary mass revealed metastatic melanoma. Next generation sequencing was performed on DNA prepared from this sample. Driver mutations were identified in the telomerase promoter region, the tumor suppressor CDK2NA (discussed previously), and BRAF.

11
How do telomerase and BRAF contribute to the pathogenesis of cancer?
Show answer

Telomerase maintains telomeres, thereby preventing senescence/cell death associated with telomere erosion over time in proliferating cells and contributing to immortality. BRAF encodes a serine/threonine kinase that participates in the MAP kinase pathway, which lies downstream of RAS and promotes cell growth and survival. The most common BRAF mutation in melanoma, a substitution of valine for glutamate at residue 600, is a gain-of-function mutation that is found in many different cancers, including 40%-60% of melanomas.

A screening computed tomography (CT) scan was performed 3 days after the patient’s surgery. Several bilateral lung nodules were identified. In view of the patient’s BRAF mutation, BRAF inhibitor therapy was initiated. The patient had an excellent response with resolution of her lung nodules; however, 8 months later, she experienced a seizure. Head CT showed a 3-cm enhancing parietal mass and additional nodules in her liver.

12
What is the basis for the development of BRAF-inhibitor resistance?
Show answer

Tumors evolve genetically during their growth and spread under the pressure of Darwinian selection (survival of the fittest). During this process, there is competition among tumor cells for access to nutrients and microenvironmental niches, and subclones with the capacity to overgrow their predecessors tend to “win” this Darwinian contest and dominate the tumor mass, only to be replaced by other, still more malignant subclones. This pernicious tendency of tumors to become more aggressive over time is referred to as tumor progression. As a result, even though malignant tumors are clonal in origin, by the time they become clinically evident, their constituent cells are often extremely heterogeneous genetically, particularly those tumors with a mutator phenotype. Effective targeted therapy presents the tumor cells with a new, powerful selective force, one that eliminates sensitive clones and provides a large selective advantage to subclones that are resistant to targeted therapy. It is important to understand that most evidence suggests that resistant subclones are present before targeted therapy is initiated, but have no selective advantage until treatment commences. This experience highlights the problematic nature of tumor heterogeneity and the challenge it presents for curing patients with advanced cancers.


13
What is the likely molecular basis for the development of resistance to the BRAF inhibitor? Which treatment options remain for the patient?
Show answer

Almost all tumors in which BRAF-inhibitor resistance develops show evidence of reactivation of the MAP kinase pathway due to outgrowth of subclones with additional mutations, often in a kinase called MEK1 that lies immediately downstream of BRAF. This allows the pathway to signal even in the presence of the drug. The logical approach is to treat such tumors with a MEK1 inhibitor. An alternative approach is to begin treatment with a combination of BRAF and MEK1 inhibitors, which may avoid or delay the development of resistance, much like multidrug therapy for infectious diseases such as tuberculosis.

14
Melanoma is a tumor with a high mutational burden. Given this, what acquired cancer hallmark is likely to be particularly important in this form of cancer?
Show answer

Tumors with high mutational burdens likely express many neoantigens, and it may be particularly important for such cancers to develop defenses against the host immune response. Melanomas, for example, often downregulate expression of class I MHC antigens, or even have mutations involving MHC class I genes.

Resequencing of a needle core biopsy of a liver metastasis from this patient reveals the presence of a glutamine-to-proline substitution at residue 56 of MEK1, which is known to convey resistance to BRAF inhibitors. She is started on a MEK1 inhibitor, but cannot tolerate the drug due to toxicity.

15
In patients with tumors with a high mutational burden, such as this one, which therapy should be considered?
Show answer

A logical choice would be a so-called immune checkpoint inhibitor. Antibodies that block the PD-1 receptor, which mediates signals that trigger T cell exhaustion, and antibodies that block CTLA-4, a decoy molecule that interferes with T cell activation, can trigger effective T cell responses against melanoma. The combination of both agents produces greater anti-tumor responses than either agent alone, but also is more likely to be associated with complications.


16
What are some of the complications of immune checkpoint therapies?
Show answer

These inhibitors can breakdown immune tolerance of normal host tissue, leading to serious autoimmune reactions. Among the most common of these complications are colitis, hepatitis, endocrinopathies, and pneumonitis.

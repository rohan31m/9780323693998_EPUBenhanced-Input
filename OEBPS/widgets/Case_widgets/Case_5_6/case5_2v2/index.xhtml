Objectives
  1. • List the hallmarks of cancer and their molecular basis.
  2. • Review the principles of signal transduction in cells and identify the different classes of growth factors, growth factor receptors, signal-transducing molecules, and nuclear regulatory factors.
  3. • Discuss the functions of the RAS, ABL, and MYC gene products and the types of neoplasms that carry alterations in these genes.
  4. • Relate the metabolic basis of Warburg effect to the growth of cancer cells and its clinical relevance.
  5. • Explain the concept of tumor clonality and its usefulness in diagnosis.
  6. • Discuss the concept of tumorigenesis as a multistep process involving the accumulation of genetic alterations within precursor lesions.

A 44-year-old woman presented to her primary care physician with a 2-month history of fatigue and weight loss and a 2-week history of low-grade fever, early satiety, and left upper quadrant pain. On physical examination, she had no palpable lymphadenopathy, but there was evidence of splenomegaly.

Examination of the peripheral blood smear revealed leukocytosis (white blood cell [WBC] count of 225,000/mm3) consisting mostly of myeloid lineage cells at different stages of maturation, including granulocytes (neutrophils, basophils, and eosinophils), myelocytes, and a few myeloblasts. In the higher power image, the cell with a large nucleus and scant cytoplasm is a myeloblast. The remaining white blood cells are granulocytes at various stages of differentiation.

Cytogenetic analysis of spontaneously dividing peripheral blood WBCs revealed the presence of a reciprocal (9;22)(q34;q11) translocation.

1
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

A reciprocal translocation of chromosomes 9 and 22 fuses the BCR and ABL genes located at q11 and q34, respectively. The derivative chromosome 22 is referred to as the Philadelphia chromosome, which is seen in chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) and some B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemias.

  • Karyotype
  • Philadelphia chromosome
  • Chr 9 translocation
2
What is the key oncogenic driver mutation in chronic myeloid leukemia (CML)?
Show answer

The BCR-ABL fusion gene encodes a constitutively active BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase. In 90% of CML cases the BCR-ABL fusion gene is created by the reciprocal chromosomal translocation that yields the Philadelphia chromosome, while the remaining cases of CML are associated with other, more complex chromosomal rearrangements that also create BCR-ABL fusion genes.

3
How does BCR-ABL contribute to the pathogenesis of chronic myeloid leukemia?
Show answer

The potent constitutive tyrosine kinase activity of the chimeric BCR-ABL protein mimics the effects of normal growth factor receptors and activates the RAS pathway and the PI3 kinase pathway. Thus, BCR-ABL+ progenitor cells proliferate excessively. Because differentiation is relatively unaffected, the production of normal granulocytes and platelets is increased (red cell production is usually mildly suppressed, for uncertain reasons).


4
How is the BCR-ABL fusion gene detected?
Show answer

This fusion can be identified with several specific tests. One is fluorescent in-situ hybridization (FISH). In this case, tumor cells are hybridized to a DNA probe for ABL with a red fluorescent tag and a BCR probe that is labeled green. When the two probes are in close proximity, as occurs in the (9;22) translocation, the overlapping probes produce a yellow signal in interphase nuclei. Pairing of sister chromatids during metaphase may produce a single dot or a pair of closely spaced dots The presence of the fusion gene can also be inferred from detection of a BCR-ABL fusion mRNA transcript, which is done through a method that involves reverse transcription of RNA into DNA followed by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) with BCR- and ABL-specific primer pairs (RT-PCR).



5
What are the classes of normal regulatory genes that are frequently mutated in cancer?
Show answer

Growth-promoting proto-oncogenes, growth-inhibiting tumor suppressor genes, genes that regulate programmed cell death (apoptosis), genes that are responsible for DNA repair, and genes that regulate interactions between cancer cells and the immune system.

6
How do mutations in proto-oncogenes cause cancer?
Show answer

Mutations that activate proto-oncogenes convert them into oncogenes, which encode constitutively active proteins (oncoproteins) that transmit progrowth signals even in the absence of growth factors. Because these mutations cause a “gain-of-function,” they can transform cells despite the presence of a normal copy of the same gene.

8
How do chromosomal translocations activate proto-oncogenes?
Show answer

Proto-oncogenes can be activated by promoter or enhancer substitution, in which the translocation results in overexpression of a proto-oncogene by swapping its regulatory elements with those of another gene, typically one that is highly expressed. It can also be caused by formation of a fusion gene, in which the coding sequences of two genes are fused in part or in whole, leading to the expression of a novel chimeric protein with oncogenic properties.

9
How do mutations in tumor suppressor genes cause cancer?
Show answer

Mutations that affect tumor suppressor genes generally cause a “loss-of-function,” and in most instances both alleles must be damaged before transformation can occur. Thus, mutated tumor suppressor genes usually behave in a recessive fashion.

11
How do mutations in DNA repair genes cause cancer?
Show answer

Loss-of-function mutations affecting DNA repair genes contribute to carcinogenesis indirectly by impairing the ability of the cell to recognize and repair nonlethal genetic damage in other genes. As a result, affected cells acquire mutations at an accelerated rate, a state referred to as a mutator phenotype that is marked by genomic instability.

13
How does BCR-ABL contribute to the pathogenesis of CML?
Show answer

This fusion gene encodes a BCR-ABL hybrid protein containing the ABL tyrosine kinase domain and a BCR domain that self-associates, an event that unleashes constitutive tyrosine kinase activity. Of interest, the BCR-ABL protein activates all of the signals that are downstream of RAS, making it a potent stimulator of cell growth.


14
What is the role of RAS in normal cells?
Show answer

RAS is a member of a family of small G proteins that bind guanosine nucleotides (guanosine triphosphate [GTP] and guanosine diphosphate [GDP]). Activated RAS stimulates downstream regulators of proliferation by several interconnected pathways that converge on the nucleus and alter the expression of genes that regulate growth, such as MYC.


15
What is the role of MYC in normal cells?
Show answer

The MYC proto-oncogene encodes a nuclear transcription factor that belongs to the immediate early response genes, which are rapidly and transiently induced by RAS/MAPK signaling following growth factor stimulation of quiescent cells. MYC activates the expression of many genes that are involved in cell growth and can be considered a master transcriptional regulator of pro-growth anabolic metabolism. Among the consequences of changes in metabolism induced by genes that are regulated by MYC is a switch to aerobic glycolysis, the so-called Warburg effect.

16
What is the Warburg effect, and what is its clinical relevance?
Show answer

Even in the presence of ample oxygen, cancer cells demonstrate a form of cellular metabolism characterized by high levels of glucose uptake and increased conversion of glucose to lactose (fermentation) via the glycolytic pathway. This phenomenon is called the Warburg effect (also known as aerobic glycolysis). Beyond lactate production, the Warburg effect also generates metabolic intermediates from the glycolytic pathway and tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, also known as the Krebs cycle; these are then used in other pathways to make cellular building blocks (amino acids, fatty acids, nucleotides). Clinically, the “glucose-hunger” of tumors is used to visualize tumors via positron emission tomography (PET) scanning, in which patients are injected with 18F-fluorodeoxyglucose, a glucose derivative that is preferentially taken up into tumor cells (as well as normal, actively dividing tissues such as bone marrow).

What is the role of polymerase chain reaction (PCR) in gauging the response of chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) to therapy?
Show answer

Because PCR amplifies specific nucleic acid segments, it can be used to detect rare DNA or RNA sequences. In this case, the presence of the BCR-ABL fusion gene is detected by converting RNA into complementary DNA (cDNA) using reverse transcriptase, and then performing PCR on the cDNA template with one primer located in exon 9 of the BCR gene and the other located in exon 3 of the ABL gene. Because the regions recognized by each primer have to be on the same cDNA for amplification to occur, a PCR product is generated only if the sample contains the BCR-ABL mRNA fusion transcript. In real-time PCR, the product is detected at the end of each PCR cycle with a fluorescently labeled probe. No product is generated from the negative control, which is a cell line without a BCR-ABL fusion gene, or from the control lacking RNA. The positive control is a cell line with the BCR-ABL translocation. A strong signal is seen in the patient sample. By including controls for housekeeping gene transcripts, it is possible to quantify the level of BCR-ABL transcript, which is now done routinely to follow the response of CML to BCR-ABL inhibitors.


A different drug was prescribed but had intolerable side effects. The patient developed fever and bone pain. Physical examination revealed the reappearance of splenomegaly and mild lymphadenopathy. Cytogenetic analysis showed a predominance of cells morphologically consistent with lymphoblasts with reduplication of the (9;22) translocation consistent with development of lymphoid blast crisis from chronic myeloid leukemia. A peripheral blood smear showed marked leukocytosis. However, in contrast to the patient's blood smear at diagnosis, very few maturing myeloid cells were present; instead, the vast majority of the cells were of intermediate size (larger than neutrophils and mature lymphocytes, but not as large as the myeloblasts seen in acute myeloid leukemia), with dispersed chromatin and distinct nucleoli consistent with lymphoblasts. Flow cytometry confirmed that the cells were positive for the B cell markers CD19 and CD10 and terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase (TdT) but negative for myeloperoxidase, confirming the diagnosis of lymphoid blast crisis of the patient's chronic myeloid leukemia. Although myeloid blast crisis is more common, in 20%-30% of cases of chronic myeloid leukemia undergoing transformation to acute leukemia the blasts have a lymphoid phenotype indistinguishable from that of de novo B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL).

What is the significance of the Philadelphia chromosome in this lymphoid leukemia?
Show answer

The presence of the Philadelphia chromosome in these lymphoid cells confirms that they are derived from same clone that gave rise to original CML. The original cell transformed in this leukemia was a pluripotent stem cell that produced the abnormal myeloid cells in the first phase of the disease. Subsequently, additional mutations affected B lymphoid lineage cells derived from the malignant clone, producing the blast phase.

What is the significance of the additional chromosomal abnormalities seen in the second presentation?
Show answer

The presence of additional chromosomal abnormalities signifies genomic instability and progression of the malignancy.

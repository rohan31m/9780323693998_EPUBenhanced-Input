Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast the clinical findings and pathogenesis of Turner syndrome and Klinefelter syndrome.
  2. • Explain why imbalances in sex chromosomes are better tolerated than imbalances in autosomal chromosomes.
  3. • Describe the process of X inactivation and its clinical significance in imbalances of sex chromosomes.

A 15-year-old girl presented to her primary care physician because she had not reached menarche or developed breasts. Physical examination revealed a girl of short stature (4 feet tall) with a webbed neck, broad chest with wide set nipples, high arched palate, multiple pigmented nevi, and low set ears. She was a Tanner stage 1 (no breast development). Cardiovascular exam was positive for a systolic ejection click. Blood pressure in both arms was 125/95 mmHg, but her pulse was barely palpable in her lower extremities.

  • Clinical presentation
  • Webbed neck
  • Multiple pigmented nevi
1
Which cardiovascular defect is the most likely cause of the patient’s elevated upper extremity blood pressure with minimal lower extremity pulses?
Show answer

Differential blood pressure between the upper and lower extremities is highly suggestive of coarctation of the aorta.

An echocardiogram confirmed a bicuspid aortic valve and an angiogram of the aorta revealed coarctation of the aorta just distal to the left subclavian artery. An abdominopelvic ultrasound showed a horseshoe kidney and streak ovaries.

2
What would be the likely karyotypic findings?
Show answer

The physical findings (webbed neck, short stature, broad chest with widely spaced nipples, high arched palate, and low set ears) combined with coarctation of the aorta and horseshoe kidneys are consistent with Turner syndrome. Turner syndrome is caused by complete or partial monosomy of the X chromosome.

A blood sample yielded a 45,X karyotype, the most common karyotype found in Turner syndrome. Alternatively, many patients with Turner syndrome have a second karyotypically abnormal cell line with 46 chromosomes, including one normal X chromosome and one structurally abnormal X chromosome.

  • Karyotype
  • Single sex chromosome
3
What is X inactivation?
Show answer

In 1961, Mary Lyon outlined the idea of X inactivation, now commonly know as lyonization. It states that (1) only one of the X chromosomes is genetically active; (2) the other X of either maternal or paternal origin undergoes inactivation through epigenetic modifications; (3) inactivation of either the maternal or paternal X occurs at random in all the cells of the blastocyst on or about day 5.5 of embryonic life; and (4) inactivation of the same X chromosome persists in all the cells derived from each precursor cell.

4
Phenotypically typical females with a 46,XX karyotype have only one active X chromosome due to X inactivation; why don’t they have a Turner syndrome phenotype?
Show answer

Not all of the genes on the X chromosome are subject to inactivation; some on the “inactive” X chromosome remain active throughout life and are presumed to be necessary for normal growth and development. Many of the genes that escape X inactivation are at the ends of the X chromosome arms in so-called “pseudoautosomal regions." Such genes have functional homologues on the Y chromosome, providing males with two active copies of these genes as well. The inactive X chromosome is selectively reactivated in oogonia before the first meiotic division, so two X chromosomes are presumably necessary for female gametogenesis. It is hypothesized that decreased dosage (haploinsufficiency) of genes that normally escape X inactivation is responsible for the Turner phenotype.

5
Is Turner syndrome associated with advanced maternal age (>34 years)?
Show answer

No, this is a feature of trisomies and Klinefelter syndrome.

6
Why do patients with Turner syndrome have a webbed neck?
Show answer

Fetuses with Turner syndrome typically have a large, cervical cystic hygroma composed of fluid-filled, massively dilated lymphatic channels and surrounding connective tissue. These lesions resolve by the time of birth, but the webbed neck of Turner syndrome is evidence of their earlier presence. Neonates with Turner syndrome often have puffy hands and feet, also related to lymphatic abnormalities.

7
Why is there substantial variation in phenotypes associated with Turner syndrome?
Show answer

Some affected patients are genetic mosaics, in which some cells have the Turner’s genotype (45,X) and others are normal (46,XX). Mosaicism results from mitotic errors in early development that give rise to two or more populations of cells with different chromosomal complements in the same individual. The phenotype in these patients may be milder, depending mainly on the proportion of 45,X cells in various tissues.  

8
How often are pregnancies with a 45,X karyotype spontaneously aborted?
Show answer

Spontaneous abortion occurs in 99 of pregnancies with 45,X karyotype.

9
For which diseases are patients with Turner syndrome at increased risk?
Show answer

Approximately 50 of patients develop autoantibodies that react with the thyroid gland, and up to half of these patients develop clinically manifested hypothyroidism. Glucose intolerance, obesity, and insulin resistance are seen in a minority of patients.

10
Is this girl likely to be intellectually disabled?
Show answer

No. Intellectual disability is not part of Turner syndrome.

11
Which numerical abnormalities of chromosomes are associated with intellectual disability?
Show answer

Down syndrome (trisomy 21), increased numbers of X chromosomes (e.g. XXY, XXXY), and Klinefelter syndrome are all associated with intellectual disability.

12
What is the cause of Klinefelter syndrome and what are its characteristic phenotypic features?
Show answer

Klinefelter syndrome is a condition of males caused by the presence of at least one extra X chromosome (47,XXY). The main phenotypic features are hypogonadism, testicular atrophy, and infertility; an increase in length between the soles and the pubic bone, which creates the appearance of an elongated body; eunuchoid body habitus, with reduced facial, body, and pubic hair and gynecomastia; and variable, usually mild, intellectual impairment.

13
Why doesn’t X-chromosome inactivation normalize the extra X chromosomes in Klinefelter syndrome?
Show answer

X-inactivation (lyonization) of the extra copy (or copies) of the X chromosome occurs, but the pseudoautosomal regions escape inactivation. Thus, there is at least one extra copy of each active gene in the pseudoautosomal region (the same genes that are haploinsufficient in Turner syndrome), and “overactivity” of these genes causes the observed abnormalities.

14
How is Turner syndrome similar to Klinefelter syndrome?
Show answer

In both diseases, there is (1) infertility (an important effect of numerical abnormalities in the X chromosome); (2) evidence of hypogonadism (more pronounced in Turner syndrome than in Klinefelter syndrome: girls with Turner syndrome usually have no secondary sexual development or puberty, whereas boys with Klinefelter syndrome develop facial and pubic hair with underdeveloped genitalia); and (3) normal intelligence or mild mental impairment.

15
What are the differences between Turner syndrome and Klinefelter syndrome with respect to their clinical features and the source of the causative X chromosome abnormalities?
Show answer

In addition to altered sexual development, Turner syndrome is associated with developmental malformations (e.g., developmental abnormalities of the lymphatic system, coarctation of the aorta, horseshoe kidney). Developmental malformations are not increased in Klinefelter syndrome. Most patients with Turner syndrome are diagnosed due to altered sexual development, whereas many males with Klinefelter syndrome are never diagnosed. Klinefelter syndrome is associated with advanced maternal age; Turner syndrome is not. DNA analysis has shown that the extra X chromosome in about half of patients with Klinefelter syndrome is of maternal origin, and those cases are strongly associated with advanced maternal age, similar to other trisomies. By contrast, DNA analysis has shown that the single X chromosome in most (approximately 80) Turner syndrome patients is of maternal origin (i.e., a normal egg was fertilized by a sperm without a sex chromosome).

16
What is the pathogenesis of Klinefelter syndrome?
Show answer

The classic pattern of Klinefelter syndrome is associated with a 47,XXY karyotype (90 of cases). This complement of chromosomes results from nondisjunction during the meiotic divisions in the germ cells of one of the parents. Maternal and paternal nondisjunction at the first meiotic division are roughly equally involved.

17
Patients with Klinefelter syndrome are at increased risk for which diseases?
Show answer

Patients with Klinefelter syndrome have a higher risk for breast cancer (20 times more common than in unaffected males), extragonadal germ cell tumors, and autoimmune diseases such as systemic lupus erythematosus.

18
How do imbalances in sex chromosomes compare to imbalances in autosomal chromosomes?
Show answer

Genetic diseases associated with changes involving the sex chromosomes are far more common than those related to autosomal aberrations because imbalances (excess or loss) of sex chromosomes are much better tolerated than are similar imbalances of autosomes. In large part, this latitude relates to two factors that are peculiar to the sex chromosomes: (1) lyonization or inactivation of all but one X chromosome and (2) the modest amount of genetic material carried by the Y chromosome.

Objective
  1. • Compare and contrast the different lysosomal storage diseases in terms of pathologic findings, clinical presentation, and natural history.
  2. • Relate the clinical features of lysosomal storage diseases to the underlying molecular abnormality.

A 49-year-old woman presented to her primary care physician with a 3-week history of increasing pain in her right leg and hip and a 3-month history of worsening fatigue and abdominal fullness. At presentation, she could no longer bear weight on her right leg. Her medical history was unremarkable except that her obstetrician had noted mild splenomegaly during her third pregnancy, when she was 30 years old. She declined further evaluation at that time and had felt well since then before the onset of her current symptoms. Family history revealed three healthy children and a younger brother with “back trouble.”

Physical examination revealed marked splenomegaly, hepatomegaly, and pallor of her mucous membranes. X-rays of her right hip and leg showed two lytic lesions in the right femur and a linear fracture in the femoral head and neck. A magnetic resonance image (MRI) of her right leg showed abnormalities of the marrow density suggestive of an infiltrative process replacing marrow fat. A bone scan showed no areas of uptake in the lytic lesions.

What is the significance of the clinical history, physical examination, radiologic evaluation, and laboratory values?
Show answer

The patient’s history of fatigue and pale mucosal membranes correlate with laboratory values indicative of anemia. The MRI finding of an infiltrative process could account for her anemia, as well as her thrombocytopenia. Massive splenomegaly may also contribute to cytopenias, particularly thrombocytopenia, which is common in those with splenomegaly due to platelet sequestration. The coagulation studies (PT and aPTT) are normal, despite the patient’s thrombocytopenia, because these tests do not assess platelet function; these studies are typically performed in patients with thrombocytopenia to exclude disseminated intravascular coagulation (Chapter 9). Lytic lesions in bone can occur in the setting of metastatic malignancy, multiple myeloma, and infection. Infection is unlikely due to the lack of constitutional symptoms and low WBC, and the “cold” bone scan argues against involvement of bone by infection or metastatic disease.

After a transfusion of platelets, the patient was taken to surgery, where a pathologic fracture of her right femoral neck was confirmed and a bone marrow biopsy was performed at the time of right hip replacement. The biopsy showed sheets of enlarged macrophages and reduced hematopoietic elements. On higher power, the enlarged cells demonstrated fibrillar cytoplasm.

What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

Enlargement of liver and spleen and replacement of the bone marrow by sheets of enlarged macrophages is indicative of a “storage disorder.” The presence of enlarged macrophages with a “crumpled tissue paper” appearance is very suggestive of Gaucher disease.

3
Which test should be performed to confirm the diagnosis?
Show answer

Gaucher disease is caused by mutations of the gene that encodes glucocerebrosidase, which results in reduced levels of this enzyme. Confirmation is made by laboratory testing of peripheral blood leukocytes for glucocerebrosidase levels. Causative mutations are quite heterogeneous, complicating DNA sequence analysis, but DNA analysis is useful in evaluating family members.

Glucocerebrosidase levels were approximately 10% of normal. Enzyme-replacement therapy (ERT) was initiated; however, the patient’s platelet count continued to drop and, therefore, a splenectomy was performed. The spleen weighed 2 kg (normal 150 g).

4
What accounts for the morphologic features of the Gaucher cell?
Show answer

The crumpled tissue paper appearance of the cytoplasm of Gaucher cells is caused by enlarged, elongated lysosomes filled with glucocerebroside.

5
How did this disease cause her symptoms?
Show answer

Gaucher cells tend to accumulate in the bone marrow, spleen, and liver. Marrow accumulation can produce cytopenias (by displacing normal marrow elements) and also is associated with bone disease, possibly because of local release of cytokines. This can lead to osteopenia or osteonecrosis, possibly explaining her hip fracture. Accumulations of Gaucher cells produce splenomegaly and sometimes hepatomegaly as well; extramedullary hematopoiesis to compensate for the compromised marrow space also contributes to enlargement of these organs. The enlarged spleen has increased activated phagocytes that destroy red cells and platelets, which contributes to this patient’s anemia and thrombocytopenia; for this reason, splenectomy is beneficial in these patients.

6
What are the different types of Gaucher disease?
Show answer

There are three types of Gaucher disease: (1) type I (chronic nonneuronopathic form), in which the accumulation of glucocerebrosides is limited to mononuclear phagocytes throughout the body; (2) type II (acute neuronopathic Gaucher disease), characterized by the appearance of severe central nervous system disease during infancy; and (3) type III, which is intermediate in severity between types I and II, with patients developing progressive central nervous system disease in adolescence or early adulthood. Reduced but detectable levels of glucocerebrosidase activity are found in patients with type I, while in type II there is virtually no detectable glucocerebrosidase activity.

7
Which type of Gaucher disease does the patient have?
Show answer

She has adult onset Gaucher disease, which is type I disease. Types II and III are associated with early-onset disease and neurologic involvement.

8
Are her brother and children likely to have Gaucher disease?
Show answer

Gaucher disease is an autosomal recessive condition caused by defects in the enzyme glucocerebrosidase. Therefore, her brother has a 25% chance of having the disease and a 50% chance of being a carrier. If her husband has a wildtype genotype, all of their children will be carriers and none will be affected.

9
What is the source of the glucocerebroside accumulation in these cells?
Show answer

Glucocerebroside is a component of cell membranes. Macrophages are responsible for “clean up” of senescent red cells and other cells that die, via other mechanisms. Glucocerebrosides are derived from the membranes of these cells.

10
To which category of genetic diseases does Gaucher disease belong?
Show answer

Gaucher disease is the most common of the lysosomal storage diseases, a diverse group of disorders in which enzyme defects lead to the accumulation of various substrates within lysosomes (Table 6.2.1). Subgroups of lysosomal storage diseases include glycogenoses (e.g., Pompe disease), sphingolipidoses (e.g., Tay-Sachs disease), and mucopolysaccharidoses (e.g., Hurler syndrome).

What is the pathogenesis of the lysosomal storage diseases?
Show answer

An inherited deficiency of a functional lysosomal enzyme gives rise to two pathologic consequences. Catabolism of the substrate of the missing enzyme remains incomplete, leading to the accumulation of the partially degraded insoluble metabolite within the lysosomes. This is called primary accumulation.

Stuffed with incompletely digested macromolecules, lysosomes become large and numerous enough to interfere with normal cell functions. Since lysosomal function is also essential for autophagy, impaired autophagy gives rise to secondary accumulation of autophagic substrates such as polyubiquinated proteins and old, effete mitochondria. The absence of this quality control mechanism causes accumulation of dysfunctional mitochondria with poor calcium buffering capacity and altered membrane potentials. This can trigger generation of free radicals and apoptosis.


12
How are lysosomal storage diseases treated?
Show answer

There are three general approaches to the treatment of lysosomal storage diseases. The most obvious is enzyme replacement therapy, currently in use for several of these diseases. Another approach, the substrate reduction therapy, is based on the premise that if the substrate to be degraded by the lysosomal enzyme can be reduced, the residual enzyme activity may be sufficient to catabolize it and prevent accumulation. A more recent strategy is based on the understanding of the molecular basis of enzyme deficiency. In many disorders, exemplified by Gaucher disease, the enzyme activity is low because the mutant proteins are unstable and prone to misfolding, and hence degraded in the endoplasmic reticulum. In such diseases, an exogenous competitive inhibitor of the enzyme can, paradoxically, bind to the mutant enzyme and act as the template that assists proper folding of the enzyme and thus prevent its degradation. Such molecular chaperone therapy is under active investigation.

13
What determines the clinical manifestations of the different lysosomal storage diseases?
Show answer

In general, the distribution of the stored material, and hence the organs affected, is determined by two interrelated factors: (1) the tissue where most of the material to be degraded is located and (2) the location where most of the degradation normally occurs. Because cells of the mononuclear phagocyte system are especially rich in lysosomes and are involved in the degradation of a variety of substrates, organs rich in phagocytic cells, such as the spleen and liver, are frequently enlarged in several forms of lysosomal storage disorders, including Gaucher disease.

14
What is the enzyme deficiency in Tay-Sachs disease?
Show answer

Tay-Sachs disease is a GM2 gangliosidosis, a group of three lysosomal storage diseases caused by an inability to catabolize GM2 gangliosides. Degradation of GM2 gangliosides requires three polypeptides encoded by three distinct genes. Tay-Sachs is the most common and is caused by loss-of-function mutations in the HEXA locus, which encodes the α-subunit of the enzyme hexosaminidase A.

15
How does hexosaminidase deficiency affect the body?
Show answer

GM2 ganglioside accumulates in many tissues (e.g., heart, liver, spleen, nervous system), but the involvement of neurons in the central and autonomic nervous system and retina dominates the clinical picture. On histologic examination, the neurons are ballooned with cytoplasmic vacuoles, each representing a lysosome filled with gangliosides. In time, there is progressive destruction of neurons, proliferation of microglia, and accumulation of complex lipids in phagocytes within the brain substance. A similar process occurs in the cerebellum as well as in neurons throughout the basal ganglia, brain stem, spinal cord, dorsal root ganglia, and autonomic nervous system. The ganglion cells in the retina are similarly swollen with GM2 ganglioside, particularly at the margins of the macula. A cherry-red spot (arrowhead) thus appears in the macula, representing accentuation of the normal color of the macular choroid contrasted with the pallor produced by the swollen ganglion cells in the remainder of the retina.

What are the clinical manifestations of hexosaminidase A deficiency?
Show answer

Infants with Tay-Sachs appear unaffected at birth but begin to manifest signs and symptoms at about 6 months of age. There is relentless motor and mental deterioration, beginning with lack of motor coordination, muscular flaccidity, blindness, and increasing dementia. Over the span of 1 or 2 years, a complete vegetative state is reached, followed by death at age 2 to 3 years.

17
Which populations are most commonly affected by this disease?
Show answer

This disease is especially prevalent among Jews of Eastern European (Ashkenazic) origin, in whom a carrier rate of 1 in 30 has been reported.

18
What are the mucopolysaccharidoses (MPSs)?
Show answer

These lysosomal storage disorders are a group of closely related syndromes that result from genetically determined deficiencies of enzymes involved in the degradation of mucopolysaccharides (glycosaminoglycans). The glycosaminoglycans that accumulate in MPSs are dermatan sulfate, heparan sulfate, keratan sulfate, and chondroitin sulfate. The enzymes involved in the degradation of these molecules cleave terminal sugars from the polysaccharide chains disposed along a polypeptide or core protein. In the absence of enzymes, these chains accumulate within lysosomes in various tissues and organs of the body.

19
What is the inheritance pattern of the MPSs?
Show answer

There are seven MPSs, MPS I to MPS VII, each resulting from a deficiency of one specific enzyme. All the MPSs except one are inherited as autosomal recessive traits; the exception, Hunter syndrome, is an X-linked recessive trait.

20
What is the clinical presentation of the MPSs?
Show answer

In general, MPSs are progressive disorders, characterized by coarse facial features, clouding of the cornea, joint stiffness, and intellectual disability. Hepatosplenomegaly, skeletal deformities, valvular lesions, subendothelial arterial deposits (particularly in the coronary arteries), and lesions in the brain are common findings in all of the MPSs. Urinary excretion of the accumulated mucopolysaccharides is often increased.

21
What is the effect on the tissues of the enzyme deficiency in the MPSs?
Show answer

The accumulated mucopolysaccharides are generally found in mononuclear phagocytic cells, endothelial cells, intimal smooth muscle cells, and fibroblasts throughout the body. Common sites of involvement are thus the spleen, liver, bone marrow, lymph nodes, blood vessels, and heart.

22
How does Hurler syndrome differ from Hunter syndrome?
Show answer

Hurler syndrome, also called MPS I-H, results from a deficiency of α-l-iduronidase. It is one of the most severe forms of MPS. Children with this disease appear unaffected at birth but develop hepatosplenomegaly by age 6 to 24 months. Their growth is retarded, and, as in other forms of MPS, they develop coarse facial features and skeletal deformities. Death occurs by age 6 to 10 years and is often due to cardiovascular complications. Hunter syndrome, also called MPS II, differs from Hurler syndrome in mode of inheritance (X-linked), absence of corneal clouding, and milder clinical course.

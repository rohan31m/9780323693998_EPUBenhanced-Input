A Critical Mass

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Compare grading and staging in the determination of prognosis.
  2. • Discuss the prognostic factors in breast carcinoma.
  3. • List the different paraneoplastic syndromes and their associated malignancies.

A 41-year-old woman presented with a two-month history of a mass in her left breast. Two months ago, during self-examination, she noticed a small nodule that has doubled in size since then. Her past medical history was noncontributory. Family history is significant for a maternal aunt who died of breast cancer at age 73; her mother, two aunts, and two older sisters had no history of breast cancer.

Physical examination revealed a left breast mass that was firm and fixed. No signs of edema or nipple changes were evident. No palpable lymph nodes were identified. Mammography showed a 2.5 cm mass with scattered calcifications. Computed tomography scans of the chest and abdomen and a bone scan were negative. Serum calcium was elevated at 12.5 mg/dL, but she had no history of nausea, vomiting, muscle weakness, or confusion. Serum PTH was slightly low.


1
What might be the cause of this patient’s hypercalcemia?
Show answer

Hypercalcemia may occur because of (1) osteolysis resulting from metastatic bone lesions or (2) the production of hypercalcemic hormonal substances by extraosseous neoplasms. The latter is considered a paraneoplastic syndrome. Paraneoplastic syndromes are clinical findings that are not due to the local or regional spread of tumor, or by the production of hormones indigenous to the tissue from which the tumor arose. Hypercalcemia is a common paraneoplastic syndrome. Serum PTH should be low with hypercalcemia. In this case, hypercalcemia is associated with an appropriately low PTH level and a negative bone scan, suggesting that this neoplasm is producing parathyroid hormone related protein (PTHrP). Hypercalcemia resulting from osteolysis secondary to bone metastasis is not considered a paraneoplastic syndrome.

The patient underwent a breast biopsy that revealed an invasive carcinoma. She was scheduled for simple mastectomy and sentinel lymph node biopsy.

3
What is a sentinel node biopsy and what is its clinical utility?
Show answer

In the absence of distant metastases, axillary lymph node status is the most important prognostic factor for invasive carcinoma. Clinical assessment of lymph node status is unreliable, and biopsy is necessary for accurate assessment. A “sentinel” lymph node is the first regional lymph node that receives lymph flow from a primary tumor. Lymphatic vessels in most breast carcinomas drain first to one or two sentinel nodes. The sentinel lymph node(s) is identified during surgery using radiotracer and/or colored dye injected into the tumor. A frozen section is performed and if the biopsy is positive for metastasis, an axillary dissection may be performed. However, if a biopsy is negative, it is unlikely that other, more distal nodes will be involved, and the patient can be spared the morbidity of a complete axillary dissection.


The frozen section analysis of the lymph node showed metastatic carcinoma. Two other lymph nodes were also positive for carcinoma on frozen section. A completion axillary dissection was performed at the time of mastectomy. Fifteen lymph nodes were isolated from the axillary dissection; one additional lymph node was positive for metastatic carcinoma. The tumor measured 2.5 cm in greatest dimension.

4
How is tumor stage determined?
Show answer

The staging of solid cancers is based on the size of the primary lesion, its extent of spread to regional lymph nodes, and the presence or absence of blood-borne metastases. The major staging system currently in use is the American Joint Committee on Cancer (AJCC) Staging. This system uses a classification called the TNM system—T for primary tumor (T1, T2, T 3 indicate increasing size of the primary), N for regional lymph node involvement, and M for metastases. TNM staging varies for specific forms of cancer. Based on the AJCC guidelines, this patient is a T2N2M0, which corresponds to stage IIIA.

Grossly, the tumor was a hard, gray-white mass that could not be easily demarcated from the surrounding fat.

  • Breast mass, cut surface
  • Tumor
5
Why is the tumor poorly demarcated?
Show answer

The tumor is invasive, and it sends out infiltrating fingers of cells that penetrate the surrounding normal breast tissue. This invasive property accounts for poor demarcation. By contrast, benign tumors are usually well circumscribed.


6
What challenges does this tumor’s infiltration raise for surgical treatment?
Show answer

The tumor cannot be simply “shelled out.” Surgical removal requires a wide excision so that the invasive component at the edges is also removed.

7
What is the basis of the hard, fixed consistency of the tumor?
Show answer

Invasive carcinomas interact with fibroblasts by releasing factors that activate these cells. The fibroblasts are then stimulated to synthesize extracellular matrix proteins such as collagen, giving the tumor a hard consistency. The formation of abundant collagenous stroma is referred to as desmoplasia.

8
How is tumor grade determined?
Show answer

Grading of a cancer is based on the degree of differentiation of the tumor cells and, in some cancers, the number of mitoses and the presence of certain architectural features such as necrosis. Necrosis is an indication that the tumor cells are proliferating so rapidly that they have outgrown the vascular supply. Mitotic activity is also an indication of proliferation. Higher histologic grade is usually associated with a faster growth rate. Although histologic grading is useful in identifying aggressive tumors, the correlation between histologic appearance and biologic behavior is less than perfect. In carcinoma of the breast, grade depends on (1) the amount of tubule formation, (2) mitotic activity, and (3) nuclear pleomorphism. Well-differentiated breast carcinomas (A) predominantly display tubular architecture and mild cytologic atypia without brisk mitotic activity. By contrast, poorly differentiated breast carcinomas (C) show minimal tubule formation and, instead, consist of sheets or cords of pleomorphic cells. Moderately differentiated carcinomas (B) are intermediate between these appearances.

  • Carcinoma grades
  • Tubular architecture
  • Mild cytologic atypia
  • Sheets of cells
  • Pleomorphic cells

Histologic examination showed moderate tubule formation, no mitotic figures, and moderate nuclear pleomorphism. Therefore, the diagnosis was moderately differentiated invasive ductal carcinoma. Dense collagen, consistent with the gross appearance, was seen.

  • Breast histology
  • Nests of tumor cells
  • Dense collagen
9
To what does the term pleomorphism refer?
Show answer

Pleomorphism is the term used to describe wide variations in the shape and size of cells and nuclei. In contrast, cells that appear similar are referred to as monomorphic. Increased pleomorphism is usually associated with higher grade tumors.

10
How are tumor grade and stage related to prognosis?
Show answer

Both have predictive value, but staging is of greater clinical value than grading in determining prognosis.

11
Which additional histologic features of the primary tumor are important in determining prognosis?
Show answer

Lymphatic and vascular invasion is another important prognostic feature. In other tumors, such as melanoma, the presence of ulceration and the pattern of growth (radial vs vertical) determine prognosis.

12
What does tumor growth fraction refer to?
Show answer

The fraction of cells that are in cycle is called the growth fraction. Tumors with high growth fractions grow more rapidly. In clinical practice currently, growth fraction in breast cancers is estimated by immunohistochemical staining for cell cycle–specific proteins or by performing gene expression profiling for genes that correlate with proliferation.

13
How does growth rate relate to tumor grade?
Show answer

Tumors that are high grade (i.e., undifferentiated) usually have a high growth rate as well.

14
How does the cell cycle time of tumor cells relate to the increase in the size of the tumor? What are the therapeutic implications?
Show answer

If one measures the cell cycle time of the cells that are proliferating within the tumor, it is usually around 2 days. Yet doubling of tumor size occurs in months. The explanation of this apparent paradox is that most of the cells within a clinically recognizable tumor are not proliferating. In individual cells, this may result from terminal differentiation, reversible cell cycle arrest, or programmed cell death. This observation has important therapeutic implications. Proliferating cells are more sensitive to most chemotherapeutic agents and radiation than are resting cells. These agents are generally given in multiple low doses separated by days to weeks to reduce toxicity and to allow resting cells to enter the cell cycle before the next therapeutic cycle.

15
What are the three most important biologic markers used to assess breast cancers and how are they assessed?
Show answer

Estrogen receptor (ER), progesterone receptor (PR), and HER2 status are evaluated in all breast cancer patients. All three can be assessed by immunohistochemistry. HER2 amplification may also be assessed by fluorescent in-situ hybridization (FISH), which determines HER2 gene copy number.

16
How is immunohistochemical staining performed?
Show answer

Immunohistochemical staining requires an antibody against the target protein to be detected. The antibody is conjugated to an enzyme (e.g., horseradish peroxidase) that converts a colorless compound into a chromogen. In this way, any antibody binding to the tissue can be visualized. ER and PR are located in the nucleus; therefore, the nuclei stain dark brown with the chromogen. Other antibodies bind to antigens that localize to the cytoplasm or cellular membrane (e.g. HER2) and stain those cellular compartments instead of the nucleus.

  • ER IHC
  • Nuclear staining
  • HER2 IHC
  • Membranous staining
17
How is FISH performed?
Show answer

DNA probes specific for a region of the genome containing a gene of interest: in this case, the HER2 gene (red), and a control region on the same chromosome (green), are hybridized to tissue sections taken from the patient's tumor (left panel) and from a control (right panel). The multiple red signals seen in the patient’s specimen confirm HER2 gene amplification.


18
What are the two types of chromosomal structures that are created by amplification of genomic regions in cancer cells?
Show answer

Two mutually exclusive patterns are seen: multiple small, extrachromosomal structures called double minutes, and homogeneously staining regions (HSRs). The latter derive from the insertion of the amplified genes into new chromosomal locations, which may be distant from the normal location of the involved genes; because regions containing amplified genes lack a normal banding pattern, they appear homogeneous in a G-banded karyotype. Generally speaking, double minutes and HSRs are seen when the extent of gene amplification is high (>5 copies; under some circumstances, there may be as much as 300-fold amplification). Double minutes and HSRs are common in cancer cell lines but uncommon in primary tumors other than certain specific types, such as neuroblastoma, glioblastoma, and HER2-amplified breast cancer. Lower copy number gains are much more widely seen in diverse cancers and are typically associated with more subtle changes, such as tandem duplications, that are not seen by karyotyping of metaphase chromosomes but can be identified by other analytic methods, such as next generation sequencing and array comparative genomic hybridization.

19
How does ER expression contribute to the pathogenesis of breast cancer?
Show answer

In the presence of estrogen, ER functions as a transcription factor that turns on genes that support the proliferation and survival of breast epithelial cells. Other hormone-responsive malignancies include endometrial carcinoma and prostate adenocarcinoma.

20
Which risk factors for breast cancer can be explained by increased exposure of the breast to estrogen?
Show answer

Essentially all variables that increase the exposure of the breast to estrogen in the absence of the countervailing effects of progesterone increase the risk of breast cancer. These include (1) early age of menstruation; (2) late menopause; (3) nulliparity; (4) obesity; and (5) post-menopausal estrogen replacement therapy.

21
How can ER expression be exploited in treatment of patients with ER+ tumors?
Show answer

There are two main therapeutic strategies to interrupt the effects of estrogen/ER: (1) use of tamoxifen to interrupt the binding of estrogen to its receptor and (2) aromatase inhibitors to prevent estrogen production.

22
Which family of cell surface receptors includes the HER2 protein?
Show answer

HER2 belongs to the epidermal growth factor receptor family of tyrosine kinases. HER2 (also known as ERBB2) is amplified in approximately 20% of breast cancers and in a smaller fraction of adenocarcinomas of the lung, ovary, stomach, and salivary glands. Another family member, ERBB1, the EGF receptor, is overexpressed in 80% of squamous cell carcinomas of the lung, 50% or more of glioblastomas, and 80% to 100% of epithelial tumors of the head and neck.

23
How do tyrosine kinase oncoproteins such as HER2 contribute to oncogenesis?
Show answer

Oncogenic tyrosine kinases such as HER2 behave like constitutively active growth factor receptors. They activate multiple downstream signaling pathways, particularly the RAS and PI3K pathways, to promote cell cycle progression (DNA replication), cell survival, and changes that increase the biosynthesis of cellular building blocks, all of which contribute in an integrated fashion to drive cellular proliferation.

24
How can HER2 amplification be exploited therapeutically?
Show answer

HER2-amplified breast cancers tend to be highly proliferative and aggressive with a poor prognosis. Monoclonal antibodies that bind HER2 (e.g., trastuzumab) are highly active therapeutic agents. They work by inhibiting HER2 signaling, possibly in part by promoting HER2 internalization and degradation, and also by promoting antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity.

The patient was treated with trastuzumab and an aromatase inhibitor, but 10 months later presented with headaches and blurred vision. Cerebrospinal fluid was analyzed and showed atypical cells, including mucin-containing signet-ring cells and mitotic figures.

  • Signet-ring cell
  • Mucin
  • Mitosis
25
How does the presence of carcinoma cells in the cerebrospinal fluid influence tumor staging and grading?
Show answer

The presence of cells in the cerebrospinal fluid indicates distant metastases, and the tumor stage is increased to stage IV. Because the cells still apparently have a differentiated feature (mucin secretion), the tumor grade is not affected by this finding.

The patient has two young daughters and is worried that she has a hereditary cancer syndrome.

26
Is it likely that this patient has a genetic syndrome that predisposes her to malignancy?
Show answer

Although it is difficult to sort out the hereditary and nonhereditary contributions, patients with a hereditary predisposition for cancer typically present at a younger age and may have multiple different malignancies. Often, several family members are affected, depending on the mutation. This patient has only one affected relative. Although she is relatively young for a diagnosis of carcinoma, it is more likely that this is a sporadic tumor and not related to a germline mutation.

27
Which germline mutations are most closely associated with breast cancer?
Show answer

Mutations in two genes, BRCA1 and BRCA2, account for 25% of cases of familial breast cancer. In addition to breast cancer, women with BRCA1 mutations have a substantially higher risk of epithelial ovarian cancers, and men have a slightly higher risk of prostate cancer. Likewise, mutations in the BRCA2 gene increase the risk of breast cancer in both men and women, as well as cancer of the ovary, prostate, pancreas, bile ducts, stomach, melanocytes, and B lymphocytes. Although linkage of germline mutation BRCA1 and BRCA2 to familial breast cancers is well established, these genes are rarely mutated in sporadic cases of breast cancer.

28
What is the proposed mechanism for carcinogenesis when BRCA1 and BRCA2 are mutated?
Show answer

The BRCA proteins participate in a DNA-damage response network whose purpose is to repair certain types of DNA damage using the homologous recombination repair pathway. Defects in this pathway leads to the activation of an alternative pathway called the nonhomologous end joining pathway, which leads to error-prone repair, formation of dicentric chromosomes, bridge-fusion-breakage cycles, and aneuploidy.


Genetic analysis confirmed the absence of   BRCA1 and  BRCA2 mutations in this patient.

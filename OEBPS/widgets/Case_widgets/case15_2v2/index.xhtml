Objectives
  1. • Explain the relationship between endometrial hyperplasia and endometrial adenocarcinoma.
  2. • Describe the risk factors, pathogenesis, clinical features, and spread of endometrial carcinoma.
  3. • Compare and contrast the morphologic features and etiologies of leiomyomas and leiomyosarcomas.

A 53-year-old obese (5 feet 3 inches, 275 lb), sexually active, nulligravida woman presented to her gynecologist with a 1-year history of vaginal spotting. Her medical history included non–insulin–dependent diabetes and medically controlled hypertension, both diagnosed at age 43. Her gynecologic history included menarche, age 11; coitarche, age 20; lifetime sexual partners, two; regular menses until age 51, when she became menopausal and her menstrual periods stopped. Transvaginal ultrasonography revealed an endometrial thickness of 6 mm (normal 5 mm).

1
What is the differential diagnosis of a thickened endometrium?
Show answer

The two most likely causes of a thickened endometrium in a postmenopausal woman are endometrial hyperplasia and endometrial carcinoma.

2
What is the major risk factor for endometrial hyperplasia?
Show answer

Unopposed estrogenic stimulation is the major risk factor for endometrial hyperplasia. This may occur with endocrine disorders such as polycystic ovarian disease, functional granulosa cell tumor of the ovary, or estrogen replacement therapy not combined with progesterone. Obesity is an increasingly common cause of estrogen excess since adipose tissue converts steroid precursors into estrogens.

3
What are the histologic features of endometrial hyperplasia?
Show answer

Endometrial hyperplasia is categorized based on the presence of cytologic atypia: hyperplasia without atypia and hyperplasia with atypia. Hyperplasia without atypia is recognized by an increase in the gland-to-stroma ratio, the normal ratio being 1:1. The glands show variation in size and shape and may be dilated. Although there may be focal back-to-back glands, some intervening stroma is usually retained. Atypical hyperplasia is composed of complex patterns of proliferating glands displaying nuclear atypia. The glands are commonly back to back and may branch. Individual cells are rounded and lose their normal perpendicular orientation to the basement membrane.

4
Why is it important to differentiate hyperplasia without atypia from hyperplasia with atypia?
Show answer

The presence of cytologic atypia correlates with the development or concurrent finding of endometrial carcinoma. Hyperplasia without cellular atypia carries a low risk (between 1% and 3%) for progression to endometrial carcinoma, whereas hyperplasia with atypia, also called endometrial intraepithelial neoplasia (EIN), is associated with a much higher risk (20%–50%).

An endometrial biopsy yielded abundant tissue diagnostic of endometrial hyperplasia without atypia. The patient was scheduled for a follow-up visit but did not return for care.

Seven years later, the patient was brought to the emergency department after fainting at home. She stated that her vaginal spotting had gradually increased since her last visit and that she was using 1–2 pads/day. Her hemoglobin was 5 g/dL (normal,12–16 g/dL). An abdominal CT scan showed an enlarged uterus. A repeat endometrial biopsy was positive for endometrioid adenocarcinoma, a subtype of endometrial carcinoma. Hysterectomy and bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy were performed. Sectioning of the uterus revealed multiple polypoid masses filling the endometrial cavity. The tumor invaded into the myometrium and involved the endocervical canal.

  • Polypoid masses
  • Endocervical canal
5
How does endometrioid carcinoma differ histologically from endometrial hyperplasia?
Show answer

Myometrial invasion is one hallmark of carcinoma that is not seen in hyperplasia. Furthermore, the glands show cribriform architecture in which the sheets of epithelium have a “sieve-like” appearance with punched out spaces without intervening stroma. Grading in endometrioid adenocarcinoma is based on the extent of solid architecture: grade 1 is composed almost entirely of well-formed glands, while grades 2 and 3 have up to 50% and more than 50% solid architecture, respectively.

  • Histology, low power
  • Myometrial invasion
  • Histology, higher power
  • Cribriform glands
6
How can adenocarcinoma invading into the myometrium be differentiated histologically from adenomyosis?
Show answer

Adenomyosis consists of benign endometrial glands and stroma in the myometrium, whereas in endometrial adenocarcinoma, only malignant glands invade the myometrium, without associated stroma.

8
What are the most common driver mutations in type 1 endometrial carcinoma?
Show answer

Mutations in mismatch repair genes and the tumor suppressor gene PTEN are early events in the stepwise development of endometrioid carcinoma. Women with germline mutations in PTEN (Cowden Syndrome) and germline alterations in DNA mismatch repair genes (Lynch Syndrome) are at high risk for this cancer. TP53 mutations occur but are relatively uncommon and are late events in the genesis of this tumor type.


9
What is the most common driver mutation in type 2 endometrial carcinoma?
Show answer

Nearly all cases of serous carcinoma have mutations in the TP53 tumor suppressor gene, whereas mutations in DNA mismatch repair genes and in PTEN are rare. Serous tumors are preceded by a lesion called serous endometrial intraepithelial carcinoma (SEIC) in which TP53 mutations are often detected, suggesting an early role for such mutations in the development of this form of endometrial carcinoma.


10
What are the histologic features of serous endometrial carcinoma?
Show answer

Serous endometrial carcinoma has a similar appearance to serous carcinoma of the ovary: it grows in small tufts and papillae and has marked cytologic atypia.


11
Which mesenchymal malignancy is most commonly seen in the uterus?
Show answer

Leiomyosarcoma (a malignant tumor of smooth muscle cells), although much less common than endometrial carcinoma, can arise in the myometrium.

12
What are the histologic features of leiomyosarcoma? How can it be differentiated from leiomyoma?
Show answer

Leiomyosarcoma is distinguished from leiomyoma based on several features: (1) leiomyoma is well circumscribed whereas leiomyosarcoma has infiltrative borders and (2) leiomyoma is composed of bland spindled smooth muscle cells while leiomyosarcoma displays hypercellularity, cytologic atypia, mitotic activity, and often necrosis. Genetically, leiomyosarcoma is usually aneuploid, harboring numerous cytogenetic abnormalities. By contrast, leiomyoma frequently has rearrangements of chromosomes 6 and 12 and mutations in MED12, a gene that encodes a component of the RNA polymerase transcription complex; aneuploidy and complex cytogenetic abnormalities are absent.

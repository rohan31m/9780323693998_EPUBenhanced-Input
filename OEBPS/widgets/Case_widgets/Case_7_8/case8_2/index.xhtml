Under Pressure

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Relate the control of blood pressure to the etiology and pathophysiology of hypertension.
  2. • Relate the clinical consequences of hypertension to the vascular changes caused by increased blood pressure.
  3. • Enumerate the risk factors of aortic dissection and aneurysm and describe how they relate to the pathogenesis and morphologic findings of these conditions.

A 53-year-old obese woman (height 5’5”, weight 290 pounds) presented to her primary care physician for her first annual physical in many years. She had a 30-pack-year smoking history and ate a diet rich in fried foods and salt. Physical exam was significant for a blood pressure of 170/95 mm Hg; cardiovascular, pulmonary, and abdominal exam were within normal limits. No lower extremity edema or clubbing was noted.

1
What is the significance of the patient’s elevated blood pressure?
Show answer

This patient has hypertension, which is defined as a systolic blood pressure >130 mm Hg and a diastolic pressure > 80 mm Hg. Hypertension may be primary (essential) or secondary to a variety of causes.

2
Which risk factors for primary hypertension does this patient have?
Show answer

The patient is obese, eats a high sodium diet, and smokes, three risk factors for primary hypertension. Additional risk factors include advancing age, severe alcohol abuse, and family history.

3
What are some causes of secondary hypertension?
Show answer

Hypertension may be due to medications (e.g., nonsteroidal antiinflammatory drugs, corticosteroids), illicit drugs (e.g. cocaine, amphetamines), primary renal disease, primary aldosteronism, renovascular hypertension, obstructive sleep apnea, coarctation of the aorta, and a variety of endocrine disorders.

4
Which renal diseases cause hypertension? What is the mechanism?
Show answer

Any renal disease leading to renal failure (glomerulonephritis, chronic renal disease, polycystic kidney disease)may lead to hypertension by decreasing the glomerular filtration rate and renal clearance of water and sodium. High levels of renin also produce hypertension by activating the renin/angiotensin /aldosterone axis, increasing vascular tone, and decreasing renal clearance of sodium and water. Renin levels may be elevated by renal artery stenosis, which decreases renal perfusion and leads to increases in the production of renin from juxtaglomerular cells, and by renal tumors, which may produce renin.

5
Which endocrine disorders cause hypertension and what is the mechanism?
Show answer

Endocrine diseases that can cause secondary hypertension include a diverse group of adrenal diseases: pheochromocytoma (a neoplasm of the adrenal medulla), Cushing disease and syndrome, adrenal neoplasms with hyperaldosteronism, hyperthyroidism, and congenital or hereditary adrenogenital syndromes. Pheochromocytoma causes hypertension by releasing norepinephrine, which increases vascular tone and cardiac output, whereas hyperthyroidism sensitizes peripheral tissues to the effects of norepinephrine. Other endocrine disorders directly or indirectly increase aldosterone levels, leading to increased renal resorption of sodium and water. Secondary causes of hypertension are relatively rare (5%–10% of all cases) and, therefore, screening tests to exclude pheochromocytoma or Cushing disease are not part of the standard workup for a patient newly diagnosed with hypertension.

Laboratory values were within normal limits; specifically, her BUN and creatinine were normal. An electrocardiogram and cardiac ultrasound confirmed left ventricular hypertrophy. The patient was prescribed a thiazide diuretic and encouraged to lose weight, exercise, and adopt a healthier diet. A follow-up appointment was scheduled, but the patient did not return.

6
What is the pathophysiologic basis for the left ventricular hypertrophy? What are the negative consequences of left ventricular hypertrophy?
Show answer

Left ventricular hypertrophy is a myocardial adaptation to chronically elevated afterload (high peripheral vascular resistance). The negative consequences of left ventricular hypertrophy are increased myocardial oxygen demand due to increased myocardial mass in the face of decreased myocardial perfusion caused by elevated ventricular wall pressures.

7
How does systemic (left-sided) hypertensive heart disease differ morphologically from pulmonary (right-sided) hypertensive heart disease?
Show answer

In systemic hypertensive heart disease (A), there is marked concentric thickening of the left ventricular wall and interventricular septum causing reduction in lumen size. There may be left atrial dilation due to stiffening of the left ventricle and impaired diastolic relaxation, leading to atrial volume overload. By contrast, pulmonary hypertensive heart disease (chronic cor pulmonale) (B) is characterized by marked dilation and hypertrophy of the right ventricle with a thickened free wall and hypertrophied trabeculae. The shape and volume of the left ventricle may be distorted by the enlarged right ventricle.

  • Heart, gross
  • Concentric thickening
  • Thickened free wall
  • Hypertrophied trabeculae
  • Left ventricle

Three years later, the patient returned to her primary care physician with a 3-month history of dyspnea on exertion. She had not been taking her medications. Physical exam revealed worsening bilateral ankle edema, basilar rales, and hepatomegaly (liver percussed to 10 cm; normal is 7 cm). Her blood pressure was 250/135 mm Hg. Laboratory values were notable for an alkaline phosphatase of 110 U/L (normal 20–70 U/L), AST of 28 U/L (8–20 U/L), ALT of 26 U/L (normal 8–20 U/L), BUN of 30 mg/dL (normal 7–18 mg/dL) and creatinine of 2.9 mg/dL (normal 0.6–1.2 mg/dL).

8
What is the significance of the elevation of the patient’s blood pressure to 230/135 mm Hg?
Show answer

The patient now has malignant hypertension. Malignant hypertension is characterized by a rapidly progressive course and is fatal within 1 to 2 years unless promptly treated. It is associated with systolic pressure above 200 mm Hg and diastolic pressure more than 120 mm Hg, renal failure, and retinal hemorrhages, with or without papilledema. Malignant hypertension may arise de novo but is more commonly superimposed on previously existing “benign” essential hypertension. The extremely high blood pressures are associated with widespread endothelial injury and thrombosis and sometimes with microangiopathic hemolytic anemia. Hyperplastic arteriolosclerosis is a classic histologic finding and is characterized by concentric rings of hyperplastic smooth muscle cells that thicken the media and intima and surround narrow vascular lumina.

  • Vessel, histology
  • Narrowed lumen
  • Hyperplastic smooth muscle cells
  • Thickened media and intima
9
What is the etiology for this patient's elevated serum BUN and creatinine?
Show answer

Elevation of serum BUN and creatinine reflects progressive loss of renal function.

Grossly, the kidneys of patients with long-standing hypertension are symmetrically atrophic with finely granular surface caused by fine scarring. In some cases the kidneys have pitted and irregular cortical surface due to focal loss of renal cortex secondary to patchy ischemic atrophy. In between larger areas of (arterial) scarring, the cortical surface is finely granular, reflecting ischemic injury in a small-vessel (arteriolar) distribution. Histologically, hyaline arteriolosclerosis of the small vessels of the kidney can be seen and is manifested as thickening and replacement by a homogeneous pink hyaline material. intimal fibrosis can also be seen. There will be many globally sclerotic or partially sclerotic glomeruli. As the glomeruli are lost, the distal nephron components atrophy and drop out as well. These changes are responsible for the loss of renal function.

  • Patchy cortical scars
  • Histology, medium power
  • Hyaline arteriolosclerosis
  • Intimal fibrosis
  • Sclerotic glomerulus
  • Atrophic tubules
  • Histology, high power
  • Pink hyaline material
10
What is the etiology of the patient’s dyspnea, rales, hepatomegaly, and lower extremity edema?
Show answer

Although her heart was initially able to compensate for the pressure overload caused by systemic hypertension, the patient is now in left heart failure, which causes pulmonary venous congestion and edema. Her dyspnea and rales are due to pulmonary edema. Left-sided heart failure eventually gives rise to right-sided heart failure because increased pulmonary venous pressure gets transmitted to the right heart. Hepatomegaly, splenomegaly, and lower extremity edema is due to right-sided heart failure secondary to her left-sided heart failure.

A chest x-ray showed a widened mediastinum. While the patient was waiting for a chest computed tomography scan, she experienced sudden, sharp chest pain and collapsed. She was found to be hypotensive with unequal pulses. Despite efforts at resuscitation, she died. An autopsy was performed.

11
What are some possible causes of sudden death in a patient with hypertension?
Show answer

Hypertension is an important risk factor for coronary atherosclerosis. Sudden death can occur as a result of a fatal arrhythmia associated with ischemic heart disease from coronary atherosclerosis. Aortic dissection can also cause sudden collapse.

At autopsy, the pericardium was filled with blood (hemopericardium). No findings suggestive of acute or remote myocardial infarction were seen grossly; however, examination of the aorta showed a hematoma pushing apart the media of the aorta.

  • Aorta, gross
  • Aortic adventitia
  • Aortic intima
  • Hematoma
12
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

This patient has had an aortic dissection with rupture leading to hemopericardium, accounting for the widened mediastinum seen on chest x-ray. When an intimal tear occurs, blood can enter the aortic media and split it apart. Most aortic dissections begin in the proximal aorta. Proximal aortic dissections can rupture into the pericardium, leading to hemopericardium and death, as in this case. The pulses may be uneven when pressure from the dissection occludes arterial outflow.

13
How are aortic dissections classified?
Show answer

Proximal (type A) lesions are the most common and the most dangerous. Most aortic dissections occur within the first 10 cm of the proximal aorta. Proximal dissections can rupture through the aortic media into the pericardium or pleural spaces; such events are often fatal. Dissection can extend proximally toward the heart and distally to involve subdiaphragmatic vessels. Dissections that do not involve the ascending aorta (type B) are less common and have a better prognosis.


14
Which histologic abnormality may be observed in the aortic wall of patients dying of aortic dissection?
Show answer

Systemic hypertension can cause significant narrowing of arterioles of the vasa vasorum (e.g., in the aorta), which causes outer medial ischemia. Medial ischemia may lead to “degenerative changes” of the aorta with scarring (and loss of elastic fibers), inadequate extracellular matrix synthesis, and production of increasing amounts of amorphous ground substance (glycosaminoglycan). Histologically, these changes are collectively recognized as cystic medial degeneration and can be visualized by an elastin stain. This stain highlights elastin fragmentation (black) and areas devoid of elastin that resemble cystic spaces but are actually filled with proteoglycans (asterisks). Normal media for comparison shows the regular layered pattern of elastic tissue.


15
Which inherited disease is associated with aortic dissection and what is the etiology of the vascular pathology?
Show answer

Marfan syndrome is an autosomal dominant disorder resulting from mutations in the FBN1 gene, which encodes fibrillin, a glycoprotein secreted by fibroblasts. Fibrillin forms microfibrillar aggregates in the extracellular matrix that serve as scaffolding for deposition of elastic fibers. Fibrillin loss of function also increases the bioavailability of TGF-β, and increased TGF-β–dependent signaling in aortic stromal cells may contribute to the weakening of the aortic wall.

16
What is another condition of the aorta that is attributable to hypertension?
Show answer

Aneurysms (localized abnormal dilation) can arise in any portion of the aorta. The two most important causes of aortic aneurysms are atherosclerosis and hypertension; atherosclerosis is a greater factor in abdominal aortic aneurysms, while hypertension is the most common etiology associated with ascending aortic aneurysms. Smoking is strongly associated with aortic aneurysms which may be related to an elevated risk of hypertension and atherosclerosis, as well as the effects of toxins in tobacco smoke.

17
How does hypertension increase the risk for stroke (cerebrovascular accident)?
Show answer

Hypertension is the most important risk factor for stroke. Uncontrolled hypertension may be associated with hemorrhagic stroke, a bleed into the brain. It also may set the stage for embolic stroke, due to its associations with atherosclerosis of the carotid arteries and resultant thromboembolism. Hypertension may give rise to heart failure (potentially leading to atrial fibrillation and mural thrombosis). Approximately one-third of patients with untreated hypertension die of stroke. In this example, there is an area of acute hemorrhage in the thalamus. Hypertension-associated acute central nervous system hemorrhage often begins in the deep gray matter of the cerebral hemispheres (putamen, thalamus), in the pontine tegmentum, or cerebellar hemispheres.

  • Brain, gross
  • Frontal cortex
  • Ventricles
  • Occipital cortex
  • Thalamic hemorrhage

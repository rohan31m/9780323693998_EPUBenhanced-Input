Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast the different types of congenital heart disease.
  2. • Compare and contrast major clinical consequences, pathophysiology, and morphology of left-to-right and right-to-left shunts.
  3. • Describe the pathophysiology and pathogenesis of pulmonary hypertension in the setting of congenital heart disease.

A 1-day-old girl was referred to a pediatric cardiologist when the labor and delivery nurse noted that the baby turned blue when crying. The infant was born after an uncomplicated full-term gestation and delivery to a G1, P1 mother with no family history of heart disease. The infant appeared well developed, but faintly dusky just after birth. A more intense cyanosis was noted a short while later when the infant began to cry.

1
What is the most likely cause of cyanosis in this case?
Show answer

Cyanosis results from inadequate oxygenation of blood. In a newborn, this is most likely due to congenital heart disease with a right to left shunt.

2
What are the three major structural categories of congenital heart disease?
Show answer

Most of the various structural anomalies in congenital heart disease can be organized into three major categories: (1) malformations causing a left-to-right shunt; (2) malformations causing a right-to-left shunt; and (3) malformations causing an obstruction.

3
Which congenital heart diseases present with cyanosis at birth?
Show answer

The presence of cyanosis relates to the direction of the shunt. When blood from the right side of the circulation flows directly into the left side (right-to-left shunt), hypoxemia and cyanosis (a dusky blueness of the skin and mucous membranes) result because the pulmonary circulation is bypassed and poorly oxygenated venous blood shunts directly into the systemic arterial supply. Tetralogy of Fallot, transposition of the great arteries, tricuspid atresia, total anomalous venous return, and persistent truncus arteriosus can cause right-to-left shunts and variable degrees of cyanosis.


4
When do most congenital heart defects arise in development?
Show answer

Most congenital heart disease arises from faulty embryogenesis during gestational weeks 3 through 8, when the heart and major vessels form and begin to function. The most severe anomalies are incompatible with intrauterine survival, and significant heart malformations are common in premature infants and in stillborns.

6
What are the risk factors for congenital heart disease?
Show answer

Most affected patients have no identifiable genetic association, and even in those that do, the nature and severity of the defect are highly variable. Thus, environmental factors, alone or in combination with genetic factors, have a likely role in congenital heart disease. Factors associated with increased risk include congenital rubella infection, gestational diabetes, and teratogen exposure (including some therapeutic drugs). Nutritional factors may also influence risk. For instance, folate supplementation during early pregnancy may reduce congenital heart disease risk. In most cases, however, a specific environmental exposure is not apparent, and the mechanistic link between environmental factors and the developmental anomaly is obscure.

A systolic murmur was audible. The lungs sounded clear and the remainder of the physical examination was normal. A chest film showed no cardiac enlargement. The pulmonary vascular markings were diminished. An electrocardiogram showed right ventricular hypertrophy and right atrial enlargement. An echocardiogram showed a large ventricular septal defect, a large aorta, subpulmonic stenosis, and very small pulmonary arteries. There was a severe pressure gradient across the pulmonic valve. Pressures in the right and left ventricles were equal, and no gradient was measured across the septal defect.

7
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

These findings are consistent with tetralogy of Fallot (TOF). TOF is defined by the presence of (1) ventricular septal defect (VSD); (2) overriding aorta; (3) obstruction to right ventricular outflow; and (4) right ventricular hypertrophy. It is the most common form of cyanotic congenital heart disease.


8
What is the pathogenesis of TOF?
Show answer

All of the features of TOF are due to anterosuperior displacement of the infundibular septum during embryologic development.

9
What determines the clinical severity in TOF?
Show answer

The degree of right ventricular outflow obstruction and the size of the VSD are the key anatomic features that determine the severity of the clinical symptoms. When the pulmonic stenosis is mild and the VSD is large, the physiology is similar to that of VSD. The patient may have left-to-right shunt and no cyanosis. Many of these patients live decades with few symptoms. The more severe the pulmonic stenosis, the higher the resistance to blood flow in the pulmonary circuit. If pulmonary resistance exceeds systemic resistance, a right-to-left shunt occurs, and the patient has cyanosis.

The infant was begun on prostaglandin E1 infusion. A palliative surgical repair was performed at a tertiary care center, followed by definitive correction of the cardiac defects at age 8 months.

10
What is the rationale for prostaglandin E1 treatment?
Show answer

With extreme degrees of pulmonic stenosis/pulmonic atresia, blood flow to the lungs occurs by retrograde flow through a patent ductus arteriosus or bronchial artery collaterals. In these patients, keeping the ductus open is lifesaving and can be achieved by administering prostaglandin E1.

11
What is the function of the ductus arteriosus before birth?
Show answer

The ductus serves to allow blood to flow from the pulmonary artery to the aorta, bypassing the unoxygenated lungs. In healthy term infants, functional closure of the duct by constriction is complete within 1 to 2 days after birth. It may remain patent in neonates who are hypoxic, particularly premature infants. Complete, irreversible closure occurs within the first few months of life.

  • Heart
  • Aorta
  • Pulmonary trunk
  • Ductus arteriosus

12
Which type of shunt occurs in the early stages of a patent ductus arteriosus (PDA)?
Show answer

A left-to-right shunt occurs in the early stages of a PDA, typically associated with a harsh, continuous, “machinery-like” murmur.

13
What are the anatomic features of transposition of great vessels (TGA), another malformation that results in a right-to-left shunt and congenital cyanosis?
Show answer

TGA produces ventriculoarterial discordance: the aorta is anteriorly displaced and arises from the right ventricle, while the pulmonary artery is relatively posterior and emanates from the left ventricle. The atrium-to-ventricle connections are normal (concordant), with the right atrium joining the right ventricle and the left atrium emptying into the left ventricle.

  • Heart
  • Right ventricle
  • Aorta
  • Left ventricle
  • Ductus arteriosus
  • Pulmonary artery

14
What is the physiologic consequence of TGA?
Show answer

There is separation of the systemic and pulmonary circulations, a condition incompatible with postnatal life unless a shunt (VSD, atrial septal defect [ASD], PDA, or patent foramen ovale) exists that allows oxygenated blood to reach the right ventricle or aorta. With time, right ventricular hypertrophy becomes prominent because this chamber functions as the systemic ventricle. Concurrently, the left ventricle becomes thin-walled (atrophic) because it supports the low-resistance pulmonary circulation. Without surgery, most patients die within months.

15
How does the presentation of left-to-right shunts differ from that of right-to-left shunts?
Show answer

Unlike right-to-left shunts, which present with neonatal cyanosis, left-to-right shunts may be asymptomatic for many years.

16
Which congenital malformations present as left-to-right shunts?
Show answer

VSD is the most common. Other malformations include atrial septal defects (ASD), atrioventricular defects, and patent ductus arteriosus.

17
Where do VSDs typically occur?
Show answer

Approximately 70% of VSDs occur in the region of the membranous interventricular septum (membranous VSD); the remainder occurs below the pulmonary valve (infundibular VSD) or within the muscular septum.

  • Heart, gross
  • Probe through VSD

18
Do all VSDs require treatment?
Show answer

Small VSDs are often asymptomatic, and up to 50% of small muscular VSDs close spontaneously. Closure may occur by fibrous obliteration progressing from the margins or by adherence of the tricuspid valve to the defect.

19
What are the late complications of left-to-right shunts?
Show answer

Left-to-right shunts chronically elevate both volume and pressure in the normally low-pressure, low-resistance pulmonary circulation. To maintain relatively normal distal pulmonary capillary and venous pressures, the muscular pulmonary arteries (<1 mm in diameter) initially respond by undergoing medial hypertrophy and vasoconstriction. However, prolonged pulmonary arterial vasoconstriction stimulates the development of irreversible obstructive intimal lesions. The right ventricle responds to the pulmonary vascular changes by undergoing progressive hypertrophy. Eventually, pulmonary vascular resistance approaches systemic levels and blood flow reverses, producing a right-to-left shunt that introduces poorly oxygenated blood into the systemic circulation (Eisenmenger syndrome). When this occurs, cyanosis develops.

  • Artery, histology
  • Tight stenotic lumen
  • Medial hypertrophy

20
Why is pulmonary hypertension less common in patients with atrial septal defects (ASDs) than ventricular septal defects (VSDs)?
Show answer

ASDs also increase the volume load in the pulmonary circulation, but at lower pressures than VSDs; as a result, they are better tolerated by the pulmonary vasculature.

21
How do ASDs typically present?
Show answer

ASDs result in a left-to-right shunt, largely because pulmonary vascular resistance is considerably less than systemic vascular resistance and also because the compliance (distensibility) of the right ventricle is much greater than that of the left. Pulmonary blood flow may be two to eight times normal. A murmur is often present as a result of excessive flow through the pulmonary valve and/or through the ASD. Despite the right-sided volume overload, ASDs are generally well tolerated and usually do not become symptomatic before age 30. Irreversible pulmonary hypertension is unusual.

22
What is a paradoxical embolus?
Show answer

The lung normally prevents thromboemboli arising in the peripheral venous circulation from reaching the systemic circulation and causing infarcts because emboli are trapped in pulmonary capillaries. In the presence of an ASD or a VSD with a right-to-left shunt, emboli may bypass the lung and enter the systemic circulation, producing an infarct (e.g., stroke). This is termed a paradoxical embolus. ASD or VSD with a right-to-left shunt is also associated with a higher incidence of cerebral abscesses because bacteria fail to be filtered out by the pulmonary vascular bed.

23
What are the obstructive forms of congenital heart disease?
Show answer

Coarctation of aorta, pulmonary stenosis and atresia, and aortic stenosis and atresia.

24
What are the two forms of coarctation of the aorta?
Show answer

The “infantile” form of coarctation of the aorta is often symptomatic in early childhood and consists of tubular hypoplasia of the aortic arch proximal to a PDA. By contrast, the “adult” form has a discrete, ridgelike infolding of the aorta just opposite the closed ductus arteriosus (ligamentum arteriosum) distal to the arch vessels. Although coarctation of the aorta may occur as a solitary defect, in 50% of cases there is also a bicuspid aortic valve. Coarctation may be associated with congenital aortic stenosis, ASD, VSD, mitral regurgitation, or berry aneurysms of the circle of Willis.

25
How does PDA affect the clinical presentation and natural history of coarctation of the aorta?
Show answer

Coarctation of the aorta with a PDA usually manifests early in life. The delivery of unsaturated blood through the PDA produces cyanosis localized to the lower half of the body. Many such infants do not survive the neonatal period without surgical or catheter-based intervention to occlude the PDA. In coarctation of the aorta without a PDA most children are asymptomatic unless the aortic constriction is severe and the disease is discovered in adulthood. Typically there is hypertension in the upper extremities with weak pulses and hypotension in the lower extremities, associated with manifestations of arterial insufficiency (i.e., claudication and coldness). Collateral circulation between the pre-coarctation and post-coarctation arteries develops through enlarged intercostal and internal mammary arteries, often producing radiographically visible erosions (“notching”) of the undersurfaces of the ribs.

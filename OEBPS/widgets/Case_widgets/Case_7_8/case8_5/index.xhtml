Stroke of Bad Luck

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Describe the etiology, pathology, and natural history of acute rheumatic fever and rheumatic heart disease.
  2. • Compare and contrast the different types of valvular vegetations.
  3. • Discuss the risk factors, clinical setting, and natural history of infective endocarditis.

A 25-year old woman who immigrated to the United States eight years ago from Mexico was brought to the emergency department by her father. She had been febrile with headaches for the previous two days, and when she awakened that morning, she was unable to move her left arm or leg and she couldn’t speak. She had no reliable access to health care, but had a tooth extracted a week earlier. Over the last two years, she had worsening dyspnea on exertion and, one month ago, had experienced a temporary loss of vision in her left eye.

Physical exam revealed an aphasic woman with no voluntary movement of her left-sided extremities. She had subungual hemorrhages. She was tachypneic (30 breaths/minute) with a temperature of 41°C. Basilar rales were auscultated. Cardiovascular examination was significant for a loud S1, best heard at the apex of the heart, and a low decrescendo-crescendo diastolic murmur.

1
What are the causes of a diastolic murmur?
Show answer

Cardiac murmurs are due to turbulent blood flow and can be categorized as systolic or diastolic murmurs. The former begin with or after S1 and end before or at S2. By contrast, diastolic murmurs begin with or after S2 and end before or at S1. Causes of diastolic murmurs include aortic or pulmonic regurgitation and mitral or tricuspid stenosis.

A head computed tomography scan revealed changes consistent with an ischemic stroke. An electrocardiogram (ECG) showed left atrial fibrillation. An echocardiogram revealed left atrial enlargement, mitral stenosis, mitral vegetations, and a mural thrombus.

2
What is the most likely cause of her mitral stenosis?
Show answer

Rheumatic heart disease is by far the most common cause of mitral stenosis. Other, less common, causes include tumors, blood clots, and radiation therapy.

Upon further questioning, the patient’s father stated that when she was about 10 years old, she had had a sore throat for nearly a week. About three weeks later, she had a high fever and painful swelling of her joints that first affected the knees and then the elbows. He recalled that the pediatrician told him that she had a new heart murmur. The patient had several additional episodes of sore throats accompanied by joint pain over the next two years.

3
What was the likely cause of the patient’s migratory polyarthritis, fever, and heart murmur?
Show answer

The patient most likely had acute rheumatic fever (ARF), an immunologically mediated, multisystem inflammatory disease classically occurring a few weeks after an episode of group A streptococcal pharyngitis. The diagnosis of ARF is made based on serologic evidence of previous streptococcal infection in conjunction with two or more of the Jones criteria: (1) carditis; (2) migratory polyarthritis of large joints; (3) subcutaneous nodules; (4) erythematous annular skin rash (erythema marginatum); and (5) Sydenham chorea, a neurologic disorder characterized by involuntary purposeless, rapid movements. Minor criteria such as fever, arthralgias, ECG changes, or elevated acute phase reactants also can help support the diagnosis. ARF is now rare in the United States due to early recognition and treatment of streptococcal pharyngitis but remains common in lower income parts of the world.

4
What is the pathogenesis of acute rheumatic fever (ARF)?
Show answer

ARF results from host immune responses to group A streptococcal antigens that lead to cross-recognition of host antigens. In particular, antibodies and CD4+ T cells directed against streptococcal M proteins can also, in some cases, recognize cardiac self-antigens. Antibody binding can activate complement and recruit Fc-receptor bearing innate immune cells (neutrophils and macrophages); cytokine production by antigen-stimulated T cells leads to macrophage activation. Damage to heart tissue may thus be caused by a combination of antibody- and T cell–mediated reactions.

5
What would be the histologic findings seen in the heart?
Show answer

Inflammatory lesions are found widely scattered in the valves, pericardium, and myocardium. These lesions, called Aschoff bodies, consist of plump macrophages (Aschoff or Anitschkow cells), some of which are multinucleated (Aschoff giant cells). A central zone of fibrinoid necrosis can be seen in Aschoff bodies in early stages of ARF.

6
What was the cause of the patient’s childhood heart murmur?
Show answer

Immunologically mediated inflammation of the valve substance activates and injures overlying endothelial cells, initiating thrombosis and the formation of vegetations (arrows) along the closing edge of the valve. The inflammation and vegetations damage the valve leaflets, preventing their closure and leading to regurgitation. The mitral and aortic valves are most commonly affected. Valve damage is believed to be mediated by binding of cross-reactive antibodies; it is possible that the hemodynamics of the left side of the heart favor antibody binding/deposition. As part of the healing process, there is thickening of the chordae tendineae.


  • Valvular tissue
  • Chordae tendineae
7
What is the cause of the patient’s current mitral valve stenosis?
Show answer

The patient now has chronic rheumatic heart disease. As discussed previously, during episodes of ARF, the valvular tissue is injured, resulting in vegetations, and, over time, thickening of the chordae tendineae. Dense fibrosis distorts and thickens the valve leaflets. Commissural fusion (arrows) may also occur.


8
Why is the patient dyspneic?
Show answer

The patient has dyspnea due to severe mitral stenosis. As the cross-sectional area of the orifice of the affected valve grows smaller, there is progressive obstruction to blood flow from the left atrium to the left ventricle. Blood first backs up into the left atrium, which enlarges. Pressure increases in the atrium and pulmonary vascular tree. Elevated pulmonary vascular pressure leads to pulmonary edema, which caused her dyspnea. With longstanding severe mitral stenosis, permanent structural changes in the pulmonary vasculature may limit flow even after the abnormality is corrected. Severe mitral stenosis also leads to decreased filling of the left ventricle and low cardiac output, leading to symptoms of left-sided heart failure.

9
What was the cause of the patient’s temporary unilateral vision loss one month earlier?
Show answer

This symptom is likely due to an embolus from the mural thrombus seen in the left atrium, leading to temporary obstruction of the retinal, ophthalmic, or ciliary artery. The stenotic mitral valve and dilated left atrium allow blood to stagnate, which facilitates thrombosis. Endocardial damage due to regurgitant jets may also provide a nidus for thrombus formation.

10
What is the cause of the patient’s fever and subungual hemorrhages?
Show answer

Fever and subungual hemorrhages in a patient with chronic rheumatic heart disease are suggestive of infective endocarditis.

11
How is infective endocarditis (IE) classified?
Show answer

Traditionally, IE has been classified on clinical grounds into acute and subacute forms. Acute infective endocarditis is typically caused by infection of a previously normal heart valve by a highly virulent organism (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus) that rapidly produces necrotizing, destructive lesions. These infections may be difficult to cure with antibiotics alone and usually require surgery. Despite appropriate treatment, death can ensue within days to weeks. By contrast, subacute IE is caused by insidious infections with organisms of lower virulence (e.g., viridans streptococci) that involve previously deformed valves and lead to less overall destruction. In such cases, the disease may pursue a protracted course of weeks to months, but cures can be achieved with antibiotics.

12
Which factors increase risk for IE?
Show answer

Conditions that predispose to IE include: (1) abnormal flow (e.g., hemodynamically abnormal valves, congenital shunts); (2) bacteremia (e.g., instrumentation, intravenous drug use, surgery); and (3) immunodeficiency (e.g., neutropenia, iatrogenic immunosuppression, etc.). In abnormal hearts, as in this case, streptococci, most often S. viridans, are often causative.

13
Which factors predispose a patient to tricuspid valve endocarditis?
Show answer

Right-sided endocarditis typically occurs in intravenous drug users because pathogens enter the venous circulation and reach the right side of the heart. In this specimen, there are large, bulky vegetations on all the leaflets of the tricuspid valve that may have been regurgitant. Pulmonary symptoms may occur due to septic emboli arising from these vegetations.

14
What are the complications associated with infective endocarditis?
Show answer

The complications of infective endocarditis may be cardiac or extracardiac. Cardiac complications include valve insufficiency, myocardial abscesses (ring abscesses), suppurative pericarditis, and, rarely, hemopericardium. Embolic complications include infarcts and abscesses. Their location depends on whether the right-sided or left-sided valves are affected. Renal complications include, in addition to infarcts and abscesses, focal glomerulonephritis (due to microemboli) and diffuse glomerulonephritis secondary to antigen-antibody complex formation.

15
Which organisms are commonly associated with infective endocarditis?
Show answer

The causative organisms differ depending on the underlying risk factors; 50% to 60% of cases occurring on damaged or deformed valves are caused by Streptococcus viridans, a part of normal oral flora that is usually harmless. The more virulent S. aureus can attack healthy as well as deformed valves; it also is the major cause of infections occurring in intravenous drug users. Additional bacterial agents include enterococci and the so-called “HACEK group”(Haemophilus, Actinobacillus, Cardiobacterium, Eikenella, and Kingella), all commensals found in the oral cavity. In about 10% of cases of endocarditis, no organism is isolated from the blood (“culture-negative” endocarditis).

16
What can be done to reduce the risk of infective endocarditis?
Show answer

Patients with abnormal heart valves and congenital shunts should have antibiotic prophylaxis before procedures likely to produce bacteremia, such as tooth extraction.

The patient was admitted to the hospital. Blood cultures grew Streptococcus viridans and intravenous antibiotic therapy was started. However, the patient developed cardiac arrest and died despite efforts at resuscitation. An autopsy was performed.

Sections of the brain revealed a recent infarct in the territory of the right middle cerebral artery; a septic embolus was seen histologically. Sections of the heart showed large, friable vegetations (arrows) on the mitral valve. There was total occlusion of the left main coronary artery by a septic embolus.

17
What are the components of a vegetation, and which factors predispose to their formation?
Show answer

A vegetation is an intracardiac thrombus composed mainly of fibrin with variable numbers of platelets and, in infectious cases, microbes. They often form on valves that are hemodynamically abnormal or following injury to the valve endocardium. Sterile vegetations may occur secondary to diseases such as rheumatic fever, lupus, or congenital heart disease, or may occur on normal valves in hypercoagulable states, sometimes in association with chronic illnesses. Sterile vegetations are prone to becoming infected.

 
18
What are the four types of valvular vegetations and what are their characteristics?
Show answer

Rheumatic heart disease (RHD) is marked by small, warty vegetations along the lines of closure of the valve leaflets. Infective endocarditis (IE) is characterized by large, irregular masses on the valve cusps that can extend onto the chordae. Nonbacterial thrombotic endocarditis (NBTE) occurs in debilitated patients and exhibits one or more small, bland vegetations, usually attached at the line of closure. Libman-Sacks endocarditis (LSE) arises in the setting of systemic lupus erythematosus and is characterized by the presence of small- or medium-sized vegetations on either or both sides of the valve leaflets.


19
What are some clinical conditions associated with nonbacterial thrombotic endocarditis?
Show answer

Hypercoagulable states and valve trauma from indwelling catheters are associated with nonbacterial thrombotic endocarditis. The most common cause of hypercoagulability is advanced malignancy, which underlies 80-90% of cases. The vegetations are small, tan masses located along the lines of closure. This type of vegetation is usually loosely attached to the valve leaflet and can break off and cause embolic phenomena. Occasionally, they can become infected by circulating bacteria, leading to infective endocarditis.

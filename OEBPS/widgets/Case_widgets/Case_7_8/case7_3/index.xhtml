A 37-year-old man presented to the Emergency Department with a 1-month history of fatigue and a two-week history of a “rash”. He is from Botswana, visiting family in the United States, and noticed multiple lesions on his back. He was unmarried and had unprotected sex with multiple partners. On physical examination, he was thin but otherwise in no apparent distress. The skin lesions were reddish purple in color, nontender, and nonblanching to palpation. Several similar lesions were observed in the oropharynx. Laboratory tests were ordered.

What is the significance of the laboratory tests?
Show answer

The patient has an absolute lymphopenia. This finding, combined with a history of high-risk behavior and his geographic origin, raises the possibility of human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) infection. A CD4 T cell count and a HIV immunoassay should be obtained.

The CD4 T cell count was 52 cells/mm3 (normal, 460–1600 cells/mm3). Immunoassays were positive for HIV-1. A diagnosis of HIV infection and acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS) was rendered. A skin biopsy was performed, which revealed a dermal proliferation of spindle cells with many extravasated red cells and scattered lymphocytes. “Spindle cells” have elongated cylindrical nuclei that may look round when cut in cross section.

2
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

This patient has Kaposi sarcoma, a malignant vascular neoplasm that is considered to be an intermediate grade, or “borderline,” tumor. It is the extravasated red blood cells that give the skin lesions their dark color.

3
What is the pathogenesis of Kaposi sarcoma (KS)?
Show answer

KS is uniformly associated with infection by human herpesvirus-8 (HHV8), also known as Kaposi sarcoma herpesvirus (KSHV). The spindle cells in the proliferation have some features of endothelial cells and are latently infected with HHV8, which encodes homologs of several proto-oncogenes, including cyclin D and inhibitors of p53. Normally, HHV8 infection is held in check by a T cell response to viral antigens; in the absence of an effective T cell response, latently infected cells may begin to proliferate, resulting in Karposi sarcoma.

An immunohistochemical stain for HHV8 is positive in the spindled nuclei. The antibody is specific for an HHV8-encoded protein called latency associated nuclear antigen-1 (LANA1).

4
What is the proposed mechanism for the vascular proliferation in KS?
Show answer

It is thought that a virally encoded constitutively active G protein-coupled receptor induces VEGF production, stimulating endothelial growth, and that cytokines produced by inflammatory cells recruited to sites of lytic infection may create a local proliferative milieu.

5
What are the clinical settings in which Kaposi sarcoma seen?
Show answer

AIDS-associated (epidemic) KS, as in this patient, is an AIDS-defining illness; worldwide it represents the most common HIV-related malignancy. With the advent of retroviral therapy, the incidence of KS has fallen more than 80%; however, the incidence is still much higher (1000-fold) in HIV-infected patients than in the general population. AIDS-associated KS often involves lymph nodes and disseminates widely to viscera early in its course. Classic KS is a disorder of older men of Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, or Eastern European descent (especially Ashkenazi Jews); it is uncommon in the United States. It can be associated with other malignancies or altered immunity but is not associated with HIV infection. Endemic African KS typically occurs in younger (under 40 years of age) HIV-seronegative individuals and can follow an indolent or aggressive course; it involves lymph nodes much more frequently than in the classic variant. Transplantation-associated KS arises in the setting of T cell immunosuppression. It is aggressive, and although lesions typically regress with reduction in immunosuppression, there is a risk of organ rejection. Classic KS and endemic African KS occur in HIV-negative patients, but the cofactors are unknown. All forms are associated with HHV8.

6
What are the different stages of progression of cutaneous KS lesions?
Show answer

Particularly in classic KS, the cutaneous lesions progress through three distinct stages: patch, plaque, and nodule. Patches are pink, red, or purple macules, typically confined to the distal lower extremities. With time, the lesions spread proximally and become larger, violaceous, raised plaques. Eventually, nodular, more overtly neoplastic lesions appear.

7
What is the preferred treatment for this patient?
Show answer

Restoration of T cell function is an effective strategy in KS related to immunodeficiency. Antiretroviral therapy is the treatment of choice, and is the only treatment necessary in patients with early stage disease without evidence of rapid progression.

The patient received combination antiretroviral therapy, which achieved a 2 log reduction in viral load by 4 weeks and a reduction of viral load below detectable levels by 8 weeks. Two weeks after commencing therapy, his skin and mucosal lesions began to fade, and by 8 weeks they had mostly resolved. Arrangements were made for him to receive antiviral therapy in Botswana, and he was able to return home.

8
Which other vascular proliferation is associated with AIDS?
Show answer

Bacillary angiomatosis is a vascular proliferation in immunocompromised hosts (e.g., patients with AIDS) caused by opportunistic Gram-negative bacilli of the Bartonella family. Cutaneous lesions can appear as nodules, plaques, or may be pedunculated. They can also involve bone, brain, and other organs. Histologically, there is a proliferation of capillaries lined by prominent epithelioid endothelial cells, which exhibit nuclear atypia and mitoses.

Two species have been implicated:


  • • Bartonella henselae, whose principal reservoir is the domestic cat; this organism causes cat-scratch disease (a necrotizing granulomatous inflammation of lymph nodes) in immunocompetent hosts.
  • • Bartonella quintana, which is transmitted by human body lice; this microbe was the cause of “trench fever” in World War I.

9
Which mechanism is thought to cause the vascular proliferation seen in bacillary angiomatosis?
Show answer

The bacteria induce host tissues to produce hypoxia-inducible factor-1α (HIF-1α), which drives VEGF production and vascular proliferation.

10
What is the range of clinical behavior seen in vascular neoplasms?
Show answer

Tumors of blood vessels and lymphatics constitute a spectrum from benign hemangiomas (e.g., lobular capillary hemangiomas, also known as pyogenic granulomas) to borderline lesions (e.g., Kaposi sarcoma, hemangioendotheliomas) that are locally aggressive but infrequently metastasize, to rare, highly malignant angiosarcomas.

11
What are the histologic features associated with benignancy in vascular tumors?
Show answer

Benign vascular tumors are typically composed of vascular channels lined by a bland monolayer of endothelial cells. The lumens are filled with blood cells or lymph. Although circumscription is a general feature of benignancy, benign vascular tumors may be somewhat infiltrative.

What are the risk factors for angiosarcoma?
Show answer

Risk factors include chronic lymphedema after axillary lymph node dissection, radiation therapy (including of breast cancer), and exposure to vinyl chloride (linked to angiosarcoma of the liver).

14
What is the histologic appearance of angiosarcoma?
Show answer

Angiosarcomas range from highly differentiated tumors resembling hemangiomas to wildly anaplastic lesions with pleomorphic cells that line branching, vascular channels.

  • Angiosarcoma
  • Vascular channels

15
What is the clinical behavior of angiosarcomas?
Show answer

Older adults are more commonly affected, without gender predilection; lesions can occur at any site, but most often involve the skin, soft tissue, breast, and liver. Clinically, angiosarcomas are aggressive tumors that invade locally and metastasize. Current 5-year survival rates are only about 30%. Prognosis varies depending on the tumor grade.

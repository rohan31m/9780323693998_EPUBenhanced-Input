Broken Hearted

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Describe the risk factors, clinical presentation and complications of aortic stenosis.
  2. • Compare and contrast the different types of prosthetic valves.

A 75-year-old man was brought to the hospital for evaluation of chest pain. He described feeling weak and dizzy after a walk around the block that morning. The episode of dizziness was followed by 10–15 minutes of chest pain that quickly resolved when he rested. He also described a 2-month history of similar but shorter episodes of chest pain and dizziness after working in his yard. He had been told several years ago that he had a slight heart murmur, but he had not been evaluated further. He had been otherwise healthy all his life.

1
What is the significance of the patient’s transient chest pain?
Show answer

This patient has stable angina pectoris (literally, chest pain) due to ischemia that induces pain but is insufficient to cause myocyte death. Angina can be stable (occurring predictably at certain levels of exertion, as in this patient), can be caused by vessel spasm (Prinzmetal angina), or can be unstable (occurring with progressively less exertion or even at rest).

Cardiovascular exam was significant for a mid-systolic crescendo-decrescendo murmur. Transthoracic echocardiography showed features consistent with aortic stenosis. Ultrasound of the patient’s carotid artery showed the pulse to be reduced in amplitude (“parvus”) and delayed in occurrence relative to the heart’s contraction (“tardus”). Cardiac troponin I and T levels were not elevated, and other laboratory tests were also normal. An electrocardiogram showed atrial fibrillation and left ventricular hypertrophy.

2
What are the causes of systolic murmurs?
Show answer

Cardiac murmurs are due to turbulent blood flow and can be categorized as systolic or diastolic murmurs. The former begin with or after S1 and end before or at S2. By contrast, diastolic murmurs begin with or after S2 and end before or at S1. Causes of systolic murmurs include mitral or tricuspid regurgitation, ventricular septal defect, and aortic valve stenosis.

3
What is the significance of a pulsus tardus and parvus?
Show answer

A carotid artery pulse that is delayed relative to the cardiac pulse suggests a prolonged left ventricular ejection time; the peak of the carotid artery pulse is usually close to the S1 heart sound. A weakened pulse is due to decreased stroke volume. Both of these findings are seen in aortic valve stenosis.

4
What is the pathophysiologic basis of this patient’s chest pain?
Show answer

He has chest pain because the aortic stenosis causes a pressure drop across the valve, decreasing aortic pressure relative to intraventricular pressure. If stenosis is severe, the coronary artery pressure may fall to a level that is insufficient to deliver adequate blood to the myocardium during diastole, particularly to the subendocardium at times of increased workload (e.g., with physical exertion). Hypoperfusion of subendocardial zones leads to ischemia, cardiac dysfunction, and angina. The problem is worsened by left ventricular hypertrophy, which inevitably occurs in response to the increased left ventricular pressure and increases the workload (and thus oxygen consumption) of the heart.

His aortic valve was replaced with a mechanical valve. Gross examination of the explanted valve revealed large, dense, calcific masses that approached, but did not involve, the free edges of the cusps.

  • Commissures
  • Aortic lumen
  • Coronary artery origins
  • Calcific masses
  • Aortic cusps
5
What is the effect of this calcification on valve function?
Show answer

The cusps are distorted and frozen in a nonphysiologic position by the calcified nodules, such that they can just barely open during systole and may not be able to close fully with diastole. In this case, the commissures of the valve are not fused. Fusion of the commissures would suggest a pre-existing inflammatory process, such as previous rheumatic heart disease or a prior bout of infective endocarditis. Aortic stenosis of previously normal valves usually comes to clinical attention in the seventh to ninth decades of life whereas stenotic bicuspid valves tend to become clinically significant 1 to 2 decades earlier.

6
What determines the severity of symptoms associated with aortic stenosis?
Show answer

The severity of symptoms is proportional to the degree of stenosis, which determines the pressure difference between the left ventricle and the aorta.

7
What causes this type of calcification?
Show answer

This is an example of dystrophic calcification, defined as calcification that occurs secondary to tissue injury. The pathogenesis of the tissue injury that leads to aortic calcific stenosis is incompletely understood, but the disorder is associated with the same risk factors as atherosclerosis, suggesting that the process is initiated by endothelial injury and inflammation caused by hyperlipidemia and exacerbated by mechanical stresses (e.g., hypertension). It also is associated with bicuspid aortic valve, suggesting that mechanical wear and tear also has a role.

8
What is the basis for the patient’s dizziness?
Show answer

The patient probably is dizzy because his brain is inadequately perfused. Just as myocardial perfusion is diminished due to the pressure drop across the stenotic valve, perfusion of the brain also may be decreased.

9
What caused this patient’s left ventricular hypertrophy?
Show answer

Aortic stenosis creates resistance to outflow of the blood, increasing left ventricular pressure and myocardial work. The anatomic manifestation of increased work is concentric left ventricular hypertrophy. The left ventricular wall is thickened, and histologic sections show hypertrophy of individual cardiac myocytes.

  • Heart, gross
  • Stenotic aortic valve
  • Mitral valve
  • Left ventricular hypertrophy
10
What are the risk factors for developing calcific aortic stenosis?
Show answer

Senile calcific aortic stenosis rarely occurs in patients younger than 60. Bicuspid aortic valve, a congenital abnormality that affects 1% to 2% of the population, is associated with the development of calcific aortic stenosis one to two decades earlier. In a congenitally bicuspid aortic valve, there are only two functional cusps, usually of unequal size, with the larger cusp having a midline raphe resulting from incomplete commissural separation during development; less frequently, the cusps are of equal size and the raphe is absent. The raphe is frequently a site of calcium deposits. Bicuspid valves may produce abnormal mechanical forces leading to valve injury.

  • Bicuspid aortic valve
  • Median raphe
  • Cusps
  • Calcification
11
What is the prognosis of patients with calcific aortic stenosis?
Show answer

Once symptoms appear (angina, syncope, heart failure), the prognosis is poor without treatment, with most patients dying within 5 years. With valve replacement, prognosis improves substantially.

12
What is the typical cause of aortic stenosis in children?
Show answer

In children, aortic stenosis is most often an isolated congenital defect (80% of cases). Congenital narrowing and obstruction of the aortic valve can occur at three locations: valvular, subvalvular, and supravalvular. With valvular aortic stenosis the cusps may be hypoplastic (small), dysplastic (thickened, nodular), or abnormal in number (usually acommissural or unicommissural). Subaortic stenosis is caused by a thickened ring or collar of dense endocardial fibrous tissue below the level of the cusps. Supravalvular aortic stenosis is a congenital aortic dysplasia with thickening of ascending aortic wall and consequent luminal constriction.

13
What are the two types of artificial valves?
Show answer

Artificial valves may be mechanical or bioprosthetic. Mechanical valves consist of different configurations of rigid nonphysiologic material. Tissue valves (bioprostheses) may be made from porcine aortic valves, bovine pericardium, or frozen human valves from deceased donors.

14
Which types of complications are most likely in patients receiving bioprosthetic valves (e.g., pig or cow valves) versus mechanical valves?
Show answer

Bioprosthetic valves (presumably because of their more physiologic surfaces) are less likely to be complicated by thromboembolic disease, and more likely to suffer a mechanical failure, which can lead to severe valvular insufficiency. Severe turbulence associated with mechanical valves sometimes causes red cell hemolysis (blender effect), leading to the appearance of anemia and red cell fragments (schistocytes) in peripheral blood smears.

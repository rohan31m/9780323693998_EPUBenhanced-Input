Big-Hearted

Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast the different types of cardiomyopathy with respect to etiology and clinical features.
  2. • Describe the etiology, histologic features, and clinical consequences of myocarditis.

A 16-year-old boy presented to his pediatrician following an episode of syncope. He had been playing basketball with some friends and noticed mild dyspnea and chest pain immediately before the event. At the time of examination, he felt no chest pain. He had no significant past medical history.

The patient’s vital signs were blood pressure 110/74 mm Hg, heart rate 75 bpm, respiratory rate 18 breaths/minute and temperature 37°C. Cardiovascular exam revealed a forceful systolic heave and an apical precordial impulse displaced far to the left on the chest wall. A harsh systolic ejection murmur was auscultated. A chest radiograph showed cardiomegaly with slight atrial enlargement. An electrocardiogram confirmed the presence of left ventricular hypertrophy.

1
What is the differential diagnosis of cardiomegaly?
Show answer

Cardiomegaly may be secondary to many causes, including hypertension (systemic or pulmonary), left-sided heart failure due to any cause, dilated cardiomyopathy, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, amyloidosis, glycogen storage disease (type II, Pompe disease), and Fabry disease.

2
What are the different types of cardiomyopathy?
Show answer

Cardiac diseases due to intrinsic myocardial dysfunction are termed cardiomyopathies (literally, heart muscle diseases); these may be primary—that is, principally confined to the myocardium—or secondary—that is, presenting as the cardiac manifestation of a systemic disorder. They are a diverse group of diseases that includes inflammatory disorders (e.g., myocarditis), immunologic diseases (e.g., sarcoidosis), systemic metabolic disorders (e.g., hemochromatosis), muscular dystrophies, and genetic disorders of myocardial fibers.

Laboratory tests were within normal limits, and serum markers of cardiac injury (troponins) were negative for myocardial infarction. The patient was referred to a cardiologist for an echocardiogram. However, while running to catch a bus, the patient experienced a cardiac arrest and died. The medical examiner’s office performed an autopsy.

Evaluation of the heart revealed marked thickening (>2cm) of the left ventricular wall and the interventricular septum. The septal muscle bulged into the left ventricle just below the aortic outflow tract, causing the left ventricular lumen to assume a classic “banana-like” appearance. The right ventricle was also hypertrophied and had a slit-like ventricular lumen. No valvular pathology was seen.

  • Heart, gross
  • Left ventricular wall
  • Interventricular septum
  • Right ventricle free wall
  • Subaortic septal bulge
  • Ventricular lumen
3
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

The gross finding of left ventricular wall thickening >1.5 cm in the absence of hypertension or valvular disease is diagnostic of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM). Usually there is disproportionate thickening of the ventricular septum relative to the free left ventricular wall. In approximately 10% of cases, the hypertrophy is concentric and symmetrical, similar to that seen in left ventricular hypertrophy, due to systemic hypertension.

4
What would the echocardiogram have shown?
Show answer

Echocardiogram often shows systolic anterior motion of the mitral valve and dynamic obstruction of left ventricular outflow, an effect that increases ventricular pressure and further compromises cardiac function.

5
What are the three main patterns of cardiomyopathy?
Show answer

The three main patterns of cardiomyopathy are hypertrophic, dilated, and restrictive. Hypertrophic and dilated cardiomyopathies often have a genetic basis. Restrictive cardiomyopathy is caused by stiffening of the ventricle, usually due to infiltrative disorders, such as cardiac amyloidosis. Before the term cardiomyopathy can be applied, the clinician must rule out cardiac disease secondary to hypertension, coronary artery disease, valvular heart disease, or congenital malformations of the heart.

6
What are the typical histologic findings in hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM)?
Show answer

The cardiac myocytes are massively hypertrophic. Myocardial fiber disarray is often seen, particularly in the myocytes of the interventricular septum. Instead of being arranged in parallel arrays, the myocytes appear disorganized and individual fibers branch in many different directions.

  • Heart, HCM
  • Myocytes
7
What is the etiology of HCM?
Show answer

HCM is caused by mutations in genes that encode components of the sarcomere, the contractile apparatus of the cardiomyocyte. These mutations alter sarcomere function such that the cell “senses” increased workload, leading to hypertrophy (much as cardiomyocytes with truly increased workloads, such as in the setting of systemic hypertension, undergo hypertrophy).

8
Is genetic testing routinely performed in cases of suspected HCM?
Show answer

HCM is associated with more than 1500 different known mutations, mostly missense mutations, in 11 different genes, many of them large. In the research setting, mutations in the genes encoding β-myosin heavy chain (β-MHC), cardiac troponin T, α-tropomyosin, and myosin-binding protein C (MYBP-C) have been identified in 70% to 80% of cases. However, due to the large number of potentially responsible mutations and genes, genetic testing is not routinely performed in the clinic.

9
How does hypertrophic cardiomyopathy affect cardiac function?
Show answer

Hypertrophy of the myocardium stiffens the ventricle and decreases the size of the ventricular chamber, leading to diastolic dysfunction. As a result, even though ejection is forceful, it is ineffective because the amount of blood in the ventricle is so small.

10
What are the treatment options for patients with HCM?
Show answer

Symptoms may improve with pharmacologic therapy that promotes ventricular relaxation (e.g., beta blockers, calcium channel blockers); partial surgical excision or controlled alcohol-induced infarction of septal muscle also can relieve the outflow tract obstruction.

11
How do the mutations that cause dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) differ from those that cause hypertrophic cardiomyopathy?
Show answer

Like hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, many cases of dilated cardiomyopathy are caused by mutations in genes that encode components of the sarcomere. However, unlike hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, the mutations found in dilated cardiomyopathy lead to loss of function, an effect that over time leads to a dilated, flabby, and ultimately failing heart. Patients present with symptoms of progressive congestive heart failure.

12
What are the acquired causes of DCM?
Show answer

DCM may occur in the setting of myocarditis (particularly viral), alcohol/toxin exposure, during pregnancy (late in gestation or up to several months postpartum) and in cases of iron overload (e.g., hereditary hemochromatosis or multiple transfusions).

13
What are the physiologic consequences of DCM?
Show answer

The fundamental defect in DCM is ineffective contraction. Thus, in end-stage DCM, the cardiac ejection fraction typically is less than 25% (normal is 50%–65%). Secondary mitral regurgitation and abnormal cardiac rhythms are common, and embolism from intracardiac (mural) thrombi can occur.

14
What are the treatment options for patients with DCM?
Show answer

Cardiac transplantation is the only definitive treatment, although implantation of long-term ventricular assist devices is increasingly utilized; in some patients, a course of mechanical assistance can produce durable regression of cardiac dysfunction.

15
What is the primary physiologic problem in restrictive cardiomyopathy?
Show answer

The physiologic problem in restrictive cardiomyopathy is a stiff ventricle that fills and empties poorly. As with HCM, there is diastolic dysfunction.

16
What is senile cardiac amyloidosis and which type of cardiomyopathy does it represent?
Show answer

Cardiac amyloidosis may present as a restrictive cardiomyopathy and can be a consequence of systemic amyloidosis (e.g., multiple myeloma) or can be limited to the heart, particularly in the elderly (senile cardiac amyloidosis). The latter characteristically occurs in individuals 70 years and older, and has a far better prognosis than systemic amyloidosis. Biochemically, cardiac amyloid in systemic amyloidosis caused by plasma cell neoplasms (e.g., multiple myeloma) is comprised of immunoglobulin light chains, while in senile cardiac amyloidosis it is comprised of normal or mutant transthyretins. Grossly, the ventricles are of normal size or slightly enlarged, but the atria are usually dilated due to restricted ventricular filling and increased pressure loads. Microscopically, amyloid appears as smudgy eosinophilic material. It is deposited in vessel walls and between cardiac muscle fibers. Following Congo red staining, amyloid appears red on light microscopy (see below), and as apple-green birefringent material in polarized light.


17
What are some inflammatory causes of cardiomyopathy? How can they be distinguished morphologically?
Show answer

Myocarditis is characterized by inflammation and the consequent degeneration and necrosis of myocardial fibers. Offending agents are usually identified by serologic studies showing rising antibody titers or through molecular diagnostic techniques using infected tissues. However, in some cases there are characteristic histologic findings on cardiac biopsy. The most common cause of myocarditis in North America is viral infection, especially with influenza and enteroviruses such as coxsackievirus and echovirus; viral infections are characterized by lymphocytic infiltrates. The most important cause in South America is Chagas disease, in which myofibers can be seen to be infected by the trypanosome, Trypanosoma cruzi. In addition, myocarditis can also be caused by immune-mediated reactions (e.g., rheumatic fever, drug hypersensitivities) or unknown causes (e.g., giant cell myocarditis). Hypersensitivity myocarditis is characterized by perivascular eosinophil-rich inflammatory infiltrates.

  • Viral myocarditis
  • Lymphocytic infiltrates
  • Trypanosomes
  • Trypanosome
  • Hypersensitivity myocarditis
  • Eosinophils
18
How do viruses cause myocyte damage?
Show answer

While some viruses cause cell death directly, in most cases the injury results from an immune response directed against virally infected cells; this is analogous to the damage inflicted by virus-specific T cells on hepatitis virus–infected liver cells. In some cases, viruses trigger a reaction against cross-reacting proteins such as myosin heavy chain.

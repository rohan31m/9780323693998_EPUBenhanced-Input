My Aching Head

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast the different forms of vasculitis in terms of the vessels involved, histologic findings, and etiologies.
  2. • Describe the role of antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies in noninfectious vasculitis.

A 76-year-old man presented to his primary care physician with a several-week history of fatigue, intermittent low-grade fevers, and stiffness in his shoulders and hips. Further questioning revealed that he has also recently developed occasional generalized headache and jaw pain when chewing meat. Past medical history was significant for mild essential hypertension, treated with an angiotensin converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitor.

Physical examination was unremarkable: temperature was 38.5° C, heart rate was 77 bpm, and respiratory rate was 14 breaths/min. Blood pressure was 135/72 mm Hg in the left arm and 138/74 mm Hg in the right arm. A chest x-ray was normal.

The complete blood count (CBC) revealed a normal white blood cell count (5.6 × 103/mm), mild normocytic anemia (hematocrit, 36), and mild thrombocytosis (platelet count, 480 × 103/mm). Additional laboratory studies revealed that the erythrocyte sedimentation rate was 100 mm/hour (normal, <20) and the serum C-reactive protein level was 35mg/dL (normal, <3).

1
Based on the laboratory findings and the clinical presentation, which general category of disease does this patient have?
Show answer

The history of fever and fatigue, mild anemia, thrombocytosis, and elevated erythrocyte sedimentation rate and C-reactive protein level are consistent with a chronic systemic inflammatory disorder.

2
What is the differential diagnosis based on these findings?
Show answer

The differential diagnosis is broad and includes cancer, infection (e.g., tuberculosis), and noninfectious inflammatory disorders, such as vasculitides and certain arthritides.

3
Which other clinical features are helpful in narrowing the differential diagnosis?
Show answer

Headache and jaw claudication may be symptoms of giant cell (temporal) arteritis. Also supporting this possibility are the joint symptoms, which may be a manifestation of polymyalgia rheumatica, a form of arthritis that accompanies giant cell arteritis in almost 50% of cases; and the age of the patient, as most patients with giant cell arteritis are older than 70 years of age.

Because of the suspicion of giant cell arteritis, the patient underwent a temporal artery biopsy under local anesthesia. A 1.8-cm portion of the right temporal artery was resected, carefully sectioned, and entirely submitted for histologic examination. Sections showed giant cells within the media near the internal elastic lamina and intimal thickening. An elastin stain showed the destruction of elastic fibers in the wall of the artery.

  • Giant cells
  • Histology
  • Intimal thickening
  • Elastic fiber destruction
4
What is the diagnosis?
Show answer

Giant cell (temporal) arteritis, a chronic inflammatory disorder, typically presents with granulomatous inflammation and principally affects large- to small-sized arteries in the head.

5
Why was such a large (1.5 cm) biopsy taken in this patient?
Show answer

The pathologic changes are notoriously patchy along the length of affected vessels. A large biopsy is necessary to be sure that the pathologic process is sampled.

6
Broadly, what are the pathogenic mechanisms that underlie the two major categories of vasculitis?
Show answer

Vasculitis is a general term for vessel wall inflammation. The two most common pathogenic mechanisms of vasculitis are immune-mediated inflammation and direct vascular invasion by infectious pathogens. Giant cell arteritis is a type of immune-mediated vasculitis.

7
What are the major types of immune-mediated vasculitides and how are they classified?
Show answer

The major immune-mediated vasculitides include giant cell arteritis, Takayasu arteritis, polyarteritis nodosa, Kawasaki disease, microscopic polyangiitis, granulomatosis with polyangiitis, Churg-Strauss syndrome and thromboangiitis obliterans (Buerger disease). There is considerable clinical and pathologic overlap among some of these entities. Classification schemes attempt (with variable success) to group them according to vessel diameter, role of immune complexes, presence of specific autoantibodies, granuloma formation, organ specificity, and even population demographics.


8
What are the different mechanisms of immunologic injury in noninfectious vasculitis?
Show answer

Immunologic injury in noninfectious vasculitis may be caused by immune complex deposition, antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies, antiendothelial cell antibodies, and autoreactive T cells.

9
In which diseases is immune complex deposition the mechanism of injury?
Show answer

This form of vasculitis is seen in immunologic disorders such as systemic lupus erythematosus that are associated with autoantibody production. In addition, immune complex deposition is seen in some cases of polyarteritis nodosa, drug hypersensitivity vasculitis, and vasculitis secondary to infections.

10
What is the pathogenesis of giant cell arteritis?
Show answer

Giant cell arteritis likely occurs as a result of a T cell–mediated immune response to an as-yet uncharacterized vessel wall antigen. Pro-inflammatory cytokines (especially TNF) and anti-endothelial cell antibodies also contribute. The characteristic granulomatous inflammation, an association with certain MHC class II haplotypes, and the excellent therapeutic response to steroids all strongly support an immune etiology. The predilection for vessels of the head remains unexplained, although one hypothesis is that vessels in various parts of the body develop from distinct anlagen and may, therefore, express unique antigens.

11
Why is early recognition of giant cell arteritis important?
Show answer

Untreated giant cell arteritis can result in abrupt blindness due to involvement of branches of the carotid artery such as the ophthalmic artery. Treatment with immunosuppressive drugs is highly effective in preventing blindness. Typically, if giant cell arteritis is suspected, immunosuppressive therapy is begun even before the biopsy results are reported.

The patient was started on high dose glucocorticoids, with resolution of his symptoms after 3 days.

12
What is Takayasu arteritis and how does it compare to giant cell arteritis?
Show answer

Like giant cell arteritis, Takayasu arteritis is characterized by granulomatous inflammation and is a disease of large- to medium-sized arteries. However, Takayasu arteritis is more common in younger patients and affects medium- and large-sized arteries, often involving the branches of the aortic arch, causing diminution of pulses (“pulseless disease”). Grossly, there is marked intimal thickening and luminal narrowing due to fibrosis. Histologically, A mononuclear infiltrate and multinucleate giant cells can be seen.

13
What is a vasculitis of large- and medium-sized vessels that primarily affects children, and what is its proposed etiology?
Show answer

Kawasaki disease is an acute, febrile, usually self-limited illness of infancy and childhood: approximately 80% of patients are younger than 4 years of age. Kawasaki disease primarily involves large- to medium-sized vessels. Involvement of the coronary arteries can result in aneurysms that rupture or thrombose, causing myocardial infarction. In genetically susceptible individuals, a variety of infectious agents (mostly viral) have been posited to trigger the disease. This is consistent with recent observations showing that infection of children with SARS-CoV2 (the agent of COVID-19) is associated in a small minority of cases with a vasculitis resembling Kawasaki disease.

14
How does polyarteritis nodosa differ from giant cell arteritis?
Show answer

In contrast to the large to medium-sized arteries affected by giant cell arteritis and Takayasu arteritis, polyarteritis nodosa (PAN) tends to involve small- to medium-sized muscular arteries. Kidney, heart, liver and gastrointestinal tract vessels are affected in descending order of frequency. This disease lacks granulomatous inflammation and instead, the lesions are necrotizing, often leading to fibrinoid necrosis and thrombosis of the involved vessel. About 30% of cases are associated with immune complexes containing hepatitis B antigens. The etiology of the remaining cases is uncertain. There is no association with antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies. Both PAN and giant cell arteritis can be patchy with areas of uninvolved vessel.

15
What is the classic presentation of PAN?
Show answer

A “classic” presentation involves involve some combination of rapidly accelerating hypertension due to renal artery involvement; abdominal pain and bloody stools caused by gastrointestinal lesions; diffuse muscular aches and pains; and peripheral neuritis predominantly affecting motor nerves. Renal involvement often is prominent and is a major cause of death.

16
What are anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies (ANCAs) and what is their clinical significance?
Show answer

ANCAs are a heterogeneous group of autoantibodies directed against constituents (mainly enzymes) of neutrophil primary granules, monocyte lysosomes, and endothelial cells. ANCAs are very useful diagnostic markers; their titers generally mirror clinical severity, and a rise in titers after periods of quiescence is predictive of disease recurrence. Although a number of ANCAs have been described, the two most important are (1) antiproteinase-3 (PR3-ANCA), previously called c-ANCA; and (2) antimyeloperoxidase (MPO-ANCA), previously called p-ANCA. ANCAs can directly activate neutrophils, stimulating the release of reactive oxygen species and proteolytic enzymes; in vascular beds, this may lead to endothelial cell injury.

17
Which vasculitides are associated with ANCAs?
Show answer

PR3-ANCAs are associated with granulomatosis with polyangiitis (previously known as Wegener granulomatosis) whereas MPO-ANCAs are associated with microscopic polyangiitis and eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis (previously known as Churg-Strauss vasculitis).

18
What is the pathogenesis of granulomatosis with polyangiitis?
Show answer

The pathogenesis is uncertain. The granulomatous inflammation points to a role for activated Th1 helper T cells in the tissue injury. Because the respiratory tract is often involved, inhaled environmental antigens have been implicated. The precise role of ANCAs is controversial. There is some evidence that PR3 and MPO are displayed on the surface of neutrophils and that binding of ANCAs to these antigens activates neutrophils and enhances their proinflammatory activities, such as production of reactive oxygen species that damage endothelium and other cells within tissues. As with other autoimmune disorders, the basis for ANCA formation is not understood.

19
What are the three pathologic findings in granulomatosis with polyangiitis?
Show answer

The three pathologic findings in granulomatosis with polyangiitis are (1) necrotizing granulomas of the upper-respiratory tract (ear, nose, sinuses, throat) or the lower-respiratory tract (lung) or both; (2) necrotizing or granulomatous vasculitis, affecting small- to medium-sized vessels (e.g., capillaries, venules, arterioles, and arteries), most prominently in the lungs and upper airways but other sites as well; and (3) focal necrotizing, often crescentic, glomerulonephritis

20
What are the two major subgroups of small vessel vasculitis, and what are the major entities belonging to these subtypes?
Show answer

Small vessel vasculitis can be divided into subtypes that are associated with ANCAs and those that are associated with immune complex deposition or autoantibody binding to vessel walls. ANCA-associated vasculitides include microscopic angiitis, granulomatosis with polyangiitis and eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis. Non-ANCA subtypes associated with deposition of immune complexes or autoantibody binding to small vessel walls include antiglomerular basement membrane disease (previously known as Goodpasture syndrome) and IgA immune complex disease (previously known as Henoch-Schonlein purpura).

21
Which vasculitis is strongly associated with cigarette smoking?
Show answer

Thromboangiitis obliterans (previously known as Buerger disease) typically occurs in young male adults who are heavy tobacco smokers, leading to the hypothesis that the disorder is initiated by a hypersensitivity response to antigens in tobacco smoke. However, no smoke-associated antigens have been found. The disease is characterized by segmental, thrombosing, acute and chronic inflammation of medium-sized and small arteries, principally the tibial and radial arteries, with occasional secondary extension into the veins and nerves of the extremities. Microabscesses may be seen in thrombi. It is a distinctive disease that often leads to vascular insufficiency, typically of the extremities.

  • Thrombus
  • Microabscesses

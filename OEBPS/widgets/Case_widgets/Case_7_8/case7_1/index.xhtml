Battle of the Bulge

Objectives
  1. • Describe the pathogenesis, common location, and histology of abdominal aortic aneurysms.
  2. • Discuss the pathogenesis, common location, and histology of dissecting aortic aneurysms.

A 62-year-old man with a 50-pack/year smoking history presented to his primary care physician with a 1-week history of persistent cough and sputum production. Past history was notable for essential hypertension, treated with an ACE inhibitor, and elevated total cholesterol and triglycerides, treated with a statin drug. His vital signs were blood pressure 152/88, mm Hg, pulse rate 74 bpm, and respiratory rate 14 breaths/min. Physical examination also revealed a pulsatile abdominal mass located just below the level of the umbilicus.

1
What is the most likely cause of the pulsatile mass?
Show answer

A pulsatile mass in the abdomen is highly suggestive of an abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA).

2
What is the difference between a “true” aneurysm and a “false” aneurysm?
Show answer

When an aneurysm involves an attenuated but intact arterial wall or thinned ventricular wall of the heart, it is called a “true” aneurysm. Atherosclerotic, syphilitic, and congenital vascular aneurysms, as well as ventricular aneurysms that follow transmural myocardial infarctions, are of this type. By contrast, a false aneurysm (also called pseudo-aneurysm) is a defect in the vascular wall leading to an extravascular hematoma that freely communicates with the intravascular space (“pulsating hematoma”). Examples include a ventricular rupture after myocardial infarction that is contained by a pericardial adhesion, or a leak at the sutured junction of a vascular graft with an artery.


3
Which of the major risk factors for the development of AAA does this patient have?
Show answer

Male sex, age > 50 years, tobacco smoking, and atherosclerosis are all associated with AAA. Once AAA develops, hypertension can contribute to more rapid aneurysmal dilation.


Chest x-ray and respiratory studies revealed changes consistent with mild chronic obstructive pulmonary disease. Ultrasonography revealed a 5.5-cm aneurysmal dilation of the abdominal aorta just superior to the ilial-aortic bifurcation. The patient was treated with antibiotics, advised to participate in a smoking cessation program, and scheduled for a follow-up visit with a vascular surgeon the next day.

That night, the patient developed sudden onset severe flank and back pain, then collapsed. Examination by EMTs in the ambulance revealed a blood pressure of 85/40, a pulse rate of 124 bpm, and a respiratory rate of 20/min while on 100% oxygen.

4
What do these clinical findings suggest?
Show answer

The low blood pressure and elevated heart rate are suggestive of hypovolemic shock.

Shortly thereafter he suffered a cardiac arrest and could not be resuscitated. An autopsy was performed and revealed moderately severe multivessel coronary artery disease (~50% stenosis in the left circumflex and right coronary arteries) and pulmonary changes consistent with emphysema and chronic bronchitis. Opening of the abdominal cavity revealed hemoperitoneum. A ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm was identified with laminated thrombus within the aneurysm.


  • Abdominal aortic aneurysm
  • Aortic rupture
  • Laminated thrombus
5
What accounts for the laminated thrombus within the aneurysm?
Show answer

Dilation of the aorta interrupts laminar flow, leading to areas of stasis where a thrombus can form, especially in areas of atherosclerotic plaque.


6
What is the pathogenesis of AAA?
Show answer

The pathogenesis of AAA is incompletely understood. Atherosclerosis may contribute by weakening the arterial wall, but most patients with atherosclerosis do not develop AAA. The infrarenal aorta above the bifurcation is prone to turbulent blood flow, which may contribute to endothelial dysfunction, chronic inflammation, and release of metalloproteases that weaken the vessel wall. Smoking is the most important modifiable risk factor and may also contribute to disease by injuring the endothelium.

7
What is inflammatory AAA?
Show answer

Inflammatory AAAs are uncommon lesions (5%–10% of cases) that occur in patients who are on average about 10 years younger than those with non-inflammatory AAAs. They are defined by imaging as an AAA that has >1 cm of fibrotic thickening on the outside of the affected aorta. Affected patients have elevated inflammatory markers such as C-reactive protein. Microscopically, the fibrotic rim contains an abundant lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate. The proposed cause is a localized immune response to the abdominal aortic wall.

8
What is a mycotic aneurysm and what is its etiology?
Show answer

Mycotic aneurysms arise due to infection and can originate (1) from embolization of a septic embolus, usually as a complication of infective endocarditis; (2) as an extension of an adjacent suppurative process; or (3) by circulating organisms directly infecting the arterial wall.


9
Which factor is most important in the risk of AAA rupture?
Show answer

The risk of rupture is directly related to the size of the aneurysm, varying from nil for AAAs 4 cm or less in diameter to 1% per year for AAAs between 4 and 5 cm, 11% per year for AAAs between 5 and 6 cm, and 25% per year for AAAs larger than 6 cm. Most aneurysms expand at a rate of 0.2 to 0.3 cm/year, but 20% expand more rapidly.


10
Which disorders are associated with thoracic aortic aneurysms (TAAs)? How do these disorders contribute to their development?
Show answer

Marfan syndrome; giant cell arteritis; and aging are associated with TAAs. Marfan syndrome is caused by inherited mutations in fibrillin-1, a structural protein that also regulates TGF-β signaling. Both weakening of the aortic wall and excessive TGF-β signaling are thought to contribute to aneurysmal dilation. Giant cell arteritis may involve the thoracic aorta and lead to sufficient damage to cause aneurysmal dilation. TAA may also occur in older individuals with the same risk factors as for AAA. Historically, TAA also was associated with advanced untreated syphilis (now rarely seen). Here, endarteritis (the histologic hallmark of syphilis) involving the vaso vasorum leads to ischemia, injury of the aortic wall, and eventually aneurysmal dilation.


11
How do TAAs present?
Show answer

TAAs can present with signs and symptoms referable to (1) respiratory difficulties due to encroachment on the lungs and airways; (2) difficulty in swallowing due to compression of the esophagus; (3) persistent cough due to compression of the recurrent laryngeal nerves; (4) pain caused by erosion of bone (i.e., ribs and vertebral bodies); (5) cardiac disease because the aortic aneurysm leads to aortic valve dilation with valvular insufficiency or narrowing of the coronary ostia, causing myocardial ischemia; and (6) rupture.


12
What is aortic dissection?
Show answer

Aortic dissection occurs when the laminar planes of the media separate to form a blood-filled channel within the aortic wall. Aortic dissection may or may not be associated with radiologically detectable aortic dilation.

  • Aortic dissection
  • Blood-filled channel
13
Which patient populations are susceptible to aortic dissections?
Show answer

Aortic dissection occurs principally in two groups of patients: (1) men aged 40 to 60 years with antecedent hypertension (more than 90% of cases) and (2) younger adults with systemic or localized abnormalities of connective tissue affecting the aorta (e.g., Marfan syndrome).


14
Why would this patient be at decreased risk for an aortic dissection?
Show answer

Dissection is unusual in the presence of substantial atherosclerosis or another cause of medial scarring (such as syphilis), presumably because the medial fibrosis inhibits propagation of the dissecting hematoma. His hypertension, however, is one of the major risks for aortic dissection.

Objectives
  1. • Compare and contrast the different types of angina.
  2. • Describe the risk factors for myocardial infarction.
  3. • Describe the pathologic correlates of sudden death.
  4. • Define acute myocardial infarction (MI) and list the sequential effects of ischemia and their morphologic correlates.
  5. • Explain the clinical presentation and the tests that are used to diagnose acute MI.
  6. • List the complications of acute MI.
  7. • Describe the different types of myocardial rupture.

A 54-year-old diabetic woman presents to her primary care physician for routine check up. Her medical history was significant for long-standing hypertension and angina for many years. Her angina usually lasted up to 10 minutes and was relieved by nitroglycerin. Percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) was performed seven months ago and the patient remained symptom free for 6 months, but noticed that the angina returned with exercise. She had a 30-pack-year smoking history and her medications included antihypertensives and cholesterol-lowering agents.

1
Which type of angina does this patient have?
Show answer

The patient had stable angina. Angina pectoris is characterized by paroxysmal and usually recurrent attacks of substernal or precordial chest discomfort caused by transient (15 seconds to 15 minutes) myocardial ischemia that is insufficient to induce myocyte necrosis. Stable (typical) angina is the most common form of angina; it is caused by an imbalance in coronary perfusion (due to chronic stenosing coronary atherosclerosis) relative to myocardial demand, such as that produced by physical activity, emotional excitement or psychological stress. It is usually relieved by rest (decreasing demand) or by the administration of vasodilators, such as nitroglycerin and calcium channel blockers (increasing perfusion).

One month later, the patient presents to her primary care physician with a two-week history of daily anginal attacks that occurred while she was at rest. The episodes usually lasted 30 minutes or more and did not improve with nitroglycerin.

2
Which type of angina does the patient now have?
Show answer

The patient has unstable (crescendo) angina, a pattern of increasingly frequent, prolonged (>20 min), or severe angina or chest discomfort that is defined as pain precipitated by progressively lower levels of physical activity or even occurring at rest that is not relieved by nitroglycerin. In most patients, unstable angina is caused by the disruption of an atherosclerotic plaque with superimposed partial thrombosis, embolization and/or vasospasm. Arterial spasm is also important in variant angina (Prinzmetal angina). Approximately one-half of patients with unstable angina have evidence of myocardial necrosis; for others, acute MI may be imminent.

3
What would be the most likely appearance of this patient’s coronary arteries?
Show answer

Stable angina occurs due to inadequate perfusion and is typically due to atherosclerotic disease with greater than 70% fixed stenosis (so-called critical stenosis); fixed obstructions that occlude less than 70% of a coronary vessel lumen typically are asymptomatic, even with exertion. Therefore, her coronary arteries should show at least 70% fixed stenosis. Her progression to unstable angina could be due to progression of her atherosclerosis, causing narrowing of her arteries to >90%, the threshold for angina at rest. Alternatively, the patient’s fixed stenosis may not have progressed, but she could have had further occlusion due to plaque rupture or mural thrombus. The degree of occlusion and the presence of plaque rupture or mural thrombus can be determined grossly, histologically, and by radiologic imaging.

The patient now presents to the Emergency Department with a 4-hour history of chest pain, nausea and dyspnea that was not relieved by nitroglycerin. Physical exam revealed an obese woman with diaphoresis, pale skin and labored respirations. Rales were heard over both lung fields. Vital signs were HR 105 beats/min, BP 100/50 mm Hg (usual BP was about 155/95 mm Hg), and temperature 40C°.

What are the key diagnostic tests used to confirm the clinical impression of acute myocardial infarction (MI)?
Show answer

ECG and serum cardiac troponin levels are typically ordered. ECG changes are divided into ST segment elevations, which often are associated with transmural MI, and changes other than ST elevations, which are often associated with subendocardial MI. Cardiac troponin elevations are highly specific for myocardial injury, but do not differentiate between MI and other causes of myocardial injury. Previously, myocardial isozyme of creatine kinase (MB-CK) was used as a serum biomarker of MI, but troponins are more sensitive and specific markers and have largely rendered MB-CK testing obsolete. CK testing remains useful in diagnosing damage of skeletal muscle, for example in the context of rhabdomyolysis.

An ECG and serial cardiac markers were ordered. The ECG showed S-T elevations in V3 and V4.

What are contraction bands?
Show answer

Contraction bands are bright eosinophilic bands of condensed contractile proteins that run at right angles to the long axis of the cardiac myocyte. They form in zones of myocardium irreversibly injured by ischemia and then reperfused after angioplasty or clot-lysing drugs. This image of an acute myocardial infarct also shows the characteristic neutrophilic infiltrate seen 1-3 days after an acute infarct.

What are the various steps in the development of atherosclerosis and what roles do endothelial injury, hyperlipidemia and inflammatory cells play in the process?
Show answer

The pathogenesis of atherosclerosis is embodied in the response to injury hypothesis illustrated here:

  1. 1. Endothelial injury and dysfunction, causing (among other things) increased vascular permeability, leukocyte adhesion, and thrombosis
  2. 2. Accumulation of lipoproteins (mainly LDL and its oxidized forms) in the vessel wall
  3. 3. Monocyte adhesion to the endothelium, followed by migration into the intima and transformation into macrophages and foam cells
  4. 4. Platelet adhesion
  5. 5. Factor release from activated platelets, macrophages, and vascular wall cells, inducing smooth muscle cell recruitment, either from the media or from circulating precursors
  6. 6. Smooth muscle cell proliferation, extracellular matrix production, and recruitment of T cells.
  7. 7. Lipid accumulation both extracellularly and within cells (macrophages and smooth muscle cell)

Which class of drugs is useful in reducing the risk of atherosclerotic heart disease, and what is the mode of action?
Show answer

Hypercholesterolemia is a strong risk factor for atherosclerosis. Statins reduce the risk of coronary atherosclerosis by lowering serum cholesterol. They do so by inhibiting the enzyme HMG-CoA reductase and thus promote greater synthesis of LDL receptors.

What are the potential complications following an acute myocardial infarction (MI)?
Show answer

Multiple complications can follow acute myocardial infarction: 1) contractile dysfunction; 2) papillary muscle dysfunction; 3) arrhythmias; 4) myocardial rupture; 5) pericarditis; and 6) ventricular aneurysm. MIs affect left ventricular pump function in proportion to the extent of damage and often result in some degree of left ventricular failure. Although papillary muscles rupture is infrequent, dysfunction with decreased contractility can lead to postinfarct mitral regurgitation. Much later, papillary muscle fibrosis and shortening or global ventricular dilation also can cause mitral valve insufficiency. Approximately 90% of patients develop some form of rhythm disturbance, with the incidence being higher in STEMIs versus NSTEMIs. Rupture complicates only 1% to 5% of MIs but is frequently fatal when it occurs. Left ventricular free wall rupture is most common, typically 2 to 4 days after MI, particularly in older patients without angina, and usually results in rapidly fatal hemopericardium and cardiac tamponade. Rupture of the ventricular septum leads to ventricular septal defect (VSD) and left-to-right shunting. Least commonly, a papillary muscle can rupture, causing acute onset of severe mitral regurgitation. Transmural MIs can elicit a fibrinohemorrhagic pericarditis; this is an epicardial manifestation of the underlying myocardial inflammation. Aneurysms of the ventricle are late complications that most commonly result from a large transmural anteroseptal infarct that heals with the formation of a thinned wall of scar tissue. Progressive heart failure is also seen in many patients after MI.

13
Which factors determine the risk of these different complications and the patient’s prognosis?
Show answer

The risk for complications and the overall prognosis depends on infarct size, site, and type (subendocardial versus transmural infarct). Thus, large transmural infarcts are associated with a higher probability of cardiogenic shock, arrhythmias, and late CHF, and patients with anterior transmural MIs are at greatest risk for free wall rupture, expansion, aneurysm formation, and formation of mural thrombi. By contrast, posterior transmural infarcts are more likely to be complicated by conduction blocks, right ventricular involvement, or both.

The patient was discharged home and 4 days later the patient became acutely short of breath and rapidly unresponsive, leading to death. Postmortem exam revealed a posteromedial papillary muscle rupture.

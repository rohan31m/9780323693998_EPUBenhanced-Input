Objectives
  1. • Discuss the mechanisms of alcoholic liver damage.
  2. • Describe the pathology of the three forms of alcoholic liver disease: fatty liver, steatohepatitis, and cirrhosis.
  3. • List the major causes and clinical signs and symptoms of cirrhosis.
  4. • Describe the different causes of cirrhosis.

A 61-year-old man was brought to the Emergency Department by his daughter after an episode of hematemesis and confusion. His daughter related that he had consumed alcohol for many years, drinking up to 1 liter of liquor per day. Over the previous few days, he had been increasingly somnolent and confused, although he continued to imbibe alcohol. She said that over the previous 6 months he had gradually lost weight, although his abdominal girth had increased to the point that he needed new pants.

Physical examination revealed a cachectic man with a protuberant abdomen. His blood pressure was 90/60 mm Hg, pulse 120 bpm, and temperature 36.8° C. He had difficulty remaining upright for his vital signs. He was jaundiced and had palpable breast tissue. Multiple small telangiectasias were present on the skin of his neck and chest and there were bruises on his arms and legs. His palms were erythematous.

Cardiac and pulmonary examinations were unremarkable. His abdomen was protuberant with a fluid wave and his spleen was palpable. His liver percussed to a span of 6 cm (normal, 10.5 cm). A few large blood vessels were noted in the periumbilical area. There was bilateral pedal edema to the mid-calves. Neurologically, the patient was somnolent but arousable. He was oriented only to name. On voluntary dorsiflexion of his hands, a flapping motion (asterixis) was noted.

Laboratory results included:

Hematocrit 25% (41%–53%)
WBC 10,500/µL (4,500–11,000/µL)
Prothrombin time 16.3 seconds (11–15 seconds)
AST 190 U/L (8–20 U/L)
ALT 65 U/L (8–20 U/L)
Alkaline phosphatase 170 U/L (20–70 U/L)
Gamma-glutamyl transpeptidase 1510 U/L (9–48 U/L)
Total bilirubin 7.4 mg/dL (0.1–1.0 mg/dL)
Total protein 5.3 g/dL (6.0–7.8 g/dL)
Albumin 2.8 g/dL (3.5–5.5 g/dL)
Alcohol 110 mg/dL
Ammonia 110 µg/dL (39–90 µg/dL)
Amylase 52 U/L (25–125 U/L)
Lipase 64 U/L (23–85 U/L)
HBsAg Negative
Anti-HBc Total–negative
Anti-HCV Negative

Urinalysis revealed 2+ bilirubin

1
What is this patient’s diagnosis?
Show answer

This patient has alcoholic liver disease (cirrhosis) complicated by portal hypertension and hepatic encephalopathy. Elevated levels of aminotransferases, low serum albumin, prolonged prothrombin time, and hyperbilirubinemia (with bilirubinuria) demonstrate hepatocyte injury. Furthermore, an AST to ALT ratio greater than 2 (2.9 in this patient) is very suggestive of alcoholic liver disease. The basis for the lesser elevation of ALT relative to AST is not well understood, but the ratio is a useful indicator of alcohol-induced liver injury. The patient’s hyperbilirubinemia would be expected, given his jaundice and icterus.

2
What is the etiology of this patient’s hematemesis, increasing abdominal girth, prominent periumbilical vasculature, splenomegaly, and pedal edema?
Show answer

These are all symptoms associated with portal hypertension. The four major clinical consequences of portal hypertension are (1) ascites (accumulation of excess fluid in the peritoneal cavity); (2) portosystemic venous shunts; (3) congestive splenomegaly; and (4) hepatic encephalopathy. The degree of splenic enlargement varies widely and may reach as much as 1000 gm, but it is not necessarily correlated with other features of portal hypertension. The pathophysiology of ascites is complex and involves multiple mechanisms: sinusoidal hypertension, percolation of lymph into the peritoneal cavity, splanchnic vasodilation, hyperdynamic circulation, and hypoalbuminemia due to reduced synthesis in the liver. Portosystemic shunts develop when blood flow is reversed from the portal to systemic circulation. Most notably, venous bypasses develop wherever the systemic and the portal circulations share common capillary beds. The most clinically important of these are esophagogastric varices, which can cause massive, sometimes fatal, hematemesis. Abdominal wall collaterals appear as dilated subcutaneous veins extending from the umbilicus toward the rib margins (caput medusae) and are an important clinical hallmark of portal hypertension. Pedal edema results from reduced colloid osmotic pressure of the blood due to hypoalbuminemia from reduced albumin synthesis.

3
Which factors contributed to the bruising seen on the patient’s arms and legs?
Show answer

The liver is responsible for production of vitamin K-dependent and -independent clotting factors. Thus, with massively impaired hepatic synthetic function, coagulopathy develops. Easy bruisability is an early sign of this process, which can lead to life-threatening or fatal intracranial bleeding. The liver also helps to remove activated coagulation factors from the circulation, and loss of this function in some instances can lead to chronic disseminated intravascular coagulation, further exacerbating the bleeding tendency.

4
What is the etiology of the patient’s breast tissue, palmar erythema and spider angiomata?
Show answer

Hyperestrogenemia due to impaired estrogen metabolism in male patients with chronic liver failure can give rise to palmar erythema (a reflection of local vasodilation) and spider angiomas of the skin. Such male hyperestrogenemia also leads to hypogonadism and gynecomastia.

5
What is the etiology of the patient’s neurological symptoms?
Show answer

Hepatic encephalopathy is a spectrum of disturbances in consciousness, ranging from subtle behavioral abnormalities, to marked confusion and stupor, to deep coma and death. Asterixis, a particularly characteristic sign, is manifested as nonrhythmic, rapid extension-flexion movements best seen when the arms are held in extension with dorsiflexed wrists. Hepatic encephalopathy is correlated with elevated ammonia levels in blood resulting from failure of the liver to clear gut-derived ammonia from portal blood and because of portosystemic shunts. There is also a failure of the liver to detoxify ammonia by converting it into urea, which is normally excreted in the urine.


6
What are the three types of morphologic alterations that can be seen in the liver in alcoholic liver disease?
Show answer

The most common histologic alteration is fatty liver (steatosis); less common but more serious are steatohepatitis and cirrhosis. After even moderate intake of alcohol, lipid droplets accumulate in hepatocytes, increasing with amount and chronicity of alcohol intake. In steatosis, the lipid begins as small droplets that coalesce into large droplets that distend the hepatocyte and push the nucleus aside. Fatty change is completely reversible if there is abstention from further intake of alcohol.

In addition to steatosis, which histologic features are characteristic of steatohepatitis?
Show answer

The three primary features of steatohepatitis (also known as alcoholic hepatitis) are hepatocyte ballooning, Mallory-Denk bodies (arrow), and inflammatory cell infiltration. Single or scattered foci of cells undergo swelling and necrosis, giving a “ballooned” appearance; as with steatosis, these features are most prominent in the centrilobular regions. Mallory-Denk bodies consist of tangled skeins of intermediate filaments (including ubiquitinated keratins 8 and 18, seen in the inset) and are visible as eosinophilic cytoplasmic inclusions in degenerating hepatocytes. An infiltrate of neutrophils and lymphocytes may permeate the lobule and accumulate around degenerating hepatocytes, particularly those containing Mallory-Denk bodies.

The patient was treated with intravenous fluids and blood transfusions. An abdominal paracentesis was performed that yielded 1.5 L of fluid.

What would laboratory analysis of the ascitic fluid show?
Show answer

Ascitic fluid is generally serous, having less than 3 gm/dL of protein (largely albumin). The serum albumin level typically exceeds the ascites albumin level by ≥1.1 gm/dL. The fluid may contain a scant number of mesothelial cells and mononuclear leukocytes. Influx of neutrophils suggests infection, whereas the presence of red cells points to possible disseminated intraabdominal cancer. With long-standing ascites, seepage of peritoneal fluid through transdiaphragmatic lymphatics may produce hydrothorax, more often on the right side.

The fluid obtained was clear and golden yellow. The laboratory reported that the fluid had a total protein of 1.7 gm/dL, an albumin of 1.0 gm/dL, and a white cell count of 180/μL with a differential count of 10% neutrophils, 10% lymphocytes, and 80% monocytes/mesothelial cells. A Gram stain was negative.

The patient was scheduled for endoscopic examination of the esophagus; however, he experienced an additional episode of hematemesis. Despite attempts at resuscitation, the patient died. Autopsy examination of the esophagus revealed dilated blood vessels in the distal portion of the esophagus and proximal stomach area with focal adherent blood clot. Grossly, the liver was composed of numerous, fairly uniform, small nodules separated by depressed areas of fibrous scar tissue.

What would histologic examination of this liver show?
Show answer

Microscopically, nodules of regenerating hepatocytes consist of disordered cords of cells of irregular thickness, many of which are two or more cell layers thick (usually, the cords are only two cell layers thick). Central veins are absent in these regenerative nodules. Separating the nodules is fibrous tissue and scar containing variable amounts of chronic inflammatory cells and areas of bile ductular proliferation. In a Masson trichrome stain, the entrapping fibrous tissue that surrounds the nodules stains blue(left image). Fatty accumulation may not be seen in the “burned out” stage of the disease.

10
What are the mechanisms responsible for alcoholic liver damage?
Show answer

Alcohol is toxic to the liver through several mechanisms. Excess NADH production, impaired production and secretion of lipoproteins by hepatocytes, and increased catabolism of fat throughout the body combine to cause steatosis. Intrahepatic glutathione is consumed during the metabolism of alcohol, sensitizing the liver to oxidative injury. This vulnerability is particularly important because oxidation of alcohol in microsomes produces free radicals that can react with cell membranes and proteins. The conversion of alcohol to acetaldehyde, the major metabolite of alcohol, induces lipid peroxidation and impairs microtubular function.

11
What are some causes of cirrhosis other than alcoholic liver disease?
Show answer

The etiology of cirrhosis varies, both geographically and socially. In the United States, alcoholic liver disease accounts for well over 50% of the cases of cirrhosis. Other important causes include viral hepatitis (HBV and especially HCV), nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (which is increasing in frequency due to the obesity “epidemic”), biliary tract disease, primary hemochromatosis, α1-antitrypsin deficiency, and Wilson disease.

12
What are the most frequent causes of death in patients with end-stage alcoholic liver disease?
Show answer

Immediate causes of death may be hepatic failure, gastrointestinal hemorrhage, infection, hepatorenal syndrome, or hepatocellular carcinoma (3%–6% of patients).

Objectives
  1. • Describe the features of T cell-mediated hypersensitivity and the types of antigens that elicit such reactions.
  2. • Describe the roles of different cytokines in this reaction.
  3. • Compare and contrast the different types of hypersensitivity reactions in terms of etiology, histopathology, and prototypical disorders.

A 15-year-old boy presented to the school nurse with a pruritic rash on his right wrist. Two days earlier, he had been clearing brush with his father in the forest near his house. There were vaguely linear arrays of erythematous vesicles filled with clear yellow fluid.

1
What would be the expected histologic findings if the rash were biopsied (this is not typically done)?
Show answer

Histologically, the blisters appear as intraepidermal vesicles with adjacent intercellular edema (spongiosis ). The typical lesion would show perivascular infiltrates of mononuclear cells, mostly lymphocytes and macrophages. Immunohistochemical staining would reveal that the majority of the lymphocytes are CD4+ T cells.

2
What is the nature of the pathologic process occurring in this patient’s skin, and what is the likely etiology?
Show answer

The perivascular mononuclear cell infiltrates, consisting of lymphocytes and macrophages, are characteristic of a delayed type hypersensitivity (DTH) reaction (type IV hypersensitivity). The inciting agent is likely to be an environmental chemical that the patient encountered while clearing brush; the most plausible culprit is contact with poison ivy or poison oak, both of which produce β-urishiol, small resins that penetrate the epidermis, bind to self proteins, and chemically modify these proteins to produce neoantigens that are recognized as foreign. The subsequent T-cell response recruits and activates macrophages, producing a chronic inflammatory reaction.

3
What are the two major mechanisms of T-cell mediated reactions and how do they cause tissue injury?
Show answer

Cell-mediated reactions can consist of inflammation caused by cytokines produced by CD4+ T cells and cell killing by CD8+ T cells. In the first process, CD4+ T cells are activated by exposure to a protein antigen and differentiate into Th1 or Th17 effector cells. Subsequent exposure to the antigen results in the secretion of cytokines such as IFN-γ and IL-17 by Th1 and Th17 cells respectively. IFN-γ activates macrophages to produce substances that cause tissue damage and promote more inflammation. IL-17 and other cytokines recruit leukocytes, further exacerbating inflammation. CD4+ T cell–mediated inflammation is the basis of tissue injury in many organ-specific and systemic autoimmune diseases, such as rheumatoid arthritis and multiple sclerosis, as well as diseases probably caused by uncontrolled reactions to bacterial commensals, such as inflammatory bowel disease. In CD8+ T cell-mediated reactions, CD8+ cytotoxic lymphocytes (CTLs) specific for an antigen recognize cells expressing the target antigen and kill these cells. CD8+ T cells also secrete IFN-γ. Tissue destruction by CTLs may be an important component of some T cell–mediated diseases, such as type 1 diabetes.


4
What is a common clinical test that relies on DTH for a positive result?
Show answer

DTH is the basis for the purified protein derivative (PPD or tuberculin) skin test, in which antigens from Mycobacterium tuberculosis are injected into the skin. A positive reaction is induration >10 mm at the injection site within 48 hours of antigen challenge. It is presumptive evidence of prior exposure to M. tuberculosis and sensitization.

5
How does the DTH reaction differ from immediate hypersensitivity reactions in the skin?
Show answer

Immediate hypersensitivity (allergic) reactions present with swelling (“wheal”) and redness (“flare”) occurring within minutes of antigen challenge. The pathogenesis, pathology, and clinical manifestations of immediate hypersensitivity and DTH are fundamentally different.

6
What are the main steps in a DTH reaction?
Show answer

The development of a DTH reaction requires (1) capture and presentation of the antigen by antigen-presenting cells (APCs) in the skin, mostly Langerhans cells in the epidermis; (2) migration of APCs bearing antigens to draining lymph nodes; (3) priming of specific T cells, primarily within the draining lymph nodes (note that priming also requires costimulators, which are expressed on activated APCs); (4) migration of effector T cells back to the epidermis; and (5) production of cytokines that recruit monocytes from the blood and activate macrophages in the tissue.

7
Which are the four types of hypersensitivity reactions and which mediators are involved in the process?
Show answer

In immediate hypersensitivity (type I hypersensitivity), the injury is caused by Th2 cells, IgE antibodies, and mast cells and other leukocytes. In antibody-mediated disorders (type II hypersensitivity), secreted IgG and IgM antibodies injure cells by promoting their phagocytosis or lysis and injure tissues by inducing inflammation. In immune complex–mediated disorders (type III hypersensitivity), IgG and IgM antibodies bind antigens usually in the circulation, and the antigen-antibody complexes deposit in tissues and induce inflammation. In cell-mediated immune disorders (type IV hypersensitivity), sensitized T lymphocytes (Th1 and Th17 cells and CTLs) are the cause of the tissue injury.

Table 4.2.2 Mechanisms of Hypersensitivity Reactions

Type Immune Mechanisms Histopathologic Lesions Prototypical Disorders

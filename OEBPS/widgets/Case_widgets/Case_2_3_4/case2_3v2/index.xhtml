Objectives
  1. • Summarize how phagocytes destroy microbes and eliminate dead cells.
  2. • Describe the process of phagocytosis and how antibodies and complement proteins enhance this process.
  3. • Describe the generation, activities, and regulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), and the concept of the neutrophil respiratory burst.
  4. • Describe the functions of nitric oxide (NO) in macrophages.

A 3-year-old boy was referred to a tertiary care hospital following unsuccessful treatment for three recent episodes of pneumonia. His clinical history was significant for recurrent episodes of otitis media that required multiple courses of antibiotics, sinusitis, and pneumonia since birth. His family history is notable for a brother who died at age two.

1
Which features of the clinical presentation suggest that this is a primary (congenital) immunodeficiency disease?
Show answer

Repeated infections from early childhood, incomplete resolution with antibiotic treatment, and a family history of early death of a sibling (possibly also related to infections) all suggest an inherited immune defect.

Physical exam revealed multiple subcutaneous abscesses on his back, fever of 39.5° C, bilateral otitis media with effusion, and dullness over the left lung fields. Laboratory values were significant for a WBC count of 16,200 (normal 3.8 – 14.0 x 103 cells/mm 3) with 85% PMN, C-reactive protein of 25.1 mg/L, a negative HIV antibody test, and normal T-cell and B-cell subsets in the peripheral blood. Blood IgG levels were increased; however, IgM and IgA levels were normal. A PPD skin test for infection with tubercle bacilli was negative. The skin abscess was excised and cultured. Cultures revealed Staphylococcus aureus.

2
What is the typical immune response to infection with bacteria such as S. aureus?
Show answer

Host defense against S. aureus and other pathogens is mediated by the early innate immune response and the later adaptive immune response (Chapter 4). The major effectors of innate immunity are phagocytes, and their activation is the focus of this case. In response to microbial products, cytokines and various inflammatory stimuli, the components of phagocyte oxidase (also known as NADPH oxidase) are rapidly assembled in the membrane of the phagolysosome. Phagocyte oxidase is an enzyme complex consisting of at least seven proteins that uses NADPH (reduced nicotinamide-adenine dinucleotide phosphate) in a reaction that reduces oxygen to superoxide anion. Because this process consumes oxygen, it is called the respiratory burst.



A biopsy of the lesion on his back was performed; low power examination showed a dense accumulation of inflammatory cells in the dermis. High power examination revealed tissue necrosis, mononuclear cell infiltrates (predominantly monocytes and macrophages) and activated macrophages (epithelioid cells). There were scattered multinucleate giant cells.

3
Which type of inflammation is this? What is an explanation for the finding of this type of inflammation in this patient?
Show answer

Typically, infection with S. aureus evokes a neutrophilic response and the formation of pus, characteristic of acute inflammation. The presence of activated macrophages and multinucleate giant cells in association with tissue necrosis is diagnostic of granulomatous inflammation. Granulomatous inflammation suggests that the initial neutrophil defense is inadequate to clear the pathogen, calling in the next wave of phagocytes: the blood monocyte-derived macrophages. Macrophages activated by microbial products try to wall off the infection and at least contain, if not eliminate, it. Granulomatous inflammation is characteristic of infection with tubercle bacilli, but the negative PPD test does not favor tuberculosis. In general, when there are granulomas, tuberculosis must be ruled out by special stains for the bacilli and by culture.


Chest radiograph showed a left sided pleural effusion and focal bilateral opacities. A thoracic chest computed tomography scan revealed multifocal ill-defined consolidations involving both lungs and lymphadenopathy of the paratracheal and parahilar lymph nodes, raising a differential diagnosis of infectious, inflammatory, and neoplastic causes. A biopsy of one of the lung lesions was performed that showed a poorly formed granuloma with epithelioid cells (activated macrophages) and central necrosis. Microbial culture grew Burkholderia cepacia, a catalase-producing organism that can also cause significant complications in patients with cystic fibrosis.

In view of the clinical history and histologic findings, the DHR (dihydrorhodamine) assay was performed. In this assay, neutrophils are activated and conversion of an internalized substrate to a fluorescent product by NADPH oxidase is measured by flow cytometry. This test showed deficient NADPH oxidase activity.

4
What is this patient’s diagnosis?
Show answer

Deficiency of NADPH oxidase (also called phagocyte oxidase) is seen in chronic granulomatous disease (CGD). In this disease, neutrophils are defective in phagocyte oxidase and therefore cannot produce reactive oxygen species (ROS).

5
Which organisms are most pathogenic in patients with this disease?
Show answer

These patients can usually successfully kill catalase-negative organisms (which includes most anaerobes) but are susceptible to catalase-positive organisms such as Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Aspergillus fumigatus, Candida sp. and the Enterobacteriaceae (especially Klebsiella pneumoniae and Serratia marcescens).


6
Why is a patient with chronic granulomatous disease more susceptible to catalase-positive microbes?
Show answer

Catalase is an enzyme that catalyzes the breakdown of hydrogen peroxide in many organisms. In infections caused by organisms that lack catalase (catalase-negative), the host with CGD is successfully able to "borrow" hydrogen peroxide being made by the organism and use it to fight off the infection. In infections by organisms that have catalase (catalase-positive), this "borrowing mechanism" is unsuccessful because the catalase enzyme breaks down any hydrogen peroxide that would be borrowed from the organism. Therefore, in a patient with chronic granulomatous disease, hydrogen peroxide cannot be used to make oxygen radicals to fight infection, leaving the patient vulnerable to infection.

7
What is the genetic basis for this disease?
Show answer

NADPH oxidase consists of 5 component proteins and its assembly requires several other factors, and defects in any of these components can lead to CGD. The most common variant shows X-linked inheritance. In this patient, since his brother died at an early age, it is likely that they both had an X-linked mutation.


8
What are some of the mechanisms by which reactive oxygen species are generated?
Show answer

Phagocyte oxidase plays an important role in the generation of reactive oxygen species. As mentioned previously, this enzyme complex generates the superoxide anion. Superoxide is converted to H2O2 spontaneously and by the action of the enzyme superoxide dismutase. H2O2 reacts with superoxide to form the hydroxyl radical, which, in the presence of a halide such as chlorine, is converted to hypochlorite (OCl2-) by the enzyme myeloperoxidase (MPO), which is found mainly in the neutrophil phagolysosome.


9
How do reactive oxygen species destroy microbes and damage cells? How is their activity controlled?
Show answer

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) destroy ingested bacteria and dead cells by many mechanisms. The principal mechanism may be that ROS activate granule enzymes such as elastase, which degrade ingested substances. In addition, ROS may directly damage proteins and lipids by peroxidation. Hypochlorite destroys microbes by halogenation (in which the halide is bound covalently to cellular constituents). Nitric oxide (NO), produced from arginine by the action of nitric oxide synthase, is also a potent microbicidal agent. NO reacts with superoxide (O2-) to generate the highly reactive free radical peroxynitrite (ONOO-), which destroys ingested material much like ROS. The activity of ROS is controlled by several mechanisms to prevent unnecessary damage. Among these are the enzymes catalase and glutathione peroxidase, which degrade H2O2. ROS are produced mainly in neutrophils and NO in macrophages, but this distinction is not absolute.

  1. • Describe the therapeutic applications of hematopoietic stem cell transplantation.

A 24-year-old man with chronic myeloid leukemia in chronic phase that became resistant to BCR-ABL inhibitors, received high-dose cyclophosphamide and total body irradiation followed by an infusion of hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) from a human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-matched unrelated donor.

1
What is HLA typing and how is it relevant to organ transplantation?
Show answer

Major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules, also called HLA, are classified as class I or class II MHC. Class I MHC molecules are heterodimers consisting of a polymorphic α chain that is non-covalently linked to a smaller protein (β2 microglobulin). The α chains are encoded by 3 genes: HLA-A, HLA-B and HLA-C. Class II MHC molecules are also heterodimers and are composed of an α chain and a β chain. Class II MHC molecules are encoded in a region called HLA-D that consists of three subregions, HLA-DP, HLA-DQ and HLA-DR. The combination of HLA alleles in any one individual is referred to as the HLA haplotype and consists of these 6 loci (HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-C, HLA-DP, HLA-DQ, and HLA-DR). HLA typing is the identification of an individual’s HLA haplotype. In the past typing was done with antibodies specific for different HLA allelic proteins, but now it is done by molecular methods, most recently by next generation sequencing. The greater the number of matches, the lower the risk that the transplanted organ will be rejected. HLA matching is not considered for transplants of liver, heart and lung since other factors (e.g. availability of organs, anatomical compatibility, severity of disease, minimizing the time of organ storage) are more critical.

In the past, HSCs were obtained from the bone marrow, so this procedure was called bone marrow transplantation. Now, HSCs are usually harvested from the peripheral blood of donors following treatment with hematopoietic growth factors or agents that inhibit stable binding of HSCs to their marrow “niche,” both of which serve to mobilize HSCs into the circulation; thus, the procedure is now referred to as hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT).

On day 15, circulating neutrophils were noted on the peripheral blood smear, an early sign of HSC engraftment. On day 17, the patient developed hyperbilirubinemia (total bilirubin of 2.3 mg/dL; normal 0.1–1.0 mg/dL), profuse diarrhea, and a maculopapular rash on his face, trunk, proximal extremities, palms, and soles that involved approximately 70% of his body surface area. 2
Based on the clinical features and biopsy findings, what is the diagnosis?
Show answer

This lesion is characteristic of acute graft-vs-host disease (GVHD), a severe complication of HSCT. The characteristic rash of GVHD generally appears within 6 weeks of transplantation. Histologically, there is vacuolar degeneration and necrosis of basal cells and epidermal cells. Individual apoptotic keratinocytes are often seen in association with lymphocytes infiltrating the epidermis (satellitosis). Intraepidermal edema is also present that loosens intercellular junctions and pushes keratinocytes apart, a feature called spongiosis. As these spaces coalesce, small blisters (bullae) may form. In extreme cases, this can lead to widespread epidermolysis. A perivascular mononuclear infiltrate is typically present.

3
What is the pathogenesis of GVHD?
Show answer

GVHD occurs when mature T lymphocytes from the donor that are present in the HSC inoculum recognize the recipient’s antigens as foreign and react against them. This is the opposite of the process of rejection in solid organ transplants, in which the recipient’s immune system attacks the transplanted organ. GVHD is most common in the context of HSCT but can occur following transplantation of organs that are rich in lymphoid cells (e.g., the liver).


A transplant from a donor who is genetically different from the recipient is referred to as an allogeneic graft. HSCT requires careful HLA matching of donor and recipient because small differences can induce severe graft versus host reactions. However, even if perfectly matched for HLA antigens, unrelated donors and recipients still differ in minor histocompatibility antigens (encoded by non-HLA genes). These differences are sufficient in most cases to induce GVHD due to recognition of these minor histocompatibility antigens by donor-derived CD8+ cytotoxic T cells, which attack and kill recipient cells.

4
Which organs are primarily affected by acute GVHD?
Show answer

In addition to the skin changes shown earlier, the gastrointestinal epithelium and the bile duct epithelium are most commonly affected by GVHD. Histologic findings include apoptosis, necrosis, and lymphocytic infiltrates. This biopsy of the terminal ileum from a patient with severe acute GVHD shows necrosis of the superficial mucosa with focal denudation. In extreme cases, there can be total denudation of the mucosal surface; such cases can be fatal. Liver biopsy of acute GVHD is characterized by a mononuclear infiltrate that is concentrated in the portal triads and attacks the bile ductular epithelium. Hepatocellular damage can also be seen, but is not as common. Histologic examination of the small bile ducts shows changes ranging from irregular cell and nuclear shape and disorganization to necrosis. Lumens may contain necrotic debris, but rarely have bile plugs.

How can GVHD be prevented? What are the problems associated with preventive strategies?
Show answer

Because GVHD is caused by mature T cells in the stem cell inoculum, eliminating them before transplantation is one approach. However, this has serious deleterious consequences.


In cases where HSCT is used to treat leukemia, the donor T cells attack the leukemic cells and induce remission; depletion of these T cells is associated with a high recurrence rate. Also, the donor T cells secrete growth factors that contribute to engraftment by stimulating the proliferation of hematopoietic precursors. One way to circumvent the second problem is to deplete T cells from the stem cell inoculum and treat the recipients with growth factors such as G-CSF. Unfortunately, this strategy does not seem to fully correct the engraftment deficit, suggesting that the T cells are providing other functions. More importantly, it does not correct the defective elimination of malignant leukemic cells.

The patient was treated with steroids. Within 1 week, the hyperbilirubinemia, rash, and diarrhea had improved; the steroid dosage was slowly tapered to a lower level, and this, along with cyclosporine, was continued for several weeks, but could not be tapered further because of rash and diarrhea.

What are the potential infectious complications of long-term steroid therapy? Which steps can be taken to prevent these complications?
Show answer

Steroids suppress the immune system, which is why they are useful in reducing the severity of graft versus host disease. However, immunosuppressed patients are at increased risk for viral infections, both de novo and due to reactivation (e.g., cytomegalovirus, herpes simplex virus, varicella zoster virus, and polyoma virus), as well as EBV-driven B cell tumors. Patients are also at risk for non-viral illnesses such as pneumococcal pneumonia and urinary tract infections. Patients may receive prophylactic treatment to prevent reactivation of cytomegalovirus infection (ganciclovir) or to reduce the symptoms of herpes simplex virus (acyclovir).

At day 40, he developed a fever of 102° F with shaking chills. He was started empirically on broad-spectrum antibiotics, and a blood culture drawn at the time of the temperature spike revealed Pseudomonas aeruginosa. With antibiotic therapy, the infection cleared and the patient was stable for another 6 months. 7
What is the diagnosis? How does this process differ from acute GVHD?
Show answer

This patient has chronic GVHD, which may follow the acute syndrome or develop insidiously. Acute GVHD is rapid in onset and is characterized by death of epithelial cells mediated primarily by CD8+ CTLs. By contrast, the chronic form of GVHD is associated with gradual tissue injury, for example, obliteration of bile ducts, the so-called “vanishing bile duct syndrome” in the liver and bronchiolitis obliterans in the lungs. Chronic GVHD also is associated with manifestations of chronic autoimmunity, and the skin lesions may resemble those in systemic sclerosis. The pathogenesis is attributed to activation of self-reactive B cells by CD4+ helper T cells that recognize host antigens on B cells and antigen-presenting cells. These B cells produce autoantibodies and the helper T cells also activate macrophages to trigger inflammation. These patients are at increased risk for recurrent and life-threatening infections.

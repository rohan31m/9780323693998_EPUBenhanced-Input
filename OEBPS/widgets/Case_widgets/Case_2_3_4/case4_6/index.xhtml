Objectives
  1. • Describe properties of amyloid proteins
  2. • Discuss the pathogenesis and classification of amyloidosis
Summary of case:

A 42-year-old woman presented to her primary care physician with a 5-month history of fatigue and loss of appetite. On examination, she had normal blood pressure and afebrile. Past history revealed that the patient had hypertension, thin basement membrane nephropathy leading to end stage renal disease (ESRD), requiring hemodialysis. Lab tests demonstrated Hb 10.4 with normal red cell morphology, elevated β2-microglobulin, and serum calcium 10.6 mg/dL. Bone marrow study revealed 5% monoclonal plasma cells with lambda light chain restriction. Serum free lambda light chains were elevated with increased lambda/kappa ratio. MRI abdomen showed massive hepatosplenomegaly. Liver biopsy revealed normal parenchyma with amorphous deposits in the walls of arteries and sinusoids; the deposits were pink-red (thin arrows) in Congo red stain and showed apple-green birefringence under polarizing microscopy (thick arrows).

1
What is the most likely diagnosis in this case?
Show answer

It’s a case of amyloid light chain (AL) amyloidosis with hepatic involvement. Malignant plasma cells often secrete free light chains (here it is lambda light chain) that are deposited in the tissue as amyloid. AL amyloidosis is generally systemic in distribution.

2
What is amyloidosis?
Show answer

Amyloidosis is a generic term for disorders resulting from extracellular deposits of amyloid, a misfolded protein bound to 5% glycoprotein (called the P component). These misfolded proteins (monomers) have a tendency to form oligomers and then aggregate resulting in fibril formation. These insoluble, fibrillary proteins are deposited in extracellular space that cause tissue damage and functional impairment.

3
Describe properties of amyloid proteins.
Show answer

Amyloids are continuous, nonbranching fibrils with a diameter of 8-10 nm. Individual fibrils are basically stacks of protofilaments having a characteristic β-pleated structure that imparts distinctive Congo red staining of amyloid proteins. Amyloid predominantly consists of fibril proteins (around 95% of amyloid) and lesser amount of serum amyloid P (SAP) and other glycoproteins. Most common forms of amyloid proteins are amyloid light chain (AL) protein, transthyretin amyloid (ATTR) and amyloid-associated (AA) protein.

5
What is the pathogenesis of amyloidosis?
Show answer

Misfolded proteins in healthy individual are managed by a quality control process that involves degradation of these abnormal proteins intracellularly in proteosome or extracellularly by macrophages. Amyloidosis can occur in two situations, 1) excessive production of abnormal protein with inherent tendency to misfold and 2) normal production of variant proteins. The mechanism of deposition of amyloid varies from case to case depending on their types. Amyloidosis can be localized when the deposits are primarily limited to the site of production of amyloidogenic protein or systemic where the deposits are away from the production site. Excessive deposition may cause architectural distortion leading to organ dysfunction. Amyloid itself is not toxic.

5
How do you classify amyloidosis?
Show answer

Amyloidosis is classified based on the occurrence in diverse clinical settings. It includes three different categories, namely systemic (generalized), localized and hereditary amyloidosis. Systemic amyloidosis includes three major forms – AL (amyloid light chain) amyloidosis, ATTR (transthyretin amyloid) amyloidosis and AA (amyloid A) amyloidosis. Some of the localized amyloidosis are senile cerebral amyloidosis in Alzheimer disease, isolated atrial amyloidosis and other rare forms involving various endocrine organs like islets of pancreas in type 2 diabetes and thyroid in medullary carcinoma of thyroid. A variety of rare familial forms of amyloidosis have been described including familial Mediterranean fever, and several other types of familial amyloidotic neuropathies.

6
Why does the patient have hypercalcemia?
Show answer

AL amyloidosis in association with extensive plasma cell infiltration can present with hypercalcemia. Although the amount of bone marrow monoclonal plasma cell percentage is less (<10%) in this case, the cells can stimulate osteoclast activity leading to bone resorption and hypercalcemia. Additionally, amyloidogenic protein in the liver may impair calcium homeostasis by some mechanisms that are yet to be elucidated. In general, hypercalcemia (usually >11.5 mg/dL) is strongly associated with multiple myeloma (bone marrow clonal plasma cells ≥10%); however it can also occur in other plasma cell disorders.

7
What is AA amyloidosis?
Show answer

Increased production of serum amyloid A (SAA) proteins by liver, with incomplete breakdown of SAA result in abnormal deposition in extracellular space as amyloid. AA amyloidosis is mostly associated with chronic inflammation such as, chronic osteomyelitis, bronchiectasis, rheumatoid arthritis, heroin use and solid tumors like renal cell carcinoma, Hodgkin lymphoma etc.

8
Why is β2-microglobulin elevated in this patient?
Show answer

The patient had a history of long-term hemodialysis for her end stage renal disease. Dialysis membrane could not filter β2-microglobulin, hence retaining more β2-microglobulin in the plasma which can aggregate to form amyloid.

During follow up, the patient developed shortness of breath and palpitations. Transthoracic echocardiography demonstrated left ventricular hypertrophy with speckled ground glass appearance of myocardium.

9
What accounts for the patient’s cardiac symptoms?
Show answer

Accumulation of amyloid proteins in the heart muscle disrupts myocyte architecture and functional impairment, eventually leading to restrictive cardiomyopathy. Heart can be affected by both amyloid light chain (AL) and transthyretin (ATTR) amyloidosis. Genetic mutation or aging of transthyretin (TTR) proteins can lead to pathological misfolding resulting in transthyretin amyloidosis. Wildtype TTR aggregates usually produce amyloid deposits (ATTR-wt) in the heart of elderly patients; whereas variant TTR aggregates may account for familial cardiomyopathy, known as ATTRv-CM. Endomyocardial biopsy is crucial to diagnose cardiac amyloidosis. Congo red stain displays pink red amyloid materials that demonstrate characteristic ‘birefringence’ under polarized microscopy.

10
Can a patient have both systemic and localized amyloidosis at the same time?
Show answer

Yes, in fact, a localized amyloidosis may sometimes portend a developing systemic amyloidosis. Hence, a localized amyloidosis always warrants further investigations to look for any systemic involvement.

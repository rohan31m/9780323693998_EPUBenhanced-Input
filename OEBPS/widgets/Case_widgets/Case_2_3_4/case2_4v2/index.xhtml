Objectives
  1. • Describe the causes, nature of the response, and tissue changes seen in chronic inflammation.
  2. • Describe the pathways of macrophage activation, their stimuli, and their physiologic and pathologic roles.
  3. • Define granuloma and list the causes and consequences of granulomatous inflammation.
  4. • Describe the role of delayed type hypersensitivity in the development and clinical diagnosis of tuberculosis infection.

A 34-year-old woman presented to her primary care physician with recent onset of night fevers and chills, a 6-week history of non-productive cough, and a 4-month history of unintentional weight loss. She was originally from India but immigrated to the United States two years ago. Six months ago, she was diagnosed with rheumatoid arthritis and was treated with a tumor necrosis factor (TNF) antagonist and a course of corticosteroids. Clinical exam is significant for harsh breath sounds with crackling rales over the right posterior lung base.

1
What is the most likely cause of fever and cough in this patient?
Show answer

Rheumatoid arthritis is an immunologically mediated disease and is being treated with TNF antagonist and steroids, both of which are immunosuppressive agents. Because of immune suppression, this patient is at risk for development of microbial infections. Abnormal breath sounds suggest that she has some lung infection.

To investigate the cause of her symptoms, a chest X-ray was performed. There were cavitary lesions in the apices of both lungs and areas of consolidation in the upper lobes. The X-ray was consistent with pulmonary tuberculosis involving both upper lobes. Breakdown of the associated caseous necrosis produced the cavities. Caseous necrosis often undergoes dystrophic calcification, which can result in calcified densities.

  • Cavitary lesions
  • Patchy consolidation

Abdominal computed tomography scan revealed diffuse lymphadenopathy. Bacterial culture of the sputum did not grow out organisms within 4 days. However, rare acid-fast bacilli were identified on a sputum smear.

2
Which tests are useful to diagnose tuberculosis infection?
Show answer

Identification of acid-fast bacilli on a sputum smear is diagnostic of Mycobacterium infection; however, many infected patients are “smear negative”. Culture of M. tuberculosis on solid agar takes 3 to 6 weeks to show growth; in contrast, culture in liquid media may take only 2 weeks. Polymerase chain reaction (PCR)-based molecular testing is faster; furthermore, an assay has been developed that not only confirms the presence of the organism, but also determines whether or not it is resistant to rifampin and other drugs. PCR is as sensitive as culture in acid-fast smear-positive patients, but is slightly less sensitive in smear-negative patients and significantly less sensitive in children.


Other assays detect not the bacteria but the cellular immune response directed against M. tuberculosis. The two tests commonly used are skin tests with PPD (purified protein derivative) and an in vitro IFN-γ release assay (IGRA). PPD is a cocktail of mycobacterial proteins that generate a T cell–dependent local inflammatory reaction. The PPD test is an example of type IV (T cell–mediated) hypersensitivity. Upon intracutaneous injection, the proteins contained in M. tuberculosis are recognized by memory CD4+ Th1 cells in patients who have been previously exposed, eliciting delayed type hypersensitivity. This type of hypersensitivity is called “delayed” because it takes 48 to 72 hours for the local inflammatory reaction to appear in the form of induration. The diameter of the induration varies from 5 to 15 mm depending on the underlying immune status of the individual. Upon repeat exposure to the antigen, the memory Th1 cells secrete cytokines, primarily IFN-γ, activating macrophages by the classical pathway. A positive PPD test signifies T cell–mediated immunity to mycobacterial antigens but does not differentiate between latent infection and active disease. False negative reactions can occur in the setting of patients who have been recently (less than 2 weeks) infected, certain viral infections, Hodgkin lymphoma, immunosuppression, and overwhelming active tuberculosis disease. In patients with AIDS, HIV-induced killing of CD4+ T cells abrogates the response to the tuberculosis proteins. False positive tests can be seen in infection by atypical mycobacteria or in prior vaccination or treatment with Bacillus Calmette-Guerin (BCG), an attenuated strain of M. bovis that is used as a vaccine in some countries.


IGRA assays involve stimulating patient leukocytes in vitro with TB proteins and measuring production of IFN-γ. It is slightly more specific for M. tuberculosis than PPD, and therefore can help distinguish false positive PPD results.


Culture remains the gold standard for diagnosis.

The patient was treated with a multidrug regimen; however, on day 3, she developed neck stiffness, headache, nausea, and vomiting. A lumbar puncture was performed and revealed a protein level of 7000 mg/dL (normal 40 mg/dL) A computed tomography scan of the head revealed ventricular dilation and superficial meningeal enhancements suggestive of tuberculous meningitis. The patient was transferred to the intensive care unit; however, despite appropriate care, her condition continued to deteriorate, and she died 3 weeks later. An autopsy was performed.

Examination of the brain showed a dense exudate on the basal surface of the brain. Examination of the lungs revealed apical cavitary lesions and consolidation.

3
Which type of inflammation would be expected on histologic examination?
Show answer

Tuberculosis is associated with granulomatous inflammation, which consists of epithelioid macrophages and multinucleate giant cells typically surrounding areas of caseous necrosis. Granulomatous inflammation is a specific morphologic type of chronic inflammation that is characteristic of tuberculosis but not diagnostic because granulomas can also be seen in other conditions. Granulomas are often destructive lesions; in fact, in tuberculosis, the inflammation may be a greater cause of tissue destruction than factors made by the bacteria.

  • Granuloma
  • Caseous necrosis
  • Giant cells

An acid-fast stain was performed on the section. Acid-fast stains bind to the dense lipid cell walls of acid-fast bacteria such as Mycobacterium tuberculosis and Mycobacterium avium complex. The morphologic features and clinical history support a diagnosis of tuberculosis.

  • Lung, acid-fast stain
  • Acid-fast bacteria
4
How does this type of inflammation differ from acute inflammation?
Show answer

In contrast to acute inflammation, which is manifested by vascular changes, edema, and predominantly neutrophilic infiltration, chronic inflammation is characterized by (1) infiltration by mononuclear cells such as macrophages, lymphocytes, and plasma cells; (2) tissue destruction by the offending agent or the inflammatory cells (particularly macrophages); and (3) attempts at healing, including angiogenesis and fibrosis.

5
What is the difference between granulation tissue and granulomas?
Show answer

Granulation tissue contains new small blood vessels, fibroblasts, and mononuclear cells in an edematous extracellular matrix; it is part of the repair response. A granuloma is a circumscribed collection of activated macrophages (called epithelioid cells) usually surrounded by lymphocytes; it is a form of chronic inflammation.

6
What is the mechanism for granuloma formation? Which type of macrophage activation is seen in this process?
Show answer

When a microbe is difficult to eradicate, chronic stimulation by persistent microbial antigen results in a strong T cell response, IFN-γ production and macrophage activation, resulting in granuloma formation. The macrophages that dominate in granulomas are so-called classically activated (M1) macrophages, which are potent destroyers of microbes and inducers of inflammation. By contrast, alternatively activated (M2) macrophages play a more important role in tissue repair and fibrosis. How clear these distinctions are, and whether other phenotypically and functionally different subsets of activated macrophages exist, are topics of research.



7
Which clinical settings favor granulomatous inflammation?
Show answer

Granulomatous inflammation is most often seen with microbes that are difficult to eradicate (since they cause repeated and persistent activation of immune cells) and that induce strong T cell–mediated reactions. Granulomatous inflammation is also seen in some hypersensitivity diseases in which a specific infectious pathogen may not be involved; the best example is Crohn disease, an inflammatory bowel disease that may be initiated by unregulated T cell responses to intestinal commensal bacteria (which are normally harmless). Granulomas are also characteristic of sarcoidosis, an inflammatory disease of unknown etiology. In contrast to the granulomas seen in tuberculosis, the granulomas in Crohn disease and sarcoidosis are usually non-caseating (no central necrosis). Granulomas are seen in these few conditions; identifying them is helpful in arriving at the correct diagnosis.

  • Lymph node, histology
  • Non-caseating granulomas

Objectives
  1. • Explain the role of Virchow triad in the formation of thrombi.
  2. • Discuss the common risk factors for thrombosis.
  3. • Describe the clinical presentation, evaluation and natural history of deep venous thrombosis.
  4. • Compare and contrast red and white infarcts.
  5. • Compare and contrast the different types of emboli.

A 55-year-old man was brought to the Emergency Department (ED) because his wife witnessed him collapse after he sat up in bed. He had awakened earlier and said he felt weak and sweaty. In the ED, the patient was noted to be pale but was conscious and coherent. He had no chest or abdominal pain and no history of bleeding or thrombosis. He had a history of longstanding hypertension and had recently developed signs of congestive heart failure. One week ago, he went on a hiking trip and had been taking up to 800 milligrams of ibuprofen per day to relieve muscle pain. In addition, he had been taking a baby aspirin every day on the advice of his sister.

On examination, the patient’s blood pressure was 130/80 mm Hg and his heart rate was 72 bpm while supine, and 80/40 mm Hg and 120 bpm respectively while sitting up. His temperature was 37.6°C. He had bilateral pitting edema to mid-calf.

1
What do the patient’s vital signs suggest?
Show answer

The patient has orthostatic hypotension. Causes of hypotension include volume depletion due to dehydration or blood loss, cardiac or endocrine disorders, or certain medications.

A guaiac test was positive for fecal occult blood. Initial bloodwork was remarkable for a hematocrit of 28 (normal, 41–53%); the platelet count, WBC, PT, and PTT were normal.

2
Which tests should be performed next?
Show answer

Orthostatic hypotension is consistent with volume loss, and the low hematocrit suggests recent bleeding. The most likely source of bleeding is the GI tract, which should be evaluated by endoscopy.

The patient was admitted to the ICU for hemodynamic monitoring and was given several liters of normal saline. On repeat testing, the hematocrit was 21. Aspiration of stomach contents through a nasogastric tube produced brownish blood, and upper endoscopy revealed the presence of superficial gastric erosions that were not actively bleeding. The patient later produced several large melanotic stools.

3
If the patient was not actively bleeding, why did the hematocrit drop following volume resuscitation?
Show answer

When bleeding is massive and acute, both plasma and red cells are lost from the intravascular space. As a result, the drop in hematocrit does not fully reflect the extent of red cell loss until the intravascular volume is restored.

4
What is the most likely cause of bleeding in this patient? What are the contributing factors?
Show answer

The presence of blood in the nasogastric tube places the source of bleeding above the ligament of Treitz (in the proximal duodenum or the stomach). The patient’s history of ibuprofen use suggests that this may be the etiology of his bleeding. Ibuprofen is a nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug that can cause acute gastritis. It does so by inhibiting cyclooxygenase and thereby reducing the synthesis of prostaglandins E2 and I2, both of which normally protect the gastric mucosa from injury caused by gastric acid by stimulating secretion of bicarbonate ions, and mucus, and by increasing gastric mucosal blood flow.

The patient received additional normal saline and 7 units of packed red blood cells. He developed superficial thrombophlebitis at an IV site, but otherwise had an unremarkable course in the hospital. He was discharged after 5 days and told not to take NSAIDs or drink alcohol. However, 10 days following discharge, the patient developed dull pain and swelling in his left calf that was not relieved by ice or by leg elevation. He returned to the ED, where additional testing was performed.

5
What is the most likely cause of the patient’s symptoms?
Show answer

In a recently hospitalized, immobilized patient, the possibility of a deep venous thrombosis (DVT) must be considered; about 50% of DVTs are associated with local pain and edema.

6
What are the principal factors that predispose to thrombosis?
Show answer

The primary abnormalities that lead to thrombosis include (1) endothelial injury; (2) stasis or turbulent blood flow; and (3) hypercoagulability of the blood. These are often referred to as Virchow triad.


7
How does endothelial injury contribute to thrombosis?
Show answer

Endothelial injury leading to platelet activation almost always underlies thrombus formation in the heart and arterial circulation. Severe endothelial injury may trigger thrombosis by exposing von Willebrand factor (vWF) and tissue factor. However, inflammation and other noxious stimuli also promote thrombosis by shifting the pattern of gene expression in the endothelium to one that is “prothrombotic.” This change can be produced by diverse exposures, including physical injury, infectious agents, abnormal blood flow, inflammatory mediators, metabolic abnormalities (e.g., hypercholesterolemia, homocystinemia), and toxins absorbed from cigarette smoke.

8
Broadly, what are the two major flow abnormalities that lead to hypercoagulable states?
Show answer

Turbulence contributes to arterial and cardiac thrombosis by causing endothelial injury or dysfunction, as well as by forming countercurrents that contribute to local pockets of stasis. Stasis is a major contributor in the development of venous thrombi. Turbulence and stasis (1) promote endothelial activation, enhancing procoagulant activity and leukocyte adhesion in part through flow-induced changes in the expression of adhesion molecules and pro-inflammatory factors; (2) disrupt laminar flow and bring platelets into contact with the endothelium; and (3) prevent washout and dilution of activated clotting factors by fresh flowing blood and the inflow of clotting factor inhibitors.

9
What are the different types of hypercoagulable states?
Show answer

Hypercoagulability has a particularly important role in venous thrombosis and can be divided into primary (genetic) and secondary (acquired) disorders.

10
What is the principal concern in a patient with a DVT? How should the patient be treated?
Show answer

DVTs, particularly within the legs, are likely sources of pulmonary emboli, which may prove fatal. The patient needs to be treated with an anticoagulant, despite the recent bleeding episode.

11
Which leg vessels are most commonly associated with clinically significant pulmonary emboli?
Show answer

The large, deep veins of the leg above the knee joint are the usual sites of origin for clinically significant pulmonary emboli. These vessels include the popliteal veins, femoral veins, and iliac veins. Thrombi in these vessels often do not produce local symptoms. By contrast, thrombi in superficial veins often produce pain, edema, and varicose ulcers, but usually do not embolize.

On questioning, the patient stated that he had a brother who had developed a DVT at the age of 21 and subsequently had a second DVT at the age of 25. Ultrasonography of the patient’s left leg revealed a thrombus that arose in the popliteal vein and extended into the proximal femoral artery.

12
What are the major (most common) and minor (less common) inherited causes of thrombophilia?
Show answer

Roughly 5–6% of people with northern European ancestry carry Factor V Leiden. Also common is a variant in the noncoding region of the prothrombin gene that raises prothrombin levels. Both of these variants significantly increase the risk of DVT. Less common causes of inherited thrombophilia include mutations that abolish the function of anticoagulant factors, particularly antithrombin III, protein C, and protein S.

Molecular testing was sent and revealed that the patient was heterozygous for Factor V Leiden.

13
How does Factor V Leiden promote coagulation?
Show answer

Factor V is a cofactor for activated Factor X, which leads to the conversion of prothrombin to thrombin. The activity of Factor V is normally terminated by the activated Factor C/Factor S complex, which cleaves activated Factor V at arginine residue 506. Factor V Leiden is defined by the presence of a mutation that changes arginine 506 to a glutamine, which is resistant to inactivation by Factor C/Factor S complex.

14
How do antithrombin III, protein C, and protein S deficiency lead to thrombosis?
Show answer

These three factors are all anticoagulants. The protein C/protein S complex generates active protein C, which inactivates cofactors V and VIII. Protein C itself is activated by thrombin after the latter binds to thrombomodulin on the endothelium. Antithrombin III is activated by binding to heparin-like molecules on the endothelium; antithrombin binds and inhibits factors IXa, Xa, and XIa, as well as thrombin by forming complexes with these factors that are cleared from the circulation. Its ability to do so is increased approximately 1000-fold in the presence of heparins. The anticoagulant heparin and related drugs function by augmenting antithrombin III function. Deficiency of any of these factors leads to thrombophilia.

15
What are the major acquired risk factors for DVT?
Show answer

The major risk factors can broadly be divided into conditions that are associated with altered flow (venous stasis) and altered blood constituents. Conditions associated with stasis include immobilization (such as during hospitalizations, particularly following surgery), obesity, and pregnancy. Acquired factors that affect the make-up of the blood to favor thrombosis include cancers (which may release procoagulants), birth control pills (which increase the levels of certain coagulation factors), and smoking (which damages endothelium). Age greater than 50 and male sex is also associated with increased risk.

16
How do risk factors for DVT interact?
Show answer

Generally, risk factors are multiplicative; hence, the likelihood of a DVT rises sharply as risk factors multiply. This patient had four risk factors: mutation of Factor V Leiden, immobilization, male sex, and age greater than 50.

17
Why is aspirin not very effective in preventing DVT?
Show answer

The major effect of aspirin and other nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) on coagulation is inhibition of thromboxane A2 production in platelets. Thromboxane A2 is a potent activator of platelet aggregation and NSAIDs are effective for treating arterial thrombosis, which is a major factor in coronary arterial disease. It follows from this that platelets have a central role in the formation of arterial thrombi (e.g., in arteries) and a lesser role in the genesis of DVTs. By contrast, inhibitors of coagulation factors are effective in preventing DVT formation and propagation, whereas NSAIDs are not.

The patient was started on heparin in the hospital for his DVT. Once a therapeutic level of anticoagulation was achieved, the patient was started on warfarin and was discharged. After 6 months, warfarin was discontinued because his DVT was attributed to immobilization during hospitalization.

The patient was well for 12 months, but suddenly developed pleuritic left chest pain and returned to the ED. Vital signs and CBC were normal. A D-dimer test was elevated (700 ng/mL, normal <500).

18
What is the most likely cause of the patient’s left chest pain?
Show answer

D-dimers are derived from fibrin clots, and an elevation is indicative of a clot that is undergoing fibrinolysis. D-dimers are elevated in patients with DVT, intracardiac thrombi, and disseminated intravascular coagulation. Sudden onset pleuritic chest pain is a common symptom of pulmonary embolism (PE). Depending on the size and number of the PE(s), other symptoms may include cardiovascular compromise due to sudden onset right-sided hypertension, hypoxia due to ventilation/perfusion mismatch, and sudden death.

An angiogram with CT showed multiple small filling defects in both lungs. Repeat ultrasonography failed to identify a DVT. The patient was readmitted and was started on heparin.

19
Why was no DVT identified?
Show answer

Presumably the DVT that caused the PE completely broke away, fragmented, and embolized, explaining the multiple filling defects in the lung.

The patient was discharged on a Factor X inhibitor, a new class of anticoagulant that has fewer drug interactions than warfarin.

20
Why is the patient being anticoagulated?
Show answer

Heterozygous Factor V Leiden patients, such as this one, who develop a DVT in the absence of any controllable risk factor are anticoagulated for life to prevent recurrent DVT/PE. By contrast, patients with DVT who are homozygous are typically anticoagulated for life, even if controllable risk factors are present.

21
In what respects do fat embolism and amniotic fluid embolism significantly differ from a typical venous pulmonary thromboembolism?
Show answer

Fat embolism occurs after fractures of long bones, major soft tissue trauma, or severe burns. Most patients with fat embolism are asymptomatic, similar to patients with venous thromboembolism. In cases that are symptomatic ( 10%), besides pulmonary insufficiency, patients also develop neurologic symptoms, skin rash, and sometimes anemia and thrombocytopenia. Microscopically, the emboli consist of fat or marrow particles. Amniotic fluid embolism is a grave condition, with mortality >80% because of respiratory insufficiency, shock, disseminated intravascular coagulation, seizures, and coma. This condition is a rare complication of labor (1 in 50,000 deliveries). Microscopically, pulmonary vessels contain squamous cells and mucin (contents of amniotic fluid) derived from fetal skin and intestinal tract.

  • Lung
  • Squamous cells

Three months after discharge, the patient decided to stop taking his Factor X inhibitor. One month later, he had another episode of pleuritic chest pain. His wife called 911; however, her husband collapsed and could not be resuscitated. An autopsy was performed. Multiple wedge-shaped lesions were noticed in the periphery of his lungs.

  • Lung
  • Wedge-shaped lesions
22
What is the lesion seen on gross examination of the lung?
Show answer

This is an infarct.

23
What are infarcts and how are they classified?
Show answer

An infarct is an area of ischemic necrosis caused by occlusion of either the arterial supply or the venous drainage. Infarcts are classified according to color: red (hemorrhagic) or white (anemic) and the presence or absence of infection (septic vs bland, respectively).

24
In which settings do red infarcts occur?
Show answer

Red infarcts occur (1) with venous occlusions (e.g., testicular torsion); (2) in loose, spongy tissues (e.g., lungs) where blood can collect in the infarcted zone; (3) in tissues with dual circulations (e.g., lungs and small intestine) that allow blood to flow from an unobstructed parallel supply into a necrotic zone; (4) in tissues previously congested by sluggish venous outflow; and (5) when flow is reestablished to a site of previous arterial occlusion and necrosis (e.g., following angioplasty of an arterial occlusion).

25
In which settings do white infarcts occur?
Show answer

White infarcts occur with arterial occlusions in solid organs with end-arterial circulation (e.g., heart, spleen, kidney) and where tissue density limits the seepage of blood from adjoining capillary beds into the necrotic area.

  • Spleen
  • White infarct
26
Why are pulmonary infarctions rare in the setting of a pulmonary embolism?
Show answer

The lung has two supplies of arterial blood: the pulmonary and bronchial arteries. The latter usually provides sufficient oxygen to prevent infarction when the pulmonary artery is blocked. In patients with congestive heart failure, such as this one, forward flow is compromised, and the diminished bronchial artery flow is insufficient to maintain viability of the affected lung.

27
What would histologic examination of the patient’s pulmonary infarct show?
Show answer

The dominant histologic characteristic of infarction is coagulative necrosis. In this type of necrosis, the architecture of dead tissues is maintained for a span of days.

  • Coagulative necrosis
  • Retained architecture

In addition, a mass was noticed in the left popliteal vein. Sectioning showed the vascular lumen to be distended by a firm, red, laminated mass that was adherent to the wall of the vessel.

  • Vein
  • Thrombus
28
What is the difference between a postmortem clot and a thrombus?
Show answer

Postmortem clots are not attached to endothelium; they are gelatinous, rubbery, dark red at the ends, and yellowish elsewhere. Thrombi are attached to the vessel wall and are traversed by pale gray fibrin strands that can be seen on a cut section, giving them a laminated appearance (lines of Zahn); they are firmer than post-mortem clots, but still liable to shed fragments or detach entirely from the vessel wall.

29
What are the causes of arterial thrombosis?
Show answer

Arterial thrombosis is caused by injury to the endothelium and unusual conditions that enhance platelet function. The former includes atherosclerosis, vasculitis, and trauma. The latter includes heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT), in which pathogenic antibodies against heparin/platelet factor 4 complexes activate platelets.

30
What are the various fates of thrombi?
Show answer

If the patient survives the initial thrombosis, in the ensuing days to weeks thrombi undergo some combination of the following four events: (1) propagation, in which thrombi accumulate additional platelets and fibrin; (2) embolization, in which thrombi dislodge and travel to other sites in the vasculature; (3) dissolution, in which recent thrombi are completely broken down by the process of fibrinolysis; and (4) organization and recanalization, in which endothelial cells, smooth muscle cells, and fibroblasts grow into the thrombus and eventually form capillary channels that reestablish the patency of the vessel. In this example of a thrombosed artery stained for elastin, the original lumen is delineated by the internal elastic lamina and totally filled with organized thrombus.

  • Thrombosed artery
  • Elastic lamina
  • Organized thrombus
31
Which of these fates is clinically most significant in the arterial circulation versus the venous circulation?
Show answer

The most significant problem with arterial thrombi is propagation (enlargement) leading to luminal obstruction and infarction of the tissue supplied. Important examples include myocardial and cerebral infarction. The most significant problem with venous thrombi is embolism, particularly to the lung.

Objectives
  • • Describe the life cycle of HIV and how HIV infection leads to loss of CD4+ T cells.
  • • Describe the clinical consequences of the immune deficiency caused by HIV.

A 42-year-old man with no significant past medical history presented to his primary care physician with a 6-month history of cough and fatigue. In addition, he had noticed that he had become forgetful and had episodes when he felt confused and disoriented. He reported an unintentional 30-pound weight loss. His social history was significant for unprotected sex with multiple male partners, but he had been in a monogamous relationship for the last seven years. He had no history of intravenous drug use. On physical exam, he was febrile (39.5°C) and very thin, but he had no thrush. His laboratory results were notable for a WBC of 1300/mL (510 neutrophils, 420 lymphocytes, 320 monocytes).

1
What are the risk factors in this case for HIV infection?
Show answer

HIV infection occurs through the exchange of blood or body fluids that contain the virus or virus-infected cells. The three primary modes of transmission are (1) unprotected sexual contact (as in this case); (2) parenteral inoculation; and (3) vertical transmission from mothers to newborns. Populations at greatest risk for HIV infection are (1) homosexual and bisexual men; (2) intravenous drug users; (3) recipients of blood, blood components, or organs; (4) sexual partners of members in high-risk groups; and (5) infants born to HIV-positive mothers.

2
Which tests are used to diagnose HIV infection?
Show answer

The ELISA (enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay) HIV-1 antibody test is the first-line test to assess whether the patient has an HIV infection. It has excellent sensitivity and specificity (>99%); however, due to the gravity of the diagnosis, a confirmatory Western blot test is done to further establish the presence of the HIV antibody. In this test, HIV antigens are electrophoresed in a gel, transferred to a membrane, and overlaid with the patient's serum. The HIV p24 antigen test also uses an ELISA method to look for viral particles in the serum before antibodies rise. The recommended diagnostic algorithm combines very specific HIV antibody and very sensitive HIV antigen tests to ensure that the correct diagnosis is made, even very early after acquisition of the infection. Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) amplification for HIV-1 is used to diagnose perinatally-acquired HIV infection, because the HIV-1 antibody test in the perinatal period reflects the mother's serological status instead of the infant's; therefore, the antibody test has limited value until about 15 months of age. A CD4 count and quantitative PCR for HIV viral load assessment are useful to determine the extent of disease progression.

An ELISA, Western blot, and p24 HIV test were positive, with a viral load of 110,000 copies/mL, and his CD4+ T cell count was 29/µL (normal 500–1500/µL), which was 8% of total lymphocytes (normal 30–60%). A chest X-ray showed a diffuse pulmonary infiltrate.

3
What are the three phases of HIV infection and how are they distinguished? In which clinical phase is this patient?
Show answer

The course of HIV infection can be divided into three phases: acute, chronic (or latent), and AIDS. Acute HIV syndrome is a self-limited flu-like illness characterized by non-specific symptoms including sore throat, myalgias, and fever.

Since this is a new infection and the patient has no pre-established immunity, initially there is rapid replication of the virus and very high viral loads. However, after the host develops partial immunity (full “sterilizing” immunity to HIV is never seen), the viremia usually falls to low but detectable levels. In the chronic phase (clinical latency), patients are typically asymptomatic, but HIV replication and immune cell destruction continue in lymphoid tissues (lymph nodes, mucosal lymphoid tissue, and spleen). With time, continued CD4+ cell destruction culminates in AIDS. Patients with AIDS have very high titers of virus and develop severe, life-threatening clinical disease. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention classifies HIV infection based on serum CD4+ T cell count. When patients have very low CD4+ T cell count (<200 cells/μL), they are at increased risk for opportunistic infections, cancer (particularly types caused by oncogenic viruses), and clinically significant neurologic disease. Based on the patient’s findings, he would be diagnosed as having AIDS.

4
Why was his CD4+ T cell count severely reduced?
Show answer

HIV has a direct cytopathic effect on CD4+ T cells and kills infected cells. Early in the course of infection, new T cells can replace the ones that are lost, so there is little or no decline in blood CD4+ T cell counts. Over time, this compensatory replacement cannot keep pace with the cell loss, and a progressive decline in blood CD4+ T cells is seen.

5
What is the differential diagnosis for the patient’s fatigue and cough? Which tests should be ordered to confirm the diagnosis?
Show answer

In this clinical setting of AIDS and chronic respiratory symptoms, tuberculosis and opportunistic infections should be considered. A PPD test may be falsely negative due to anergy (the inability to mount a delayed-type hypersensitivity skin reaction to tuberculin because of immunosuppression). Despite the fact that immunosuppressed patients typically have higher bacterial loads than immunocompetent patients, sputum smears may be negative, possibly because the cavitation and bronchial damage that enable the bacilli to enter the bronchial tree are less prominent in immunosuppressed patients. Culture for Mycobacterium tuberculosis can take 6 to 8 weeks to yield a positive result, but is one of the more sensitive tests. PCR based testing is a useful adjunct and while it has high specificity, the sensitivity is much less so.

6
Which other pathogens should be considered? How does the clinical setting help narrow the differential diagnosis?
Show answer

Due to their immunocompromised state, patients with AIDS are susceptible to pathogens that rarely affect immunocompetent hosts. In addition to Mycobacterium tuberculosis, other pathogens such as Pneumocystis jiroveci, cytomegalovirus (CMV), and Mycobacterium avium complex should be considered as well as the “usual” pneumonia-causing pathogens (S. pneumoniae, S. aureus, H. influenzae, and gram-negative rods), which also cause disease more frequently in AIDS patients. The CD4+ T cell count often is useful in narrowing the differential diagnosis: bacterial and tubercular infections usually occur with mildly suppressed CD4+ counts (more than 200 cells/µL); Pneumocystis pneumonia usually occurs at CD4+ T cell counts below 200 cells/µL, while CMV and M. avium complex infections are uncommon until the very late stages of the disease (CD4+ T cell counts below 50 cells/µL).

Because of the high clinical suspicion for pulmonary tuberculosis, the patient was admitted to the hospital with special respiratory isolation to prevent transmission to others. The patient’s PPD test was negative. Three sputum samples were sent for microbial culture and staining. All three samples had acid-fast bacilli visualized by direct staining. While awaiting cultures, pharmacologic therapy for tuberculosis was initiated. The patient was also started on combination antiretroviral therapy for HIV. Six weeks later, cultures were positive for Mycobacterium tuberculosis.

7
Why is combination antiretroviral therapy used in the setting of HIV infection? What is the expected clinical course following initiation of treatment?
Show answer

Highly active antiretroviral therapy (HAART) consists of a combination of drugs that target enzymes involved in various steps of virus entry and replication, including the viral reverse transcriptase, protease and integrase. These drugs are used in combination to reduce the development of resistance and reduce HIV replication to below the level of detection (<50 copies of RNA per milliliter). This status is maintained as long as the patient is compliant. Over a period of years, the population of CD4+ T cells gradually increases.

Three weeks after starting his antiretroviral treatment, his HIV viral load had decreased to 273 copies/mL. His sputum stains were negative for acid fast bacilli (AFB), and he was discharged from the hospital to complete his therapy as an outpatient.

One week later, his partner brought him to his primary care physician. The patient was agitated and confused. He was oriented to person, but not place or time. He could recall one of three items immediately, but he could not remember them after 5 minutes. A computed tomography scan of the brain showed diffuse cortical, symmetric atrophy but no mass lesions. After returning to the emergency department from the radiology department, he developed intractable generalized seizures. Despite intubation and aggressive treatment, his seizures persisted, followed by cardiac arrest. He was unable to be resuscitated.

8
What are the causes of neurologic impairment in AIDS patients?
Show answer

Altered mental status in AIDS patients can be due to diseases related to immunodeficiency or syndromes related to HIV itself. The former includes viral infection (cytomegalovirus [CMV], progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy [PML]), bacterial infections, fungal infections (especially cryptococcosis, but also histoplasmosis and coccidioidomycosis), and parasitic infections (toxoplasmosis). Primary central nervous system lymphoma (most of which are Epstein-Barr virus[EBV]-related) should be considered in the differential diagnosis of altered mental status in an AIDS patient. HIV-associated neurocognitive disorder (HAND) is the most common noninfectious central nervous system disease in patients with AIDS and is characterized by a progressive decline in cognitive function, manifested first by memory loss and withdrawal.

An autopsy was performed. Numerous nodules were identified in the lungs.

Why is there only minimal granulomatous inflammation in this patient?
Show answer

CD4+ T cells of the Th1 subset elaborate interferon-𝛾, which is a potent macrophage activator that causes macrophages to aggregate and form granulomas. Patients with decreased numbers of CD4+ T cells are unable to mount an effective macrophage response. Granulomas may be poorly formed or absent, even in the setting of disseminated tuberculosis infection.

Which features would be expected on histologic examination of his brain?
Show answer

HIV-associated neurocognitive disorder (HAND) is characterized by widely distributed microglial nodules with macrophage-derived multinucleate cells. Some cases show white matter disorder as well with multifocal or diffuse areas of myelin pallor, visualized in a Luxol fast blue stain), axonal swellings, and gliosis.

12
Which neoplasms have an increased incidence in patients with AIDS?
Show answer

Patients with AIDS have a high incidence of certain tumors, especially Kaposi sarcoma (KS), B-cell lymphoma, cervical cancer in women, and anal cancer in men. A common feature of these tumors is that they are caused by oncogenic DNA viruses, specifically Kaposi sarcoma herpesvirus, also called human herpesvirus 8 (Kaposi sarcoma), Epstein-Barr virus (B-cell lymphoma), and human papillomavirus (cervical and anal carcinoma). The incidence of all of these has decreased markedly in the HAART era, though they are still prevalent in patients who are not on triple therapy.

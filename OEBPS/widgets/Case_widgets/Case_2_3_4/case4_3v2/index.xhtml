Objectives
  1. • Compare different types of hypersensitivity, with respect to their etiologies and mechanisms.
  2. • Describe the forms of antibodies that deposit in tissues and the mechanisms by which they cause tissue injury and disease.
  3. • Relate the major clinical manifestations of systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) to the underlying mechanisms.

A 42-year-old woman presented with a history of fever for 2 weeks, pleuritic chest pain, and intermittent joint pain in her hands, feet, and knees. Physical exam revealed a rash on her nose and cheeks as well as erythematous areas on her trunk and extremities. She stated that sun exposure exacerbated the erythema.

A punch biopsy of the rash was performed and showed apoptotic keratinocytes and liquefactive degeneration of the basal layer of the epidermis. Immunofluorescent staining for IgG revealed granular deposition of immune complexes along the basement membrane, at the dermoepidermal junction. (Skin biopsy is typically not performed for this clinical presentation.)

Further examination revealed a pleural friction rub, a slightly swollen tender right knee, and enlarged axillary, cervical, and inguinal lymph nodes. A plain radiograph of the chest showed a small right pleural effusion; joint films were normal except for a small amount of soft tissue swelling in the right knee. Hemoglobin was 10 g/dL, and urinalysis revealed 4+ proteinuria and leukocytes, red cells, and red cell casts in the urine sediment. Additional laboratory tests revealed slight elevations of blood urea nitrogen (BUN) and creatinine, and moderate hypoalbuminemia and hypergammaglobulinemia.

An antinuclear antibody (ANA) test was positive at a titer of 1:2560 and was reported as showing a speckled pattern. A dsDNA antibody determination was positive at 1:320, and an extractable nuclear antigen (ENA) antibody determination was reported as RNP (U1-RNP) and Sm antibody present.

1
What is the ANA test? What are the antibodies that were detected in the patient’s serum and what do they indicate about the underlying disease?
Show answer

Antinuclear antibodies are autoantibodies that react to self nuclear components. Virtually all patients with systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) have these antibodies in their serum. Some, such as antibodies against double stranded DNA (ds-DNA) and small ribonucleoproteins (the Sm antigen), are quite specific for SLE. The most widely used test for antinuclear antibodies is indirect immunofluorescence to detect antibodies in the serum. Different binding patterns are associated with antibodies of different specificities.

Staining patterns of anti-nuclear antibodies. (A) Homogeneous or diffuse staining of nuclei is typical of antibodies reactive with ds-DNA, nucleosomes, and histones, and is common in SLE. (B) A speckled pattern is seen with antibodies against various nuclear antigens, including Sm and RNPs. (C) The pattern of staining of anticentromere antibodies is seen in some cases of systemic sclerosis, Sjögren syndrome, and other diseases. (D) A nucleolar pattern is typical of antibodies against nucleolar proteins. (From Wiik AS, Høier-Madsen M, Forslid J, et al: Antinuclear antibodies: a contemporary nomenclature using HEp-2 cells. J Autoimm 35:276, 2010.)

In view of the patient’s increasing blood urea nitrogen (BUN) and creatinine, a renal biopsy was performed.

The renal biopsy showed a glomerulus that was globally hypercellular (i.e., all of the lobules and segments are hypercellular). This pattern is called diffuse lupus nephritis (as opposed to focal lupus nephritis). There are six classes of lupus nephritis (Chapter 11); class IV (diffuse lupus nephritis) is the most common. The global hypercellularity is due to infiltrating leukocytes and endothelial and mesangial cell proliferations. In addition, glomeruli in lupus nephritis can show “wire loop” lesions representing extensive subendothelial deposits of immune complexes (periodic acid-Schiff stain). In severe glomerular injury, leakage of coagulation factors and infiltration of leukocytes into Bowman space can lead to the formation of cellular “crescents” composed of fibrin, leukocytes, and proliferating epithelial cells.

What are the expected pathologic findings in the kidney biopsy?
Show answer

Based on the ANA studies and clinical presentation, the increasing BUN and creatinine are suggestive of lupus nephritis. Lupus nephritis is caused by deposition of preformed circulating immune complexes on the glomerular basement membrane. In response to these complexes, there are varying degrees of mesangial, endothelial and epithelial cell proliferation and accumulation of mesangial matrix. Infiltrating neutrophils may also contribute to glomerular hypercellularity.


3
What would be the expected findings of immunofluorescence microscopy?
Show answer

Most of the systemic lesions in SLE are caused by immune complexes (type III hypersensitivity). These complexes can be visualized by immunofluorescence staining in which fluorescently tagged antibodies against IgG or complement protein are incubated with sections of kidney. The glomerulus shows granular (“lumpy-bumpy”), bright green deposits, confirming the presence of immune complexes. This pattern contrasts with the smooth linear pattern seen in Goodpasture disease, in which the antibodies are directed against antigens within the basement membrane itself.


  • Granular
  • Linear

What is another technique to visualize the glomerular pathology?
Show answer

Electron microscopy can be used to detect deposits of electron-dense material. These deposits of antigen-antibody complexes are subendothelial and may create a circumferential thickening of the capillary wall (the so-called “wire loop” lesion).

What do the light microscopy, immunofluorescence, and electron microscopy reveal about the pathogenesis of this disease?
Show answer

The granular deposits seen by immunofluorescence and the dense deposits present in electron microscopic evaluation confirm the deposition of immune complexes in the glomerular wall. The antibodies in the complexes activate complement, and complement products recruit leukocytes, mainly neutrophils, which accounts for the increased cellularity seen on light microscopy. Subsequent glomerular damage and proteinuria are the result of this local inflammation.

Why does this patient have low serum complement? How does this finding relate to the pathologic features of this disease?
Show answer

Circulating and deposited immune complexes activate complement, primarily by the classical pathway, resulting in inflammation. When activation is sufficiently strong, complement is consumed, and serum levels decrease. Complement levels tend to return to normal when the disease remits spontaneously or with therapy.

The patient was treated with prednisone, 60 mg/day. After 4 weeks, the urine protein was 2+, the sediment had cleared, and BUN and serum creatinine had returned to normal.

8
Which type of hypersensitivity disease is this, and how does it differ from other types of hypersensitivity?
Show answer

Hypersensitivity diseases are classified into four major types based on the immune mechanisms responsible for tissue injury and clinical abnormalities: I, immediate hypersensitivity (allergy); II, antibody-mediated; III, immune complex-mediated; and IV, T cell–mediated. These immune reactions occur in response to self antigens (as in autoimmunity), or environmental antigens (allergies), or microbial antigens (inflammatory bowel disease, tuberculosis). SLE is considered the prototypic human autoimmune immune complex disease (type III hypersensitivity). However, some of the symptoms are the result of antibodies binding to various tissue antigens (type II hypersensitivity) such as antibodies against platelets and red cells causing depletion of these blood cells; antibodies against phospholipids inducing coagulation (so-called antiphospholipid syndrome); and antibodies against neurotransmitter receptors and ion channels, causing neuropsychiatric problems.

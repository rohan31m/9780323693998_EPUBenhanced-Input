Shocking Infection

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Describe the pathophysiology, clinical settings, and morphologic features of septic shock.
  2. • Compare and contrast cardiogenic and hypovolemic shock.
  3. • Discuss the pathophysiology, clinical progression, and morphologic features of disseminated intravascular coagulation.

A 25-year-old man presented to the Emergency Department with a 2-day history of high fever, chills, and rigors. He stated that he had been using IV drugs for 4 years. On examination, he was found to have a murmur in the tricuspid valve area. His face and extremities were flushed. His vital signs were blood pressure 90/60 mm Hg, temperature 39°C, and heart rate 110 bpm.

1
What does the presence of a heart murmur in an IV drug user suggest?
Show answer

In an IV drug user, one must be suspicious of the possibility of right-sided endocarditis. These infections are often destructive and can lead to tricuspid dysfunction and the development of tricuspid insufficiency, which may produce a murmur.

Laboratory evaluation revealed an elevated leukocyte count of 15,000/mm3(normal, 4500–11,000/mm3) due to increased numbers of neutrophils (71%; normal, 54–62%).

2
What do the fever and leukocytosis indicate?
Show answer

They are consistent with systemic inflammation. Certain circulating mediators, particularly TNF and IL-1, cause fever and increase the release of neutrophils from the bone marrow.

Blood cultures grew Gram-negative bacteria. He was treated with IV antibiotics, but his hypotension worsened and his urinary output declined to 100 mL/24 hr (normal, 800–2000 mL/24 hr).

3
What is the pathophysiology of the low blood pressure, oliguria, and renal failure in this case?
Show answer

The patient is displaying classic symptoms of septic shock, in this case caused by infective endocarditis with Gram-negative septicemia. Bacterial endotoxins trigger the release of inflammatory mediators such as TNF. Sustained, high levels of cytokines produce systemic activation of the endothelium, which becomes leaky, leading to edema in many tissues. Endothelium activation also increases the release of factors such as nitric oxide, which secondarily produce relaxation of smooth muscle and vasodilation, leading to hypotension and further compromising the delivery of nutrients and removal of wastes from tissues. These cardiovascular abnormalities result in tissue hypoperfusion, cellular hypoxia, and metabolic derangements, leading to organ dysfunction and, if severe and persistent, organ failure and death. In this case, diminished renal perfusion is the cause of the renal dysfunction.


4
What are the other types of shock?
Show answer

There are three general categories of shock: (1) septic shock, as in this patient; (2) cardiogenic shock; and (3) hypovolemic shock. Cardiogenic shock results from low cardiac output caused by myocardial pump failure. This can be due to intrinsic myocardial damage (infarction), ventricular arrhythmias, extrinsic compression (e.g., cardiac tamponade), or outflow obstruction (e.g., pulmonary embolism). Hypovolemic shock results from low cardiac output caused by low blood volume, such as can occur with massive hemorrhage, severe dehydration, or fluid loss from severe burns.

5
What are the stages of cardiogenic and hypovolemic shock?
Show answer

Cardiogenic and hypovolemic shock tend to evolve through three general (albeit somewhat artificial) phases: (1) an initial nonprogressive phase, during which reflex compensatory mechanisms are activated and perfusion of vital organs is maintained; (2) a progressive stage, characterized by tissue hypoperfusion and onset of worsening circulatory and metabolic imbalances, including lactic acidosis; and (3) an irreversible stage, which sets in after the body has incurred cellular and tissue injury so severe that even if the hemodynamic defects are corrected, survival is not possible.

The patient developed bleeding from venipuncture sites and mucosal surfaces.

6
What is the pathophysiology of his bleeding?
Show answer

He has likely developed disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), a common complication of septic shock. DIC is characterized by systemic activation of the coagulation cascade, leading to the formation of microthrombi throughout the microcirculation of the body. Consumption of platelets, fibrin, and coagulation factors, along with activation of the fibrinolytic system, leads to a bleeding diathesis, as has occurred in this patient.


7
How can a diagnosis of DIC be confirmed?
Show answer

Laboratory tests that show decreased platelet count (consumptive coagulopathy), increased PT and aPTT, decreased plasma fibrinogen, and a high D-dimer level all support a diagnosis of DIC. D-dimers are breakdown products of fibrin (often called fibrin split products).

8
What are the two major triggers of DIC?
Show answer

The two major triggers of DIC are the systemic release of tissue factor or other procoagulants and widespread endothelial injury. Systemic release of procoagulants may occur with extensive tissue injury, such as following trauma or burns. Widespread endothelial injury may be seen in any severe systemic inflammatory condition.

9
What are some common sources of procoagulants that can trigger DIC?
Show answer

Common sources include major trauma to tissues, major trauma to the placenta in obstetric complications, mucus released by adenocarcinomas, the granules of leukemic cells in acute promyelocytic leukemia, bacterial endotoxins that stimulate release of tissue factor from monocytes, and IL-1 and TNF that stimulate the expression of tissue factor on endothelial cell surfaces.

10
What is the spectrum of the clinical course of DIC?
Show answer

The clinical presentation and course of DIC vary depending on the cause and the extent of individual organ involvement. Acute DIC, dominated by bleeding, is associated with obstetric complications, septic shock, or major trauma. Chronic DIC, dominated by thrombotic complications, is more often associated with cancer.

11
What are the common morphologic features of DIC?
Show answer

Morphologic features include microthrombi in many organs, such as brain, heart, lungs, adrenal glands, and kidney; microinfarcts in kidney and brain; hyaline membranes in lungs; adrenal hemorrhage in meningococcemia (Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome); necrosis of the pituitary gland (Sheehan syndrome); and fragmented red cells and thrombocytopenia on the blood smear.

  • Adrenal cortex
  • Microthrombi
12
What is the mechanism of DIC in sepsis?
Show answer

Bacterial endotoxins promote increased synthesis and release of tissue factor from monocytes. Activated monocytes also release IL-1 and TNF, which increase the expression of tissue factor on endothelial cells while decreasing the expression of thrombomodulin on endothelial cells. Increased tissue factor activates the clotting system. Reduced thrombomodulin levels decrease protein C activation. There is simultaneous activation of the clotting system and inhibition of coagulation control, leading to the formation of widespread microthrombi.

13
What are the important factors that normally prevent DIC?
Show answer

Intact endothelium is normally an effective anticoagulant surface. When thrombin encounters intact endothelium, it binds to thrombomodulin, an event that inhibits thrombin’s ability to cleave fibrinogen and instead stimulates its ability to cleave and activate protein C, an inhibitor of the essential procoagulant cofactors Factor V and Factor VIII. The endothelium also has surface heparin-like molecules that activate antithrombin III, thereby inhibiting thrombin and several other activated factors. Finally, any activated coagulation factors that are washed away by flowing blood from the site of an injury are rapidly diluted and removed efficiently from the circulation by the liver.

Despite aggressive care, the patient died three days later. An autopsy was performed and revealed large, irregular, friable thrombi on the cusps of the tricuspid valve, consistent with infective endocarditis.

  • Heart
  • Friable thrombi
14
How does septic shock cause death?
Show answer

Shock is a progressive disorder that, if uncorrected, leads to death. The exact mechanism(s) of death from sepsis are still unclear; aside from increased lymphocyte and enterocyte apoptosis, there is only minimal cell death, and patients rarely have refractory hypotension, suggesting that organ failure secondary to edema and the attendant tissue hypoxia has a central role.

15
What is the pathophysiology of acute renal failure in septic shock?
Show answer

A distributive effect occurs: systemic vasodilation leads to peripheral pooling of blood and a consequent reduction in visceral blood flow, including renal perfusion. Grossly, the renal cortex appears pale and bloodless. By comparison, the medulla looks darker red and congested. Microscopically, acute tubular necrosis can be seen: although the glomeruli appear normal, there is necrosis of the epithelium of the proximal tubules. In such tubules, the normal cuboidal epithelium is replaced by eosinophilic, structureless debris in which cellular outlines and the nuclei are obscured. There are adjacent, uninjured tubules that show a well-preserved layer of epithelial cells that with distinct nuclei.

  • Gross of kidney
  • Cortex
  • Medulla
  • Kidney histology
  • Glomeruli
  • Necrotic tubules
  • Uninjured tubules
16
What are the major morphologic changes in multiple organ failure in a patient who dies of shock?
Show answer

Morphologic changes are as follows: kidneys—acute tubular necrosis (described in question 15); brain—laminar cortical necrosis; lungs—shock lung (diffuse alveolar damage) with hyaline membranes; heart—foci of necrosis, hemorrhage, contraction band necrosis; gastrointestinal system—hemorrhages; liver—central hemorrhagic necrosis, fatty change. Compare the thinned and discolored cerebral cortex on the left side with the relatively preserved cortex on the right side. Neurons in different areas of the brain have different susceptibilities to global ischemic damage, such as may occur in shock. Most patients with shock who are diagnosed and treated early have no or only mild ischemic damage to the brain, which may result only in a confusional state. More severe, prolonged, and irreversible shock can lead to laminar cortical necrosis.

  • Brain, gross
  • Normal cortex
  • Laminar necrosis

Don’t Fail Me Now

Normal Reference Range Table
Objectives
  1. • Describe which donor antigens in a solid organ allograft are recognized by the recipient’s immune system.
  2. • Compare and contrast hyperacute, acute cellular, acute humoral and chronic rejection of solid organ grafts with respect to mechanisms, and the resultant morphology and clinical features.
  3. • Describe how rejection can be reduced and treated, and the complications of the therapy.

A 24-year-old man with bilateral polycystic disease of the kidney developed hypertension and chronic renal failure. Both kidneys were removed, and he was treated with hemodialysis until a native kidney donor could be found. Human leukocyte antigen (HLA) typing was performed using serology and DNA sequence analysis for HLA-A, HLA-B, and HLA-DR genes, and after a 3-year wait a cadaveric donor kidney with a match (6 out of 6 alleles) became available. The patient did well initially. His creatinine level fell to 1.1 mg/dL (normal 0.6–1.2 mg/dL) in the week following the transplant. However, routine testing 6 weeks after transplant revealed a creatinine level of 2.2 mg/dL (normal 0.5-1.4 mg/dL) and blood urea nitrogen (BUN) of 42 mg/dL (normal 7–18 mg/dL).

1
Based on the clinical history, this is most likely which type of rejection?
Show answer

The types of transplant rejection are hyperacute, acute humoral, acute cellular, and chronic. For this patient, the timing of the worsening renal function (6 weeks) is consistent with acute rejection; however, pathologic evaluation of the kidney biopsy is necessary for definitive diagnosis.

A renal biopsy is performed.

2
What type of rejection does the biopsy show?
Show answer

The first image shows extensive interstitial inflammation with lymphocytic infiltration of tubules, referred to as tubulitis, associated with focal tubular injury. The second image shows a small artery with inflammation of the endothelium and intima (intimal arteritis). The endothelial cells are swollen and, in places, the lymphocytes can be seen between the endothelium and the vessel wall. These two findings (tubulitis and intimal arteritis) are the cardinal features of acute cellular rejection. Acute cellular rejection is mediated by T lymphocytes, including CD8+ cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) that injure graft tissues via direct cytotoxicity and CD4+ T cells that cause injury indirectly by elaborating proinflammatory cytokines.

3
What is the difference in the histologic manifestations of acute cellular versus humoral (antibody-mediated) rejection?
Show answer

In contrast to the tubulitis and intimal arteritis seen in acute cellular (T cell–mediated) rejection, acute antibody-mediated rejection is manifested mainly by damage to glomeruli and small blood vessels. Typically, the lesions show inflammation of glomeruli and peritubular capillaries associated with deposition of the complement breakdown product C4d, which is a useful diagnostic marker for activation of the complement system by the antibody-dependent classical pathway. Small vessels may also show focal thrombosis. The recognition of cellular rejection is important because, in the absence of an accompanying humoral rejection, patients respond well to immunosuppressive therapy.

4
Rejection developed even though 6/6 HLA loci were matched between donor and recipient; what antigens might be triggering the rejection?
Show answer

In routine practice, the three HLA loci that are tested for donor-recipient matching are A, B, and DR because these are the most polymorphic and therefore vary the most between individuals. The match was exact for these three loci (six, because every individual inherits one from each parent). The reasons for rejection could be mismatches for sequence variations in these HLA molecules; mismatches in other remaining MHC genes that were not evaluated (HLA-C, DQ, or DP); or differences in minor histocompatibility antigens, which are non-HLA–encoded molecules that differ between donor and recipient and can be recognized as foreign.

The patient was treated with anti-thymocyte globulin, and doses of prednisone, azathioprine, and tacrolimus were increased. The patient’s renal function returned to normal and he did very well except for occasional infections, including cryptococcal meningitis.

5
Why did the patient develop occasional bouts of infection?
Show answer

The immunosuppressive therapy that the patient received depletes T cells (anti-thymocyte globulin), inhibits T-cell activation (tacrolimus), and suppresses inflammation (prednisone). These drugs inhibit multiple components of the immune system and make patients susceptible to infections. Many of these infections are caused by reactivation of latent viruses such as Epstein-Barr virus and cytomegalovirus, which are kept in check by a healthy immune system. Patients also become prone to developing various cancers because of the failure of immunosurveillance.

Three years later, the patient’s laboratory testing showed an increase of his creatinine from the baseline of 1.5 to 5.0 mg/dL (normal 0.6–1.2 mg/dL) and his BUN from 25 to 65 mg/dL (normal 7–18 mg/dL) over a four-month period. In addition, although he had no proteinuria at baseline, over the same period, he developed 2+ proteinuria. A renal biopsy was performed.

Based on the clinical history and histologic findings, this is most likely which type of rejection?
Show answer

A clinical history of progressive renal failure manifested by a rise in serum creatinine over a period of months to years combined with the histologic findings seen in these two figures (trichrome stain and silver stain) is diagnostic of chronic rejection. Chronic rejection is dominated by (1) vascular changes, which include intimal thickening with minimal associated inflammation; (2) glomerulopathy, with duplication of the basement membrane, likely secondary to chronic endothelial injury; and (3) peritubular capillary injury with multilayering of peritubular capillary basement membranes (not visible in these micrographs). Interstitial fibrosis and tubular atrophy with loss of renal parenchyma may occur secondary to ischemia caused by the vascular lesions. Chronically rejecting kidneys usually have interstitial infiltrates of mixed mononuclear inflammatory cells, including T cells, macrophages, and plasma cells.

What are the morphologic features of hyperacute and chronic rejection?
Show answer

In hyperacute rejection of the kidney, marked attenuation and pallor of the cortex is seen due to ischemia and, possibly, coagulative necrosis. The medullary pyramids are hyperemic. In this example, there is a large hemorrhagic area in the pelvic region. By contrast, in chronic rejection, gradual ischemia leads to loss of parenchyma and replacement by fibrosis, resulting in a shrunken, atrophic kidney. The pale, mottled appearance is due to widespread loss of glomeruli and tubules and fibrotic replacement.

Objectives
  1. • Describe the beneficial and harmful effects of inflammation.
  2. • Describe the major vascular and cellular reactions of acute inflammation.
  3. • Compare and contrast the morphologic patterns of acute inflammation.

A 23-year-old woman presented to the Emergency Department with a 2-day history of periumbilical pain, anorexia, and fevers. Six hours before admission, the pain became more intense and localized to the right lower quadrant. On physical exam, she had a fever of 38° C, rebound tenderness, and voluntary guarding. An ultrasound revealed an enlarged appendix and periappendiceal fluid. Laboratory analysis was significant for a WBC count of 19,500 cells/mm3, C-reactive protein of 300 mg/L (normal <3.0 mg/L) and an erythrocyte sedimentation rate of 104 mm/h (normal 0-20 mm/h). A peripheral smear was performed and showed increased numbers of mature and immature neutrophils. Immature neutrophils do not show the typical nuclear segmentation and instead have nuclei that look like “bands”. The appearance of bands in the peripheral blood is referred to as a left shift.

1
What is the most likely diagnosis?
Show answer

This patient has acute appendicitis. She has pain, fever, and swelling of the appendix (on ultrasound). These are three of the five cardinal signs of inflammation (the other two are redness and loss of function, which could not be determined because of the intraabdominal location of the appendix). In addition, there is neutrophilic leukocytosis, increased erythrocyte sedimentation rate and elevated C-reactive protein, all systemic signs of acute inflammation. The appendix is a hollow organ with one end open to the enteric bacteria. It is thought that the open end becomes obstructed by fecaliths or hyperplastic lymphoid tissue, leading to trapping of bacteria within the hollow organ. Although infection is one of the most common causes of acute inflammation, it can follow tissue necrosis of any cause, including ischemia (“sterile injury”), trauma, toxins, and immunological reactions.

2
What is the significance of the patient’s rebound tenderness?
Show answer

Rebound tenderness and the ultrasound findings raise the possibility that the appendix has ruptured and the patient now has peritonitis.

3
What is the cause of the elevation of C-reactive protein and the increased erythrocyte sedimentation rate?
Show answer

In the setting of inflammation, cytokines (especially IL-6) stimulate the liver to synthesize acute-phase proteins, such as C-reactive protein (CRP), serum amyloid associate protein (SAA), and fibrinogen. CRP binds to microbial cell walls and is thought to act as an opsonin and to fix complement. Fibrinogen binds to red cells, causing them to adhere to each other in stacks (rouleaux). These clumps sediment more quickly than individual red cells, resulting in increased erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR). The CRP and ESR are simple tests for an inflammatory response caused by any stimulus. In general, CRP is considered a better indicator of inflammation.

An exploratory laparotomy was performed and the appendix was removed. It was hyperemic, edematous, and covered by a yellow-tan serosal exudate. There was a focal defect consistent with perforation. Sectioning of the appendix revealed a distended lumen containing hemorrhagic material and a fecalith that apparently obstructed the lumen.

Histologic evaluation showed ulceration of the mucosa and an associated neutrophilic infiltrate. Neutrophils and blood are present within the appendiceal lumen. A portion of the fecalith can be seen in the upper part of the section.

4
Which cell type is dominant in the peripheral blood and the tissue and why?
Show answer

In acute inflammation, the dominant cell type is the neutrophil (polymorphonuclear leukocyte, PMN). There are several reasons why neutrophils are the predominant cell type early in acute inflammation: (1) they are the most abundant leukocyte in the blood and (2) of leukocytes, they are the most rapidly chemotactic in response to external signals (e.g., chemokines, leukotrienes, complement products). Because neutrophils die by apoptosis within hours to a few days after entering tissues, they are less prominent in chronic inflammation. When the inflammatory stimulus is significant, cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor (TNF) mobilize leukocytes from the bone marrow, and other cytokines such as GM-CSF (granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor) increase the production of neutrophils from hematopoietic stem cells. Immature granulocytes (bands) are released into the bloodstream before they have completely matured (left shift). Neutrophils are recruited from the blood to sites of infection and tissue injury. In more prolonged and chronic inflammation, other cells such as macrophages and lymphocytes become progressively more numerous.

5
What is the cause of the tissue edema seen grossly?
Show answer

Vascular reactions of acute inflammation consist of changes in the flow of blood and the permeability of vessels that promote the movement of plasma proteins and leukocytes out of the circulation and into the site of infection or injury. One of the hallmarks of acute inflammation is vasodilation, which is mediated by histamine. Vasodilation increases blood flow to the area, causing the heat and redness (erythema) associated with inflammation. Next, vascular permeability increases to allow fluid, proteins, and blood cells to move from the vascular system into interstitial tissues or body cavities. This process is called exudation. This exudate is responsible for the edema seen grossly.

6
What are the steps necessary for neutrophils to travel from the intravascular space to the surrounding tissue?
Show answer

Neutrophils circulating in the blood must be slowed, stopped, and extravasate at the site of tissue damage. Because of a combination of changes in vascular flow and caliber that lead to stasis, blood flow slows early in inflammation, allowing leukocytes to assume a peripheral position along the endothelial surface. This process of leukocyte redistribution (called margination) allows leukocytes to interact with adhesion molecules expressed by activated endothelium. By a series of interactions involving selectins and integrins, leukocytes transmigrate through the vessel wall into the perivascular space. Further movement of leukocytes towards the site of injury occurs via chemotaxis, defined as locomotion along a chemical gradient. (See Case 2.2 for more details)


7
Which form of damage occurs to host tissues during this reaction? What causes this damage, and what are some of the complications of this injury?
Show answer

In certain bacterial infections, host tissues typically undergo liquefactive necrosis. Tissue necrosis is caused by toxins liberated by bacteria and products released by inflammatory cells. Neutrophils produce reactive oxygen species (see case 2.3) and granule enzymes, which not only destroy bacteria but also injure tissues at the site of inflammation. Necrotic, liquefied debris and dead and dying neutrophils form pus. This is called purulent inflammation, and an enclosed area of pus is called an abscess. Liquefactive necrosis of the wall of the gut can lead to perforation and spread of the infection, as in this case, in which the inflamed appendix perforated and the infection spread first to the adjacent peritoneum, causing localized peritonitis (responsible for rebound tenderness). In the absence of appropriate medical care, peritonitis can become widely disseminated, resulting in sepsis.

8
What are the four morphologic patterns of acute inflammation and which would be seen in this patient’s specimen?
Show answer

There are four main morphologic patterns of acute inflammation: (1) serous inflammation; (2) fibrinous inflammation; (3) purulent (suppurative) inflammation; and (4) ulceration. A fibrinous exudate would be expected on the surface of this patient’s appendix and is characteristic in the setting of inflammation in the lining of body cavities, such as the meninges, pericardium and pleura. Fibrinous inflammation occurs when the vascular leaks allow coagulation factors and large proteins such as fibrinogen to escape and there is a procoagulant stimulus. Purulent inflammation is present within the appendiceal lumen and ulceration of the appendiceal mucosa is most likely due to necrosis secondary to ischemia caused by pressure from the fecalith in the lumen. Serous inflammation is characterized by the exudation of cell-poor fluid into the space created by an injury to surface epithelia or into body cavities lined by the peritoneum, pleura, or pericardium; it is not seen in this case.

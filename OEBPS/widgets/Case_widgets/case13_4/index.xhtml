Objectives
  1. • Discuss the etiologies, laboratory results, and pathology of primary biliary cholangitis and primary sclerosing cholangitis.
  2. • Describe the liver pathology of host-vs-graft disease and the rejection of liver transplants.

A 42-year-old woman presented to her physician with a 6-month history of fatigue. She reported that she also had experienced pruritus for many years, and that it was steadily worsening. Her history was negative for alcohol use, hepatitis, blood transfusion, and intravenous drug use. She also said she had experienced no recent illnesses or surgical procedures.

Physical examination revealed a mildly obese woman in no distress. Excoriations were present over her upper arms and chest. She had small yellow papules around her palpebral fissures, and larger cutaneous nodules were present over her elbows.

There was no ascites. Her liver percussed to 14 cm (normal 7 cm) and extended 5 cm below the right costal margin. Her liver was firm and had a smooth edge. Her spleen tip was palpable.

Which additional tests should be ordered?
Show answer

The laboratory results indicate liver pathology (elevated AST, ALT, and alkaline phosphatase). The AST:ALT ratio is <2, which would make acute alcoholic hepatitis less likely. The basis for the lower elevation of ALT relative to AST is not well understood, but the ratio is a useful indicator of alcohol-induced liver injury. Hypercholesterolemia can be seen in familial hypercholesterolemia, which does not typically have associated liver pathology, and in cholestatic disorders such as primary biliary cholangitis. Viral and autoimmune etiologies must be excluded. Therefore, viral serologies (HBsAg, anti-HBc, anti-HCV) and autoantibodies (antinuclear, antimitochondrial, and anti–smooth muscle antibody) should be assessed.

What is the most likely diagnosis for this patient?
Show answer

The most likely diagnosis is primary biliary cholangitis (previously called primary biliary cirrhosis), a chronic autoimmune liver disorder that is most commonly associated with antimitochondrial antibodies. Autoantibodies are a significant feature of this disease: antimitochondrial antibodies are found in over 90% of cases. The markedly elevated alkaline phosphatase out of proportion to minimally elevated hepatic aminotransferases (AST and ALT), is indicative of a cholestatic pattern of injury. Most patients with primary biliary cholangitis present with normal serum bilirubin. A liver biopsy is required to establish the diagnosis and exclude other diseases that can cause cholestasis.

A liver biopsy was performed and showed lymphoplasmacytic inflammation destroying interlobular bile ducts. A granulomatous reaction to bile duct destruction (florid duct lesion) was also seen. There are fibrous bridges between portal areas.

What is the recommended therapy for this patient?
Show answer

In recent years, early treatment with oral ursodeoxycholic acid has dramatically improved outcomes by slowing disease progression. Its mechanism of action remains unclear, but is presumably related to the ability of ursodeoxycholate to enter the bile acid pool and alter the biochemical composition of bile.

4
What are the postulated mechanisms for this disease?
Show answer

While there is much evidence to support an autoimmune etiology in primary biliary cholangitis (such as presence of autoantibodies and association with other known autoimmune diseases), the inciting antigens are not known, and a definitive cause of the disorder has not been found.

5
What are some other causes of biliary cirrhosis?
Show answer

Other causes include primary sclerosing cholangitis, extrahepatic biliary atresia, and processes that lead to chronic obstruction of the biliary tract such as an impacted gallstone in the common bile duct, biliary strictures, or tumors of the biliary tree or pancreas.

  • Degenerating bile duct

6
How does primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC) differ from primary biliary cholangitis (PBC)?
Show answer

Like PBC, PSC is a presumed autoimmune disease. It is characterized by inflammation and obliterative fibrosis of intrahepatic and extrahepatic bile ducts with dilation of preserved segments. Inflammatory bowel disease, particularly ulcerative colitis, coexists in approximately 70% of individuals with PSC. PSC tends to occur in the third through fifth decades of life and males predominate 2 : 1, a distribution similar to inflammatory bowel disease. Histologically, inflammation of large bile ducts with acute neutrophilic infiltration of the epithelium superimposed on a chronic inflammatory background is seen. Inflamed areas develop strictures due to narrowing of the lumen by edema and inflammation or due to subsequent scarring. The smaller ducts typically show a striking circumferential “onion skin” fibrosis around an increasingly atrophic duct lumen, eventually leading to complete obliteration, without significant associated inflammation.

Which process is suggested by the clinical presentation and laboratory values?
Show answer

The mildly elevated liver function tests and liver enlargement/tenderness are suggestive of acute rejection. Other considerations in the clinical differential diagnosis, such as hepatic vein thrombosis and hyperacute rejection, would be associated with fulminant hepatitis and necrosis. In that setting, transaminases would be markedly elevated (>1000 U/L).

8
What is the pathophysiology of acute transplant rejection?
Show answer

Acute rejection is mediated by recipient T cells that are activated by alloantigens in the graft and may occur within days or weeks after transplantation. This is the most common cause of early graft failure. Acute rejection is classified as acute cellular rejection or acute antibody-mediated (vascular or humoral rejection). In acute cellular rejection, CD8+ CTLs may directly destroy graft cells, or CD4+ cells secrete cytokines and induce inflammation, which damages the graft. T cells also may react against graft vessels, leading to vascular damage. Acute antibody-mediated (vascular or humoral) rejection in liver is unusual. In this type of rejection, antibodies bind to vascular endothelium and activate complement via the classical pathway. The resultant inflammation causes graft failure.

A liver biopsy was performed.

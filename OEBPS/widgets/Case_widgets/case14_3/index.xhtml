A 24-year-old man presented to his primary care physician with a 1-week history of an ulcerated lesion on his penis. He said it was not painful. His sexual history was relevant for unprotected sex with multiple partners.

Physical exam revealed a 1-cm shallow, erythematous ulcer on the shaft of the penis. Upon further questioning about sexual contacts, the patient stated that his partner had had a rash about a month before, present over almost his entire body, but particularly on his palms.

What is the significance of these laboratory tests?
Show answer

The rapid plasma reagin (RPR) test detects antibodies in the serum that are not specifically directed against Treponema pallidum but against cardiolipin, a phospholipid, present both in Treponema pallidum and host tissues. The “1:64” refers to the titer, which is the greatest dilution of the patient’s serum that yields a positive result and is a rough measure of the amount of antibodies present. Another nontreponemal test that is commonly ordered for screening is the venereal disease research laboratory (VDRL) test. These tests become positive after a week of infection and are highly sensitive (positive in almost all cases with syphilis) but not specific (also positive in some conditions unrelated to syphilis such as autoimmune diseases). Hence, if either of these tests is positive, the diagnosis is confirmed with specific treponemal test (microhemagglutin assay for T. pallidum [MHA-TP] or fluorescent treponemal antibody absorption test [FTA-Abs]), performed on a blood sample. Because of the high false negative rate of the nontreponemal tests in tertiary syphilis, a specific treponemal test should be ordered to help confirm the diagnosis if the screening nontreponemal test is negative. PCR may be performed on scrapings of the lesions and direct visualization of T. pallidum with specific antibodies is also possible in scrapes of lesions or in tissue biopsies.

What is the natural progression of syphilis?
Show answer

Syphilis can evolve through stages known as primary, secondary, and tertiary. Several weeks after infection, a primary lesion that is usually painless called a chancre, appears at the point of spirochete entry. Systemic dissemination of organisms occurs while the host mounts an immune response that typically fails to clear the organisms. Even without therapy, the primary chancre resolves within 4–6 weeks; however, approximately 25% of patients progress to secondary syphilis, which is characterized by lymphadenopathy and mucocutaneous lesions. These lesions also resolve without therapy. Patients with untreated syphilis next enter an asymptomatic, late latent phase of the illness, defined as being more than 1 year after the initial infection. In about one third of cases, new symptoms develop over the next 5 to 20 years. This late symptomatic phase, or tertiary syphilis, is marked by the development of lesions in the cardiovascular system, central nervous system, or, less frequently, other organs.

What is the cause of this patient’s urethral discharge?
Show answer

This patient is infected with Neisseria gonorrhoeae (gonococcus), a pathogen that is second only to Chlamydia trachomatis among reported communicable diseases in the United States, and the cause of gonorrhea. Coinfection with other sexually transmitted diseases (STDs) is common, particularly Chlamydia trachomatis, which is found in 30% of males with gonorrhea.

6
What are some of the potential consequences of gonorrhea infection in men?
Show answer

Ascending infection may result in the development of acute prostatitis, epididymitis, or orchitis. Abscesses may complicate severe cases. Disseminated infection is much less common than local infection, occurring in 0.5% to 3% of cases of gonorrhea. It is more frequent in females than in males. Manifestations include, most commonly, tenosynovitis, arthritis, and pustular or hemorrhagic skin lesions. Endocarditis and meningitis are rare presentations.

7
What are some of the potential consequences of gonorrhea in women?
Show answer

The most significant consequence for women is the development of pelvic inflammatory disease (PID). PID occurs when untreated cases of gonorrhea may be complicated by ascending infection, leading to acute inflammation of the fallopian tubes (salpingitis) and ovaries and sometimes tuboovarian abscesses. The acute inflammatory process is followed by the development of granulation tissue and scarring, with resultant strictures and other permanent deformities of the involved structures (PID). PID is associated with infertility and an increased risk for ectopic pregnancy. Gonococcal infection of the upper genital tract may spread to the peritoneal cavity, where the exudate may extend up the right paracolic gutter to the dome of the liver, resulting in gonococcal perihepatitis.

Which other organism is commonly associated with pelvic inflammatory disease?
Show answer

Chlamydia trachomatis is also associated with PID, in addition to epididymitis, prostatitis, pelvic inflammatory disease, pharyngitis, conjunctivitis, perihepatic inflammation, and, among individuals who engage in anal sex, proctitis. Another important manifestation of chlamydial infection is reactive arthritis (formerly known as Reiter syndrome), predominantly in patients who are HLA-B27 positive. It is most likely an autoimmune reaction triggered by the microbe. This condition typically manifests as a combination of urethritis, conjunctivitis, arthritis, and generalized mucocutaneous lesions.

9
How is C. trachomatis diagnosed?
Show answer

C. trachomatis is a small, Gram-negative bacterium that is an obligate intracellular pathogen. Organisms are not visible in Gram-stained sections and, in contrast to the Neisseria gonorrhoeae, C. trachomatis cannot be isolated with the use of conventional culture media. The diagnosis is best made by a nucleic acid amplification test on voided urine, which is now the gold standard.

How are HSV-1 and HSV-2 infections differentiated?
Show answer

Although both herpes simplex virus 1 (HSV-1) and HSV-2 can cause anogenital or oral infections, most cases of anogenital herpes are caused by HSV-2. However, recent years have seen a rise in the number of genital infections caused by HSV-1, in part due to the increasing practice of oral sex. The morphology of lesions caused by HSV-1 and HSV-2 is essentially identical. They can be differentiated by molecular (PCR-based) testing or by serologic testing for antibodies specific for HSV-1 or HSV-2.

12
Is genital herpes infection associated with significant morbidity and mortality?
Show answer

The primary infection with herpes results in locally painful vesicular lesions that may be accompanied by dysuria, urethral discharge, local lymph node enlargement and tenderness, and systemic manifestations, such as fever, muscle aches, and headache. Signs and symptoms may last for several weeks during the primary phase of disease. Recurrences are milder and of shorter duration than in the primary episode. In immunocompetent adults, genital herpes generally is not life-threatening. However, HSV poses a major threat to immunosuppressed patients, in whom fatal, disseminated disease may develop. Also life threatening is neonatal herpes infection, which occurs in about one half of infants delivered vaginally of mothers with either primary or recurrent genital HSV infection. The viral infection is acquired during passage through the birth canal.

13
Which other virus has intranuclear inclusions identical to HSV but does not cause genital ulcers?
Show answer

Varicella-zoster virus has intranuclear inclusions identical to HSV; however, it does not cause genital ulcers.

Objectives
  1. • Describe the pathogenesis and pathologic and clinical features of nodular hyperplasia of the prostate (benign prostatic hyperplasia, BPH).
  2. • Describe the pathologic and clinical features of prostatic carcinoma and the importance of grading and staging in prognosis.
  3. • Discuss the relationship between the anatomy of the prostate and the development of BPH and carcinoma and how this relationship affects the clinical presentation of each entity.
  4. • Describe the natural history of prostate cancer and the role of androgens in its development, progression, and treatment.

A 63-year-old man presented with urinary hesitancy, frequency, and nocturia. Digital rectal examination revealed a large, nodular, and rubbery prostate gland with no firm areas. A serum prostate specific antigen (PSA) was 6 ng/mL (0–4 ng/mL).

1
Is the patient’s elevated PSA indicative of malignancy? Why or why not?
Show answer

No. PSA is a product of prostatic epithelium and is normally secreted in the semen. Although it is elevated in most cases of prostatic adenocarcinoma, benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), prostatitis, prostatic infarcts, instrumentation of the prostate, and ejaculation all may increase serum PSA levels. Conversely, 20% to 40% of patients with organ-confined prostate cancer have PSA values below the cutoffs used to identify patients who are likely to have prostate cancer. Thus, PSA levels are neither sensitive nor specific for cancer of the prostate. Therefore, prostate biopsy is essential for the diagnosis of prostate cancer. Although measurement of PSA is a highly imperfect and controversial screening test for prostate cancer, once a diagnosis of cancer is made, serial PSA levels are very useful in assessing the response of cancer to therapy. For example, a rising PSA level after surgical removal of cancer is indicative of recurrent or metastatic disease.

2
What is the likely cause of this patient’s symptoms?
Show answer

The two main pathologic processes that affect the prostate are nodular hyperplasia (BPH) and prostatic adenocarcinoma. BPH arises in the central portion of the prostate gland, and the hyperplastic nodules frequently compress the urethra to a slit, causing symptoms of urinary obstruction, as in this patient. The digital rectal exam finding of a diffusely enlarged, nodular prostate is also suggestive of BPH. By contrast, prostatic adenocarcinoma arises in the peripheral portions of the prostate (70%–80% of cases) and typically is not associated with urinary obstruction. Digital rectal exam reveals a hard nodule in a subset of cases. However, prostatic adenocarcinoma can co-exist with BPH; biopsy evaluation is necessary to definitively exclude malignancy.

A needle biopsy was performed and showed no evidence of malignancy. The patient underwent a transurethral resection of prostate.

3
Based on the specimen pictured, what is the diagnosis?
Show answer

The histologic features are typical of BPH, which is characterized by hyperplastic nodules composed of variable proportions of proliferating glandular elements and fibromuscular stroma. The hyperplastic glands are lined by tall, columnar epithelial cells and a second deeper layer of flattened basal cells. Identifying the basal cells, which are absent in adenocarcinoma, may be challenging; immunohistochemical stains for markers of basal cells such as high molecular weight keratins can help to confirm the presence of these cells.

  • Prostate, histology
  • Proliferating stroma
  • Proliferating glands
  • Histology, higher power
  • Basal cells
  • Columnar epithelium
4
What would be the gross appearance of the prostate in such a case?
Show answer

As illustrated, there are multiple nodules of hyperplastic tissues that have compressed the urethra.

  • Prostate gland, nodule
  • Hyperplastic nodules
  • Urethra
5
What is the etiology of BPH?
Show answer

Dihydrotestosterone (DHT), the ultimate mediator of prostatic growth, is synthesized in the prostate from circulating testosterone by the action of the enzyme 5α-reductase, type 2. DHT binds to nuclear androgen receptors, which regulate the expression of genes that support the growth and survival of prostatic epithelium and stromal cells. Although the cause of BPH is incompletely understood, excessive androgen-dependent growth of stromal and glandular elements has a central role. One type of pharmacologic therapy is therefore aimed at inhibiting the formation of DHT from testosterone (such as 5α-reductase inhibitors).

No malignancy was seen and the patient was prescribed medical therapy with an α-adrenergic blocking agent to relax the smooth muscle at the bladder outlet. He experienced multiple episodes of hypotension and was switched to a 5α-reductase inhibitor with improvement of his symptoms.

6
Is nodular hyperplasia of the prostate gland a risk factor for prostatic carcinoma? Have precursor lesions for prostate cancer been identified?
Show answer

No, BPH is not a premalignant lesion. However, in view of the high incidence of both BPH and prostatic adenocarcinoma, it is not uncommon for patients to present with both diseases. Prostatic intraepithelial neoplasia (PIN), a lesion that possesses the cytologic features of prostatic carcinoma but is noninvasive, is believed to be a precursor of invasive prostatic carcinoma.

Fifteen years later, he presented with low back pain of several months’ duration. Further questioning revealed recurrent urinary hesitancy with recent onset of dysuria. Digital rectal examination revealed a hard and irregular prostate gland.

Laboratory data included alkaline phosphatase 386 U/L (38–126 U/L) and PSA 103 ng/mL (0–4 ng/mL).

7
What is the significance of the patient’s laboratory results?
Show answer

The markedly elevated PSA is consistent with a diagnosis of prostatic adenocarcinoma. The increased alkaline phosphatase is highly suggestive of bony metastases since this enzyme is a marker of osteoblastic activity induced by the metastatic cells.

A radionuclide bone scan revealed widespread hot spots, and an x-ray of the spine revealed numerous radiodense bony lesions. Transrectal biopsies of the prostate gland were performed, and 4 of 12 cores were positive for adenocarcinoma. A biopsy of one of the bone lesions confirmed metastatic prostatic adenocarcinoma.

8
What are the histologic features of prostatic adenocarcinoma?
Show answer

Most prostate cancers are moderately differentiated adenocarcinomas composed of well-defined glands that may be crowded or show a cribriform (sieve-like) pattern. The glands are typically smaller than benign glands and are lined by a single uniform layer of cuboidal or low columnar epithelium with prominent nucleoli. The basal cell layer seen in benign glands is absent. The appearance of the glands is varied, but they are often crowded and back-to-back. There also is often a desmoplastic stromal response that results in a rock-hard consistency.

  • Histology, low power
  • Crowded glands
  • Cribriform glands
  • Histology, higher power
  • Malignant glands
  • Desmoplastic stroma
  • Nucleoli
9
How is prostate adenocarcinoma graded?
Show answer

Prostate cancer is graded by the Gleason system, in which cancers are stratified into five grades based on the extent of glandular differentiation. Grade 1 is the best differentiated and grade 5 tumors show essentially no glandular differentiation. Since most tumors contain more than one pattern, a grade is assigned to the dominant pattern and a second grade to the next most frequent pattern. These two numerical grades are then added to obtain a combined Gleason score. Tumors with only one pattern are treated as if the primary and secondary grades were the same. Presently, Gleason scores are combined into five “grade groups” that correlate with prognosis: grade group 1 has the best prognosis and grade group 5 has the worst. However, the most important factor in this patient’s prognosis is the tumor stage which, in view of the vertebral bone metastases, is high (Stage IV).

Grade Group 2 (3 + 4) Predominantly well-formed glands with a lesser component of poorly formed, fused or cribriform glands
Grade Group 3 (4 + 3) Predominantly poorly formed/fused/cribriform glands with a lesser component of well-formed glands
Grade Group 4 (4 + 4/3 + 5/5 + 3) Only poorly formed/fused/cribriform glands or predominantly mix of well-formed glands and lack of glands
Grade Group 5 (4 + 5/5 + 4/5 + 5) Lack gland formation (or with necrosis) with or without poorly formed/fused/cribriform glands

Histologic evaluation of the prostate needle cores showed a high-grade, aggressive tumor: Gleason score 3+5 (8), Gleason group 4. The patient was treated with a gonadotropin-releasing hormone agonist and localized radiation therapy for his back pain.

10
Why is a gonadotropin-releasing hormone agonist the appropriate treatment for this patient?
Show answer

In patients with clinically localized prostatic adenocarcinoma, standard treatment is radical prostatectomy and radiation therapy. In the setting of metastatic disease, the goal is androgen deprivation: like normal prostatic tissue, prostate cancer cells depend on trophic signals produced by the binding of dihydrotestosterone to androgen receptor, which drives the expression of genes that maintain the growth and survival of the tumor cells. This can be accomplished by bilateral surgical orchiectomy, as well as treatment with gonadotropin-releasing hormone agonists. Pharmacologic doses of gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) initially increase the release of luteinizing hormone (LH), which is essential for testosterone production in the testis, but with chronic exposure to GnRH the LH-producing cells in the anterior pituitary become desensitized and the release of LH is suppressed, resulting in “chemical castration.”

The patient’s serum PSA initially declined and his back pain improved; however, two years later, his PSA levels increased. A radionuclide scan showed increased growth of his previous skeletal lesions and additional metastases to the lungs. The patient was entered into a clinical trial but died six months later. An autopsy was performed.

11
What is the explanation for the patient’s disease progression?
Show answer

Although anti-androgen therapy induces remissions, androgen-independent clones almost invariably emerge, leading to disease progression and death. These mutant clones commonly continue to express many genes that in normal prostate are androgen-dependent, suggesting that mechanisms arise that reactivate androgen receptor function, even in the face of androgen deprivation therapy.

The prostate showed both BPH and prostatic adenocarcinoma. Sections of the vertebral column showed multiple foci of metastatic prostatic adenocarcinoma, consistent with the radiodense lesions seen on plain radiograph. Prostatic adenocarcinoma typically causes osteoblastic lesions (as opposed to osteolytic lesions).

  • Prostate, gross
  • Hyperplastic nodules
  • Adenocarcinoma
  • Vertebrae, gross
  • Metastatic carcinoma
12
What are some common metastatic sites for prostatic adenocarcinoma?
Show answer

Pelvic lymph nodes, bone, and lungs are common metastatic sites.

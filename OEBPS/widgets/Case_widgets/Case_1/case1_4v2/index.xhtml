  1. • Describe the clinical settings in which hyperplasia and hypertrophy occur.
  2. • Explain the mechanism by which hyperplasia and hypertrophy develop.

A 68-year-old man presented with urinary hesitancy, frequency, and nocturia. Digital rectal examination revealed a large, nodular, rubbery prostate gland with no hard regions. A serum prostate specific antigen (PSA) was 2 ng/mL (reference range 0–4 ng/mL).

A biopsy was negative for malignancy and, subsequently, a transurethral resection of prostate (TURP) was performed to improve his symptoms. The specimen consisted of multiple nodules composed of proliferating glandular elements and fibromuscular stroma.

A diagnosis of benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) was rendered. BPH is an extremely common cause of prostatic enlargement resulting from proliferation of stromal and glandular elements. Although the cause of BPH is incompletely understood, excessive androgen-dependent growth of stromal and glandular elements has a central role.

What is the difference between physiologic and pathologic hyperplasia?
Show answer

Physiologic hyperplasia ocscurs when there is a need to increase functional capacity, as in breast acini during lactation and hyperplasia of the thyroid acini in iodine deficiency. By contrast, pathologic hyperplasia results from inappropriate growth signals, as in BPH or hyperplasia of the thyroid gland in Graves disease caused by antibodies that mimic thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) action on thyroid acini, or physical stimuli, such as the left ventricular cardiac wall in response to hypertension.

How does hyperplasia differ from hypertrophy?
Show answer

Hyperplasia is an increase in cell number whereas hypertrophy is an increase in the size of the cells. Hyperplasia is an adaptive response in cells capable of replication, whereas hypertrophy occurs when cells have a limited capacity to divide. In pure hypertrophy there are no new cells, just larger cells containing increased amounts of structural proteins and organelles. Both result in an increase in the size the organ.

How does hyperplasia differ from neoplasia?
Show answer

Both physiologic and pathologic hyperplasias are limited by mechanisms that restrain cellular proliferation. In neoplasia, cell growth becomes autonomous (independent of growth factors) due to mutations that activate growth-promoting genes (oncogenes) and inactivate genes that restrict cell growth (tumor suppressor genes), in many cases becoming completely or largely growth factor independent.

4
What are the types of stimuli that induce hyperplasia and hypertrophy?
Show answer

Hyperplasia is induced mainly by growth factors, which stimulate cell division (mitosis) and proliferation of target cells, or stimuli (such as antibodies in Graves disease), that mimic the activity of growth factors. Hypertrophy occurs most often in response to mechanical triggers, such as increased workload. These may, in turn, induce the production of growth factors that stimulate cell metabolism so as to increase the synthesis of cellular components (such as organelles and cytoskeleton) without also stimulating cell proliferation. Hypertrophy typically occurs in post-mitotic cells that have left the cell cycle, such as cardiac muscle, skeletal muscle, and smooth muscle, which do not divide significantly because of their low proliferative ability.

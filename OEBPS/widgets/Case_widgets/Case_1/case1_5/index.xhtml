Objective
  1. • Compare and contrast the clinical settings and morphologic features of intracellular accumulations.

A 45-year-old, obese woman (BMI of 36) with a 10-year history of diabetes and hypertension was noted to have elevated liver enzymes (alkaline phosphatase and amino transferases) at her annual medical check up. Physical exam revealed hepatomegaly. She stated that she drank alcohol socially and did not smoke. Laboratory studies were negative for hepatitis A, B, and C. A liver biopsy was performed.

A mix of small and large fat droplets (seen as clear vacuoles) was seen within hepatocytes. Inflammation was not seen. A portal triad is present.

  • Liver, histology
  • Fat droplets
  • Portal triad
1
What is this condition called?
Show answer

This condition is steatosis, also called fatty change. It refers to any accumulation of triglycerides within parenchymal cells. It is most often seen in the liver, because this is the major organ involved in fat metabolism, but also may occur in heart, skeletal muscle, kidney, and other organs.


Steatosis may be caused by toxins, protein malnutrition, diabetes mellitus, obesity, or anoxia. alcohol use disorder, metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease, obesity, and adult onset diabetes are the most common causes of fatty change in the liver (fatty liver) in the western world.

2
What is the mechanism by which lipids accumulate in hepatocytes?
Show answer

Fat deposition is the result of multiple abnormalities, including: increased lipid loads (as in obesity), leading to increased mobilization of fatty acids from fat stores and increased uptake by hepatocytes; reduced catabolism and increased synthesis of lipids in the liver; and reduced production of lipoproteins, which transport lipids out of hepatocytes. Many of these abnormalities contribute to fatty liver in alcohol-associated liver disease and metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease.

3
Which other lipids can accumulate within cells?
Show answer

In addition to the triglycerides seen in steatosis, cholesterol and cholesteryl esters may accumulate in macrophages and smooth muscle cells within atherosclerotic plaques. If these cells rupture, extracellular cholesteryl esters may crystallize in the shape of long needles; these crystals are dissolved during the preparation of tissue sections, resulting in distinctive, empty clefts in the tissues. Cholesterol also may be seen within macrophages in the lamina propria in the gallbladder, a finding called cholesterolosis.

  • Atherosclerosis
  • Vessel wall
  • Fibrous cap
  • Cholesterol clefts
  • Cholesterolosis
  • Macrophages
4
What are some other intracellular accumulations that can be seen?
Show answer

In addition to lipids, proteins, glycogen, and pigments may accumulate within cells. Intracellular accumulations of proteins usually appear as rounded, eosinophilic droplets, vacuoles, or aggregates in the cytoplasm. Excessive intracellular deposits of glycogen are seen in patients with an abnormality in either glucose or glycogen metabolism (glycogen storage diseases, or glycogenoses). The most common exogenous pigment is carbon (coal dust), a ubiquitous air pollutant in urban areas. In the current age, it is also common to find pigment from tattoos within macrophages in skin biopsies or in biopsies of lymph nodes draining tattooed skin. Endogenous pigments include lipofuscin, an insoluble pigment that is also known as lipochrome or wear-and-tear pigment;  melanin; and hemosiderin.

  • Liposfuscin
  • Liver
  • Hemosiderin

Objectives
  1. • Describe the clinical settings in which metaplasia occurs.
  2. • Explain the mechanism by which metaplasia develops.
  3. • Recognize the long-term consequences of metaplasia.

A 41-year-old man presented to his primary care physician with a 6-month history of heartburn, regurgitation, and epigastric pain. Endoscopy revealed a large erythematous area in the distal esophagus suggestive of reflux esophagitis. Biopsy specimens were taken and showed squamous and glandular mucosa. In the glandular mucosa, abundant goblet cells were seen.

  • Biopsy specimen
  • Squamous
  • Glandular
  • Goblet cells
1
What is the significance of the goblet cells?
Show answer

Goblet cells are not usually present in the distal esophagus or the stomach; therefore, this is an example of metaplasia. Metaplasia is a change in which one adult cell type is replaced by another adult cell type. The vast majority of metaplasia involves one type of epithelium being replaced by a second type of epithelium. In this type of cellular adaptation, a cell type sensitive to a particular stress is replaced by another cell type better able to withstand the adverse environment. In this case, the glandular mucosa was exposed to gastric acid during reflux events and has undergone a change to become intestinal mucosa, which is better able to withstand low pH. In the esophagus, intestinal metaplasia is referred to as Barrett esophagus.

2
How does metaplasia occur?
Show answer

Metaplasia is thought to arise from the reprogramming of stem cells to differentiate along a new pathway rather than from a phenotypic change (transdifferentiation) of cells that have already differentiated.


3
What are some examples of metaplasia of columnar to squamous cells?
Show answer

In habitual cigarette smokers, the normal, ciliated columnar epithelial cells of the trachea and bronchi often are replaced by stratified squamous epithelial cells. Squamous metaplasia can also be seen in the urinary bladder in patients with chronic urinary tract infections or indwelling catheters.


4
What are potential negative consequences of metaplasia?
Show answer

Although the metaplastic epithelium has survival advantages, it may be dysfunctional and, for reasons that are not completely understood, is often at increased risk for neoplastic transformation. An example where important protective mechanisms are lost is seen with squamous metaplasia of lung airways, which results in loss of mucus secretion and ciliary clearance of particulate matter. As a result, smokers are prone to pulmonary infections. A cardinal example of increased risk of neoplasia in a metaplastic epithelium is seen in patients with Barrett esophagus, who are at increased risk for adenocarcinoma of the esophagus and undergo regular biopsy surveillance to allow early detection of malignant change.

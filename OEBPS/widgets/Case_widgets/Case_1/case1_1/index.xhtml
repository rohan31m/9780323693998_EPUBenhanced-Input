Objective
  1. • Compare and contrast the clinical setting and morphologic features of the different types of necrosis.

A 26-year-old woman presented to an antenatal clinic in the first trimester of pregnancy. She grew up in Mexico and moved to the United States two years ago. Review of systems revealed a 6-week history of non-productive cough and, despite her pregnancy, a 5-pound weight loss. Physical exam revealed a thin woman with coarse rales in her right upper lung field. Laboratory tests showed her to have a previously undiagnosed HIV infection with 354 CD4+ T cells/mm3(normal, 1000-1800 cells/mm3). A chest X-ray showed consolidation of the right upper lobe and mediastinal lymphadenopathy. A sputum test was positive for acid-fast bacilli and a PPD test was positive (18 mm of induration).

1
What would morphologic evaluation of her lymph node show?
Show answer

This patient has tuberculosis, a disease closely associated with caseating granulomas. The term

caseous necrosis refers to the “cheeselike” appearance of the lesion on gross exam. Histologically, the necrotic area appears as a structureless collection of fragmented or lysed cells and amorphous granular debris enclosed within a distinctive collection of activated macrophages called epithelioid cells. These cells have pink cytoplasm with indistinct boundaries and, in some cases, are fused to form multinucleate giant cells. Lymphocytes are often found around the periphery of the granulomas. This type of localized host response is called granulomatous inflammation (see Chapter 2).
  • Gross of lung
  • Caseous necrosis
  • Granuloma histology
  • Necrosis
  • Giant cells
2
How does caseous necrosis differ from coagulative necrosis?
Show answer

In contrast to the structureless amorphous material seen in caseous necrosis, in coagulative necrosis, the underlying tissue architecture is preserved for at least several days after death of cells in the tissue. The affected tissues have a firm texture, unlike the friable material of caseous necrosis.

  • Kidney infarct
  • Coagulative necrosis
3
In which settings is coagulative necrosis seen?
Show answer

Coagulative necrosis is characteristic of infarcts (areas of necrosis caused by ischemia) in all solid organs except the brain.

4
Which type of necrosis is seen in brain infarcts?
Show answer

Brain infarcts are characterized by liquefactive necrosis, in which the dead cells are completely digested, transforming the tissue into a viscous liquid that is eventually removed by phagocytes. This type of necrosis is also seen in certain bacterial infections that engender brisk infiltration of the infected tissue by neutrophils (creating a creamy fluid referred to as “pus”) and some fungal infections.

  • Brain infarct
  • Liquefactive necrosis
5
What are some of the common causes and mechanisms of coagulative, liquefactive, and caseous necrosis?
Show answer

Coagulative necrosis is most often the result of ischemia without superimposed infection (“sterile” injury); individual cells die and are digested but the underlying connective tissue that gives the tissue its structure is relatively preserved. Liquefaction is often the result of infection: microbes produce enzymes or recruit inflammatory cells that liberate enzymes that digest the tissue. The brain undergoes liquefactive necrosis with severe ischemia even without infection, perhaps because the brain does not contain a supporting scaffold to maintain the structure of the affected tissue. Caseous necrosis is seen in some infections that lead to strong, sustained macrophage activation; necrosis is caused by the products of the macrophages.

6
What is fibrinoid necrosis and when does it occur?
Show answer

Fibrinoid necrosis usually occurs in immune reactions in which complexes of antigens and antibodies are deposited in blood vessel walls; it can also be seen in severe hypertension. The deposited immune complexes and plasma proteins have a bright pink, amorphous appearance on H and E preparations that resembles fibrin, hence the term fibrinoid.

  • Blood vessel
  • Fibrinoid necrosis
7
What is the difference between necrosis and atrophy?
Show answer

Necrosis is a form of cell death that results from irreversible injury. For example, sudden complete obstruction of a coronary artery causes coagulative necrosis (infarction of the myocardium, also known as a heart attack). By contrast, a gradual decrease in blood supply that develops slowly allows the tissues to avoid cell death and adapt to ischemia by undergoing atrophy, defined as a reduction in the size of the cells and the organs. In atrophy caused by ischemia, reduced oxygen supply is matched by a reduction in cell size and cellular metabolism. Atrophic kidney cells show increase in autophagy. This atrophic kidney (right; normal kidney, left) from a patient with atherosclerotic stenosis of one renal artery shows atrophy primarily involves the cortex, which contains the most metabolically active cells.

  • Normal/atrophic kidney
  • Cortex
